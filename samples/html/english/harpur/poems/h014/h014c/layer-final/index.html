A String of Gems:

(But whether of the first water, or of a quality inferior thereto, the Author affirms not. Given without further comment.)

I.

The Apple of Life.

That bright dream of passion which only may be In Life’s rosy morning drawn over the brain, When once it dissolves, as a rainbow might flee, Or impairs, as the weary Moon fades in her wane; Oh, the Apple of Life has been pluckt from the Tree, And the leaves—the leaves only remain.

‘Tis true we may yet with the joyous and free Be numbered in Gayety’s fluttering train; The bright things of Nature still precious may be, And the load-star of Beauty attract us again; Yet the Apple of Life has been pluckt from the Tree, And the fading leaves only remain.

But when in the world’s cruel struggles we see Our hopes trodden down like the grass of the plain, Oh, then, as the banished gaze homeward, of thee, Thou bright Dream, in despair, we are dreaming again! 149 For the Apple of Life has been pluckt from the Tree, And the faded leaves only remain.
