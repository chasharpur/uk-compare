Popular Irish Songs.

The following is an imitative specimen of a species of popular —that is vulgar Irish songs. The knack of these songs is a running clatter of double-ending rhymes, that are pretty well [unclear] without being particularly true; and that carry just as much sense along with them as may claim to be travelling in the same direction. In making them one has but to slap dash away with the fury of a Pindar; and if any direct meaning does hapen to result from this poetic slap-dashery, it is well: if not, meaning—actual meaning is an element in the affair that was by no means bargained for. And when these songs are made and floated abroad, they are taken by the Irish peasantry as we take our wives—for better or worse; or as we receive a gift-horse, without looking it in the mouth. But to our imitative specimen.

Lord Potather.

There’s the great Lord Potather, So lofty of stature, And his Lady—the crather With bushells of charms; And during duration Their mighty fine station Shall bring no disgrace on Their bright coat of arms.

And sure he’s the lord then That well can afford then To relave all the poor men Than on him does call: And may the sweet saviou[r]s Persave his behaviou[r]s, And hould him in favor For ever and all.

If the above imitative specimen has any marked fault, it is that the sense of it is too apparent and too well held together. In this respect it is decidedly an improvement upon its models.
