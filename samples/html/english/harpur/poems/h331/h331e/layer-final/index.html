Doubtless this cricticism will be thought by many to be too bold in me. But What if a poem has been pronounced beautiful, or even perfect, by half Europe and all America, if, notwithstanding, to me it seems objectionable? I cannot away with it a whit the more for all this critical prescription. Many pieces that are highly lauded—nay, that it is quite a fashion to laud,—do not therefore satisfy me. For example, I am either wise or foolish enough, to think that Shelley’s famous Ode to a Skylark is overstrained; and that the similitudes by which it is illustrated though beautiful in themselves, are sometimes so irrelevant to the subject that they only tend to scatter the effect of it.

Why, so far from being in any wise prosaic in their structure, Shelley’s best poems ring all over, like bell metal, with the most wildly musical lines and variously modulated cadences; and are more unlike prose, perhaps, than those of any other Poet of his day. But what adequate treatment could the verse of such a poet receive from the musical insight of a pseudo Aristarchus like this? 234e —a creature that was either as tuneless as an ox, or as presumptuously ignorant of what he was dogmatising about, as Mr. Fowler often is—in reality—of the subjects of his criticisms. I say this advisedly. And yet this is the man who would assume the most prominent post in the formation of an Australian Literature! 235e Nay, possibly, like Bully Bottom in the Play, he would assume all the prominant parts, would be Pyramus and Thisbe and the Lion too! 235f But what one sure promise 235d of supreme literary excellence is there about the character of the man, to justify the assumption? What he has already done amongst us, is rather condemnatory of it. 234g For his poetry, as he himself confesses, is next nought, and his prose, while it is very questionable in quality, is decidedly 235a vicious in style. 235g ☞ It wants weight and strength in substance, and coherence and sobriety in method and manner. It is too like a chance-medley of glass beads of all sorts and sizes, slenderly strung together, or at best it is but like a deposit of diamond dust,—not a display of gems, but a heap of shining granules, petty and valueless, from all their glitter. 235b Even when he affects in it to be neat and elegant, as he sometimes does, he reaches only to that smart small-grained precision which may be proper enough to the theme of a schoolboy or the paragraph of a Report, but which is certainly disliked by all those who love to listen to the “large utterance of our elder gods”, or something akin to it.

And yet (I say again) this man, thus obviously defective in both nature and art, has presumption enough to talk about founding an Australian Literature. I say nothing about his being ignorantly impudent enough to presume a want of culture in an Australian Poet for being Australian born, because reckless dogmatism and rash assertion are exactly the weed-crops to be expected from this rank presumption aforesaid.

Finally, in the region of Poetry at least, Mr. Fowler is, beyond all controversy, self- damned as a critic. For though it may not be held to follow, that a just judge of poetry must be also a good poet; yet nevertheless, his poetic attempts, if he make any, will be at least characterised by good taste and good sense: whereas Mr. Fowler attempts of the kind so positively evince in almost every line, a 237c horrible violation 237e + either in thoughts or expression, of the chastity of taste or of the native integrity and good sense of the pleasant intelligence 237d And thus stands he, inevitably self-convicted as a Poet and Critic before any truly-commissioned Rhadamanthus of the Muses.
