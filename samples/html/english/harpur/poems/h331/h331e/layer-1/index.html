Doubtless this cricticism will be thought by many to be too bold in me. What if a poem has been pronounced beautiful, by half Europe and all A, if, notwithstanding, to me it seems objectionable? I cannot away with it a whit the more for all this critical prescription. Many pieces that are highly lauded—nay, that it is quite a fashion to laud, do not therefore satisfy me. For example, I am either wise or foolish enough, to think that Shelley’s Ode to a Skylark is overstrained; and that the similitudes by which it is illustrated though beautiful in itself, are not always irrelevant to the subject that they only tend to scatter it as a whole.

Finally, as a critic in the region of Poetry at least, Mr. Fowler is, beyond all controversy, self- damned as a critic. For though it may not be held to follow, that a just judge of poetry must be also a good poet; yet nevertheless, his poetic attempts, if he make any, will be at least chaste and sensible: whereas Mr. Fowler attempts of the kind so positively evince in almost every line, a 237c horrible salt of the sea against good [unclear] good taste either in thought or expression. 237d And thus stands he, inevitably self-convicted before any truly-commissioned Rhadamanthus of the Muses.
