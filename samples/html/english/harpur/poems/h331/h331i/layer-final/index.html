ON BLANK VERSE.

By Charles Harpur.

In this new province of the republic of letters, wherein criticlings (not critics), in their farthing rushlight prelections before small-beer societies, are wont to prate much about “superficial harmony” (whatever that may mean), and to hold most per- versely that

[THE “NEVERS” OF POETRY]

The musical in style Is best achieved through piggled metres vile; Or that a line is racy to the core, And bold, and truly eloquent, the more It striving seems to tear itself asunder, Like this—”Down there, i’the deep heart o’the thunder!” But for which, surely (out of chaos) none Might feign to find a sanction—save in fun!

Here, I would say, and under circumstances like these, it really behoves every one who is conscious that he, at all events, has come to some soundly critical judgments upon the matters mooted, to publish these, upon occasion, for the benefit of that portion of the reading public who might otherwise be very sillily mystified by the criticlings aforesaid. But in the present paper I shall begin with, and confine my re- marks entirely to—Blank Verse.
