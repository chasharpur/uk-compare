THE “NEVERS” OF POESY.

[REPUBLISHED, WITH ADDITIONS.]

Note.—I have re-written and re-published this poem with a special object in view. It would appear, from the 8th number of the Month, that its editor, Mr. Fowler, has come hither, all the way from Fatherland, for the express purpose of founding for us natives a national literature, on a critical basis; and as I would fain have at least a finger-end in such a pie, however compounded, I have thus put forth this particular piece in an im- proved shape, as my contribution to the canonical foundation of it: that is, if aught that may happen to appear out of the classic pages of the almighty Month, or that does not flow from the golden pen, or flash from the lightning-tipt tongue of Mr. Fowler himself, can in anywise contribute to the accomplishment of so tower- ing a design. Be this, however, as it may, W. C. Went- wwworth, Dr. Lang, Martin, Norton, Parkes, Halloran, Deniehy, Dalley, my humble self, and all who have hitherto vainly endeavoured to lay down a few of the foundation stones of an Australian literature, are bound to be profoundly grateful for the generous “intentions” of this sovereign Londoner. Yea, let us sing pæans to the Samson-like might of the man who has thus come to do for us the work we could not of ourselves. May his shadow never be less! and to Gehenna with the Philis- tines who would prod out his eyes!

Never say aught in Verse, or grave or gay, That you in p

rose would be ashamed to say, Or let your head in lofty numbers tell What your heart deems best fit for doggerel. Never in rhyme pretend to tears, unless True feeling rains them in unfeigned distress, Or save when doubts that over Love may lour Like summer clouds, break in a sunny shower, Or some dream-grief, with such a mournful strain As night winds have in pine wood, stirs your brain, To shake them, dew-like, o’er the flowers that bloom In the wild dark, round Joy’s imagined tomb. Never laugh either in Song’s measured tone If all the wit is in the laugh alone, Or all the humour in a “ha-ha-hà” That should clink (very sheepishly) with “baa.” Never write madly, as if nimble wits Like Arabs, showed best in their wandering fits: Yet never dread the kingliest critic’s ire So long as genius, like a bride of fire Lies on your breast, to mission and inspire; For though thy moods to most be caviäre, They should have voice, or genius were not there. But never sing of aught (even though in love) That is not worth some solid thinking of.* Never heed whether a line strictly goes By learned rule, if,—brook like,—it warbles as it flows; Or if, in concord with the thought, it fills Fast forward, like a torrent fast flooding from the hills. Never write slipslop—never, I would say, If you can help it: only asses bray! And if you would be readable and terse, Never be metaphysical in verse. Never transform in the wild vein of Poe The rote “Poor Jacko” of some stray pet crow, (Not even with the warranty of fiction) Into a strange blue-devilish prediction. Never, like Halloran, mistake your “granny,” Though shawled and bonneted, for some blooming “Annie.”† Never in Fowler’s Shelley-mocking strain, Turn a rich sunset into a red rain Cold drenching some poor-devil Poet’s brain; Or in the wildest of your similes Make birds to “hive“—whate’er you do with bees.‡ Never think harshness the best foil to raise And relish sweetness: few love craggy lays! Yet never be you glib, when Passion’s force Should ridge your style,—as by a tempest hoarse The deep is roughened into waves that roar At heaven, or burst in thunder on the shore. Never be such a Pagan as to deem That truth or beauty would diviner seem For some deformity, grotesquely rude, Prowling for evil in its neighbourhood, If such deformity breathes not the air Of Nature—being found, not conjured there. And never to be graceless be you fain, Till to be graceful you have tried in vain. Never be cheated—never may you be— Into the cramp belief, that Poetry Must of necessity in soul be one With the mere form of Verse, if it but deftly run, Or pour, as with a mill-wheel’s vigorous cher, A rhyming clatter hard upon the ear. Never believe that Song a license knows For aught that would be balderdash in prose; Or that pure reason may at any time Be circumvented easily with rhyme; Or that because with many words you’re fraught, There must be under them some flood of thought. Never pay for your rhymes, however ringing With all the meaning of the song you’re singing, Lest the too-knowing reader reckon drily The cost, and vow you bargained for them vilely. Never set one rhyme as a trap, to clutch Another:—it hurts their fellow-feeling much! And never, like a Tinker shredding tin, Forecut your metal, and then hammer in As much of sense and metre as you may, Calling what comes on’t—a Pindaric lay. Never compel a simile that won’t Take service without forcing;—if it don’t As of itself into your poem flow, Be true to liberty, and let it go. Never inflate an image;—if it will Not suit you without straining, all your skill Shall only make this more apparent still. Never, with your poetic stylus, goad Slow thoughts or feeling; they but overload The jaded Muse, and have a hatred strong Against the being goaded into song; And what is sadder—and of this beware— Their hate is certain death to it when there. Never reject a homely sounding phrase That your whole meaning earnestly displays, For one made current by some courtly wit Which only indicates some shade of it, Or like the infusion of an alien potion, Flattens the native spirit of the notion, Or which, it may be (for it so may fall), Does not express what you would mean at all. Never, Procrustes-like, stretch out a live And regular battalion verse of five Well measured feet (just to amuse the ear) Into an Alexandrine grenadier. Never describe a cabbage, with great throes Poetic, under the proud name of Rose; And never,—if you’d rather not provoke Some grinning critic’s always frosty joke, Endeavour to transmute by dint of ryhme Mere brickbats into diämonds sublime. Never exalt vagaries to a station But due to true flights of Imagination— Gas-charged balloons, put vainly floating o’er thee For morning clouds all charged with Nature’s glory. And never let aught pass your bardic nose As sweet in verse that would smell vile in prose. And never, if you would be extant long, Imitate England’s newest sons of song; Those crude, affected, pap-fed mystics, who Rather than not sing in a manner new, Sing horrible whimsies, in a style that quite Out Crashaws Crashaw’s in its wildest flight, Or that, though meretriciously ornate, Is yet so slipshod in its metric gait, That it looks vastly like some splay-foot strain Of psalmody run mad or grown profane.** And lastly, never take for gospel all Your friends say of your genius, when they call Its merits o’er; but at the same time see That you do never think yourself to be So great an ass as your known foes declare They do most solemnly believe you are. Classical such writing can never become, until Wordsworth, Shelley, Coleridge, and even Keats are accounted barba- rians; and the reader who conceits it beautiful is but admiring his own conceit. One thing is clear,—at best it is poetry of so indeterminate a quality, as to depend wholly for its value,—high or low,—upon the mood in which we regard it.
