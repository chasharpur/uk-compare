THE “NEVERS” OF POESY.

Never say aught in Verse, or grave or gay, That you in prose would be ashamed to say, Or let your head in lofty numbers tell What your heart deems best fit for doggerel. Never in rhyme pretend to tears, unless True feeling sheds them in unfeigned distress, Or save when doubts that over Love may lour Like summer clouds, break in a sunny shower, Or some dream-grief, with such a mournful strain As night winds have in pine wood, stirs your brain, To shake them, dew-like, o’er the flowers that bloom In the wild dark, round Joy’s imagined tomb. Never laugh either in Song’s measured tone If all the wit is in the laugh alone, Or all the humour in a “ha-ha-hà” That should clink (very sheepishly) with “baa.” Never write madly, as if nimble wits Like Arabs, showed best in their wandering fits: Never be murky of set purpose, since You only thereby may the more evince, That the Sublime is then alone made sure When, like a morning alp, it breaks from the obscure: For though it blind awhile our dazzled sight, There’s no real darkness in excessive light. Yet never dread the kingliest critic’s ire So long as genius, like a bride of fire Lies on your breast, to mission and inspire; For though thy moods to most be caviäre, They should have voice, or genius were not there. But never sing of aught (even though in love) That is not worth some solid thinking of.* Never heed whether a line strictly goes By learned rule, if,—brook like,—it warbles as it flows; Or if, in concord with the thought, it fills Fast forward, like a torrent fast flooding from the hills. 134 Never write slip-slop—never, I would say, If you can help it: only asses bray! (And sure ’tis but the asinine sublime That’s best evolved though a jog-trot of rhyme, Or lines, like whinnys, hark, and ending short, Doleful and wheezy, in a kind of snort!) 135 And never, if you would be very terse, Be very metaphysical in verse. 139c Never transform in the wild vein of Poe The rote “Poor Jacko” of some stray pet crow, (Not even with the warranty of fiction) Into a strange blue-devilish prediction. Never, like Halloran, mistake your “granny,” Though shawled and bonneted, for some blooming “Annie.” 136 + For though you aim at compl[im]enting merely The “old girl”—yet, the young one‘s handled queerly. 139 Or say that aught is “fading like a star” Because its glory seems to pale afar, Since stars do not fade, but shine on no less, Though unbeheld through our weaksightedness; And since a true trope should not rest on fancy, But claim a universal relevancy. Never in Fowler’s Shelley-mocking strain, Turn a rich sunset into a red rain Cold drenching some poor-devil Poet’s brain; Or in the wildest of your similes Make birds to “hive“—whate’er you do with bees.‡ Or with him think the musical in style Is best achieved through piggled metres vile, And that a line is racy to the core, And bold, and truly eloquent, the more It striving seems to tear itself assunder, Like this one, “Down i’ the deep heat o’ the thunder”! But for which surely (out of chaos) none Might feign to find a sanction—save in fun. Never with plundered im[a]gery make A Paphian medley;—as if one should take Filched jewels of all sorts, and think to run Their several special values into one More special still; but whi[c]h, when thus recast, Turns out a Trinket Frankenstein at last;— Then call (the more its species kind to prove) The impious miscreation Life and Love! Never deem harshness the best foil to raise And relish sweetness: few love craggy lays! Yet never be you glib, when Passion’s force Should ridge your style,—as by a tempest hoarse The deep is roughened into waves that roar At heaven, or burst in thunder on the shore. Never be such a Pagan as to deem That truth or beauty would diviner seem For some deformity, grotesquely rude, Prowling for evil in its neighbourhood, 140 If such deformity breathes not the air Of Nature—being found, not conjured there. And never to be graceless be you fain, Till to be graceful you have tried in vain. Never be cheated—never may you be— Into the cramp belief, that Poetry Must of necessity in soul be one With the mere form of Verse, if it but deftly run, Or pour, as with a mill-wheel’s vigorous cher, A rhyming clatter hard upon the ear. Never believe that Song a license knows For aught that would be balderdash in prose; Or that pure reason may at any time Be circumvented easily with rhyme; Or that because with many words you’re fraught, There must be under them some flood of thought. Never pay for your rhymes, however ringing With all the meaning of the song you’re singing, Lest the too-knowing reader reckon drily The cost, and vow you bargained for them vilely. Never set one rhyme as a trap, to clutch Another:—it hurts their fellow-feeling much! And never, like a Tinker shredding tin, Forecut your metal, and then hammer in As much of sense and metre as you may, Calling what comes on’t—a Pindaric lay. Never compel a simile that won’t Take service without forcing;—if it don’t As of itself into your poem flow, Be true to liberty, and let it go. Never inflate an image;—if it will Not suit you without straining, all your skill Shall only make this more apparent still. Never, with your poetic stylus, goad Slow thoughts or feeling; they but overload The jaded Muse, and have a hatred strong Against the being goaded into song; And what is sadder—and of this beware— Their hate is certain death to it when there. Never reject a homely sounding phrase That your whole meaning earnestly displays, For one made current by some courtly wit Which only indicates some shade of it, Or like the infusion of an alien potion, Flattens the native spirit of the notion, Or which, it may be (for it so may fall), Does not express what you would mean at all. Never, Procrustes-like, stretch out a live And regular battalion verse of five Well measured feet (just to amuse the ear) Into an Alexandrine grenadier. Never describe a cabbage, with great throes Poetic, under the proud name of Rose; And never,—if you’d rather not provoke Some grinning critic’s always frosty joke, Endeavour to transmute by dint of ryhme Mere brickbats into diämonds sublime. Never exalt vagaries to a station But due to true flights of Imagination— Gas-charged balloons, put vainly floating o’er thee For morning clouds all charged with Nature’s glory. And never let aught pass your bardic nose As sweet in verse that would smell vile in prose. And never, if you would be extant long, Imitate England’s newest sons of song; 141a Those crude, affected, pap-fed mystics, who Rather than not sing in a manner new, Sing horrible whimsies, in a style that quite Out Crashaws Crashaw’s in its wildest flight, 243 Or that, at best, would be as sweet as hony Through fiddling iterations quaint and funny: For even when most ambitious and ornate, So slipshod is it in its metric gait, The As to look vastly like some splay foot strain Of psalmody run mad or grown prophane. 141c And lastly, never take for gospel all Your friends say of your genius, when they call Its merits o’er; but at the same time see That you do never think yourself to be So great an ass as your known foes declare They do most solemnly believe you are.

Note.—The Pathetic in poetry (and in all fiction) is never so truly effectual as when it is perfectly unforced, both in subject and manner. To be supremely exquisite, it must be given to flow naturally out of the matter in hand, not be cast about like a mourning cloak. Hence whenever a poet takes up his pen of set purpose to search our hearts to the very core, it is more than likely that he will only search our patience to an un- pleasant degree, or disgust us with some bloated mockery of sorrow. Any undertaker can bespangle a coffin with tinsel-headed nails; to bedrop it with tears is another matter, of which Nature, and not Artifice, must be the principal getter-up.

In so much does the truest pathos result from an un- forced treatment of a doleful matter, that we are always most nearly touched by such pathetic descriptions as appear to come in quite incidentally. There is a fine example of this in Henry Taylor’s Philip Van Artevilde. I know of no passage in any author, more sweetly sorrowful; more holy in the moral beauty of its sadness. It is a description of the appearance in death, or two children, who perished of famine during the seige of Ghent, and is spoken by Clara the sister of the hero.

“He plucked aside the curtain of the couch, And there two children’s bodies lay composed. They seemed like twins of some ten years of age, And they had died so nearly both together, He could not say which first: and being dead, He put them, for some fanciful affection, Each with its arm about the other’s neck, So that a fairer sight I had not seen, Than those two children, with their little faces So thin and wan, so calm, and sad, and sweet. 142 I looked upon them long, and for a while I wished myself their sister, and to lie With them in death as they did with each other. I thought that there was nothing in the world I could have loved so much: and then I wept.

Again:—A pathetic effect in poetry is never more entire than when, by the seeming abruptness of un- mixed passion. it is left, as it were, somewhat short of itself—but suggestively so; or than when, in its means, it is brought bluffly out, and as suddenly terminated, like a natural crag; upon whose rigid verge we are left hanging, in the agony of our sympathy, as over some dim, unmeasured profound of loss unspeakable. No doubt this fullness of effect is often thus achieved through the suggestion of art; but then it is true Art, itself the suggestion of Nature. There is a short piece by Wordsworth, which will amply illustrate my position.

“She dwelt among the untrodden ways Beside the springs of Dove, A Maid whom there were none to praise, And very few to love.

A violet by a mossy stone Half hidden from the eye,— Fair as a star, when only one Is shining in the sky.

She lived unknown and few could know When Lucy ceased to be: But she is in her grave—and oh, The difference to me!

Here this marvellous little poem ends—or rather breaks off—and here both Nature and Art declare that it should terminate, however abruptly. The entireness of the pathetic effect would be manifestly impaired by the addition of a single word. And hence, there is no poem in the language, perhaps, more suggestive of the conclusion, that true art and true nature are identical in their essence.

See on this head some of the poems of Robert Browning; a writer who by way of fishing for a “fit audience,” has taken Obscurity into his poetical system, not merely as a sometimes “welcome guest,” but as a tenth Muse—and this to the exclusion, very frequently of the whole orthodox Nine. And lo! he has his reward. Every literary mystic, who would be credited with a capacity for seeing farther than other men—not indeed through a mile stone, but through a poetical myth—has become his critical worshipper,—the worshipper, not of the light that is verily in him, but of the darkness.

Hence one of these wholesale admirers of his genius, when poring after the meaning of some of his poems (of them the most cherished) may be aptly likened to a cockney staring into some deep but turbid pond, in the hope of seeing a shining fish at the bottom. At last he sees, or thinks he sees, something that may be a fish: far down there amongst the weeds, or between those stones half imbedded in the mud. “It must be a fish,” says he to himself: “what else can it be? A fish it certainly is—and it just as certainly be one of the rarest beauty. What eyes I have!”

But admirers of this sort are never true lovers of true poetry. They are intellectually devoted to nothing but the seeming of a nice mental singularity—an exclusive ideal refinement. Hence any author who happens to be plentifully beshadowed with obscurity is sure to attach them in shoals; because these very obscurities are the vantage grounds—or rather the fastnesses—of their pretensions to a deeper critical insight than their literary neighbours.

For myself, however, I would as lief have my mouth charged with sawdust, as to be treated to poetry that I cannot make sense of, upon some ground either of fact or fable. I care not whence it may bring its freight, so long as this pertains to humanity, old or new, and answers to some felt liberty of the imagination or probable condition of the soul. I care not how wild it may be, so long as it has, or indicates, some prevailing tendency of intellect or passion. I care not even if it be mad, if it only be naturally so—the destempered semblance of a real distemper, and thence intelligible to something within us. But to have sheer absurdity or wilful nonsence palmed upon me for poetry, puts me beyond my patience, and makes me bristle into a critic.

Not the less am I aware, how- -ever, that a certain ideal vagueness is sometimes an element of great beauty in poetry. But then it must come naturally out of, and be warranted by the subject, and not for its own sake be superinduced upon it. It is, in fact in its integrity but the result of a true, though subtle, seeing and siezure in a dark terrible matter, of what lies inmost,—potentially, and tends most to determine that outward expression of character, which may still be, itself, too elusive for picture. But this is a very different thing from affected or wilful obscurity, which is always a mere trick, and mostly but on a par with the smutching of one’s face for the purpose of bewildering a simpleton.—In the description of the horse of Death in the Apocalypse, the visionary effect of the epithet ”pale” affords a striking example of the kind of vagueness I mean. 145 Pale, as applied to the color and substance of the horse of such a rider, is the word of all others, best calculated to impress us with an idealism at once vast and dreary, vague and terrible; and the more vast and terrible, because of its dreary vagueness.

In like manner, the dreadful sublimity of Milton’s description of Death, sitting at the gates of Hell, results mainly, if not entirely, from the terrible vagueness of the images: as the reader will perceive—

“————The other Shape, If shape it might be called, that shape had none Distinguishable in member, joint, or limb, Or substance might be called, that shadow seemed, For each seemed either. Black it stood as night, Fierce as ten Furies, terrible as Hell, And shook a dreadful dart; what seemed its head The likeness of a kingly crown had on.”

And afterwards, when he would incidentally characterise the same dread Being, at a stroke, by such epithetical figures (if I may so express myself) as the “grisly Terror,” and the “grim Feature” the mighti- ness of the effect comes looming upon us through the same mystic element—a certain ideal vagueness.

In a like view, and depending for its sublimity upon the same element of mysterious vagueness, is the following account of a Vision in the book of Job:—

“In thoughts from the visions of the night, when deep sleep falleth on men, fear came upon me, and trembling, which made all my bones to shake. Then a Spirit passed before my face; the hair of my flesh stood up. It (the Spirit) stood still, but I could not disern the form thereof: an Image was before mine eyes—there was silence, and I heard a Voice, saying &c.”

But it is not only in sublime poetry, that this ideal vagueness constitutes an element of beauty. Milton’s Lycidas is an example, almost throughout, of how exquisitely it may befall a subject purely pathetic. The impersonation, in that poem, of the river Cam (for instance) contains an example in point—

“Next Camus, reverend sire, went footing slow, His mantle hairy, and his bonnet sedge, Inwrought with figures dim.”

Now in this instance, no doubt the poet refers, in fact, to the filamentous tracery, which, on close inspection were observable in the sedge, composing the bonnet of the river God; but the subtle manner in which he so alludes to this unobvious charactery, as to work out of it, a farther poetic effect—namely, and indeal apprehension of a certain vague and mystic beauty, is very noticeable; and no less remarkably efficient.

The following passage has also been paraded upon us before by an Australian critic, with as great a flourish, as if Tennyson had written no other, and indeed had need to write no other, to be a very great poet.

When the flowers come again, mother, Beneath the waning light You’ll never see me more In the long grey fields at night, When from the dry dark wold The summer airs blow cool, On the oat-grass and the sword-grass And the bullrush in the pool.

But this is nature looked at as through a microscope, and broken up into tiny particulars; and even the sweetness of the verse, when considered along with the matter of it, tends only the more to impress upon a judicious reader, the inadequacy of its inspiration. The very nicety of the metrical flow of such a passage, when thus closely considered, becomes too petty and mechanical merely, to be be per- menently charming; as the airs of a musical snuffbox, the moment we examine into the square and compass methods by which they are generated, become curiously divested of their fairy character.

It is not, however, to be inferred from these remarks, that I am disposed to decry Tennyson’s genius generally. I admire many of his poems, and even my dislike to others of them 151a is, perhaps, purely natural—that is constitutional. He is an old world “Towney”—a dresser of parterres, and peeper into Parks. I am a man of the woods and mountains—a wielder of the axe, and mainly conversant with aboriginal nature. Hence Poetry, with him, should be nice and dainty rather than wise and hearty; while, to effect my admiration, it must be free, bold, and open, even at the risk of being rude. Hence too Mere refinement, though sickly, is with him Beauty; while that heated super- subtle affectation of the thing which is so much manifested in our newest English Poetry, is to me distasteful,—and even ominous of decline, like the cheek-flush of a hectic.
