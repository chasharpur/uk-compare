POEMS BY CHARLES HARPUR.

I.

THE “NEVERS” OF POESY.

Never say aught in verse, or grave or gay, That you in prose would be ashamed to say; Or let your head in lofty numbers tell What your heart deems best fit for doggerel. Never heed much the kingliest Critic’s ire So long as Genius, like a bride of fire Lies on thy breast, to mission and inspire: For though thy moods to most be caviäre, They should have voice, or Genius were not there. But never sing of aught (even though in love) That is not worth some solid thinking of. Never heed whether a line strictly goes By learned rule, if,—brook-like,—it warbles as it flows. Never write slip-slop—never, I would say, If you can help it: only asses bray! And if you would be readable and terse, Never be metaphysical in verse. Never transform, in the wild vein of Poe, The rote “Poor Jacko” of some stray pet crow (Not even with the warranty of Fiction) Into some strange blue-devilish prediction. Never think harshness the best foil to raise And relish sweetness: few love craggy lays! And never to be graceless be you fain, Till to be graceful you have tried in vain. Never be such a Pagan as to deem That Truth or Beauty would diviner seem For some deformity, grotesquely rude, Prowling for evil in its neighbourhood, If such deformity breathes not the air Of Nature—being found, not conjured there. Never be cheated—never may you be— Into the cramp belief, that Poetry Must of necessity in soul be one With the mere form of Verse, if it but deftly run: Or that pure reason may at any time Be circumvented easily with rhyme; Or that because with many words you’re fraught, There must be under them some flood of thought. Never pay for your rhymes, however ringing, With all the meaning of the song you’re singing, Lest the too-knowing reader reckon drily The cost, and vow you bargained for them vilely! Never set one rhyme as a trap, to clutch Another:—it hurts their fellow-feeling much. Never compel a simile that won’t Take service without forcing;—if it don’t As of itself into your poem flow, Be true to Liberty, and let it go. Never inflate an image;—if it will Not suit you without straining, all your skill Shall only make this more apparent still. Never, with your poetic stylus, goad Slow thoughts or feelings; they but overload The jaded Muse and have a hatred strong Against the being goaded into song: And what is sadder—and of this beware— Their hate is certain death to it when there. Never displace a homely sounding phrase That all your meanings length and breadth displays, For one made current by some courtly wit, Which only indicates some shade of it; Or which (for sometimes so ’tis found to fall) Does not express what you would mean at all. Never stretch out, Procrustes-like, a live And regular battalion verse of fire Well measured feet (just to amuse the ear) Into an Alexandrine grenadier. Never describe a cabbage, with great throes Poetic, under the proud name of Rose! And never,—if you’d rather not provoke Some grinning Critic’s always frosty joke, Endeavour to transmute by dint of rhyme Mere brickbats into diämonds sublime. Never exact vagaries to a station But due to true flights of Imagination— Gas-charged balloons, put vainly floating o’er thee, For morning clouds all charged with Nature’s glory! And never let aught pass your bardic nose As sweet in verse, that would smell vile in prose. And lastly, never take for Gospel all Your friends say of your genius, when they call Its merits o’er; but, at the same time, see That you do never think yourself to be So great an ass as your known foes declare They do most solemnly believe you are.
