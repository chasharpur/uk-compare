III.

A Look from the North Head.

I looked from the North Head—and saw the sea Spread heavenward round, like Faith’s eternity. Faintly the Moon was in the east descried, And yet more faintly mirrored in the wide Expanse of waters;—brokenly, as these In distant ridges writhed beneath the breeze: A warm and breath like breeze, that at the shore Mingled its sigh with the wave’s gentle roar: While in the offing, through the twilight grey, A Ship went dreaming on her outward way.

Note.—Were a student to ask me, in reference to this piece, why do you say of the ship, that she went dreaming on her outward way? I could by no means answer him so exactly, as to the why and wherefore of some material fact or logical conclusion. All, indeed, I could say would be, that such was the ideal impression which her appearance made upon me, as the musing observer of it:—this impression itself, perhaps, coming through some fugitive notion, that the humanity with which she was charged, as with a soul, was even then forecasting in vision, the probabilities of the voyage. 6d And I record this remark, forestalling as it were, because a critic, or rather a criticling, of the botanically hyper species, would be otherwise sure to cavil at an expression so independently imaginative.
