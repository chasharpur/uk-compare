Stanzas.

This World how bright! Or when its day with sunny beams is boon, Or when its night Is robed with stars and crownëd with the moon.

How fair and sweet! With many a flowery bank and shady grove, Where young hearts meet To melt into each other’s dream of Love.

How fond and true! With Mothers yearning o’er their cradled joy, And Fathers, who Amid their boys put on again the Boy.

How frank and brave! As the abode of some who forth would throng, And die to save Whate’er is manly from the thralls of Wrong.

How wise and rich! Temples possessing by the Muses blest, And Graves in which Sages and patriots, bards and martyrs rest.

Here then, my soul, Pause, and forbear on things less bright to seize; Nor search the scroll Of Fate aught further—be thy peace with These. x

x Several things held valuable in these verses, saith that most circumspective moral negation, the square and compass Utilitarian, are only fancifully so—particularly the Graves, however large-looking by virtue of an arbitrary capital G; all the best kind of which articles, possessed by any people of the nineteenth century, were capitally exchanged for one capital railroad—or the capital requisite for its construction. But he is wrong: they would not. The prime object of society is, or should be, the perfection of Man; not the multiplication of the physical conveniences of life, which are of value only in so far as they also conserve that object. In other words, fine human beings are its end, and all other fine things, properly used, only its means to that end. And fifty graves therefore, contain- -ing the ashes of as many [unclear] truly illustrious men, were a 173 richer inheritance to any people, for all time, than even twice that number of railroads, without there (in lieu) with an equal tally of steamers given in: for they were the pious altars of its pa- -ternal mentality, the sacred landmarks of its moral progress, and the oracular shrines of its spirit-communion with the glory of the Future.—Beyond clearing him out of the way thus far, however, I am not at all disposed to meddle with Master Utility. In calculating the interest tables, constructing the baking ovens, and eke—the railroads of social life, he is laboring commendably, and only becomes a charlatan when he would theorise the Rule of Three into a providential type of the mysterious microcosm, Man himself, with its unfathomable depths and probational shoals of thought and passion—its unpathed wildernesses of poesy— its mighty mountains of impassable will—its pyramids of suffering and monuments of fate.

C.H.
