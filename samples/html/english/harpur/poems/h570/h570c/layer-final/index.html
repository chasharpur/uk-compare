Stanzas.

This World how bright! Or when the day with sunny beams is boon, Or when the night Is robed with stars and crownëd with the moon.

How fair and sweet! With flowery bank adorned and shady grove, Where young hearts meet To melt into each other’s dream of love.

How fond and true! With Mothers yearning o’er their cradled joy, And Fathers who Amid their boys put on again the Boy.

How frank and brave! As the abode of some who forth would stand, 104 And die to save Whate’er is manly from Dishonor’s brand.

How wise and rich! Temples possessing by the Muses blest, And Graves in which Sages and patriots, bards and martyrs rest.

Here then, my soul, Pause, and forbear on things less bright to sieze; Nor search the scroll Of Fate aught further—be thy peace with these.

Note. Several things held valuable in these verses, saith that most self-circumspective moral negation, the square-and-compass Utilitarian, are only fancifully so—particularly the Graves, however large= =looking in the Text by virtue of an arbitary Capital: all the best kind of which articles, possessed by any people of the nineteenth century, were capitally exchanged for one capital railroad, or the capital requisite for its construction!

But he is wrong, wise as he is: they were not. The prime object of Society is, or should be, the per= =fection of Man; not the multiplication of the physical conveniences of life, which are of value only in so far as they also conduce to that object. In other words, fine human beings are its end, and all other fine things, properly employed, but its means to that end. And fifty Graves therefore, containing the ashes of as many truly illustrious Men /not mere Prince Alberts/ were a richer inheritance to any people, for all time, than eventwice that number of railroads /in lieu/, with an equal tally of steamers given in, to boot the bargain. For they were the pious altars of its paternal mentality: they were the sacred landmarks of its moral progress: they were 105 the oracular shrines of its spirit-communion with the glory of the future!

Beyond clearing him out of the way thus far, however, I am not at all disposed to meddle with Master Utility. In calculating the interest tables, con- -structing the baking ovens—and eke the railroads of social life, he is laboring commendably, and only becomes a Charlatan when he would theorise the Rule of Three into a providential type of the mysterious microcosm, Man himself; with its unfathomable depths and probational shoals of thought and passion—its unpathed wildernesses of poesy—its mighty mountains of impassable will— its pyramids of suffering and monuments of fate!

Precarious then in its moral results as society may be at present, still we must not endeavour, for the mere sake of a selfish safety, to saw it up into a sort of animal clockwork, such as the Chinese system of community superinduces and perpetuates—for who out of the Celestial Empire should rejoice at such a consummation? In overstepping the narrow proverbial morality, and tame social parallelism, of even a Franklin, we are often obeying, I believe, far higher laws than those we transgress, and working out results which, though not dreamt of in Paley, are yet projecting the finalities of our being into another and a mightier world: the ampler exercise our whole nature hath in this life, fitting us no doubt for a commensurately enlarged spirituality in the 106 next. It operates also, transmissively, to the developement of a fuller general capacity in our posterity. And hence, even poverty, with its inevitable trouble and heart-suffering, if educated, and thence productive of a philosophical spirit of thought and feeling, is infinitely a more prizeable thing than that sure worldly prosperity, the most obvious tendency of which is to fatten men, and even women, into uneatable bacon.
