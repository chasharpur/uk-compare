A Poet’s Home.

As erewhile pictured in a sweet day-dream Amid the cloudlands of Hope vernal sky, In soul-born colors—such as only beam, To enhance all beauty, through the Poet’s eye.

Part I.

Here in this lonely rill-engirdled spot, The world forgetting, by the world forgot, And with One vowed to me with guileless lips, How sweet to draw as covertly from Time As from its rock yon shaded fountain slips, My yet remaining prime.

Here early rising from a sinless bed, How sweet it were to view Aurora shed Her first white glances o’er the dusky trees; When powdered as with pearls the vine-bowers gleam Through the grey dawn like fairy palaces— The fabrics of a dream.

And while the clouds imbibed a ruddy hue, And purple streaks grained yon ethereal blue, By the glad voice of every early bird,— As some full lake by breezes in their glee Is rippled into smiles,—so sweetly stirred My spirit then should be.

And as like burning bullion brightened still The cloud-hung orient, o’er yon mist-ringed hill 166 I’d watch the Sun’s imperial chariot come, Flooring the glades with flakes of crystal fire, And the green spaces round my rural Home Where slept mine heart’s desire.

When, if sweet memories of her sleeping smile Should my devotion thitherward beguile, Cheating the Morn of its observance meet, My happy voice should not be wanting long, To urge her forth with adurations sweet Or wake her with a song.

“Awake my fair One! for the glowing skies Desire thee, and a thousand flowery eyes Look for thy coming from each pathway side; With all things fresh and beautiful and bright The Earth’s illumined as she were a bride— Arise, my best delight!

“Awake! arise! lest the resenting sun Ere long deny the rose of health to one So slothful, and thence backward in the dues All Beauty oweth to his sovereign state! Arise! lest even the indulgent Muse Prōnounce thee reprobate.

“What might be deeper than the heavens o’erbending? What rarer than the varied purples blending Amid the green cones of the sharpening heights? What gladder than the runnel’s silvery fall? And yet my spirit, ’mid all these delights, Asks thee—the crown of all.”Joined

Joined by the Angel of my life—the Day’s Full glory, setting the moist hills a-blaze, How lovelier now for her dear loveliness! The birds—the stream—the forest’s leafy stir— Catch from her voice a double power to bless, And the flowers breathe of her!

The dews are brighter for her love-bright eyes, And the flowers sweeter for the sweet surprise In every gesture of her gentle face! So widely Love’s invisible spirit flings The visible enrichment of its grace O’er all regarded things.

Filled with the fresh keen life that so sublimes Both mind and body, we should then betimes Repair us to our cheerful morning meal, Not more attuned by thankfulness of heart Well to enjoy, than willing in our weal To spare a stranger part.

Light cates, like snowy sponge, with eggs fresh laid; A clean-cut comb of honey well displayed Upon a shining platter; fragrant tea, Frothed freely over and made exquisite With curdless cream prepared deliciously: To such fare should we sit.

Observing through the windows, yardward out, Our damsel with the poultry thronged about, They also breakfasting—a motley crowd! With strutting chanticlere amongst them seen Conspicuously splendid, and as proud As Lucifer in his mien.

168

Yea, proud as splendid, with his brave combed head, And burnished neck,—and eke the golden red That from his wings’ sleek lustre so observed, Should to his tesselated breast and thighs, And down to where, ’mid silvery floss, outcurved The gay tail’s varied dyes.

And haply there too, half aloof, should stand A full-plumed peacock, spreading up in grand Sultanic state his orient train, all ringed With rainbow splendors circling starry eyes:— A vestige of the feathered pomp that winged The air of Paradise.

Thus should we fare—not without talk the while, And cordial looks, and many a pleasant smile, To think of pleasant bygones; or perchance Of some delightful interest at hand, With influence to make our spirits dance, Or keep our tempers bland:

And most of that no doubt, which now betwixt Our beings had clothed with sentient life and fixed Affection’s wedded pledge,—as still it lay Within close curtained in its morning sleep: An Interest genial as the open day, And as the deep night deep.

169

Sufficed and grateful, to her household care Should she betake her, and I fieldward fare, To till the thriving maize or guide the plough Through the rich loom; or while the sun’s slant shine Caressed them, to remark the melons, how They bulged from out their vine.

To stick the traling peas that, all in bloom And populous with odors, should perfume The whole air prodigally wide around; Or hill the tubers in their various kinds, That else might show above the teeming ground Their vulnerable rinds.

Or to relieve the myriad-podded yield Of gadding beans, conspicuous in the field, As clambering up their pole rows giant-high; Or dress some chosen spot whose much wrought mould, Should throw huge brassica, or broccoli As drum-like to behold

Thence to the gayer Garden: to prepare Some special mound for many a floret rare;— Those gentle tokens of how sweet a will Works at the heart of Nature; or to place Some favorite runner where it best may fill The eye with flaunting grace.

Or of its weeds to rid some border,—rich With fragrant shrubs, the folded blooms of which Might seem all dreaming of their dewy bliss: Till by the breeze awakened, they should move Like blushes rousing to the passionate kiss Of a too open love.

170

Or else to loop against its latticed frame Some wodvine’s coils, all tremulous and a-flame With crimson stars,—or hung with silver bells Such as the Fays ring when, on Summer nights, Their sparkling revels out of forest dells Scatter wild glancing lights.

Thence to the Orchard: to well trim the robes Of orange trees embossed with golden globes; Or thin the peachy tribes all ruddy cheeked, And eke the plum’s live rubies,—or the rare Bloom nectarine’s fragrant swarms so lushly streaked, That flavor ev’n the air.

To ease the apple trees whose branches bend Burthened with fruit; or in like manner tend A file of neighboring cherries, smothered through With ruddy globules intergleaming quick, Like monstrous drops of a discolored dew, Innumerably thick.

To tithe the rich pips of the apricot, Ripening apace, where in some sunny spot They gleam like nuggets hung in brilliant hoards; Or prop the pear’s long branches, loading low; Or mark if the pomegranites, lamped with gourds, Stretch in a perfect row.

To pluck the fig that in its broad-leafed shade Secretes its ripeness—even like a maid Mature for love, who yet through bashfulness Doth shun each would-be wooer’s amorous gaze; Or stay the drooping vines, whose every tress Should catch the morning rays:

171

The glad green vines! exuberant in health, And overladen with their clustered wealth Of bloomy grapes nectareous;—some all bright And half diaphanous, like a precious stone, Some darkly purple, like thick-clouded light Up from the sunset thrown.

Thence to the bee-stands, open to the rays Of morn, but shaded from the noontide blaze; To list their murmurous diligence—a coil Full of home memories; or it might be To take from some quite glutted hive, as spoil, Its superfluity.

Part II.

So should the noon draw on: when in yon shade Beside the rill, on the green couch-grass laid In careless luxury my faint limbs should be; And hearing but the splash of feathered things Then dashing downward from some neighboring tree To dip their shining wings;

Or the slow rising and most summery hum Of gorgeous insects that at times might come Over the runnel, and so voyàge by; Or the light footfall on the further brink Of some wild creature, from its covert nigh Just venturing forth to drink;—

Its covert,—yon dense brush that, like a cloud, Hangs o’er the watery gleam, and rings aloud At morn and eve with the wild-warbled joy 172 Of birds indigenous;—thus half in dream, Lone lying as my wont was when a boy By Hawksbury’s mightier stream;

I’d calmly think of all my wandering youth Had suffered, with a heart so dear to Truth That she at length had portioned it with love; And then of her who to my being’s good Was what the time of flowers and fruit doth prove In Nature’s bountihood.

Thus rested, when the fierier-wingèd hours Were quenching in the West, with freshened powers The field again in honorable toil Should hear me, ending what with morn begun— Till the dressed orchard or the well-turned soil Showed a good day’s work done.

Then while the unharnessed steers I woodward took, Or sought the kine, that in some grassy nook Were ruminating all in full fed ease, The sun should ‘light upon his western hill, Slanting his last beams through the shadowing trees, And up the gleaming rill;

Then sink, and make all heaven’s lit draperies seem As they were golden answers to the dream My heart had then of Nature, and of Her Now waiting me at our peace-hallowed board: Thus placed, who’d care amongst the great to stir, Or with the rich to hoard.

The pens secured; the final meal in haste Dispatched though savoury;—both should forth to taste 173 Eve’s breath, and find with ever new surprise Elysiums painted in the cloudy West: Oft the while looking in each other’s eyes, To feel that we were blest!

Oft too our Darling, in its Mother’s arms, Should haply crow,—as if the mystic charms Of color and of form were even in it, Occasioning such awakenings of soul As well showed how its nascent mother-wit Should yet take in the whole.

Should yet take in and mould it through the heart Into such symbols as might best impart Or raise the sense of beauty and of grace, Wherever Thought, wherever Passion moves: So much the Mind is its own royal place, And reigns o’er all it loves.

And thus emoved, up through that golden glow Thanksgivings from our mutual hearts should go Golden as it,—to Him who made the time So beautiful, that only then to be Was sure baptism into a sublime Ideal of Deity!

Observing afterwards how soon ’tis given For richest things to change. For lo, in heaven Recesses of a loveliness divine Transform, ere long, into abysms grey, And dim-drawn reefy seas that widening shine Round headlands far away;And

And thence out—infinitely out, with not One shore-like limit or one isle-like spot: Then, in a moment, all again is change, As more the congregating vapors loom, And where those sky-seas were, broad prairies range Clumped as with groves of gloom.

Or craggy glens appear and gorges dark, Browed in by mountains more abrupt and stark Than their similitudes—the Alps of Earth; High towered with castles, spectral and remote, And altering ever to some wilder birth As up the cloud-banks bloat.

More solemn, though less beautiful, so seen Is the wide bending heaven, than when the sheen Of sunset drapes in their most rich array Those western depths of wonder. Such the close Meetest to glory dying with the day— Mutation ere repose.

Then, while the gloaming followed Evening’s flight Down o’er yon hills,—whence yet a skiey light Kept mellowing upward, near to where, first seen, The glowing Leader of the starry quire Came wingèdly from out the blue serene, Even like a bird of fire;

The hushing bounties of those twilight hours Falling into our souls, as in the flowers’ Balm-breathing bosoms melt the silent dews, Should freshen every feeling mild and wise, And thence o’er all our charities diffuse The quiet of the skies.

175a

Thus meetly ushered, oft should come the Night To pore with all her solemn eyes of light Upon my happy life, and draw my soul To journey, like a star, the stars among, And homeward point from the resplendent pole Uranian beams of song.

Or while the Moon, the world’s apparent queen, Came whitening up in majesty serene, Reminding us of some dear long past night, I’d chronicle in rhyme the many things Of lovely thought that from her mystic light Had woven then their wings.

Or haply some fast Friend, whose loyal ways Had won our whole trust in less prosperous days, Should drop amongst us for an evening’s talk About old times and new; and make right good The feeling of how well it is to walk With men in brotherhood.

At length retired to our most cozy room Stored with such books as Fancy’s fires relume, Some chosen song together we’d rehearse To its meet air with a long-dying close: Or list great Milton’s or sage Wordsworth’s verse, Or Taylor’s noble prose.

Avoiding Science, ever cold in kind, And mainly mighty to make lean the mind Even when the most a boaster.x Never then 176a Let those much woo her, who with eloquent speech Would influence the higher faiths of men, And tow’rds the Ideal reach.

But reading of sweet Genevieve, whose own Great minstrel Wooer did so well intone The legend of the Lady and the Knight With nearer pleadings,—we should sweetlier know How first our hearts, too, into sudden light Rushed joining,—long ago.

Or musical movements such as foretastes are Of deeper being in some fairer star, Should flood our quietude—till it should seem The heaven-born Guardian Spirit of all love Came spell-drawn down, to perfect Feeling’s dream With dream-power from above.

Then of Endymion should the lovely rhyme With mythic pageants wile the listening time— Visions that sparkled from his starry mind Whose memory is like a fervent tune Heard richly prospering in a Summer wind Under the midnight moon.

Or by mild Spencer’s dreamy lore we’d get 177 As ‘twere to breathe Elysian air, and let Our spirits sail—borne bark-like on by this— O’er Fairy Seas all billowed with with delights, And round the wonder witching Bower of Bliss Peopled with magic sleights:

And all at once filled with a sunset blaze Of music—flushing through arboreous ways From birds and rills, voices and viols sweet, All mingled in a weird wind warbling low— Yea, such as might, so long as heard, even cheat The damnèd of their woe.

And lastly, ere the drowsy hours of sleep Came hushing o’er, our grateful hearts should steep Their happiness in prayer; acknowledging His goodness, who hath filled the day and night With strength and bounty, poured as from a spring, For all who live aright.
