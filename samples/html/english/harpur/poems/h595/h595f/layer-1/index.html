To a Comet.

[The Comet here addressed was the great one of 1841 It is necessary to note this, lest the youthful reader should suppose that the Poet’s imagination was making “bold holiday” in the expressions,

“The flaming steps Of thy unspeakable speed, which, of itself, Blows back the long strands of thy burning hair Through half the arch of night.”

So far, however, from such being the case, these expressions, are as descriptive of the Comet’s tail, are to be taken literally. For when, in declining, its necleus touched the horizon, this tail, in the shape of a slightly opened fan, did absolutely reach back nearly to the zenith.]

Thy purpose, heavenly Stranger, who may tell But Him who linked thee to the starry whole? We see thou journeyest amid the worlds, And that is all our vaunted Science knows Of thy celestial moods. For of the birth Of Motion, save as the primordial step Of God’s almighty and creative march Out from his central rest, mankind even yet But guess and speculate much as did of old Egypt’s dark seers,—observatoried high On those star-pointing piles that crumbling stand Amid grey wastes immensely stretching round On all hands,—voiceless; even as in the dim And desolate chambers of a doubly dead Though monumental Past:—or, following these, The Shephered Magi of the mystic East: 76 And this too with a path less free, less raised And thaumaturgic than was theirs, because So cramped and cumbered with mechanic helps, That palsying the wild wings of Wonder, keep Her spirit down amid the dust and heat Of so-called Matter—a vast eyeless Force For ever grinding like a mill-wheel on! And tend to make, even when they deaden least, Our bodily senses, with their objects, final; As if in all the universe besides Were nothing absolute—nothing purely God.

Wherefore, in this our darkness, be it ours The more to muse of thee, in thy career, As of some wandering symphony from amidst Those highest stellar harmonies that track Through infinite Space, and the great rounds of Time, The aulic marches of Almighty Truth— Or wild antiphony (at sure though long Returning periods) striking in amongst The planetary voices of Creation, Singing together, in their sun-led choirs, As through unmeasurable voids they wheel, The God-taught anthem of eternal Order!

Thus may we muse of thee—and I, methinks, With special privilege; for I indeed, Here, in my own Land, wholly unforewarned Of thy bright coming, chanced to see thee first: Then having wandered forth, as was my wont, To steep my heart in the rich sunset’s pomp Of visual splendors—oceaned as it were In wide and far-withdrawing depths, that more And more refined, as the tints mellower grew 77 Into recesses of a loveliness Unutterably divine—yea, full of God! Thus richly then my heart embathing—lo, I saw the fading glories of the hour Leave thee sole wonder of the twilight sky: A wonder the more palpably revealed As still, with gradual pace, the dark came on.

But now, since thou hast travelled high in heaven, Myriads of wondering human spirits are out Duly each night, with upturned looks that drink The mystery of thy advent.

In thy last Bright visitation, even thus thou saw’st The young, the lovely, and the wise of Earth,— A buried generation,—crowding out Duly each night like these, with looks upturned In wonder, to behold thee passing forth Beyond the signs of Time—and then to know In all the awful vastitude of heaven, Thy place no more! And when the flaming steps Of thy unspeakable speed, which of itself Blows back the long strands of thy burning hair Through half the arch of Night, shall rap thee forth Into the dim of the Inane, beyond Our utmost vision; all the eloquent eyes Non-opened up in welcome though in wonder— Eyes that are made habitually by Love As tender as the turtle’s, or that speak The fervent soul and the majestic mind;— All these, alas!—all these, ere thou once more Shalt drive thus fulgently around the sun Thy chariot of fire, fast closed in dust, 78 And mortal darkness, shall have given for aye Their lustre to the Grave.

But human eyes As many and beautiful—yea, more sublimed And radiant in their passion, from a more Enlarged communion with the Spirit of Truth, Shall welcome the instead, mysterious Stranger, When thou return’st anew.

And thus to think Consoles us, even while we watch thee pass Out of our times for ever—and thence to be But seen again by those who shall convene Over the darkling ashes that composed When last thou swept within the gaze of the Earth, The naturs they inherit:—Yea, although Some selfish entertainment of a truth At all times mournful, whisper us the while: “So shall it be indeed; for God abides, And Nature, born of his eternal power, Must therefore share its dateless energy, And work with it for ever—still, alas! Though thence derived, how fugitive and swift, How vague and shadow-like, this life of Man!
