SONGS, EPIGRAMS, NOTES, AND OPINIONS, &c., BY CHARLES HARPUR.

No. 7.

1.

Critical Power.

The mind that’s wisely critical, must grow Constructive in the things that made it so, However wide it run, or short it fall, Of being what is called original.

Note.—True critical abilities both imply and originate constructive ones. He who can analyse, can compound. He who can judgmatically dissect an ill-working Government, must needs learn, from doing so alone, how it might be re-organised with advantage; just as he who can cleverly pick a machine to pieces, can as cleverly put it together again, with, it is likely, an im- provement in the process. What though Criticism be chiefly concerned with details? It is step by step that we ascend the ladder, either of a cock-loft or of a system, and he, who cannot particularise well, will never generalize well; but the more he has exercised himself in the one walk, the better will he be qualified for the other: and thus the matter of itself, runs unavoidably into this conclusion—that critical power is a searching insight into the nature of things, good or bad; and that constructive power is the same insight at work on its own account, selecting particulars and compounding a whole.
