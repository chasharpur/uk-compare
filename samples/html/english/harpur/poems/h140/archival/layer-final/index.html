II.

Fortunate and Unfortunate.

All some men touch, turns straight to gold, And all they undertake, succeeds; While others, with as firm a hold, Can only gather idle weeds: And Fortune, oft, to prove the bold, Will long enthrall them to their needs— Or leave them drifting, half their days, Unsought, unsuccoured, Cast-aways.

Note.—A piece of good fortune will run, as it were, to meet some men. There would really seem to be some- thing connatural between its advent and their expec- tancy;—something of a “hail fellow, well met!” sort of fellowship, meeting, as by appointment, at the “old place,” so pat is it to their need or wish, so opportune and hearty. But, alas! I am not, and never was, one of this lucky order. On the contrary, anything of the kind —any good thing, whatever—nay, anything much desired, so far from coming swiftly, or even smoothly, is sure to fall lame of both feet, or of all four, if it have so many, long before it reaches me—so lame, indeed, sometimes, that it cannot reach me at all. Thus has it been at every turn and under every circumstance of my life. I never anxiously looked even for an “immediate” letter through the general post, that was not delayed for months, or so mislaid or misdirected, as never to come to hand, or to be of no use when it did. I never sought an office, out of which I was not jostled by some needy fellow, with manifestly inferior abilities for it—abilities, in some instances, so inferior as to be positively despic- able. Even the bits of good luck, few and far between, that have turned up to me in the inevitable course of things (that course which must be, wherever there is Being), have always carried somewhat in them, savoring of a grudge—as though Madam Fortune would have me to understand, even through the meagre fruition of such scrag ends as they, how unwilling she was to let me, of all men, steal a march upon her, and live. But how different, the reverse of the die! How mettlesome in comparison, has any piece of bad fortune ever been, that happened to have my weather-beaten brow for its butt? Egad! anything of that kind has always come rattling out of the future, with the speed and bottom of a blood horse—bounding and snorting as though it were infernally happy! And if all this be a true statement, as it verily is, how happens it that I have been able to keep myself for many years now, as independent as an Arab? Nay, how happens it that I am alive at all— alive and pretty well to do, here in 1856, at a sheep- station, under a mountain? I do not, myself, know how. There is something, altogether, unaccountable in the fact—turn it on whatever side I will. Alive after all! I say to myself, and the puzzle puzzles. And, indeed, I can only answer—or attempt to answer the question, by surmising that I am altogether unkillable;—not to be pounded to death, even by Fortune!
