CHILDREN’S PORTFOLIO.

THE KANGAROO,

A BALLAD FOR LITTLE BOYS, BY CHARLES HARPUR.

[This piece—insinuating the beauty of a helpful spirit of pity—was written expressly for the amusement and instruction of my own little boy, Washington; and as it pleases him abundantly, Isuppose it will be equally agreeable to other little boys, and to that end publish it—C. H.]

A pretty playful Kangaroo Dwelt by a mountain stream, There drinking of its waters And oft mirrored in its gleam.

And there he dwelt for many a day, Till he grew large and fair, For the grass he cropt was growthful Because of the moisture there.

So to himself one day he said In a proud and pleasant mood, No beast more beautiful than I E’er bounded through the wood;

And I have a gentle mate, and she Hath borne me a gentle child, So that never a happier creature Was free of the forest wild!

Just then the bay of a Hunter’s dog Was heard on the mountain side: It smote him through with terror, And dashed his forest pride!

And all as suddenly into sight Six hounds came looming then— Looming and leaping in their might, Cheered on by hunting men!

He fled away in mad dismay, And the greyhounds followed fast, While from a nook his wife and child Beheld them as they past.

And sorely then the wife did grieve, And to her child complain: My pretty one, we’ll never see Thy hunted sire again!

For yon destroying foes that kill The quiet of the wood, Will chase him to a cruel death, And lap his glowing blood!

And ever as the Kangaroo Fled for his life that day, He thought how lonely these would be Should he be torn away.

Still on he hurried—madly on, And the greyhounds followed still, Along the stream, and down the vale, And up the shadowy hill.

A long, long way—till he grew faint, And turning in his flight, Came bounding back upon his track Where the river wound in sight.

When rushing down the bank, he plunged Neck deep in the startled stream, Whose silver calm, thus broken, Threw up a troubled gleam.

And when the hounds plunged in as well, Still thirsting for his blood, With his fore claws he’d clasp them And drench them in the flood.

But they were many, and his strength Was wasting fast away: It seemed his doom, full surely, To die that luckless day.

But just then youthful noble-heart (A yeoman’s son was he) To the river side came riding, And saw his jeopardy.

And he was troubled at the sight, To pity ever true; And thereupon determined he To save the Kangaroo.

Saying, fear is sobbing at its heart, And glaring from its eye, And God who made me made it too And would not have it die.

For though man may be privileged With one stroke, if at all, To stay his subject brute for food When prest by hunger’s call;

Yet, of the sin of cruelty Methinks I were not void, If, in my sight, with savage pangs, Yon beast should be destroyed.

And though I hurried out of view, And witnessed not the ill, Yet should I give to a murderous deed The sanction of my will.

And see how the poor brute forlorn, To save its life doth try! Sure God who made me me it too, And would not have it die.

With that, he dashed into the stream, And drove the hounds away, And carrying the spent creature out, He bore it home that day:

Upon his saddle low astride Disposed with happy knack; While bootless to their masters The dripping hounds went back.

And the beast felt he was its friend, And to that feeling true, Said in its heart, I’ll love him, And be his Kangaroo.

And now it goes with him about, And comes at his command, And drinks from out his pannikin, And eats from out his hand.

And thus it is as happy As any thing might be, So wildly born as it was born, And once so wildly free.

But its forest mate so gentle And the lovely child she bore, Are lonely in the wildwood Where it shall come no more.

Unless the good lad, noble heart, Could learn where that may be, When straightaway he would take it there And set the creature free.
