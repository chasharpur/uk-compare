SONGS, EPIGRAMS, NOTES, AND OPINIONS, &c., BY CHARLES HARPUR.

No. 15.

1.

No Mean Dwelling.

I envy no greatness, I bow to no pride, I have Nature and Freedom enthroned at my side; Though nameless and lonely, I dwell in no more Than a rude low-roofed cottage with slabs for the floor *

And Love, if heroic, right healthful and strong, Like the passion whose amaranth bloometh in song, As bees hive their honey, its sweetness might store In a rude low roofed cottage with slabs for the floor.

And a Poet like Shakespear, the heart seer profound, Or like Milton, whose spirit but Heaven could bound, Might ripen his judgment and chasten his lore In a rude low-roofed cottage with slabs for the floor.

* But the reader will doubtless perceive that this content of mine proceeds in nowise from humility—a virtue to which (in its popular sense) I have small claims and make no pretensions. Yet I can admire humility in the truly humble—that is, whenever I can believe in it as being a genuine thing—as being thoroughly a virtue, as it is in our present Premier— and not the fulsome mask of meanness. My habitational content, however (as above expressed), is of an opposite lineage. For were my bush hut suddenly transformed into a splendid mansion, I should have no misgivings whatever as to my being its sufficient master. Nay, were it even prestoed into a castle, the same conviction of my being an occupant good enough for it, would, I doubt not, be as big at the core of me.
