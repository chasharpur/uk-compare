HAVE FAITH.

[BY CHARLES HARPUR.]

Have faith in God! for surely He Is good as he is wise and great: Have faith—for here or elsewhere, we Shall prove the harmony of Fate! Though storms the depths of Life uptoss— Though breakers strew its shores with loss, And Love be slain by Hate, Believe that Evil understood, Were but the shadowy test of still prevailing Good.

That this is worthy of belief The heart-thrill it creates declares: The holy calm it brings to Grief— The promises, like heavenly airs, It breathes into the soul, to fill The failing music of the will, When broken by despairs; Till, touched as with Apollo’s rod, Even Death’s the bard of Hope—O, then have faith in God.

Have faith in Man! for with a mind More roomy than the world; a heart That opened by true love of kind, Might inexhaustibly impart; He yet—even he, though erring long, From the dark spells of ancient wrong Shall, like a dreamer, start! With all-atoning heed to scan The way of Love and Truth:—have faith in God and Man.

Such was the language of my Lyre, As high on Kobanbonia’s bald And craggy top mine heart did fire With ardours from the past recalled; Ere the world’s warfare might prevail O’er life’s first hopes—alas! too frail, Too easily appalled! But on such Altar was it vain To pray for that large trust in God and Man again?*

* As an implicit belief in God’s goodness is the fountain-head of all true religion, so a large faith in the capacity of human nature for good, is the root of all genuine morality. To think nobly of our original is to respect ourselves, and thence come chastity, honor, and fortitude; while to believe it radically incapable and vile, is to live and die (speak- ing universally) on a level with this degrading esti- mate. It may be true—it is true that man, the individual, is often desperately wicked. Communitiesal so may be mostly corrupt, unprincipled, and in- human. Still the surviving sense of mankind, as to right and wrong, and glory and shame, is unfail- ingly just in the end. In the darkest ages—in the most vicious societies, there have always sprung up a few faithful soldiers of God and Humanity, as ready to die as to live, in the cause of Intellectual Liberty, which comprehends the interests of all Truth: and this proves sufficiently, that our capa- cities for good are primordiate and fundamental in the race; whereas our tendencies to evil have merely an abusive and not a primary existence. Here, then, there is ample room for our largest faith in the original excellence and ultimate perfectibility of our entire humanity. The soul itself can be immortal but by virtue of belonging to such a constitution as this, however probational it may be; because what is perfectible in its nature can alone possess an inde- structible essence. Nay, if it were otherwise, right and wrong, and glory and shame, would be all equally accidents. Let us have faith then both in God and Man.

Kobanbonia is the aboriginal name of a singular double-coned mountain, south of Jerry’s Plains (Comenaroy), and overlooking the Vale of Wambo.

C. H.
