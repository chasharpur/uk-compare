Virginal Love.

I love Him so, That though his face I ne’er might see, In the assurance that he so loved me, My happy heart would glow With pulses sweeter than the sweetest be That colder ones can know.

I love Him so, That to my thought twere sweet to sleep Even in death, believing he would keep, With solemn steps and slow, In sabbath memory my grave, and weep For Her who slept below.

I love Him so, That all desires when he is by, 33 Shrink even from the import of a sigh; As flowers unseen that grow, Being mute, must so remain; as in the sky Are stars that none may know.x

x This little Poem was the final product of a long and patient study of the passion it treats of, as manifested in the evident feelings and the whole under= conduct of a most interesting young creature, whose nature was at once ardent and secretive. Her love was long her burthen, and I watched the manner of it, as occasion offered, with an interest bordering on solicitude— and had eventually the pleasure of seeing her the bride of the Beloved.—My brain akes, and my heart sickens, when I contrast in memory the fortunes of my own feelings with those of hers. Not because she is happy. I do not envy her, I only pity myself.

C.H.
