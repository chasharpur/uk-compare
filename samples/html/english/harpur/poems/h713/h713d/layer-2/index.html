WENTWORTH.

(from an unpublished satire by charles harpur.)

First come the Magnates—lo, their leader—he The would be Tell of the Fraternity. His state is that so infamously sad, When talent has through selfishness run mad. In his well-masked displays of by-gone years, With democratic wrath he tore the ears Of [S]yd[n]ey s wealthiest groundlings—being then Thwarted and snubbed by Darling’s party men: But now, behold him, in his native hue, The bullying, bellowing champion of the Few!

A Patriot?—he who hath no sense nor heed Of public ends beyond his own mere need! Whose country’s ruin to his public fear Means only this—the loss of Windermere! And by the self-same Legislative rule, Australia’s growth the growth of Wentworth s wool! [H]er rights—her liberties—for Number One, A universal ten-pound right of Run! Who now his emptied purse, a second time, Would store through the base sweat of British crime! Crime that, transplanted here, would make the brand Of “Felonry[”] indelible upon the land!

A Patriot?—he from whose statistic care, All that his country’s general homes should bear Of mind and happiness, is thrust by that (Wer’t men, no matter!) which, when boiled, yields fat; And which, when given Europe-ward to swim, May purchase pomps for dunghill lords like him!

Such is yon man, and not a whit belied!— A Patriot?—let him “doff that lion’s hide,” And being, seem the scandal of his race, Venal in soul as vulgar in his face! Let him read these—and wincing, feign a laugh; No matter:—they ll outlast his epitaph!*

* It has always been a puzzle to really large-minded, full-hearted men, by what outre and antipodean combination of circumstances this man could ever have been lifted into prime political eminence. As an orator, he is a mere blearer. As a statesman, he is crudity personified. As a lawyer, he is but so so; consistency he has none: and for patriotism—but listen to Doll Tearsheets’ dissertation on the words Captain and occupy. “Captain! thou abominable 450a damned cheater, art thou not ashamed to be called—Captain? * * You a captain, you slave! for what? * * A Captain! these villains will make the word Captain as odious as the word occupy, which was an excellent good word before it was ill sorted: therefore Captains had need look to it.” Now the word patriot is undergoing a process precisely analogous to this, at the dirty hands of our Pistolian “Billy Winfends,” who will eventually make it as disre- putable as the term “white washing,” which was a good one enough before it was roguishly applied: therefore patriots had need look to it.

P.S.—The above lines were written two or three years since—all but the four in italics, which were recently introduced; and the occasion of which has set the author upon tracing out, in plain but pounding prose, the brassy career of our so called great Australian Patriot! Of his private character, of course, as the writer cares nothing, he shall say nothing. It is that huge blind boil, the legis- lative part of him, that alone which the writer has to lay open to the pestilent core. (And a nasty job it is!) It is his political consis- tency—or rather inconsistency that the writer has to compel into the broad daylight, as a big blustering, but surely detected felon is dragged, say into the Sydney Police Court—with, however, Jemmy Martin to plead for him. Long eared Jemmy, who butters Wentworth to his face, descriptively, as “that great man!” But lord, its quite natural. The veriest pimple of a human head—even Jemmy’s own— is a mountain to a louse: and the two together are perfectly and analogically suggestive of the reason whereby it comes that “Billy Wentworth” is really a great man to Jemmy Martin.

C.H.

Still, as to Wentworth, a word or two more in this place, “once for all”. 450c It is still contended that he is a great man—the “great son of the soil”, and so forth. If indeed, he is to be thus regarded as the authentic and pristine type of Australian greatness,—God help Australia! for she must be otherwise predestined to extreme moral and mental destitution. Now I grant to Wentworth, a certain measure of rude intellectual power, and even a vast amount of selfish energy; but something of beauty and grace truthfulness, in addition, are necessary, to my thinking, to establish a claim to the possession of even second rate human greatness. The qualities of justice, honesty, and fraternal faith and hope, in holy alliance with Genius, or the highest order of talent,—these constitute true greatness in man. But will Wentworth’s measure up to such a standard? Not by a thousand spiritual leagues. Productiveness of enduring good results is also an essential element and test of real greatness,—that is, of greatness worthy of being so called. But will Wentworth’s bear any such test? What “thing of beauty” has he created? What great principle has he built up and fortified for the future? X In one word, were he dead tomorrow, what noble, or truthful, or beneficient thing, of all that he has done under the sun, would survive him as a legacy to his Country? Nothing of the kind,—positively nothing! Then would it be seen, unmistakably, that in all these regards, he was but like the barren fig tree of Scripture, —a cumberer of the ground. If, however, it be argued in behalf of his greatness, that, in the Colonial past, he was a stickler for Trial by Jury,—what of that? Had he conceived or created the thing, that indeed would have been something. As it was, he was but contending, from motives of mere opposition, for an obvious right,—obvious to the veriest dullard; and which would have been granted as a matter of course so soon as the Colony forewhent that very penal condition into which he,—the same Wentworth,— would now again degrade it. And for what? That he himself, and his class, may have labor for next to nothing. Away then, forever, with this balderdash about Wentworth’s greatness! C.H.
