WENTWORTH.

from an unpublished satire by charles harpur.)

First come the Magnates—lo, their leader—he The would be Tell of the Fraternity. His state is that so infamously sad, When talent has through selfishness run mad, In his well-masked displays of by-gone years, With democratic wrath he tore the ears Of Sydney’s wealthiest groundlings—being then Thwarted and snubbed by Darling’s party men: But now, behold him in his native hue, The bullying, bellowing champion of the few.

A Patriot?—He who hath no sense nor heed Of public ends beyond his own mere need! Whose country’s ruin to his public fear Means only this—the loss of Windermere! And by the self same Legislative rule, Australia’s growth the growth of Wentworth’s wool! Her rights—her liberties—for number One, A universal ten pound right of run! Who now his emptied purse, a second time,, Would store through the base sweat of British crime! Crime that, transplanted here, would make the brand Of “Felonry” indeilble upon the land!

A Patriot?—He from whose statistic care, All that his country’s general homes should bear Of mind and happiness, is thrust by that (Wer’t men, no matter!) which, when boiled, yields fat; And which, when given Europe-ward to swim, May purchase pomps for dunghill lords like him!

Such is your man, and not a whit belied! A Patriot?—let him “doff that lion’s hide,” And being, seem the scandal of his race, Venal in soul as vulgar in his face! Let him read these—and wincing feign a laugh; No matter:—they’ll outlast his epitaph!
