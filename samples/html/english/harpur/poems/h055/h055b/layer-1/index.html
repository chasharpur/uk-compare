X The Brook.

Home from a distant shore The Wanderer was come: He stood beside the Brook once more That cooled his Childhood’s home; And while it warbled then All adown its native glen, Oh, why did I, he murmured,—why did I ever roam?

The life of other days Thrills by me in its flow; But then I miss the laughing gaze And coming footstep low, Of her, the blue-eyed Maid, Who for fancy, as she said, Used to meet me by its side—such a long time ago.

How might I here forget Those happy Summer days, When in yon woodland nook we met Beneath the bloomy sprays, While thus unchanged by time, It keeps ringing its moist chime, Like some glad-hearted Truant who singeth as he strays?

Even yet within my mind How warm the memory lies, Of all the love so gayly kind— So giddy, yet so wise, Which then, undreaming sin, She kept storing for me in The wild and piercing sweetness of her most merry eyes.

Alas! but they were flown, Those happy days at last; And I was wandering alone, The sadder for the past; With disenchanted eye, Then to view both earth and sky Through those unwholesome mists that load the world’s wintry blast.

As star by star is lost In Ocean’s clouding sky, Ere half the gulf of Life be crost, As trouble darkens by, How all the golden things To which Fancy lent her wings, Out one by one are blotted from Hope’s reverting eye!

And from more thoughtful years What gain we in their stead? 370a That self-love which nor soothes nor cheers; And /let the truth be said/ That knowledge whose stern part Is to harden still the heart; And that cramp wisdom last which so narroweth the head.

But what of Her who made This woodland Brook so dear? Here with another hath she strayed, His rural walk to cheer? Or have her once glad eyes Learned to weep ‘neath other skies? I know not—I but know that she is not with me here.

370b Note. This Poem is a partial attempt to assimilate, in effect, the pathos of moral and physical beauty, but with the physical elements still subordinate to the moral; on the principle, that the scenic excellence of a Lyric should never constitute more than the setting, as it were, for the gem of its subject, which should be something human or having a human reference. 370c

It has been remarked by some one of the difference between the poetical philosophy of Wordsworth and that of Leigh Hunt, that the former would render a stake in a hedge an interesting object by 371 metaphysical attributions, aided accessorily by the terse felicity of a Sculpturesque description of it, in its merest identity; the latter by wreathing it with a vine, or carving it into a distaff for some rural Nymph of Classical beauty: the one depending too exclusively on the pathos of the moral and primatively natural, in combination; the other upon that of physical or artistic beauty in perfection. The conclusion, however, of the distinction is faulty. Each is right in his peculiar direction. Wordsworth is right in endeavouring to appreciate as livingly as possible the coincidence which he supposes to subsist between Spirit and Matter in all the manifestations of Nature; esteeming matter, however, as relatively subordinate, and as refining into a still further and purer spiritual predominance. Hunt, on the other hand, is also right, in being satisfied with the sensuous and ornamental enjoyment of the outward and physical expressions of her inward and spiritual affinities. To blend the Poetics of both then, were to be further, if not altogether right. Not that I mean to insinuate that the Poem thus annotated is an example of this being accomplished: it being indeed, nothing beyond apassing attempt to attain such 372 an end, in so far as it limits and subject admitted.
