The Wrongs of Poland.

[Of the spirit of this Sonnet, as it applies generally to the sacredness of the rights and pieties of nations and of races, I still wholly approve. But my later readings have much altered my sentiments as to the national worth of the Poles, and my estimates of their wrongs as a down-trodden people. Greatly wronged they have no doubt been, but, as it appears to me now, their absurd constitution itself, with its single Nie pozwalam veto, and other such self-stultifications, led more than anything else to the final partition of their territories between their three great neighbors, Russia, Austria, and Prussia:—led to it, I say, and even made it a sort of political necessity. Nay, a king of their own—Casimir V—foretold them on the occasion of his abidication, a hundred years before the event, that with such a constitution, such would be their end as a nation.]

Since every drop of wrong-shed blood that cries Out of the lapse of ages, at the throne Of God is heard, still present. Since the lies Of fraudulent Power are mortal, and the groan Of patriot grief smites not as against stone At the world’s heart, though hard. And since the sighs Of exiled Love, roofed only with the skies, Must weary Mercy with perpetual moan! And since all Rights, though earth-born, do belong To heaven as well,—bowed Poland yet shall cast That thraldom off which she has borne so long; Calling retributive thunders from the Past To urge her cause, and swell the trumpet-blast Blown forth by Freedom trampling prostrate Wrong!
