TO ————.

Long ere I saw thee—years of weary days— A shape would gather from my dreams, and pour The soul-sweet influence of its gentle gaze Into my being—thrilling it to the core! Then would I wake, with lonely heart to pine O’er that nocturnal image: it was thine.

Thine! For, though still, with a fond moody heed, I sought to match it with the beauteous creatures I met in the world’s ways, ’twas but to bleed With disappointment; for all forms, all features, Yet left it void of living counterpart— The shadowy mistress of my yearning heart.

Thine! For when first seen, thou didst seem to me A being known, yet beautifully new! Thus, warranting some sage’s theorie, Amid heaven’s shining daughters, into view A long-conjectured planet’s drawn—his name To fold for ever in its virgin flame.

But I forget! Far, far away from thee, Behold, I wander mid primeval woods, Where but all savage things are wont to be; Mixing fond questionings with solitude’s Wild cadences, as round some shadowy hill Illusively she glides, dreaming her old dream still.

And now the Hunter, with a swollen speed, Rushes in thunder at my feet; but wears A softened charm, in that it seems to lead My willing glance to where rapt memory rears Thy rural home, by the bright bay, that first With surgy song my infant passion nurst.

And with the river’s torture, oft, a tone Of its far waves seems blent; an accent, too, Of the dear voice there heard—to me the one Unparagoned of mortal sound—like dew Sweetened with manna, dropping near me seems, As oft I listen—lost in memory’s dreams.

But vain these musings! Though my spirit’s bride, Thou knowst not of my love!—Though all my days To come must be inevitably dyed, Or bright or dark, through thee—this missive says Thy lot in life is cast: that thou wilt be A wife ere I again may look on thee.

The bardic doom is on me!—Poets make Beauty immortal, and yet, luckless, miss The charms they sing—martyrs at Fortune’s stake! As though their wild capacity for bliss Might else give earth too much of heaven, and kill The want that wingeth them for progress still.

Wreathe then the poet’s brows with blossoms bright! Let waters ever, and the sway of trees, Sound through his thoughts, as the renewed delight Of morn and eve flows round him in a breeze Thronged with a thousand wings—till duly, night Spread her starred mantle over him and these: For nature only (fated at his birth) May minister unto his love on earth.

Charles Harpur.

Singleton, August 27, 1842.
