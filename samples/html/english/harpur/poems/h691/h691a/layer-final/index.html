Women & Stars versus a Beefsteak.

I dote on Stars and Belles; (Wherefore Love saith I ought (and so I ought!) to have a Wife.) 462 And when I muse on those, some Miss Galbraith Trebles each rare one with her eyes, as rife As they with something too divine for scaith. Women and Stars! they soothe away all strife If largely loved, which largely Love repay’th: Both search our reins as with a golden knife, Being (in joint influence) of poetic faith The procreant well-springs, and of human life. Women and Stars!—not even the great Sun Is——But egad! I think my steak is done.

Note. Is not the Divine Art somewhat too much tampered with—too much Punchified, by poetical jokeries such as the above? But then, on the other hand, it may be asked, whether we cannot be sufficiently careful that the passing relishment of a squib of the kind, shall in nowise indispose us to be sage and serious enough upon all proper poetical occasions? And between these questions it may be as well to leave the matter.
