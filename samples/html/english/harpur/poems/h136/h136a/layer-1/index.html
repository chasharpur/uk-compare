Floridness of Diction

There is nothing deters us from a second perusal of any work, whether in prose or rhyme like an extreme floridness of diction. We care not to refresh the glare that it has left in our memory, or venture again into the super-sunny dazzle that has heretofore oppressed us; but would rather repose amid the quiet of a common green field, or by the familiar side of a shadowy hill. Hence who can read Gilfillan twice? Or who can read but a page of Atherton’s Fall of Nineva, without having a horror of the mere mention of ‘blazing diamonds’ ’flaming rubies’, ‘burnished gold’ and flashing steel’ for at least a week thereafter?

A common green bush will better beguile Both the eye and the mind, Than a mock one designed In tinsel and more when we’ve seen it awhile
