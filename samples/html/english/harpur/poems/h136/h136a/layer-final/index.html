Floridness of Diction

There is nothing deters us from a second perusal of any work, whether in prose or rhyme like an extreme floridness of diction. We care not to refresh the too unmittigated glare that it has left in our memory, or venture again into the super-sunny dazzle that has heretofore oppressed us; would rather repose our “wearied virtue” amid the quiet of a common green field, or under the familiar shelter of a shadowy hill. Hence who can read Gilfillan twice? Or, having done so, who is he that would not gladly take refuge from the effects of such a feat, in the comparative quietude of Goldsmith or Cowper, or amid other ungaudy mountain nooks and homely green pastures that impicture the pages of Wordsworth? Or who can read but a single page of Atherton’s Fall of Nineva, without having almost a horror of the mere mention of ‘blazing diamonds’ ‘flaming rubies’, ‘burnished gold’ and polished steel’ for at least a week afterwards?

A common brown bush will better beguile Both the eye and the mind, Than Rose Tree designed In tinsel —that is, when we’ve seen it awhile
