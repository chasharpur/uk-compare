Floridness of Diction

There is nothing deters us from a second perusal of any work, whether in prose or rhyme like an extreme floridness of diction. We care not to refresh the too unmittigated glare that it has left in our memory, or venture again into the super-sunny dazzle that has heretofore oppressed us; like one who has wearied himself by wandering would rather repose our “wearied virtue” amid the quiet of a common green field, or under the familiar comfort of a shadowy hill. Hence who can read Gilfillan twice? Or, having done so, who is he that would not gladly take shelter from the effects of such a feat, in the comparative quietude of Goldsmith or Cowper, or amid other untroubled mountain nooks and green pastures that impicture the pages of Wordsworth? Or who can read but a single page of Atherton’s Fall of Nineva, without having almost a horror of the mere mention of ‘blazing diamonds’ ‘flaming rubies’, ‘burnished gold’ and polished steel’ for at least a week afterwards?
