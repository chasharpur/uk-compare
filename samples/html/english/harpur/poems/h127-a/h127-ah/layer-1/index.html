The Famous Night Scene in the VIII Iliad.

By Charles Harpur.

[Pope’s version of this particular bit of Homer has been much and deservedly faulted, as being untrue to nature to nature, and no less un- -faithful to the original. But neither is Homer himself, in this instance, entirely true to nature. Such a starriness as he describes can only exist in appearance when there is no moon. Even when she has only just risen, such a starriness could only be ascribed to the clearest night with much allowance, their brilliancy being then so much eclipsed by hers. And in the first of the following renderings, I have endeavored, as the reader will percieve, to smooth away somewhat of this objection, by making the celestial part of the description commence with her newly risen appearance in the orient.]

I.

As Paraphrased in Hexameters.

Chafed and sweating, the horses straight from their traces are loosened, And where the cars, in long ranks, make long gleamy rents in the gloaming, Fastened round heaps of white barley. Out from the Town in abundance Oxen are brought, and fat sheep, wth bread fresh and wholesome, and wine too, Meant as a feast for the troops; who thereto have chopped with their axes Sticks in great store, and kindled their camp-fires, red-flaming in hundreds: Whence on the wind spreadeth soon the steam of their savoury supper.

As when the moon riseth full, and all the bared æther is breathless, Wide in her van crowding forth, the stars with a manifold splendor Throb, as if surly alive;—around in the beautiful brightness See the long rock-roughened backs and black-bulging brows of the mountains How, in their Titan-like bulk, they thrust themselves in the prospect— Up with their ledges and slopes, that erewhile lay huddled in shadow; Till the vales even, at last, are filled with a silvery glitter! While in the sky thus cleft with its marvellous surcharge of glory Noting each sign of the year, the soul of the shepherd rejoices.

So blaze the bivöuac fires before the proud city of Priam, Bringing its structures by gleams substantially out from the darkness, Temple and bastion and tower,—as if from some region Plutonian Conjured by magical craft,—and out tow’rds the trench-guarded galleys, Reddening far on their way the bickering currents of Xanthus. While round each fire keep watch a guard of fifty bold Trojans, Glaring in arms as they move; and there too the steeds of the chariots Scatter out into the dark a broken and shadowy lustre: There, too, the horses at times are heard neighing loud o’er their fodder, Fretting, as ‘twere, to behold the white-coming steps of Aurora.
