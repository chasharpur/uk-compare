The Great Fortune

Let wise men worship only the Great Fortune. Kings and the s[unclear]ing leaders of their age So big in their conceit of influence, And indispensable worth, are each and all But like the cloudlets in a summer sky, Which, by a spirit of power they wot not of, Are carried onward, wasting as they go— First one and then another. At the most From fragmentary loneliness & height They are but fuller in the general mind Than those more sluggish masses that still keep Upbanking in their wake, as by the same Invisible influence urged surely on, To travel the same track; But what indeed Is this Great Fortune? It is Providence In union with that ultimate of Manhood, Which is its rarer will,—a power expressed By Sages, Poets, and the kingliest thinkers From all foregone endeavours, all past needs True Goodness, past & presend,—and with these Their own sure feeling of a vast capacity For something nobler in the life of Man; Than yet has been attained to, though concei[v]ed; And which, through all time interpenetrating, The universal substance of that life, Is levening the whole lump.

Partly suggested by the death of Count Cavour & Lord Palmerston.
