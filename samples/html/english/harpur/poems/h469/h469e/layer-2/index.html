IX.

Burns.

My own wild Burns! these rude-wrought rhymes of thine In golden worth are like the unshapely coin Of some new realm, yet pure as from the mine; And art may well be spared with such alloy As dims the bullion to improve the die.

I love the truths of Art, but more indeed The simplest truths of Nature; and I read To find her visibly enthroned on all His Muse hath builded like a fiery wall Round national faith, and patriotic pride, And Love and Valor, both at Beauty’s side. 320 And more his outward rudeness doth impress Deep in us, feelings of the inward stress And working of a sylvan strengthiness;— Yea, but the more through his rough Doric flows The strong male spirit of his genius shows; Even as imperfect features oft enhance The intrinsic power of some fine countenance.

How various too the spirit of his lyre— How many-hued his soul’s poetic fire! In his one Muse such qualities we find Mingled, as most are several in their kind: Mirth like a billow brightening up before The blasts of Grief—to die on Misery’s shore; Humour and Scorn and Pathos, with a reach Above all effort, each exalting each! Yea, Terror wedding its own sense of evil To mother Pity—even for the Devil!

But best he moves to tears, as wakes such sighs As fan the vital fire in Beauty’s lustrous eyes. Hark! where the winding Nith, the Afton, Clyde, Rave downward, or in gleaming quiet glide, How Passion’s very soul keeps burning out 321 In his wild verse, from every field about! Or by the “bonnie Doon”, or “gurgling Ayr,” What heart-sweet memories, like perfumes, there Re-breathe of bloomy joys untimely shed, And Love that followed the belovèd dead To Heaven!—and then, while Pity weeps aloud Clad in the pale ideal of a shroud, Who would exchange the luxury of her woe For all the pleasures that the heartless know.

But should we need relief—another page Shall blow the trumpet of his warlike rage! And vilest of the villain herd is he Who to his battle-dirge’s melody Can listen, and not feel that he could lie Down in his gore for Liberty—and die! Or who, while volleys forth the charging lay Re-voicing Bannockburn’s all-glorious day, From his exalted manhood then not spurns Whate’er is traitorous, with a shout for Burns!

And now in thought I track with steps of fear The noble Peasant in his wild career. Youth’s haven’s left; the ominous untried sea 322 Of Life is loudening all around;—and she Who ’mid its perilous breakers might have stood ‘Twixt him and evil, influencing for good, His first sweet love—she is not! Heaven’s still bright, And the green hills laugh round him for delight: But ah! beneath the sun he finds no more The Eden where his genius dwelt before! And does he wander by his native Ayr? The spirit of gladness hath gone up even there— Up, like the blithe notes of the lark, when they Have faded heavenward utterly away. The more he mixes with his freres in mirth, The more he feels the homelessness of earth; Till Life’s lost charm seems beckoning him afar In the white beauty of the morning star! She is not!—only sweeter is the tone Of his wild lyre for the wild loss thus known.

But storying thus with love his native streams— Thus by the life of his poetic dreams Breathing suggestions that exalt and thrill Into the spirit of each warrior hill; Yea, lighting Scotia’s universal face With mental beauty, tenderness, and grace, 323 Yet did he die the victim of Excess? Alas! even Poesy, by her mute distress, Admits the blot; nor could she save her son, Her star-bright Rob, her love-anointed one!

Whilst yet the Bard, by fortune unsubdued, Had only, like a wild bird of the wood, Sung his own simple joys, then happy, being good— Ere he had sounded the world’s heart, and spurned The niggard test its hollowness returned, His habitudes how temperate we find From a self-pleasing tunefulness of mind.

But afterwards, that such a being, so Alive to joy and sensitive to woe, With all in sympathy of rich and rare Flushing his soul,—as in the evening air A western cloud is saturated quite With all the golden affluence of light;— Nor only thus;—but mightily endowed By Genius, too, with motives nobly proud, And full-summ’d wings of spiritual flame To mount with,—right against the burning eye of Fame, Safe from the fate Icarius met of yore— 324 The mortal type of all who vainly soar; Yet “bounded in a nutshell”, or but wooed By Fortune from his barren solitude, To breathe awhile in her art-scented air, A two-legg’d target for her minions’ stare— A sort of mental monster strange to see, Caught in a trap, as ‘twere, quite new, Amid the farms where late at large it ran Half wild, yet witty,—and almost a Man! That such a being should resort at length To whatsoever might repair the strength Of ruined Joy a moment, or inspire The heart of dying Hope, though with fallacious fire, Was, I believe, howe’er the truth appal, Almost inevitably natural.

Ah, Scotland! it behoved thee then to guard The worldly welfare of thy Peasant Bard! But no, thou wouldst not—and thy gifted son, So placed, again a like career should run— Again be naked left to Fortune’s slurs, One hound-like spirit in a land of curs!

Nay, would even we who thus lament his doom 325 (Touched by the solemn pathos of the tomb), Were he a living subject for our lay— A vital presence shadowing to-day, As erst his image in his native Ayr Fell mirror’d, while he wandered musing there, Be sedulous to succor (even we) His noble, yet so stark humanity?

But ah! if such may always be the fate Of Genius nurtured in a lowly state, For Mercy’s sake,—nay for the sake of Burns Whose spirit methinks tow’rds each poor brother yearns, Away the mask of kindred let us fling And henceforth brand it as an outcast thing! Above communion with the rude by mind Exalted, and yet shunn’d by the refined! Yea, let this warning in its face be hurled As the collective verdict of the world:—

“Enrich the age with beauty if you will, But this you must do at your peril still! The sole reward’s a life-long lack of bread, And lastly, a most desolate death-bed: And then, some century after, when the loss 326 And agony of Genius on the cross Of Passion, shall have spread into a tale Wherewith to spice the tavern-lounger’s ale, Then shall your lowly grave, long grass-o’ergrown, Become a national sentiment in stone! Yes, then a costly monument shall grace And guard it in the land,—a sacred place!”

Oh! must not Scorn have reeled with laughter—yes, Even until shocked at her own bitterness, To see by Scotland such a work up-piled In honor of her so neglected child Of grace and glory beautifully wild? But there it stands—a type (at least to me) Of intellectual hypocrisy! Sad Poesy beholding, from it turns And murmurs—What! a monument to Burns? No: ’tis a sordid scoff perpetual made— A final insult to his injured shade! The thankless Country that denied him bread Now gives this stone—for he is safely dead!

327

Robert Burns, as a Man, and as a Poet.

Burn’s social failings were great, and to be greatly deplored; yet they were mostly the fruit of the world that condemned them. He was naturally virtuous in feeling and elevated in thought, but there was too little in the men and things about him, of a like nature, to foster these better tendencies and keep them healthily active. Hence his superior manhood eventually subsided,—at least in his outward conduct,—to the ordinary level of “heads without name, unnumbered.” Sometimes he even sank below it, and that he should have done so was but too natural. We are all more or less socially imitative, and cannot persistently value in ourselves at its due rate, any species of of merit, moral or mental, which is much misvalued or misapprehended by our compeers in life—our daily associates. This is the rock upon which the morality of genius is most liable to shipwreck, and hereon perished the moral well-being of Burns. Eminently sympathetic, and with too little from without to encourage forth, and keep healthfully active, the higher motions of his nature, these stagnated inwardly, settling down and thickening as it were into dregs; and thus the very values of his spirit became at last the burthen, that depressed him—nay which sunk him at times even below the common level of his kind.

Some one has said that “conduct is fate.” But this saying, like all off hand ones, is too sweeping in its significance, and therefore carries with it a mixture of error. 328 Of course I understand the word conduct to mean here, those social dispositions and motives leading to action, which are or may be supposed to be controllable by the individual. Now the conduct of the race collectively does certainly constitute what (apart from the mightier disturbances of physical nature) may be called with some allowance its temporal destiny. But this holds for less sweepingly in the case of individuals, the fate of many of whom being fully as much determined by the “doings of others, as by their own; nay, in some instances even much more so, and in those cases especially wherein the diplomatic craft and heartlessness so strangely held proper to the ordering of public affairs, are least defensively relied upon in those of private life. At all events, the social conduct of Burns, in its most fatal activities, was, I believe, even more deeply influenced by the doings of others than by his own individual willinghood. And hence the needfulness of restricting the significance of such an saying as the above, when applying it to the career of one so sympathetic— so mobile, and yet so unguardedly placed; if we would be careful that our estimate of his character should be a thoroughly discriminate one,—charitable to the dead, and thence humanising in its influence upon the moral feeling of the living.

329 II

The literary judgment, which I here put on record, is the result of a recent perusal of Burns’ entire works. He was, as it turned out, a great Poet; and yet it strikes me that he had not any originally peculiar or specific tendency to poetry. On the contrary, there was a circumstantial bias in the nature of the man, befitting him for almost any department of mental effort, or walk of life. Had he been bred to the bar, it is likely he would have been purely a powerful lawyer. Or had fortune made him in his youth the sub-editor of a provincial newspaper, it is questionable whether he would ever have written anything but spirited prose, with perhaps some capital squibs and a few rather furious erotics. But, as it was, the pursuit of poetry was the only avenue to intellectual eminence and social notoriety that happened to be freely open to him—and he therefore chose it. I think thus now, because with all his subtle insight into the goings on of the world about him, his strength and even delicacy of mental perception and almost universality of sympathy, he had not a supreme endowment of ideality—of that faculty which is both the basis and the apex of the strictly poetic character, however much of other stuff there may be blent up with it by nature, or gathered about it by art.

To compare therefore, as some have done, his general 330 poetic powers with those of Shakespeare, is purely absurd. He had perhaps, potentially, as much passion and pathos as the great dramatist, but his imagination was altogether inferior. It was more material, and less German, without being, upon occasion, more practical and popular. The poetic greatness of Burns lay wholly under this marke;—it lay in the region of feeling and of social observation. He could endue his Doric effusions with a soul of tenderness, or a spirit of characteristic truth; but Shakespeare could humanise as well as he—with this superiority, that heal so invested with a burning robe of imagination what- ever he created from the vague materials of tradition, or copied, as an observer, from the living verities of the world.
