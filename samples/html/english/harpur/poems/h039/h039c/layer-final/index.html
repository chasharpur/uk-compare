The little Poem which follows in this section is hardly moral in the vulgar acceptation of the term:—that is, supposing some of the allusions to be exclusively credited with a sexual signi- ficance. Hence I have been more than once about to sacrifice it upon the altar of Puritanism; but my good opinion of it as poetry came as often to the rescue. And I said to myself, “Why should I defer to that morality which is but a cowardly strait-jacketting of human nature, and of which the ultimate effect is either to cramp it into some deformity, or fret it into some secretive yet gross excess? Why should the healthy blood-warmth of this poem of mine be sacrificed to the mere danger of prostitution? Or, if it be misused, who is to blame? Be the evil with him whose imagination is tainted, and whose thoughts are impure.” Besides, I have a notion that all poetic beauty—all artistic excel- lence, is essentially moral and moralizing. This position may be best illustrated by an extreme instance from the art of painting. Let us take a nude picture of surpassing female loveliness—an Eve in the Garden of Eden, or a Diana fresh from the bath. Now in this case, if I am not greatly mistaken, all sensual stimu- lation will generally become subordinated to a moral feeling and conclusion of artistic triumph. A refining sense of the Beautiful will be the permanent impression of the picture. Let us also take an instance from Sculpture—even the world-famous Apollo. In this case it is universally acknowledged, that all sense of sexual revelation or personal exposure, is instantly merged in a moral tribute to the artist’s conception of Divine beauty, as manifested in his work. And the final effect of even poetical Cyprianades of the best kind is very similar. Though in design they may have been the offspring of a moral defection in their authors, they do yet, by virtue of their artistic beauty, indirectly moralize, because they intellectualise, those very appetites which are the objects of their luxurious appeals; they so far defeat their animal grossness, as they undeniably breathe into their essence, the etherially conservative influence of the imagi- nation. Moreover, “to the pure all things are pure.”

TO HELEN.

Helen, though matchless are thine eyes In sweetening Passion’s sweet surprise; And though thy cheek be fair as aught That ever flushed a wishful thought; And though thy mouth thus closed and mute, Shows richly ripe like summer fruit; And though thy breasts so full and white, Be very mountains of delight; And though thy small waist (round’s a ring too) A miser’s arms might madly cling too; And though thine ivory ankles show So fitly turned, as sculptured so By Nature, imitating nice The pillared gates of Paradise;— Though all these obvious charms be such As through an undesigned touch, Might almost slay some love-sick Boy As with a golden sword of joy; Yet only the unblest can doubt you Have wealthier things than these about you: Deep spells and powers of love, that wear These as their formal garments rare; Loving smiles, and loving kisses, Warm delights and fertile blisses.
