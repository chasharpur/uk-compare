II.

The Sonnet.

‘Tis not the bulk of any thing on earth Gives it prime value;—’tis its quality: As smallest men are of a nobler birth Than elephants. however large they be; Or as the tiniest gem’s of greater worth Than any boulder washed up by the sea. Nor should we lour upon a beauteous flower Because it happens not to be a tree.

Note.—The sonnet is eminently suitable as a vehicle for literary compliment, and for the embodiment of a striking, yet individual, idea, or fact, or love fancy. Besides this, its untransgressible limits often have the effect of superinducing that closeness of expression and sublimation of image which are proper to the highest kind of enduring poetry—that kind which is suggestive rather than descriptive of things, and which, while it amply recompenses the imagination of the reader, exercises it as well, and thereby strengthens it for con- ception upon its own account. And having, as I think, this tendency, of course these exact limits prevent in an equal degree, that verbal dilution of the sense, which is the besetting weakness of most modern writers, both of prose and verse. Not but a well-phrased amplification of a thought may be sometimes advisable. If novel, or very comprehensive, it may even demand a double draught of expression; but to flood it with words, however well chosen and elegant in themselves, is not to clarify, but simply to drown it. Nay, though in itself as vivid and nimble as a sunbeam, it may be dulled and deadened by one superfluous word.

Ordinary readers object to the Sonnet on account of its shortness and individuality. It is not long enough nor many-featured enough, to produce or minister to popular excitement. With such novel-struck readers, it is only a poetical “nobler”—an apology for a “regular dram.” It has not grip enough for their coarse mental cravings—and apropos of this:—A country Englishman was partaking of some fruit with the members of a family of my acquaintance, which consisted of two sorts—some nectarines of exquisite flavor, but singularly small, and a quantity of large slip-stone peaches of so bad a quality as to be almost tasteless. But after demolishing two or three of the nectarines, at a mouthful, the countryman was observed to give them up with a look of some disappointment, and devote him- self exclusively to the peaches. He was asked the reason—for, said his questioner, the nectarines are excellent but the peaches, scarcely eatable. “Ess,” replied the clown, “the nectons be main good, loike— but then, there be no grip on ‘em[gap]” And the literary taste of those readers who complain of the Sonnet merely because it is short and one-thoughted, is every whit as rustical—not to say vulgar—as was this fellow’s bodily appetite, all their pretensions to refine- ment notwithstanding.
