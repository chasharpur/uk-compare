THE FORGING OF THE ARMOUR OF ACHILLES.

(From the XVIIIth Book of the Iliad.)

By Charles Harpur.

[No doubt it will be objected by many readers of the following Homeric descriptions, that all the matters mentioned in them as being represented upon the shield of Achilles, could not have been so represented in anything like the given compass however mag- nified in imagination. But in view of such an objection as this, we are to consider, first, that it has to be granted, that this par- ticular shield was designed and fabricated by a God—by a divine artificer; and, secondly, that Homer is criticising, as it were,— “in a learned spirit of observation,”—this celestial piece of han- dicraft. Struck, with his fine insight, by the life-like might and expression of the several pictures, as these are presently dis- played upon the shield, he goes on to expound, with the full privilege of a Poet, the whole story which they have eachof them suggested to his special imagination. Or by the in- spiring suggestiveness to him of the incidents which are thereon represented, he is enabled to relate to us, as an Interpreter, all and every of those foregone circumstances which must have concurred in progressively evolving them. And here we have at least two capital considerations that may be set, with almost plenary effect, against any such objection as that which is above anticipated.

But why this new version? Have we not had attempts enough already in this field, from the hands of all sorts of versifiers? Not exactly; for those versions which the present writer has happened to meet with, have nearly all of them been very ob- viously unfaithful, or very deplorably unequal, to the always strong, but always simple, spirit and style of the original. Andthey were so chiefly, as it appears to him, because habited in a poetic garb altogether too modern and ornate—or if this faultwas spared them, then because of their having proceeded from men whose life-long avocations and experiences had been eithertoo in-door and urban, or too limitedly literary.]

Her* then thus answered the illustrious Vulcan: “Take courage, nor let these things be the cause Of sorrow to thy spirit; for I would That I as surely could conceal thy son From that swift-coming doom and early death Whose steps, dread-sounding, I can hear even now, As that soon ready for his use shall be The beautiful armour thou would’st have me forge; Which shall indeed be such as all hereafter Who see it, shall admire.”

This having said, He left her, and went straightway to his Forge, And setting right the bellows, bade them work. Full twenty were there in the furnaces, Blowing a varied but well-ordered blast, At one time strenuous, at another slack, Rising and falling as he willed;—that so The work might be complete.

First in the fire He cast impenetrable brass, and tin, Then costly gold and silver. After that, The mighty anvil on its stock he set; Next grasped the hammer in his strong right hand, The forceps in his left. Then working—lo! Forthwith a shield, solid and large, he formed, Covering with figures all its ample orb. On it the Earth was shown, the sky, and sea, The never-wearying Sun, and Moon at full. And on it also all the constellations That crown the heights of heaven, were displayed: The Pleiades, the Rain-stars, and the strength Of bold Orion, with the watchful Bear— Called otherwise the Wain—and which alone Bathes not its forehead in the ocean baths.

In it were likewise two fair cities wrought, Both crowded with articulate-speaking men. In one were feasts, and marriages, and they Were leading brides from their still chambers forth, And through the public streets,—followed by trains With brilliant torches, and from whom, as high They waved them, many a bridal song arose. The youthful dancers bounded to the sound Of pipes and lyres discoursing melody; While at their doorways many a matron stood, Admiring. Elsewhere, as to hear a cause, The people were assembled: in their midst Two men appeared, contending (as it seemed) About the ransom of a wrong-slain man— The one affirming he had paid it all, And thereupon appealing to the crowd; Th’ other averring he had not—and both Desiring then and there to be adjudged. The townsmen, taking part on either side, Were cheering now the one, and now the other, So that the heralds had to hold them back. But, in a sacred circle, each on seats Of polished stone, the graver elders sat, And listened to the pleaders; in whose hands The clear-voiced herald’s office-staves were placed, As each arose alternately to speak. Moreover, in the midst, a shining pile Of gold lay summ’d—the meed of him whose claim Should best be proven.

But wide ranged around The other city were two hosts encamped, Glittering in arms and armour: and the terms They had plann’d were twofold:—or to lay it waste, Or make division of whatever wealth Its pleasant dwellings held. But they within Had yet complied not, and in secret now Were arming for a sally. Meanwhile, all Their well-beloved wives and children dear Kept watch upon the walls, and with them there Stood also those whom hoary eld possessed. But all the younger men went forth, and Mars Went with them, leading,—and Minerva, both In golden dresses;—both, too, figured large, And beautiful, and with them as they went Bearing the radiant weapons of the Gods: But they who followed were of humbler size. And now when they were come unto a place That seemed the fittest for an ambuscade, Beside a river, where was wont to come All kinds of cattle at their watering time, There did they crouch them, clad in glittering steel. There too, apart from all the rest, they set Two spies, to watch the coming of the sheep, And horn’d oxen; which ere long approached, And with them came two shepherds, who beguiled Their loneness with the music of their pipes, For they as yet had not perceived the trick. But those who lay in ambush, rose and ran Out at them, and eftsoons on ever hand Slaughtered the oxen, and the beautiful flocks Of snow-white sheep,—and likewise slew the shepherds. —Now when this rout amongst the beasts was heard By those who sat in council in the camps, At once upon their nimble-footed steeds They mounted; and, pursuing, overtook The slayers of the cattle and the shepherds. Then, upon both sides marshalling their bands, They joined in battle on the river’s bank, Wounding each other with their brazen spears; Wild Discord and loud Tumult mingling with them, And wide-destroying Fate, who held alive One man just wounded, with another whole; A third, slain outright, at her back she drew Through the fierce battle by the senseless heels; Till all her mantle from her shoulders broad Hung crimsoned with the gore of slaughtered men: While round about her, they who were but mortals Fought intermittingly, and often turned To rescue the gashed corpses of their friends.

In vain the men Press tow’rds him, urging the swift-footed dogs To drive him off:—in vain. These barking stand About the lions—very near, but still Shunning the deadly peril of their paws.
