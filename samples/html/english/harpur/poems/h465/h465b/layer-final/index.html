V.

Dryden.

Even as French habitudes became the rage In England,—from the pulpit to the stage, And mimicised the spirit of the times, Art left small room for Nature in his rhymes. And though ’tis careless art, it seldom smacks Even in its wildness, of unbeaten tracks. Of Waller’s school—but a far mightier flame Was his, illumining the skies of Fame.

Though scarce a genius, in his mind there wrought The alchymic energy of native thought, With quickness of perception—mother-wit, As he himself hath finely christened it.

A Satirist—but in satire chiefly great, Because the slave of jealousy and hate. Well versed in selfish natures, he could hit The lurking purpose with the beam of wit; And through himself, with skill unerring, sketch Some fellow sycophant—some venal wretch Whose very nature, spiced with phrases terse, 302 Lies, like a mummy, coffin’d in his verse.

None more than he accomplished to translate, And did he steal, adopt, or imitate, Much breadth of judgment to the work he brought, To clear the sense and strengthen every thought.

Proud of the logic of his car[e]less rhyme, He nor affects, nor reaches, the sublime, If we except that matchless Festal Song Melting to pity and love the frantic heart of Wrong! And may not, sometimes, venture to bepraise, As such, the fury of his jingling Plays.

Not much I love him, yet perforce admire The vigorous rage of his material fire! Though prodigal of verse, and coarse of phrase, Built durably are all his better Lays. His lines, like pleasant brooks, now warbling go, And now, like mountain floods, they thunder as they flow.

Note.

Since this Criticism was written, it has sometimes 303 struck me, that I have hardly done full justice in it to the merits of “glorious John”; and if I have not, I attribute the failure to my being at the time of its composition, somewhat too exclusively under certain modern literary influences, which I need not particularise. Still, so far as it goes, the criticism is a just one. Dryden evidently wanted that fine breadth of eye and open relish of heart for both the great and humble simplicities of nature, which are the surest indications, as the fruit of them are the most lasting manifestations, of a thoroughly poetic genius. Hence, in modernising Chaucer, who is streaked all over with evidences of their morning gladness, he conventionalised his pictures into mere stage paintings. Thus he wanted the most enduringly pleasing attri- -butes of the absolute poet; those above mentioned being such simply because they cannot find their objects in modes, or in anything short of the immortalities of Nature. Moreover, the general texture of his poetry is coarse. Even in Alexander’s Feast there is a lurking ordinariness of expression, which is not well relished by those who have a nice and subtle perception of the Beautiful. It would not be very easy, perhaps, to point out this defect in the poem, so well, upon the whole, are its materials wrought 304 together,—and yet, it is there.

From some of Dryden’s notions with regard to versification we might infer that he was but a narrow judge of the matter. Yet he certainly had not a bad ear for metrical harmony within certain limits; the flowing fulness and cadential variety of his own verses prevent the imputation. But then, we are told, he was a most miserable reader, and could not get through even his own best flowing passages with the elocutionary tact of a clever schoolboy. His attempts of this kind were always failures; and thence arose, no doubt, his practical disrelish of even the finest blank verse,— even of Milton’s. His clotty voice could not be made to flow out and modulate with its voluble ease and marvellous numerosity, and he therefore imputed to its general nature, a defect that was in himself. It needed, he thought, the assistance —that is, the recurrent check of rhyme, to be adroitly readable; and so far as he himself was concerned it certainly might need such a help, or rather such a drag, and something more.
