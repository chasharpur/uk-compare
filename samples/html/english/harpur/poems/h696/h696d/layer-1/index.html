But the Divine Intelligence not only conceives and interpenetrates, but is also superior and external to these starry systems: as a human intelligence not only gives birth and fashion to its own peculiar thoughts, but is also independently existent. They are not it, though they are of it. In other words, though they proceed from it, they do not constitute it.

And as the mind of man has power to comprehend these reflexional systems of the Universe, which cannot comprehend themselves, it must be something which in its nature is different from them—must, in fact, be an offspring having absolute kindred with the Divine Mind itself, however much lower in degree the Infinite in relation to the finite may declare it to be. Though dependent upon the Deity, as its almighty Parent, it has yet a Self,—a spiritually dictinct iden- tity, and is therefore immortal.

On the other hand, the material or bodily things of the Universe are purely attributive to the mind of God, as thoughts; and thus proceeding, however progressively mutable, they must yet be eternal. ※ In other words, whatever an eternal mind has once conceived, must of its original, be likewise eternal, however it may be subsequently tempered in its substance and elaborated in aspect. 🖙 But of course I do not mean that they are eternal in the same sense that God himself, their author, is eternal. He in being must always have been: they by being, must always be, even though concealable at his pleasure from the peoples of his creation. Our thoughts, in their finite degree, are likewise enduring, though likewise subject to progressive mutation. It is the media, the material symbols through which the human expresses its conceptions that alone are perishable /so to speak./ Were these of a like essence with it, our manifested thoughts would continue as indestructibly visible as the stars at night: Nay, this may be said to be almost the fact as it is: I mean in those cases in which, from their sympathetic mastery as the fruits of Genius, they are committed over and over again to some perpetuating channel of communication such as the Press. In reading Shakespear, for example, can we for confound his glorious thoughts with the mere print and paper of his book? True, these are instinct with them,—are the media of their 584 revelation, but they themselves exist by virtue of a distinct spirituality in union with Truth, as the eminations of an immortal intelligence, itself the personal offspring of an eternal one—the intelligence of God.
