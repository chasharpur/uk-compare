Then on the hills the hunters voice went forth, And, in the valleys, the glad chaunt of birds Chimed sweetly with the gurgling interflows And musical motions, manifold and mixed, Of rivers, and wide depths of bloomy boughs All billowing in the breeze; and echoes, housed In rocky steeps and caves, and twilight dells, Made merry with happy noise that came, Mellowed by distance, out of lowing glens, And bleating vales extending wide around Some low-roofed dwelling of an early world And field of rustling corn: till Nature taught Art music, and bright Poesy arose Out of the dayspring like a morning star Upon the awakened spirit of delight; And Form idealised in hues, or wrought From the rough rock into a life which spake The language of immutable loveliness, 628a Adorned the abodes of Learning;—sister Arts, Three sister Arts in fellowship divine— A tiune glory of exalted soul.

But dare we think the awful Law of Change, From good to better still, hath here an end? That Earth is Man’s for ever?—his and theirs, The creatures over which he towers predominant, As King, his only Kingship? No: the Hours That shall behold her the prepared abode For new successions in the scheme of life, May even now (like a long flight of storks Far looming into view) be winging up Their dim but destined way amid the clouds That hide the eternal future.

But from whence Might spring these novel Orders? From the womb Prolific of our mother Earth, wherein, Even from the first, their causes shall have slept In pregnant preparation, waiting there The evoking word of God,—expressed through laws Which are, in their designed results, the sure Expressions of his will: or rather, they Were but progressive changes in the sum And garniture of that divine Idea, Whereof the Earth’s so solid seeming bulk Is, with its bodily populations whole, The outward shape in manifold reflexion 629a Ever fulfilling, more and more, its part In that pre-ordered harmony which tests The prospering intercourse of all the worlds: Even as a human thought (so far as what Is finite, and imperfect therefore, may With infinite compare)—as knowledge grows Before it, and combines all relative things, A necessary progress undergoes In its accruing unity with Truth. ※

But come what changes may, yet is the Soul, As individualised in all mankind, Beyond the swoop of accident, and lift High o’er the whirl of matter. She, complete In her sole being, evermore aspires, A missioned Liberty with power to break All bondage. She, a Virtue purer far 631a Than that invisible ethereal fire,— The vital spirit of the world,—that breathes Through Nature’s boundless lungs, can neither know Decadence, nor corruption. Thus, a spark From God’s internal brightness, goes she forth All deathless, and to clothe for evermore Her mighty life and wondrous faculties In Earth’s so lovely and abundant beauty, And that of other—it may be of all— Of the infinite and million-featured modes Of his star-shining Thoughts:—each thought a link Of heavenly gold in one eternal chain Of progress—to Perfection. Here we rest.

※ The visible and tangible universe, is a symbolization, or language of the Divine Mind, and the order, use, and progressive design of it, are the expressions of its wisdom. In other words, the ocular appearances and organic systems of universal nature, in their relation to the soul of man, are the communicated modes of 629b the divine intelligence, in the constant developement of a conception, or scheme of being, which tends, however complexly, towards an ultimate Perfection. And the infinite degrees of this ascension may sufficiently account for what is called evil: the ascent is still from a lower to a higher good, over however rough a road.

(Hence, to instance the fact by the way, when the orbit of our Earth, now an elipse, shall have become a circle, towards which it is ob- -viously tending, its temperate or seasons,—in short, its whole physical nature in rela- -tion to sentient existence, will, it is likely, be thereby perfected in perpetuity, as so far consummated in the mind of its Author. And it is also feasible to suppose, that the whole condition, moral as well as corporeal, of its sovereign denizen, Man, will at the same time have been progressively advanced and tem- -pered to a corresponding degree of excellence; seeing that even hitherto, the faculties and aptitudes of the race have manifestly ripened and widened under the mere course of time,—or rather, through that accumulating and transmissive amount of in- -telligent being and doing which both makes and measures what is so called.)

But, as the Divine Intelligence concieves and interpenetrates these ocular appearances and organic systems of the universe, it must be at the same time exterior to, and above them: just as a human intelligence not only embues & gives birth and fashion to its own peculiar thoughts, but is at the same time existent in a manner superior to, and independant of them. They are not it, though they are of it. Or more exactly, though they proceed from it as operations, they do not constitute it.

And as the mind of man has power to comprehend, in part at least, 630b these presented forms and systems of the universe, which apart from the mind that conceives them, cannot in any- -wise comprehend themselves, it must be something which is in its nature different from them—must, in fact, be an offspring having absolute kindred with the Divine Mind itself, however much lower in degree the Infinite in relation to the finite may pronounce it to be. Though dependant, then, upon the Deity, as its Almighty parent, it has yet a self—a distant spiritual identity, and is, therefore, individually immortal.

On the other hand, the material or bodily appearances of the world, are purely attributive to the mind of God, as the manifestations or language of his thoughts and designs; and, thus proceeding, however progressively mutable within themselves, they must yet be eternal. In other words, whatever an eternal mind has once conceived, must in virtue of its original, (and since any diminution in the consciousness of such a mind is a flagrant impos- -sibility) be likewise eternal or indistructible, however it may be thenceforth transformed within itself, or progressively raised & tempered in its substance. But of course I do not mean, that the occular shows and tangible substances of the universe are eternal in the same sense in which God himself, their Author, is eternal. He, in being, must always have been; they, by being, must always be. And so our thoughts, in their finite degree, are in like manner enduring. To God, they are everduring; not because duration with him is for ever now, but because they are acts or ema- nations of Mind. It is the intervenient signs, the bodily media, through which we express our conceptions, that are alone perishable (so to speak); for were these also of a spiritual substance, or informed with a wholly divine effluence, all our willed or manifested thoughts would continue for ever thenceforth, as glowingly palpable as the stars at night, since wherever a human 631b mind was to percieve them, there would they be,—ocular and abiding. Nay, this may be said to be sometimes almost the fact as it is: I mean in those cases in which, from their sympathetic mastery, as the fruits of genius, they are committed over and over again, to some perpetuating channel of communication, such as the Press. In reading Shakespear, for example, do we for a moment confound his glorious thoughts with the print and paper of his book, or even with the living language in which they are clothed? True, these are instinct with them,—are the media of their revelation; but they themselves exist by virtue of a vested spirituality in union with truth, as the emanations of an immortal intelligence, itself the personal offpsring of an eternal one—the Intelligence of God. In which view, I say further, it is not too wild a thing to suppose, that the Spirit whose mortal name was Shakespear, is still amongst us, though we do not see it,—still amongst us, conscious of all that relates to it, and for ever reaping its reward, in the casting effects of its glory.

In some such way as this, methinks, the great fabrick of the universe, with all its wondrous developements, can only be satisfactorily accounted for, consistently with the being of an only God, and the immortality of the soul. To hold that it is other than a congeries of ideas subsisting in a Divine Mind or Spirit, is to make it in somewise co-substantial and co-efficient with God, to the circumscription of his Deity, and the consequent de- -thronement of his Almightiness. On the other hand, to hold that it has any secondary or bodily existence independent of the presence and perception of some 632 created mind, is to tend to the motion, that matter is not only the object and sensual test, but the very root and basis of our personal con- -sciousness, and thence co-essential because co-existent with it, the one terminable with the forms of the other. Or to hold that it has any such existence in a manner absolutely inclusive of the intelligence which percieves it, is to reduce the soul itself to a mere property of matter, or to some spiritous agency obtaining solely in connexion with its immediate forms, and by virtue of a particular combination of its elements.—But in our consciousness alone, there is to me sufficient evidence, that it is itself the introspection of somewhat within us that is not only essentially different from the passing shows and fugitive compounds of the material universe, which it converses with and contemplates; but vitally & eternally superior as well to all the 633 the mutations of its elements, in that order and succession of events and appearances which we designate as Time.
