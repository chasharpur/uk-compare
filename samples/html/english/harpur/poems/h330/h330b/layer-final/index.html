SONGS, EPIGRAMS, NOTES, AND OPINIONS, &c. BY CHARLES HARPUR.

No. 5.

I.

National Song.

My country, though rude yet, and wild, be thy nature, This alone, our proud love should beget and command, That there’s room in thy broad breast for Manhood’s full stature, And that Honest Endeavour’s a lord in the land!*

Though much of thy bounty, by aliens in feeling, Has been made upon heads the least worthy to fall, Their reign is nigh past, and the wrong is fast healing, And thy wide arms encircle a home for us all.

And though pigmies high placed in our Councils yet fool us, In thy woods there’s a Giant upgrowing the while— The Genius of Freedom that’s destined to rule us, And shout to the world from the great Austral Isle.

* Since the Gold Discovery this is generally true enough. Almost any man by honest endeavour may so far better his material circumstances in Australia, as to ensure his inde- pendence, or enable him after a while, to become his own master. But before the Gold Discovery it was not so, or it was only so with a great difference. So mortal hard was it then to get together the nucleus—the nest egg as it were of our fortunes, that the heart, however good at first, was all but ruined by the struggle. In producing this nest egg—that is, the first hundred or thousand pounds, we had so to toil, and pinch, and screw, and lie, compete, supplant, and haggle, that there is little to wonder at, if all that was originally genial in our social being, became in the end as blossomless, and well nigh as unimpressible, as a block of granite. It was better in those times to remain poor, than endeavour to get rich by means so sordidly griping, that the whole character was passed by them as it were through a cheese press—not to be improved by the process, like the raw curd, but to have all the blood of truth and milk of kindness squeezed out of it. But such were then the conditions of a struggle for riches, if we began it poor; and hence, in great measure, the inexorable world- liness of most of our elderly men of wealth;—their cold, cruel, almost demoniacal contempt of everything but money. But they must have already found—or they shall yet find— that the God of their devotion is not a very God. It may confer pomp, and high place, and the show of greatness—but it cannot confer real and enduring honor; nor a good con- science: nor the assurance of faith; nor moral glory; nor intellectual aptitude: nor even a fine capacity for the enjoy- ment of mere bodily pleasure. It may buy an estate—but it cannot buy love in its integrity, glowing and unselfish as a winged thing from heaven: nor more than the semblance of friendship,—not the substance: nor exemption from fear, and disquiet, and folly. What a poor God after all is this same Money! a God that can confer upon us nothing of supreme value, and procure for us nothing that is worthiest of our desire. Nevertheless, under the present constitution of society, Money is a capital means, if wisely and generously used: and the evil lies in our mistaking it for anything more, and in the prevalent misuse of it.

N.B.—On glancing over the above note, I see that it needs the addition of a few words. How, it may be asked, could such a social condition as I have described in it, have ever existed in a new country, with almost illimitable resources? The answer is easily given. It was caused by the cramping and partial influence of a profligate and alien Government, whose constant Downing-street policy was to enrich and exalt a few, to the impoverishment and serfdom of the many. Nor has this policy ever been changed by the Government:—it never had wisdom or honesty enough for that. But it has been providentially frustrated by the Gold Discovery, which opened in numberless ways to the energies of the people, an outlet from its curse. Still it continued to work for evil as before—nay, it even grew worse. But the destiny of the country was too much for it.
