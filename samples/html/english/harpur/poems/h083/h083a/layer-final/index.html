Crowds

I’m not unsocial, yet dislike a Crowd: Nor am I over sensitive, And yet in solitude I most would live: Because I love to think aloud, And throw my spirit’s motions out As men do in the forest high, Or ’mid broad pastures under the wide sky, Without a furtive look about, To ba[l]k some pilfering ear or peeping eye, That heeds not what we say or do a whit For aught it might evince of true or good But for the evil or the folly it.

Note. I more than dislike—I detest a crowd. Its animal furore disgust me. Did I even otherwise affect a crowd, the blindly incautious, fly like manner in which it ventures upon and starts away from danger, would both irk and shame me. Then its heady, rushing, impulsive, heedless curiosity annoys me as much. Its very joy is [unclear] hideous; while its rage is one of the most brutal and merciless manifestations under heaven. Hence I avoid crowds, as all do who dislike to look upon beastly faces bloated with either a stupid, a fierce, or a wild excitement; and such make up the middle mass—the thick—of all the faces we ever behold in a crowd. Still, of course, there are differences in crowds, according to the spirit that has gathered them together. For example, an assemblage met for some laudable purpose, however numerous, is not only made up of better faces, than a mere mob, but it is also less pervaded by that animal fume which is so perceptible in the press of a crowd at a public ex[e]cution, or at a pugilistic encounter,—that strong, strang, fleshly odour, the smell as it were of sin.

Yet with all this dislike of a crowd, I say again, I am not unsocial at bottom. There is nothing I love better than the presence of those I believe in. Beautiful in the mountains are the feet of a friend, coming to meet. Yea, I could even love the surging swell of the moved multitude, were the impulse deep and the tendency onward. No one, in that event, would flow with it more heartily, as a rejoicing portion of its strength. But no blind commotion!— no shallow bustle and turmoil!—no silly intrusion, can draw me into fellowship. A Friend once said to me, “a manlike you should not be solitary. You should not live so much apart from the world as you do”. But why not?
