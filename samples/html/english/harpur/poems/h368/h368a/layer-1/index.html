VI.

Our Perception and Admission of Moral Differences the Fountain of Individual Liberty.

He is not truly wise, who looking through The optic glass of mere Expediency, Kens all in which we morally agree, But not our moral differences too: For these, well seen, and nobly granted, give That individual scope to Liberty, Whereby men only can be greatly free, And whence no more in herded mobs they live.

Note. Methinks I have the shape of Paley’s forehead as plainly in my mind’s eye as if I had actually ma= =nipulated it. With large Causality and Comparison, and a full knowing region, he had, I should say, 81 Benevolence but moderately developed, and was deficient in Ideality and Wit / progress and perception of differences /. In the last mentioned organ I think he must have been greatly deficient; for it is evident that he could only perceive differences only through Comparison—and hence the narrow scope of his mental vision with regard to some things of the greatest importance. A man with a forehead thus constituted, however versed in logic, will often mistake an apparant difference or similarity for a real one. He, on the contrary, whose Wit is large, or even full only, will never do so. He distinguishes at a glance—intuitively—the real from the apparent in this respect, however approximate. In fine, Paley’s forehead, I think, had just that sort of configuration which would make a keen professional man of talent without one golden ray of genius. “What he did see”, says Dr. Channing, “he saw as through an atmosphere of light”. Yes; what he did see.
