SONGS, EPIGRAMS, NOTES, AND OPINIONS, &c., BY CHARLES HARPUR.

No. 11.

1.

Vicious Taste.

Every Sect will have its canters; Every Stage will have its ranters; Every Craft will have its mystic Doctors, greyly cabalistic; Every Art will have its pidlers, Jargoning like drunken fiddlers.

Note.—From ten to five and twenty years ago, the vicious taste of the times manifested itself in a con- ventional run upon what was called power. Every criticling thrust his nose cheek deep into any new publication, to smell after one sole requirement—the so called power. And it was a thing easily smelt; for to be sufficiently powerful, according to the current canon, an author had only to be sufficiently ghoulish and horrible. Thus, about ten years ago, a poem was strongly recommended to my perusal, as containing evidences of unusual power, which I at once got and read, to find it— what? Why a long-drawn account of a lover who c[l]ung to the dead body of his mistress, and carried it about with him, till it putrified in his arms, and stank him into madness! ‘To the devil,’ said I, ‘with such power! Its only effect is to raise one’s gorge.’ Well, that false taste died out—turned into dust—like a toadstool, and was succeeded by another, which prevailed up to a recent period, if it is not still in continuance. This evinced itself in a conventional run upon as horrible metaphysics. Nothing else was counted of any price, and they could not be too horrible. Tennyson turned his lyre to the prevailing demand; Browning tried to puzzle it; all the current minors crammed for it; and Poe’s Raven (with its name of Nevermore became a special favorite with the passing race of criticlings. Poe’s Raven! a poem good of its kind, but of a bad kind, with disease evident in every stanza, and dilirium tremens for its muse. The poet asks us in it, if Nevermore is not a queer name for a stray raven to carry about with it? and, if our judgment happens to be healthy, we answer the question with a vengance. We say that it is altogether too unlikely that a raven ever was so called, or knew anything of such an adverbial compound. We see too obviously that the circumstance has been cooked for the occasion, and we cannot therefore away with it; for elaborated horrors and dished up mysteries can only be grateful to a vicious taste.
