The Martyrs of Fortune.

Want ground the faces of the Prophets old; Our greatest Bards were only rich in song; The golden-hearted ever wanted gold; And they who wronged not ever suffered wrong. Such, in this unintelligible world, Seems still the Patriot’s, Sage’s, Poet’s fate; The mountain of its wrong upon them hurled, Or grinds to misery, or constrains to hate! To hate sometimes—but with a perfect will, To this superior they should ever be: Though sacrificed, like Jesus, loving still The injured spirit of Humanity. For God’s beholding patience makes it plain That so to suffer and to die is gain. x

x If the Known were all it would be wholly intelligible. But this not being the case, the final results of 39 whatever is unintelligible must have their solution beyond it. And as the intelligible is harmonious, so also must be the finalities of the unintelligible, through a necessity of divine agreement in God’s designs,—binding together the Known and the Unknown with the same golden chain of truth. “Though we are conscious (says Bucke) of the good- -ness of our original, yet are we no more permitted to fathom the purposes of our Creator, than the meanest soldier of an army is permitted to know the secrets of his general. Con- -tinual movements are ordered without any visible design; long and weary marches are made in the dead of night; fortresses of little apparent importance are invested; he breaks down bridges; moves along narrow defiles; animates his troops at one time, restrains their impatience at another. Wild and angry conjectures, ceaseless murmurs, innumerable complaints, are echoed through the camp. The moment, however, at length arrives. The trumpet sounds; the signal is given; the charge is made. It is irresistible! The place, the time, and manner having been well chosen. The ranks of the enemy are broken; thousands join in the pursuit; the notes of victory sound from hill to hill; murmurs and conjectures and complaints, all are at an end; the whole design is cleared up; every one gives himself to joy; every one celebrates and resounds the praises of his general.” This is a masterly passage; and they who pretend to be in advance of the position it illustrates, are on the high road to deception.

C.H.
