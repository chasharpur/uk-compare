IV.

To a Lady with a Copy of Love=poems.

Many a weary day and night Have ta’en their unregretted flight, Dawned many a hopeless morrow, Since this wild wreath ’mid scenes afar, 16 I wove in joy and sorrow. And long it was ere I could deem That the so gratefully glowing star Of Morning Love did only beam To gild a morning dream.

But so it was: although she’d die, The Maiden vowed, if Fate our lot Cast separate; nor in vows was I Behind her a single jot! But Fate, in the vile shape of pelf, At length between us thrust herself, And yet (however strange to tell) We both lived on—and—to get well.

Alas! a tear is in mine eye, To think our very truth was—what? —A golden lie! And hence, although I will it not, The Real is battling from my head All—all the dear Romance that shed O’er other days and nights Such heart-bewitching lights; And Caution dinning me the while, With—Have a care of Beauty’s smile; For though a thing so rosy-rich And tender, ’tis the stuff of which Is woven Passion’s painful thrall.

—The Notes I have thought fit to append to my poems in thus collecting them, will, I doubt not, occasionally offend against some, if not many tastes: Yet I must go on as I have begun, being conscious that they will possess at least one merit— fearlessness: that they will be of some value, as the after- thoughts of one who has determined to set forth the sum of his experience, on all occasions, undisguisedly. Still I would restrict in others, as I do in myself, any depression consequent upon this cutting into the marrow of things as they exist at present. I would prevent it from settling into morbidity, by remarking, that Experience is not always our safest counsellor; that there is a large faith in human nature, arising from a lofty consciousness of our capacities for indefinite improvement, which its darker influences should never be allowed wholly to overshadow. Would it be rational to predicate contemptuously of any individual’s maturity from the crudities of his childhood? Assuredly not: and neither is it wise 18 to augur evilly of our entire humanity, which is essen- tially progressive, from what it is and has been. And hence the best women we know, may be properly re- garded as individual manifestations of what all the sex might have been, and is capable of being, and that speedily, were it but true to its own better feelings and perceptions, in defiance of the loveless teachings of a sordid and unbelieving preceptorhood of fashion-mongers. 1847.
