To the Rhymes to a Lady with a Copy of Love Poems.

Alas, that the woman of our Imagination should be, in the main, such a very different creature from the woman of our Experience: the one, Desdemona-like, all love and devotion: the other (generally), all selfishness, frivolity, and worldliness—far beyond the worldliness of man; and with an utter disregard very often, if not insen- sibility, to real worth in the opposite sex, and even to comeliness.

It is not wisdom, virtue, valor, wit, Strength, comeliness of parts, nor amplest merit, That woman’s love can win or long inherit; But what it is, hard to say, Harder to hit, Which way soever men refer it.”

How commonly true is this. Indeed, to form what is called a thorough “[unclear]lady’s man”, Nature has but to enclose the disposition of a monkey in a man-milliner sort of prettiness, and fashion to furnish this insignificance with a pop-gun battery of small talk. A Tom-tit of this kind, He is sufficiently accomplished to carry any one of ninety-nine women out of every hundred not already preoccupied in favor of such another. Of course there are women, though but few and far between, who are only to be won by manly sense, bearing and worth. Nay, though I believe that the worst and bitterest human fallacies inhabit the shapes of women, I believe in the conviction, that the best and sweetest human truths have the same abodes of bodily beauty. In the warm faith, delicate discrimination, and quick sensibility of a virtuous and sensi woman, there is indeed some of the profoundest philosophy—some of the most beautiful wisdom in nature: for such a woman may be said to think with the heart, which, in many things, and those especially a right judgement of which is mainly to be arrived at through the medium of right feeling (and they 180a are many), is even wiser, or at least less erring, than the most powerful masculine head. 180b Still such women are but few and far between. And but for such rare exceptions to the general cen- sures conveyed above, I am afraid I should be disposed to estimate the whole sex much as I should so many laying hens and growing pullets.

Alas, too, for Love! the young heart’s rosy idol! the early imaginations bird of Paradise! the new soul’s honey-bee of affection!—alas! for in actual life, it is mostly both “common and unclean,” and rarely of an enduring nature. What a death of faith do we experience, when we find it in reality so much the creature of vulgar necessity, & worldly convenience, and mere juxta-position? a thing of shifts and turns—a marketable commodity!— nay, a mere appetite, almost as variable as any other to which “flesh is heir to”? Still, in poetry, to make poetry an earnest and affectionate temper, its character should be founded in devotion and endurance, chastity, piety, benevolence, faith fortitude and hope;—a blossom of peerless fragrance in this life, and an immortal fruit in the next.

The Notes I have thought to append to my Poems in thus collecting them, will, I doubt not, occasionally offend against some, if not many tastes. Yet I must go on as I have begun, being conscious that they will possess the prime merit offearlessness; and believing moreover, that they will be of some value as the after-thoughts of one who has deter- mined to set forth the sum of his experience, set forth on all occasions undisguisedly. Still I would restrict in others, as I do in myself, any depression consequent upon such a cutting into the marrow of things as they exist at present. I would prevent it from settling into morbidity, by remarking, that experience is not always our safest counsellor; that there is a large fault in human nature, arising out of a lofty con- 181 consciousness of our capacity for next to infinite impr- improvement, which its darker influences should never be allowed wholly to overshadow. Would it be rational to predicate conmptemptuously of any individual’s maturity from the crudities of his or her childhood and youth? Assuredly not. And neither is it wise to augur evilly of our entire hu- -manity, which is essentially progressive, from what is and has been. Hence the best women we know, may properly be regarded as individual manifestations of what all the sex might have been; and is capable of being, and that speedily, were it but true to its own better feelings and perceptions, in defiance of the loveless teachings of a sordid and unbelieving preceptor- hood of fashion-mongers.

And, finally, as to love, speaking of it as the joint property of both sexes:—If destiny vouchsafes one draft of nectar to our passions, and we through wordly wrong-headedness, will for ever keep faith- lessly alloying it with sordid mixtures, so as to dep deprave it into porter—or, at best, into brandy, we clearly deserve no better beverage. There is nothing under the sun but love that can purchase love; neither is there any thing in the great round of the world so precious as a genuine store of it; and yet, every nine out of ten men or women who [unclear] it, would be right willing to sell their best chance of its possession

“For so much trash as might be graspëd thus.”

Having no faith in the pure nature and peerless value of the thing, how can we ever know it purely and peerlessly?—for faith is the very centre of Love. As the minds become like that they chiefly contemplate;—commercial if it be commerce, logical if it be logic, beautiful if it be beauty; so the affections ever enter into and assimilate the nature of their habitual objects. Hence, if we desire riches to the neglect and disparagement of love, in becoming all-capable for their attainment we degenerate into an incapacity for its enjoyment —living lovelessly, to die rich. But the world 182a will yet be wiser in this direction, and nobler,— wiser and nobler through a purer and a larger faith.
