Falling Away.

X not in manuscript

The wintry wind is numbing, When blowing keen and high, It hunts the dull and drizzling clouds Across the rayless sky; But ah! how much more chilling Is it when, on Friendship’s death, Some old acquaintance meets us with A coldness in his breath.

It is cheerless—very cheerless To miss some well known dome, Because the golden light is quenched In night’s o’ertaking gloom But ah! yet more disheart’ning Is it when, we know not why, Some old acquaintance fronts us with A strangeness in his eye.

It is not that we’re losers Of aught the sordid prize; 33 And scorn breeds scorn, and Memory, wronged, Will teach us to despise: But oh! the waste of heart-faith, When to Friendship’s cold disgrace, Some old acquaintance meets us with A frost upon his face.
