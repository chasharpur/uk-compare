II.

SONG

The wintry wind is numbing, When blowing keen and high, It drives the dull and drizzling clouds Across the sunless sky: But ’tis not all so chilling, As when, on Friendship’s death, Some old acquaintance meets us With a coldness in his breath.

‘Tis very—very cheerless To miss some well-known way, Because the damp and darksome night Hath quenched the golden day: But ’tis not so disheart’ning, As when, we know not why, Some old acquaintance scans us With an uninviting eye.

It is not that we’re losers Of aught the sordid prize; And scorn breeds scorn, and memory, wronged, Will teach us to despise: But oh! the waste of faith, when, To Friendship’s cold disgrace, Some old acquaintance meets us With a frost upon his face.
