Looking Beyond.

Yes, it is well amid the cold and grim Realities of the world, to steal an hour For mystic meditation unconfined To this dusk point, the Earth;—to die awhile In body, and with all the powers of mind, Thus freed, to bridge with mighty beams of thought 265a The spirit’s perilous essays to span The depths of the Eternal. Even on me Such mood sometimes descends, the precious gift Of pitying Urania, wherewithal To imp me for a free far flight beyond This visible Terrene: and thus equipt, Even as a stork amid the purple clouds Of eve out flying, lo, I range at large In mid-Elysiums,—Paradises placed Haply in stars consùmmated, and where The once earth-treading votaries of Truth In soul reside ^ 265c ,—soul clothed anew in forms Ethereal, smoothed to absolute beauty all, And pure as spirit, and thence lucent all, Alive and radiant as the light within hold. So are they bodied, 265b —untill a period, when Knowledge, advancing them from height to height, And Love, grown perfect, shall have nurtured forth Angelic wings for heaven . . .

But by these I mean not such as with sour faces boast The negative goodness of our purists,—those Blind moles of Fear, who deem they honor God, By offering up on outraged human wills, As upon blood-stained altars, every gay And genial sentiment, and rosy wish That sweetens human souls: and who, convened In their dull tabernacles, all at once Behowl the Deity as dogs bay the moon, Or deprecate his wrath with grovelling rites, And boisterous groans, that from stentorian lungs Come forth in moist explosions through the nose, Even as a herd of swine, with upturned snouts, Snort at a coming gale! Oh! no: for such The Paradise of Fools full wide extends Her dismal gates!

I speak not thus in scorn For Scorn’s indulgence, now detractively. 266 The vulgar satirist I abhor! The mean Detractor, with his wintry-hearted mate, The sneering, misbelieving miscreant, who Hath not of generosity enough To say a good word even of himself! These are, of all the reptile things that, cold Loathsome and venomous, blot the light of heaven, What least from impulse and the bent of thought I would be neighbour to. But when the rights Of Man are trampled on; when villains sit In the high places of the land, and sport With what the just hold sacred; when more wealth, No matter how acquired, alone can win The “well done” of its Nestors, and the sleek Regard even of the Saints; and when Religion Itself, is ever in a bad extreme— A bloated pomp of mystery and show, Or a most crude and coarse perversity, Vile as a beggar’s raiment;—then the scorn Of indignation;—then the brave disgust Of righteous shame and honest hate, put forth In tones that burst abroad like God’s own thunder, Are things most worthy:—Scoundrels never feel them!

Enough of this. The virtuous and the good Are they, I deem, who leaving vain disputes On things that are and must be, from their kind, Mainly unknown, with faithful heed, attend To those which God hath given them light to see Strewn round their daily being; and of such Rightfully choosing, and to fitting ends Well shaping all, upbuild with honest hands A true and simple life. Nor always thus Employed alone; but in the frequent jars Of national factions, they as well, despite 267a Of frowning Kings and banning priests, affourd Unflinching aid to Freedom: who, though oft Struck down by tyrants, aimeth not the less To write in starry characters on all Exalted minds, the sacred text by Heaven First given to her muse, as in the shades Embowering Eden she reclined—That all The goods of this fair Earth—fair, although now Worn in her beauty, and deformed with bonds, And trenched with violence!—were from the first Designed by God to be dispensed alike Freely to all her children.

Such are they Rightly the virtuous called: and such as oft In spirit I encounter, ranging through The Regions of the Blest: Elysian Fields, Illustrious oft, where loveliest in themselves, With shining domes and fulgent temples vast Of quarried diamond, many-hued, and arched Like sculptured masses of a sunset sky On shafts of burning gold,—festooned between, With pending splendors, in their curving rows All raying like broad stars of evening, strung On links of sold light. And fast by these Nectarious rivers flow, 267c ^ flow, and out of which Leap gleams, that are embodiments of joy— Yea, spirited with inteligence, and thence All conscious of a life to be renewed For ever forth, though it subside as oft Because of its essential unity With the live lymph beneath,— 267b now lapsing bright Amid palm-clouded hills, whose sunny green Arrays them with such beauty that they seem Huge emeralds, and colossal amethysts,— Then lost or chekered seen in thickest shade Of trees the growth of Heaven, transplanted there; And sheltering wingëd creatures, perfect formed, And brilliant as if plumed with colored fire; And hung with fruity lustres, globes that glow Through the dense green, like lamps, and shoot abroad 268 Into the brightened air their mingled hues: Such luminous shades involve the streams of bliss, Till breaking forth afar, again they lace With silver traceries and broad golden bands Smooth slopes, and circling càmpaigns, stoned with gems As the earth’s face with pebbles, or enriched With flowers more rosy than the rose, more white Than whiteness, purpler than the purple of morn, While all their odors, intermixing, breathe Of their celestial birth;—breathe, borne abroad By airs unlike our own, not musical, But music,—living vocal spirits all! That sometimes gathering into gales of song Trumpet tempestuous rapture as they go; Or rippling over meads, or sliding through Resonant arbors, they keep pouring on By various confluence into one full tide Of audible sweetness, such as mortal ear Felt never, or but faintly through the tones Of that weird harp we name of Eolus.

Thus all that Soul there apprehends or views, Visioned or actual, is lovely all, Is beauty isled in beauty; and beheld Heavened high over with a boundless sky Of glory, whose fair morn and eve (for night Comes never there) are but the brighter glow And mellower interchange of rainbow hues In clouds of visual joy.

O ye glad Scenes! Second alone to those of Heaven, ’tis good Your habitants in vision thus to see, Or bathing in your streams, as round they pour Immeasurable bliss; or sweetly grouped 269 In shades embellished with immortal fruit. Or wreathing their resplendent forms with flowers That fade not; or all gathered in your domes— Your lustrous domes! in frequence full, to use Some love-completing rite, and feast on eates Ambrosial, shed from God. ^ (as manna erst Was showered on earth to feed a favored race, Though from a lower Heaven.) High food, which still As eaten, founds a growing gift to know Not only of the stupendous mysteries Within and bordering on the starry round Of this once unintelligible World, But those which lie beyond it, swathed in night Eternal, to the walls of Heaven itself. And thus they reap an ever ripening sense Of still unfolding being,—till dieted To the full measure of their state, they live Sphered in the everlasting glory of Truth— The robe of immortality!

And now My dreaming eyes behold them, risen all, In mazy measures moving to the tones Of heaven-wrough harps! or my intense regards, Turned from the general multitude, embrace Companies uttering joy with claspëd hands, While thronging round some new arrived, in whom Is recognised a father or a son, A mother, daughter, sister, brother, friend, Or one more dear—dear as their blessëd lot! And whom the wild winds of Vicissitude, Roughening the ever fluctuating deeps Of Fortune into thunder-roaring waves, Even from the earliest passages of this Their mortal pilgrimage, and thence throughout, Had beaten from the haven of their hope In spite of the persistency of Faith, 270 And all the noble toils of stedfast strength, And all the prayers of Love! But lo, they meet— Meet, never more, as on these shores of Death, To sunder—never more!

Here, having long Revelled in spirit, I at last become As drunken with conceptions so divine— A Corybant of Beauty, full and fierce With rage of further quest,—and as a steed Rushing to battle at the trumpet’s voice, I catch a bolder impulse from the loud Delight of my own heart, and launch at once Out into Chaos—out through starless voids And vague eternities of imagination, Peopled with Terrors, dim and shadowy routs That blindly drift along their destined track, Onward for aye, though tending life-ward more And more for ever! But I burst at length Into a world-wide avenue of Light Outlevelled from the glorious gates of Heaven! And even there find entrance: Yea, I tread,— All mortal as I am,—in vision tread The many-mansioned vestibules that lead To God’s palatial rest, a marvellous dome, Sun-roofed, and sacred to Himself; and there Encouraged by a prevalence of love Ineffably benign, I institute Free converse,—free, though grave and elevate, With Essences and Natures that have been From an eternity, and shall remain Through all eternity uncircumscribed By Pain, Fear, Doubt, and Death, the tyrants fell Of this material ball!

But who migh[t] long Such converse entertain? O’erwrought at length And thought-bewildered, I perforce resign The Vision—though, to find myself again Alone on the hard earth! where, when I would Rejoice in lore so high, there’s none to share That visionary pride: and I must feel Even like some Crusoe, who with pains hath reared Fast by the confluence of bright-winding brooks That babble of their pleasant intercourse, His Island Bower; but who, reclined in it, As under a green bloomy heaven, can yet Grieve only that so fair a shelter there Is unpartaken—is but known to one.

Yet will there come a day, though not to me, When excellence of being shall be sought Not only thus in vision, but within The actual round of this diurnal world;— A day whose light shall chase the clouds that viel[ie veil] Upon the mountain tops of old repute The imaginary Gods of wrongful power, And pierce thence downward to the vales of toil Healing and blessing all men: The great day Of absolute Freedom,—for Right must keep pace With Knowledge! Then the accident of birth, That empty imposition! or the claim Of wealth, that earthy and most gnomish cheat! Shall neither arrogate to any, proud Distinctions as of right, nor qualify Any, by a sole influence, for power Over his fellows; but all men shall stand Up proudly, under the wide roof of heaven, As God-created equals,—each the sire Of his own worth, and the joint sanctioner Of all political pertainment, all 272 Moral and social honor.

Yes: for such Is Freedom’s Charter, traced upon the heart Of capable humanity, when ’tis rid Of the foul scroff of vice,—and on the brain Built godlike, when disclouded by God’s light Of a too old distemper’s fatal rout Of arrogant, hell-suggested superstitions, And customs born of Error. And let none Despair of such an advent: for as when Some solemn Wood’s familiar cadences Deepening and deepening all around, portend The salutary storm; even so, the wide Pervading instinct of a sure revolt Against the ancient Tyrannies of the earth, Roams on before it in the living stress Of Knowledge,—omening the unborn Change By harshening still to the fine ear of Thought The daily jar of customry wrongs.

And let none fear that earthly power, or aught Less than Omnipotence, can still or stay The solemn prelude that for ever thus Keeps deepening round and onward, in the front Of that great victory over Wrong, which time Shall witness—Wrong and its abettors, all Whom lust of sway unscantioned by the truth Shall, to the last, disnature: for the Spirit It first evokes—a mighty will to think, Doth thenceforth charge it, with oracular tones That may not be mistaken.

Yea, great thoughts With great thoughts coalescing through the world, 273 Into the future of all progress pour Sure prophecies, there quickening what were else Nascent too long: as the creative rays Of kindred stars, within some procreant depth Of space concurring, to the gaze of Time Show there a gathered brightness,—even such As still shall gather, brightening till it blaze Into a new birth of remoter worlds.
