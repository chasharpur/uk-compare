IV.

The Show of Physical Strength.

A dwarf will ever be defiant Tow’rds those of larger being, From weening that he were a giant But for their misseeing.

Note.—All men, except the very reflective, and even they to some extent. are ambitions of the appearance of bodily strength. But with very diminutive persons this is especially the case—and of course laughably so. I never yet knew a man-rat.—and I believe nobody else ever did.—who was not anxious to beseem a giant done up in little, and who did not endeavour to carry out that seeming by means of some exterior appliance or doughty assumption:—a big horse, a big stick, a big voice, or a big look. Hence to get entirely into the heart of such a being, you have only to hint with well simulated admiration, at the singular width of his shoulders, or at the miraculous muscularity of his calves, for a man of his inches. Have no fear of the flattery not taking:—it will go down whole, like a buffalo through the gullet of a boa constrictor, horns, hoofs, and all.
