The Coming on of Freedom.

The day must come—for Freedom must keep pace With Knowledge—when the accident of birth, Or emptier imposition of mere pelf, Shall neither arrogate to any man Distinction as of right, nor qualify Any for power, by a sole influence, Over his fellows. But all men shall stand Up proudly, under the wide roof of heaven, As God-created equals: each the sire Of his own worth, and the joint sanctioner Of all political pertainment—all Moral and social honor! Yea, for such Is Freedom s Charter, written on the heart Of capable Humanity, when purged From the foul stains of Time, and his base rout Of arrogant hell-suggested superstitions, And customs born of Error.

And let none Despair of such an advent: for as when Some solemn Wood’s familiar cadences, Deepening and deepening all around, portend 72 The salutary storm; even so, the wide Pervading instinct of a sure revolt Against the ancient Tyrannies of the Earth Roams on before it in the living stress Of Knowledge,—omening the unborn change, By harshening still to the fine ear of Thought The daily jars of customary wrong.

And let none fear that earthly power, or aught Less than Omnipotence, can hush or stay The mighty prelude that forever thus Keeps de[e]pening round and onward, in the front Of that great victory over Wrong, which Man Shall win yet—Wrong and its abettors, all Whom lust of sway unsanctioned by the truth, Shall, to the last, disnature: for the spirit It first evokes—a mighty will to think, Doth thenceforth charge it with oraculous tones That may not be mistaken.

Yea, great thoughts With great thoughts coalescing through the world, Into the future of all progress pour Sure prophecies, there quickening what were else 73 Nascent too long: as the creative rays Of kindred stars, within some procreant depth Of space concurring, to the gaze of Time Show there a gathered brightness,—even such As still shall gather, brightening till it blaze Into a new birth of remoter worlds.

Note. I have called property in this Poem an emptier imposition than even the accident of high birth; and such, I repeat in prose, it assuredly is, when made the direct qualification to political or social power of any kind: because the latter, though in itself more questionable, is yet oftener the antecedent to at least a passable education. Otherwise, though I account great inequalities of property as amongst the greatest evils of society, I hold, at the same time, that the only means for the smoothing down of these, which can be safely entrusted to any governing power, are the preclusion of entails and laws of primogeniture. The individual’s right to possess and enjoy whatever wealth he may lawfully inherit or acquire, must ever remain at the foundation of a sound social system; for in no other way could it secure to its members that individuality of will, which is the very keystone of social,—nay, of all liberty. This prime necessity of society is fatal, then, to the Owenian or socialist philosophy.

I was once somewhat smitten by the apparent benevolence of Owen’s views, but a further observation of human character, in its individual tendencies, has 74 led me to prefer the frame-work of almost any existing form of community, to that monotonous series of mere physical harmonies, which his system would be only calculated to elicit, age after age, even from the aggregated union of all our social energies; because such a regimental sort of unity would emasculate them in the same degree, of all personal freedom of will, and consequently of all true moral glory. In fine, I can see nothing to covet in a dead social level after the Chinese pattern, however it might be smoothed into a painless perfection by the superior arts and appliances of European civilization.

As to the “jar of customary wrongs” becoming daily harsher to the “ear of Thought”,—of this, I would say, the signs are manifold. At a meeting of intelligent working men not long since in that happy country, England, held to remonstrate against certain persecutions they had undergone at the hands of their fox-hunting “pastors and masters (as the church catecism has it), one of the speakers put the matter in the following unsophisticated yet irrefutable form: “Why, in a free country, and amongst free men, should any subject be tabooed? Why should any man be molested for freely using that reason which God has given him for his moral and intellectual guidance?” But the logic of cant would put down the honest intelligence of this noble son of toil, by quoting, it is likely, the saying of Solomon, “He that encreaseth knowledge en- =creaseth sorrow”, with this compassionate conclusion tacked to the end of it: “Therefore the many, to be able 75 to bear with life at all, must leave it to the few who make them poor laws and sermons. On the other side, the logic of the aristocratical colleges would make a similar dictum the polished apex of of a more elaborate disquisition upon popular happiness; thereby proving, however, as our hard-fisted orator above quoted would certainly aver, altogether too much,— namely, that men collectively may become too wise, or that it is the final nature of knowledge to impugn and deprave itself! But the day of Bible cant and learned imposition is palpably drawing to its close in the past, and that of absolute free= =dom as evidently dawning from the future.
