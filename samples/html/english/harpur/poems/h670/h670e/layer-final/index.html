A WAR SONG FOR THE NINETEENTH CENTURY.

(by charles harpur.)

(Republished with a Note and additional Stanza.)

The march of Knowledge hasten, Charge onward, and be free! Before are Mercy, Justice, Truth, Our standard-bearers three! Heed not the threat of hirelings, Be bold in deed and word! But spare to use the murderous gun,— Nor meddle with the sword.

Oh! fought we for the despot, To plunder—not to save; To surfeit dungeons with the good— The eloquent—the brave: With patriot blood to redden The violated lands— Yes, then!—but only then!—’twere fit The sword should arm our hands.

But on! huzza for Freedom! Behold our puny foes! Crown, stars, and ribband wearers they, And such as worship those: They see us wield no weapon, But in our front shall find The artillery of the intellect— The thunder of the mind!

Note.—To this war song for the nineteenth century, in the spirit of which moral force is alone relied upon and advocated, I have since purposed to add a stanza, from which it will be seen, that I am now a physical force revolutionist as well, believing that no great and thorough political good has ever been otherwise attained to under a wrong Government; or that if any such good has ever been conceded by the powers that be to the moral force principle alone, it has been conceded so tardily as, (humanly speaking) almost or altogether to render it worthless. Worthless, I say, because arrived at by the prolongation and permission of such an aggregating amount of popular loss and misery as would go near to justify the abolition of a world.

Still the day of revolt should ever be wisely chosen—the time well bided; for upon this its success will inevitably depend. But at all events, when it is once commenced, there should be no compromises, no half measures, no trusting to aught but God in Heaven, and the might of Right upon the earth. The sword being drawn. we should throw away the scabbard. The flag once hoisted the ship should be fought while a stick remains standing. So should they have done at Rome. But there the weakness of old world veneration had smuggled the keys of the citadel. One part of the rampires could not be thoroughly defended, forsooth! lest some damage might thereby accrue to St. Peters’s; thus making man’s material works of higher account than man himself, as enlarged by liberty, with his great immortal soul and inexhaustible capabilities. So would they have done in Hungary, but for the treachery of one man, upon whose soul, as, upon his memory, may the brand of infamy burn and blister for ever.

To the above effect, with a reverted glance at an opposite notion, the additional stanza I speak of preaches, as follows:—

Such was my Muse’s war cry, Intent on bloodless weal, When Daniel’s great, but cloudy soul Was storming for Repeal; But unarmed right aye withers In wrong’s all blasting breath— Then on, ye Red Republicans, To Freedom or to Death.

By the epithet red which is prefixed in this stanza to republicans, I mean only (and I believe it is its only proper meaning) thorough republicans:—that is, men whose convictions of the political necessity of republicanism is unshakably founded upon the God- designed sovereignty of the people; and who are prepared there- fore, whenever a true occasion offers, to champion its advent at allhazards, and in all places.

Dr. Lang’s idea of an Australasian League is a fine and a compr

ehensive one; but we are not yet morally ripe enough for it. We are a self seeking and faithless generation almost to a man; and even our women, in the main, are most sordidly unheroic; and hence the apathy of our very best men. They may have high notions of public virtue, and most capable intellects, but they are too well and gloomily aware that such is our social state of things, and are thus disheartened by their very knowledge from attempting just now to mould any political good thing for their native or adopted country out of materials so base and fallacious. Now, under circumstances like these, and with such coarse elements, what can a mere moral force movement have to hope for? Nothing short of purposes and motives having the materiality and force of actual fire, might avail to smelt into unity such coarse, crude, inept elements of Revolution. The League though a birth, in a great measure worthy of the good time coming,” will yet have to “wait,” not a little, but, I fear, a great while longer; that is, in the event of all downright Cromwellian purposes having to be eschewed in its councils. If, however, it be feasible in theory, it will eventually grow into a substantial fact, or give political being to something else that shall be even better than itself.
