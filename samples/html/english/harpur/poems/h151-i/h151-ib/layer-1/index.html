Chorus of the Hours.

Alas! the veriest human clod Hath still been happier far than he On whom the majesty and the mystery Of Thought hath fall’n, like the fire of God! For all by nature gifted to pursue The Beautiful and the True, Have mostly in dishoner trod The regions they redeemed—as yet they do! All except an idle few Whom Privilege and Fortune care to claim As scions—and who thence for worldly shame Dispel those thorny shades of Darkness old That poor-born Genius else, for misery, doth infold.

And who, alone to gods upgrown, May finally dissipate this ancient gloom? Those Lights that under College wigs full blown (Grave Learning’s bushels) blink for self alone? Those sleek scholastic hypocrites, with whom 264 The worth of genius and the wealth of mind, Are but a cant wherewith they beg for place From Kingcraft’s pension-doling pundit Fum, Or play the sage-deep sycophant to some Great dillitante ‘lord, their God, his Grace!’ Away with such! Learning’s mere slaves—the day Of intellectual brotherhood no ray May owe unto a race So faithless to humanity as they!

But that this day may all the sooner come, Ye suffering sons of Genius, it is you Yourselves who must dispel the gloom That clouds you (even as of old) Yet in its worldly doom so deadly cold! With your own injuries, let stern thought Of the most desolate death-beds of those Who against this same Darkness fought, (Each in his age, whereon his spirit rose As rises some peculear star of night To burn eternally apart—) Yea, let stern thought of those No nerve you to re-urge the lengthened fight And follow Victory with unflinching heart!
