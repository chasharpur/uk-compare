VIII.

Alas! the veriest human clod Hath still been happier far than he On whom the majesty and the mystery Of Thought hath fallen, like the fire of God! For all by nature gifted to pursue The Beautiful and the True, But to alone untimely grave have trod This bright green Earth,—as yet they do! 74 Except an idle few Beside whom Privilege and Fortune dwell, As relatives, or patrons to dispell The stormy shades of darkness old That poor-born Genius aye, for misery, doth infold.

And who shall finally dissipate this gloom? Those Lights that neath the wigs full blown Of college grace, or wrapt in academic gown, (Grave Learning’s bushels) burn like vision in the blood

Those sleek scholastic hypocrites, with whom The soul of genius and the reign of mind, And all these promise unto Humankind, Are but a cant wherewith they beg for place From the all-powerful yet dull-thoughted Few, Or play the sage-deep sycophant unto Some “lord, their God, his Grace”? Away with such—Learning’s mere slaves! the day 75a Of intellectual liberty no ray Shall gather from a crew So faithless to the Beautiful and the True!

But that this day may sooner come, Ye suffering sons of Genius, it is you Yourselves who must dispel the gloom That clouds you yet, even as of old, In its doom so deadly cold! With your own injuries, let stern thought Of the most desolate death beds of those Who against this same Darkness fought Each in the age, whereon they rose To burn, like the peculiar stars of night, Eternally apart,— Now nerve ye to renew the fight, And follow Victory with unflinching heart!
