Form of a Song.

A song to be perfect in its Form should consist of three, and of only three stanzas or metrical divisions. Of course the number of verses in a ballad is to be determined by the compass of the story. So also a lyrical poem, being shorter or longer according to the subject, may be divided accordingly. But with a song it is different. By a song— specifically a song, I mean a simple outgush of emotion; an effusion artlessly spontaneous, or apparently 83 so, like the carol of a bird. And the form of such a poem appears to me to demand naturally three metrical divisions:—the prelude, the theme, and the close. Or

I. The heaving up of the subject or sentiment: II. Its full wave-like development: III. Its subsidence.

It is true that there are two or three very happy examples in Burns of songs which con- sist of but two stanzas. One of these is “Had 84 I a cave on some wild distant shore”, and another “Had we never loved so blindly” But even these, to my thinking, would be all the completer with a middle stanza, affording as it were an innermost shrine for the spirit of the subject. Or when a song has then but two metrical divisions, it should be, or appear to be a fragment only, suddenly terminating in the unspeakable, as a billow in its full strength and profluence is all at once burst 85 upon a reef. Or when from the milder nature of its subject, a two versed effusion cannot be thus abruptly ended without detriment to its lyrical purpose, still it should have (and will perhaps of necessity, for want of a middle member or culminating cone) a more incidental effect—than would be desirable or proper in a full-proportioned song. The following beautiful little thing by Shelley is an example in point.

86

Music, when soft voices die, Vibrates in the memory; Odours, when sweet violets sicken, Live within the sense they quicken;

Rose leaves, when the rose is dead, Are heaped for the beloved’s bed; And so thy thoughts, when thou art gone, Love itself shall slumber on.

And as an example of the propriety of the 87 triple division herein mooted, I will adduce a little piece of my own. I adduce it because, in the first place, it is the nearest at hand; and because, in the next, it is very brief, and will therefore the more pertinently explify the requirement which I suppose essential to the due form of a song.

[LOVE IN THE PAST]

One happy evening passed beside yon river Hath left its sunset in my soul forever: Passed by yon river side With one who should have been my happy Bride! 88 The mouth I pressed then, like a bee-broached blossom, As with new honey overflowed my bosom: Those magical sweet lips, They thrill me even to the fingers tips!

But onward, like the stream that by was flowing, Hurried that sweet time with its sunset glowing: And she is far away Who blessed my being on that happy day.
