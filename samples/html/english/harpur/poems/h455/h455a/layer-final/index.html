II.

A Common Regret.

When the Father who toiled for our welfare is dead, And the Mother who bore us is laid in the grave; What would we not give if we never had said Or done, in our frowardness, aught that e’er gave Those hearts that once loved us as none can again, One pulse of disquiet—one moment of pain?

Note.—Children under ten or twelve years of age are seldom undutiful to those who truly love them. To make them good and conformable in most cases, we have only to love with with great tenderness, though wisely and with an even temper. When somewhat older, however, an[d] thenceforward, they but too often become, from sheer conceit and thoughtlessness, not only disobedient, but strangely forgetful of their parents’ natural claims upon their good offices—to the seeding of many of their after days with a thick and thorny growth of unavailing regrets. But sons and daughters who have themselves become parents are mostly, at the same time (if not very depraved) remarkably considerate of their own, if still living and near them; because they are then enabled by the running on of the tie through a common nature, to realise more fully all those parents have been to them, and how much they must have laboured in the past for their welfare; how purely they have loved them; how they yearned over them in sickness and sorrow, and rejoiced in their health and happiness; and, moreover because they can then under- stand, on the instant, by virtue of being themselves in the same parental relation to others,

“How sharper than a serpent’s tooth it were To have a thankless child.”
