X.

The Voice of the Swamp Oak.

[The sort of oak indicated in the Poem (though a swamp oak) is a lone one, because such a tree is usually bigger topped and more densely foliaged, than it would be if growing along with ever so few others; and the response which, in consequence of these conditions, such a lone-standing tree gives to the action of the wind, is deeper and more definite, and thence more observable than it would be otherwise. The tone of this respondence is, however, the most peculiar and characteristic when the wind is gentle, or even intermittent,— fading gradually off and as gradually recurring, in such wise as to register, at precise intervals, the maximum of its pressure upon the boughs of the oak, as upon the chords of a sylvan gamut.—When many oaks grow together, in a swamp grove, or in a fringe along a river, their heads taper 72 upward almost to a point, somewhat in the manner of of a Norfolk-island pine, or of a neglected cypress.

The swamp oak (that of which I am writing) and the so called forest oak, are no doubt originally the same in kind, the latter being merely toughened in its substance, and slightly modified in its general appearance (and somewhat altered too, it may be, in its reproductive organs) by a drier soil and more elevated position.

The matter of the fourth stanza may be thought overstrained; and indeed, if applied generally, so it would be. But I was once camping near a large and solitary oak that grew on a rich bit of alluvion in the bend of a mountain creek;— when, during the night, the wind got up and blew with great force—not steadily, but in tremendous gusts, the pressure of which through the head of the oak, from some peculiarity, I suppose, in the conformation of the boughs, caused it to whistle, and even shriek, in a manner almost frightful.

I will only add, that the Poem is mainly a recollec of recollected impressions made upon me in my boyhood, while sometimes keeping my father’s sheep on the banks of the South Creek near Windsor; and that I have since—at five and thirty—tested their accuracy, to be somewhat disappointed: for I have found that my perceptions were no longer, in their delicacy, so intimately matched with their causes and occasions as they had one time been.]

Who hath lain him underneath A lone Oak by a lonely stream, He hath heard an utterance breathe Sadder than aught else may seem.

Up in its dusk boughs, out tressing Like the hair of a giant’s head, 73 Mournful things beyond our guessing Day and night are utterèd.

Even when the waveless air May only stir the lightest leaf, A lowly voice keeps moaning there Wordless oracles of grief.

But when nightly blasts are roaming Lowly is that voice no more; Then from the streaming branches coming, Elfin shrieks are heard to pour!

While between the blast on passing, And the blast that comes as oft, ‘Mid those boughs, dark intermassing, One long low wail pines aloft.

Till the listener surely deems That some weired Spirit of the air, Has made those boughs the lute of themes, Wilder, darker than despair:

Wilder than the wildest sorrow That, in Death, has still a friend; Darker than any woe whose morrow Must be travelling to an end:

Some lonely Spirit that hath dwelt For ages in one lonely tree— Some weary Spirit that hath felt The burthen of eternity.
