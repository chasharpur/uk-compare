The Voice of the Native Oak

Who hath laid him underneath An old oak by a lonely stream? He hath heard a Spirit breathe Sadder than aught else may seem.

Up in its dusk boughs, down tressing Like the hair of a giant’s head, Mournful things beyond our guessing Day and night are utterëd

Yea even when the waveless air May only stir the lightest leaf, A lowly voice keeps moaning there Dreary oracles of grief

Till who listens can but deem That Some lonely Spirit of the air Hath made that tree its wierd—and dreams its loneliest mysteries there
