The Voice of the Swamp Oak.

[The sort of Oak indicated in the Poem (though a Swamp Oak) is a lone one, because such a tree is usually bigger-topped and more densely foliaged, than it would be if growing along with ever so few others; and the re response which, in consequence of these conditions, such a lone-standing tree gives to the action of the wind, is deeper and more deffinite, and thence more observable, than it would be otherwise. The tone of this respondence is, however, the most peculiar and characteristic when the wind is very gentle, and even intermittent—fading gradually off, and as gradually recurring, in such wise as to register, at precise intervals, the maximum of its pressure upon the boughs of the oak, as upon the chords of a sylvan gamut. When many oaks grow together in a swamp-grove, or in a fringe along a river, their heads taper upward almost to a point, some- -what in the manner of a Norfolk Island pine, or of a neglected cypress.

The Swamp Oak (that of which I am writing) and the so-called Forest Oak, are no doubt originally the same in kind, the latter being merely toughened in its substance, and slightly modified in its general appearance, and finally somewhat altered too, it may be, in its reproductive functions, by a drier soil and more elevated situation.

The matter of the fourth stanza of the Poem may be thought overstrained; and indeed, would be so, if applied generally. But I was once camping near a large and solitary oak that grew upon a rich bit of alluvion in the bend of a mountain creek;—when, during the night, the wind got up and blew with great violence—not steadily, but in 223 tremendous gusts, the pressure of which through the head of the oak, from some peculiarity, I suppose, in the conformation of the boughs, caused it to whistle, and even at times to shriek, in a manner almost frightly.

I would add, that the Poem is mainly an expressing of recollected impressions made upon me in my boyhood, while sometimes keeping my father’s sheep on the banks of the South Creek near Windsor. There was one dense-topped oak in particular, under the sun- -proof shade of which I used to lie for hours, listening with all the wild ideality of the Boy, to the strange spirit-talk that seemed to be going on up aloft, amongst the hair-like foliage of its branches. But I have since—at five and thirty—re- tried these [unclear] early impressions to be somewhat disappointed: for I found, as it seemed to me, that my nervous perceptions were no longer, in their quickness, so apprehensively matched against their causes and occasions as they had at one time been.]

Who hath lain him underneath A lone Oak by a lonely stream, He hath heard an utterance breathe Sadder than aught else may seem.

Up in its dusk boughs out tressing Like the hair of a giant’s head, Mournful things beyond our guessing Day and night are utteréd.

Even when the waveless air May only stir the lightest leaf, A lowly Voice keeps moaining there Wordless oracles of grief.

But when nightly blasts are roaming, Lowly is that Voice no more; Then from the streaming branches coming, Elfin shrieks are heard to pour.

Till the listener surely deems That some weird Spirit

While between the blast on passing, And the blast that comes as oft, ‘Mid those boughs, dark intermassing, One long low wail pines aloft.

Till the listener surely deems That some weird Spirit of the air, Hath made those boughs the lute of themes Wilder, darker than despair:

Wilder than the wildest sorrow That in death has still a friend; Darker than any wh woe whose morrow Must be travelling to an end:

Some lonely Spirit that hath dwelt For ages in one lonely tree— Some weary Spirit that hath felt The burthen of eternity.
