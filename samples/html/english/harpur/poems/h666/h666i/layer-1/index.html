THE VOICE OF THE SWAMP OAK.

BY CHARLES HARPUR.

[The sort of Oak indicated in the Poem (though a Swamp Oak) is a lone one, be- cause such a tree is usually bigger-topped and more densely foliaged, than it would be if growing along with ever so few others; and the response which, in consequence of these conditions, such a lone-standing tree gives to the action of the wind, is deeper and more de- finite, and thence more observable than it would be otherwise. The tone of this re- spondence is, however, the most peculiar and characteristic when the wind is gentle, or even intermittent—fading gradually off, and as gradually recurring, in such wise as to re- gister, at precise intervals, the maximum of its pressure upon the boughs of the oak, as upon the chords of a sylvan gamut. When many oaks grow together in a swamp-grove, or in a fringe along a river, their heads taper upward almost to a point, somewhat in the manner of a Norfolk Island pine, or of a neglected cypress.

The swamp oak (that of which I am writ- ing) and the so-called forest oak, are no doubt originally the same in kind, the latter being merely toughened in its substance, and slightly modified in its general appearance (and somewhat altered too, it may be, in its reproductive organs) by a drier soil and more elevated position.

The matter of the fourth stanza of the poem may be thought overstrained; and in- deed, is applied generally, so it would be. But I was camping near a large and solitary oak that grew upon a rich bit of alluvion in the bend of a mountain creek;—when, during the night, the wind got up and blew with great violence—not steadily, but in tremen- dous gusts, the pressure of which through the head of the oak, from some peculiarity, I sup- pose, in the conformation of the boughs, caused it to whistle, and even to shriek, in a man- ner almost frightful.
