LITERARY REGISTER.

RHYMED CRITICISMS.

No. 1.

WORDSWORTH.

Lofty in sentiment, but narrow yet, And thence the bigot of a local set Of feelings, meshed around him like a net. Also his intellect, though large it be For most things, hath one prime deficiency— Of moral difference that quick sense which leads The steps of thought beyond the snares of creeds, And circles of opinion, whether they Be of the old time or of yesterday: And hence his narrow bias, I suspect, Even in poetry to attempt a sect.

Still as a Poet he is great and rare! A king of thought, upon the peak of bare And rigid majesty for power immense Enthroned for ever!—And, in Spirit, thence— (Rid of the beggary of his “Goody Blakes,” And such mere bugbears of the mystic “Lakes”) Thence let him waft us on a white wing’d dream Within the murmur of some profluent stream! And there, the while—just where a line of brakes In the remotest haze of distance shakes, On his lone rounds let “Peter Bell” be seen; Seen o’er the “White Doe,” on the herbage green, Heard breathing where she lies, and, near her there, “The oldest (seeming) Man that ever wore grey hair!” Then shall we find him the High Priest indeed Of simple Nature’s humanising Creed.*

With what a plenteousness of pure delight He triumphs on the Mountain’s cloudy height! With what a gleeing harmony of joy He wanders down the Vale, as happy as a Boy! Then in his strengthy verse, each pictured phrase Full to the eye some given shape conveys; And thus, though in the jarring City pent, Through him we reach the Country and content; Fond Memory apprehends, with gladdened eyes, All that is rich in every Wilding s dyes, As blending with the beauty and the grace Of some bright Mystery of our happiest days; Hears through the sway of greenest boughs, as heard Even then, the far voice of some favorite Bird; The murmurous industry of Bees; the low Responsive throbs of Echo,—throbbing slow Out of some Dell, as to the footing lone Of our own musing Beings—ah, so long agone! Then of some brook that gushes in his lines, Glad Fancy drinks, or on the bank reclines While of far Cloud, grey Rock, and ancient Tree, The dusky shadows on the page we see; Yea, the air sweetens as the Spells prevail, And our locks seem to wave as in a Mountain Gale.

But yet remains to tell the Charm serene Wherewith this Bard most sanctifies the Scene; ‘Tis that, with eyes of love, he deigns to find In all its Forms meet Ministers to Mind; And with rich Sympathies of his own full heart, As with a golden chain, links round us every part.

CHARLES HARPUR.

* When this was written the author had not read the “Excursion,” and therefore it is that no allusion is made to it. He has since read that great Poem with infinite delight, but, at the same time, to the further confirma- tion of the opinions expressed (or attempted to be ex pressed) in the opening passage of the above.
