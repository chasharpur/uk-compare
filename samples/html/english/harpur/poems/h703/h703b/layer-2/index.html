The World’s Victims.

They tell me He in riot now, For whom I would have died, Hath sunk all memory of the vow I made to be his Bride: But then the happy past will swim Before mine eyes again; And one wild thought of Love and Him Seems rooted in my brain.

I would not trust him more, for nought Could mend his broken vow: Nay, were the False One hither brought I would not name him now! And yet, from one long tearful gaze How might I then refrain, With this wild thought of other days So rooted in my brain?

He surely sometimes thinks of me, But can he love me yet? Alas! this mood must sinful be— My part is to forget! I feel the bright deceit is o’er— That I have loved in vain! And yet this wild regret the more Keeps rooted in my brain.

Thus day by day a Maiden pale, And oft the long-long night, Did, in her her soul’s despair, bewail Her Lover’s broken plight: But ah! her cold and cruel Friends, Regardless of her woe, For mean—for base and selfish ends, The Youth had slandered so!

And much they urged that Maiden wan, When now a year was past, To wed another—richer Man, And they prevailed at last! But then, to show how vile their part, Her Lover came again! Alas, it broke his manly heart— It turned her weary brain.

And then, in hopeless grief, away He wandered o’er the sea; And soon at rest that pale Girl lay Beneath the churchyard tree! All heart-truth shall the world thus doom To ruin evermore? And Love’s most precious flowers but bloom To perish as before? x

x ’Wrong again!’ says the mere Economist. ‘All questions are money-questions: and the friends of young ladies act wisely when they induce them by all means to wed the richer of any two men. The result of a broken heart is a romantic idea that should weigh nothing against the real presence of a whole purse.’ There is more, however, between heaven and earth than is ever dreamt of in his philosophy. ‘But reason, Sir, reason’—is an excellent thing: and yet no men have differed so widely from each other, and from all others, as mere reasoners. “When men begin to reason (merely)”, says Rousseau, “they cease to feel”— and here lies their error. They do not give fair play to their whole nature, which is threefold; two parts being compounded of sentiment and passion. But is not reason the proper governor and guide of these? Yes: but it should govern and guide them through a knowledge and feeling of their principles, and not according to theories drawn exclusively from its own for if too dogmatically relied on it becomes the mere egg-bag of heartless conventions: and at this point, I believe, we stand over the secret of the greatest anomaly of human life—that perpetual warfare between the wisdom of the heart and the wisdom of the head.⊙ 182c The dry logical definer of human proprieties has rarely any moral or mental progressiveness. Nor does the mere lying together of the dry bones of an argument require much grasp of intellect, or harmony of feeling. Hence, a merely reasoning utilitarian seldom makes a lasting impression on the heart of the world. He may present it with a frame of truth, faultlessly put together perhaps, but it asks genius in addition to “create a soul under its ribs of death.” Hence too, if a mere reasoner commences in Error, he is ever afterwards hopelessly astray. Dealing only with the successional steps of some mechanical subject, the further he goes the further he errs. A largely feeling as well as thinking intelligence, on the contrary, seldom affects the beaten and outside logic of things. It dashes rather into their principles, and yearns more after communion with the order of their inner spirit than with their material congruities. Hence, such a being can never be entirely lost to the Truth. It will often leap into its light over the very fences of error. The real and living consonance of human passions and interests, rather than their apparent and subjective agreement, being its object, becomes progressively more and more its possession. 170b To arrive then at a just idea of the rationale of society, we must give sea-room to the whole man within us, in which resides the reason of the truthful influence of genius, and out of which is evoked the prophecy of the “good time coming.” Still (of course) I am no advocate of imprudent matches as such. Far from it. They are the sources of half the household miseries that 171 afflict the world. All I would urge is, that when the affect- -ions are engaged or pledged, then their pla should be held paramount to all selfish considerations: then a single worldly re- -servation is an inlet to dishonor—from which good cannot come.
