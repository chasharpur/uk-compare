SONGS, EPIGRAMS, NOTES, AND OPINIONS,&c., BY CHARLES HARPUR.

No. 19.

I.

Love Passioning through Nature.

Why smile you, as in sweet amaze, My Mary—Mary mine, to see These olden things in Nature’s ways So pleasureful for me?

For if I cannot well command My joy in aught of glorious frame And substance—beautiful or grand, Thy influence is to blame!

When bending o’er the virgin rose, Why is such sweetness at my heart? Because I’m dreaming that it glows * Thy flowery counterpart.

When gazing at the evening skies, Why love I so their starry blue? Because I know thy loving eyes Have loved and love it too.

And why do I so fondly trace The shining river’s winding stream? Because therein I’ve seen thy face As in a silver dream.

* There can be no doubt but that we are speaking with perfect propriety when we say of the complexion of the rose in its highest degree of intensity, that it glows. But might not the epithet glowing be properly applied to any intense color having a tinge of red in it? Nay, there is even a luminous black, and a shining white, to which it might be given with good effect, perhaps, by way of poetic hyperbole. Yet that fine critic, Leigh Hunt, questions the fitness of Milton’s use of it, as applied to the violet. But the hue of the most distinctive species of that flower, is a blue inclining to red, and it has, moreover, a certain rich depth, and a covert warmth, that not only I think, justify such a characterisation, but also render it poetically effective. It has all the recherche virtue of a subtle perceptive extreme. Besides, the degrees of glowingness are manifold, ranging from the warmly serene, to the concen- tratedly brilliant—from the mild inward radiency of a blue eye, to the red refulgence of a live coal. However, as to the violet, I have myself, at this present, only an old memory of its specific appearance, and am unable, in the out of the world district in which I am now living, to test the veracity of this ancient impression. At all events, it would cost me a journey to do so, seeing that any but Australian bush flowers are quite as rare in these parts as “Angel’s visits”—unless we are to account the Muse of an angelic order, in which case, of course, they are even considerably rarer.
