The Terrors of Beauty.

Thy fairy feet, thy sylphic waist, And those twin innocents, as they Fill up like doves whose nestward haste Had flurried them; those lips that play Apart so clovenly and rich; Those pinky cheeks, and—thou sweet Witch! Those eyes, the loveliest in the world, And that fair forehead, over curled With tresses in their darkness bright, Like darkness of a starry night, And massing inward round a neck Veined, but guiltless of a speck, And throatward full, for Melodies Hived within like honey bees, 34 To store it with live sweetness;—these All fill me with such fond dismay, That still, for looking though the more In love,—for Love I’ve less to say Than when I looked before.x

x The heroine of these verses was an Imagination— not a Fact. In other words, the effusion was but a piece of poetical statuary—a sort of verbal beau ideal—designed (to use a figure) for the universal temple of Feminine Beauty. Not but that I have seen an actual woman as lovely in person as any thing I could conceive of the female form by the most elaborate process of ideal combination.— But alas! where was the angel mind, and soul of love, which should have accompanied “so fair a piece of well-formed earth”? This otherwise so beauti= -ful being—this physical perfection, had never even dreamt of these!

C.H.
