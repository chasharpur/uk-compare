II.

THE TERRORS OF BEAUTY.

Thy airy gait, and form divine Supreme in every curving line; Thy snowy feet and nymphic waist, And those twin innocents, as they Fill up like doves whose nestward haste Hath flurried them; those lips that play Apart so clovenly, (like cherries, Did Nature double-form the berries,) Disclosing living pearls of price As through a natural device; Those deep eyes that so well express Thy spirit’s deeper loveliness— Those mellow eyes so sweetly rich And moist in their ethereal blue, Like violets gloss’d with morning dew; Those dimpled cheeks, the blood of which Blooms into pinks and roses through The tender texture of the skin, To where they draw the nostril thin; That brow,—the smoothest in the world, Though on it we may sometimes see A maidenly austerity,— That ivory brow half overcurled With tresses in their darkness bright, Like darkness of a starry night, And massing downward o’er a neck Blue veined, but guiltless of a speck, And threatward full, for melodies Hiving within like honey bees, To store it with live sweetness;—these All fill me with such fond dismay, That though with every look the more In love—for Love I’ve less to say, Than when I looked before!

The heroine of these verses was an Imagination, not a Fact. In short, the subject is a piece of poetical statuary, designed as it were for the Temple of Feminine Beauty. A piece of poetical statuary, I say, in which the spirit and colour, and I might almost add, the flesh and blood of words are clothed upon the formal creation of the sculptor:—a sort of Pygmalion beau ideal wooed and worshipped into life. Not but that I have seen an actual Woman— as beautiful as aught, or even more beautiful than aught I could otherwise conceive of feminine completeness, by the most elabo- rate combinations of form and feature. But, then, she was only physically beautiful. Her manners were insipid, and the habitual expression of her countenance was stingy. Her voice, too, (that “excellent thing in woman,” when mellow and low, like the utterance of a brook,) while it was thin and jaggy in its lower tones, was harsh and strenuous in its higher ones, and an absolute squall in its highest. And if you looked deeper, the same sort of misalliance went on still more glaringly. The ten- der heart and internal sweetness which should have animated and hallowed so “fair a piece of well-formed earth,” were things that she herself had never so much as dreamt of. Hence they were wanting: so that her mere beauty, upon acquaintance, had a marred and unsatisfying effect upon even an admirer. There was something increasingly disagreeable at the core of it;—a growing, palling inconsequence, such as we apprehend while lis- tening to a beautifully-worded song accompanied by a trashy air, and vice versa. And this experience admonished me of how little account in itself is even physical perfection—beauty with- out soul; the form of loveliness without the indwelling senti- ment that should temper and authenticate it.
