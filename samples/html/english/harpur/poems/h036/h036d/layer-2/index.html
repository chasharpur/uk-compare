The Terrors of Beauty.

Thy faery feet, and sylphic waist, And those twin innocents, as they Fill up like doves whose nestward haste Hath flurried them; those lips that play Apart so clovenly, like cherries Did nature lengthwise groove the berries; 203b + Disclosing living pearls of price As through a natural device. 202c Those mellow eyes so sweetly rich And moist in their ethereal blue, Like violets gloss’d with morning dew; 201c ø Those dimpled cheeks, the blood of which Blooms into pinks and roses through The tender texture of the skin, To where they draw the nostril thin; 202d That brow,—the smoothest in the world, Though on it sometimes we may see A maidenly austerity,— That ivory brow, half overcurled With tresses in their darkness bright, Like darkness of a starry night, And massing downward o’er a neck Blue veind, but guiltless of a speck, 203a And throatward full, for melodies Hiving within like honey bees, To store it with live sweetness;—these All fill me with such fond dismay, That though with every look the more In love,—for Love I’ve less to say Than when I looked before.

Note Attached to an early manuscript of this poem I find the following note, and which I will here give as a sort of set off to whatever may be over sensuous in it.

“The heroine of this poem was an Imagination, not a feast. In short, the subject is a piece of poetical statuary, designed as it were for the Temple of Feminine Beauty. A 204a piece of poetical statuary, I say, in which the spirit and color, and I might almost add the flesh and blood of words are clothed upon the formal creation of the sculptor:—a sort of Pygmalion be an ideal woo’d and worshipped with life. Note but I have seen an actual Woman as beautiful as aught, or even more beautiful than aught I could otherwise concieve of feminine completeness, by the most laborate com- -binations of form and feature. But then she was only physically beautiful. Her manners were insipid, and the habitual expression of her countenance was stingy. Then her voice too, while it while it was thin and jaggy in its lower tones, was harsh and strenuous 204b in its higher ones, and an absolute squall in its highest. And if you looked deeper, the same sort of mis alliance went on still more glaringly. 205 The tender heart and internal sweetness which should have animated and so “fair a piece of well formed earth”, were things that she herself had never so much as dreamt of. Hence they were wanting: so that her meer beauty, upon acquaintance, had a marred and unsatisfying effect even upon an admirer. There was something progressively disgreeable at the core of it;—a growing, palling inconsequence, such as we apprehend while listening to beautiful words accompanied by a trashy air, and vice versa. And this experience admonished me of how little account in itself is even physical perfection:— Beauty without soul,—the form of loveliness without the inbreathing sentiment that should temper and authenticate it.”
