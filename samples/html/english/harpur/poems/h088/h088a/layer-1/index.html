VII.

On the Death of Shelley.

Fit shroud for Thee Was the eternal sea! Fit dirge the tempest’s slave-alarming roll! 26 For fetterless in life was still The genius of thy will, And loud the godlike thunder of thy fearless soul!

Yet tell me, Spirit bright, Was Fate not sad for thee? That day, veiled not the Sun his light When rolling over Italie? Paled not the conscious Moon, that night, When gazing on the fatal Sea? Feeling for once the eclipsive might Of Death’s disastrous spell? Resolve me—for from under them Was never reft away a richer—purer gem! O say—for in their day and night Ruin never wrought before such spite— Never triumphed over aught they loved so well!

Thus, looking from an Austral mountain cone, A Minstrel of that region rude Poured for the Dead his questioning moan, Even as then, at times, Gushed from his heart the unmeasured rhymes, And silent then, and listening stood: But all unanswered was the strain! Only that in like melancholy mood, Echo, afar, its questions asked again; And soon alone the mirth Was heard of Nature’s murmurous broods, Up coming from the streamy plain And from the dusky woods; While down upon the gladdened earth The sun in so cloudless glory shone, And the young moon, beyond the main, Sate smiling on her silver throne.

Folding his tremulous fingers hard For passionate complaint, the Bard Resumed his mournful Lay: And—Ah, so soon to fleet away To silence ever dumb! All in the noontide of his day— With much to build within—without to overcome! This was the burthen of the cry It wailed along: When from a sphered and nameless Light That, as it floated by, Waned like the moon upon his sight, A stilly Voice made sweet reply:

“Whom the Gods love die young! Flowers blighted fall where weeds still thrive apace! The battle is not always to the strong, Nor to the swift the race! But where the odours of the flowers remain, Can these not give even to Regret, At length, a soul of grace? Nor vainly forth was the lost battle set, Nor vainly urged the race, Whence flow examples yet.”

Yes, Poesie’s anointed son, Though thou didst tread the world as one Proscribed by stars unkind; While mitred Persecution dread Raged—an incarnate Fiend—behind! With Ignorance her slave abhorred! And who, in mercy as they said, With many a muttered mystic word, And baneful glance upthrown, 28 Petitioned Heaven to rain its blackest judgment down On thy devoted head! Yes, though thus tried, most gentle one, This was the meed for thee by Destiny designed: That men to nobler motions born, Should well atone their fathers’ scorn, By glorying in the fearless might of thy surpassing mind.

Even so then, all was well, Though brief—too brief thy stay! Thy Name remains, a strengthening spell To all who, banding against Error rude, Would do the unwilling world some good, Nor idly pass away— A vapour, nothing more, athwart the realm of day! And though the heart and brain be food For dusty Death, where erst,—a precious brood, Like quires of Seraphs thy bright dreams did dwell, What light around us these, remaining, fling! For lovelier splendors never fell In star-drops from Urania’s wing! And Freedom, in her promissed age, Shall constellate her spheres with glories from thy page.

But hark! yet from her haunted cell Built on that dubious brink of the Unknown, Where Fraud and Fear and Phrensy dwell, Superstition’s sullen bell Tolls thee to her deepest hell! Blind Fury! can she think alone, A soul like thine, though in its earthly race Bedimmed by many an error, Should lossward at her bidding sink in terror?— 29 Enough!—Wherever Love and Grace May soar beyond the grave—there’s its admitted place.x

x To prent my admiration of Shelley’s poetical genius and character, from being misinterpreted into a kindred atheism, and yet, at the same time, to vindicate the right he had, not only to worship at whatever shrine he chose, but also to advocate whatever he believed true; I will append to this Monody some remarks on what I consider a proper Liberty of Speculative Opinion.

To begin:—I believe in the existence of a God, the creator and providential disposer of all things. Nothing, I appre- hend, could shake me from this belief; but am I therefore to denounce the atheist, who in all social respects may be a far better man than myself; seeing that many so believing ac- cording to general notion, have been singular for the moral beauty and purity of their lives? By such a course, what do I do more than instigate him in turn to denounce his denouncer, till between us, Charity,—that which should be “all mankind’s concern”,—is hunted from our homes? If it be a damnable sin to disavow any established or peculiar Idea of the Deity, upon the disavower’s own head be it at the tribunal of Heaven—not here. But what if his avowal carry with it points of disbelief as to the God-Ideas of every other man or set of men in the world? He should be left perfectly free notwithstanding, either to believe or disbelieve, since he is fallible only in common with all other men; and openly to state and reason upon his convictions either way, so long as this be done in decent terms and without personal intrusion. Out of blending contrariety harmony aris arises; and our moral differences should but constitute the moral music of life.

I will endeavour to illustrate the opinions just stated by an Apologue. But I would previously observe, because of the shocking import of the word Atheist, that perhaps no highly intellectual man is, or ever was, what is commonly understood by this term. Shelly, for 30 instance, was not. He was eminently spiritual in his speculations; and in fact, his Energy of Love, or Good, has many of the attributes with which our best con- ceptions of the Deity endeavour to illustrate his benef- icent Supremacy.—On the other hand, the vulgar irreligionist is never an atheist. He neither believes nor disblieves: his intellectual state, with regard to spiritual things, being simply that of a brute indifference. So much by the way, and now to the Apologue.

Yet these might perceive in clear weather, from their own Island, which they called Earth, that a mighty Tree grew upon the summit of the other, which they had named Heaven.
