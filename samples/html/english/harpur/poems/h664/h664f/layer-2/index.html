A Vision of an Angel.

By Charles Harpur.

[I was once told by a literary friend whose critical judgment I still greatly value, that I had committed a chromatic sin, in having (in this Poem) visioned the wings of an Angel as being otherwise than white: that to color them was to violate the traditions, so to speak, of the sister art of Painting. x or conformed to the standards of any men custumary. But Poetry and Painting, though sisters, are not twins. Much less are they rhymed together, even in tendency, upon any concievable Darwinian theory: for, though daughters of the one mother—namely, Ideality, they are of a distinct birth, [and born each] of them with a much-differing idiosyncrasy, and to a wi[dely disparate] mission. Hence the vision [of the Poet is not that] of the [Painter even] upon the same track, or when his [...] is to describe to the imagination, more than to [paint to the eye; nor] are his pictures to be measured by literal [standards] material calm [unclear] [... their] very vagueness their very elusiveness is often their [merit.]

x Now had my angel been feminine, I might have felt that there was something —though a servile something— in this notion of his. But Even in the Vision below, as no doubt the reader will see, there is scarcely anything that could be painted, except to the imagination through the medium of words. All the colors and outlines, with the voice itself, are alive, and in the instant act of intermixing, and fusing together, from first to last.

Moreover, for the coloring of angelic wings, there is the highest poetical authority—the only kind of authority, besides that of his own ideal instinct, which should be held paramount by a greatly-conscious Poet. Here is an example from Spencer, who, be it observed, has even been called, and not without propriety, the Painter’s Poet.

Beside his head there sat a fair young man Of wondrouys beauty and of freshest years, Whose tender bud to blossom new began, And flourish fair above his equal peers; 438 His snowy front curlèd with golden hairs, Like Phœbus’ face adorned with sunny rays, Divinely shone; and two sharp wingèd shears Shining with diverse plumes, like painted jays, Were deckèd at his back to cut his airy ways.

No doubt an Angel with wings such as are here described, would be somewhat gaudy in a Painting;—that is, as beheld by the bodily eye; but when looked at through that of the imagination only, the subject is so far removed into the ideal—so far spiritualised by the process, as to become divested of all [offence] of that kind. And out of this difference of medium [through] which we look at the pictures of Poetry and at those [of the Painter], there naturally accrues to the former art much [...] sense as to treatment, and a far wilder liberty as [...] means of elaboration.

But for [the occasi]onal coloring of angelic wings, there is also the authority of Milton; part of whose description of Raphael is superbly in point.

A Seraph wing’d: six wings he wore, to shade His lineaments divine. The pair that clad His shoulders broad, came mantling o’er his breast With regal ornament; the middle pair Girt like a starry zone his waist, and round Skirted his loins and thighs with downy gold, And colors dipt in heaven.]

I had a vision of an Angel, who Within the glimmering circle of my half Awakened consciousness, arising, stood, Just where its dim verge met the dark of Sleep: Even as the Moon, on the horizon, stands 439 Within the dream-gaze of the slumbering Earth— Stood radiantly disclouded, as it seemed, From the illimitable dark of Sleep, And shadowing his snowy beauty so As but the more confirmed its glorious grace, With ample-folding wings, whose paradise plumes With every stir, inter-reflecting, glowed With colors such hues as still intenslier mix In the bright borders of an evening cloud, When, like a many-skirted fleece, it hangs Over the sunset, in the burning west.

So stood He, a resplendent Apparition! And could I now re-voice to human ears The wondrous things he spake—spake with a tongue Like those that, on the day of Pentecost, Flamed down on the first Christian[s, and] from which His words came raying, each word like a star, Though, with a subtle fusion, each and all Did flow thenceforth into one luminous wave That broke on the soul’s ear, and, breaking, chimed Such golden miracles of melodious sound As Beings attune whose breath of life is light, Not air impure, the vital draught of mortals! —Could I re-voice those oracles—Oh, then Should all who heard, in utterance jubilant, Conjoin a symphony in words like these:

“

Nations no more shall trade in war; no more Shall banish charity from the homes of earth; But all mankind, together knit in love, Shall henceforth move in concord with all truth, All excellence, all benefit, all delight, All goodness; while, as from a well whose springs 440 Are central as that peace of God round which All secular appearances revolve, And, so revolving, waste like summer clouds,— They drink eternal strength and godlike beauty From one sole Faith, whose waves of health shall flow As in the beginning—out of Paradise!”

Euroma, 1865.
