(For the Empire) Poems by Charles Harpur.

I

The English Emigrant Girl’s Departure.

She is going in the beauty and the freshness of her youth From the honest hearts that love her in the spirit of their truth; For comfort or for sorrow, to obey or to command, Beneath another heaven, in a far rude-breasted land.

There is grief upon the waters! for the anchor now is weighed, And the ship is moving outward, and the last farewel is said, By the fond old friends who knew her in her better days of yore, And whose hearts are moaning in them—We shall never see her more!

Yet there’s hope upon the waters! and waved is many a scarf From the boat of those returning, without her, to the wharf; And she is calm, though pensive, for the burst of grief is o’er, And her soul is roaming outward tow’rds a dream-depicted shore.

Could we look into the future, could we lift its cloudy viel, There, beneath a warmer heaven, in some maize-clad mountain vale, Or beside some wild-wood river, or on some prairie wide, We might see a lonely mourner, or behold a happy bride.

But vainly would we vision what the future must conceal, And let it comfort nature to wish largely for her weal: And is she not a Woman? and how can aught so dear, Amongst creatures that are human have anything to fear
