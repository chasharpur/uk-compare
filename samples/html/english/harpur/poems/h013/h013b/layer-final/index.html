III.

Antipodean Consistency.

In England Duffy stormed, or else I dream, Against our Constitution. Our condition As being adverse to it, was a theme On which he out lowed Lowe in opposition! But here he holds it quite a model scheme— The destined engine of a godlike mission! Is this consistent? Then, Sir, if you please, What do we mean by the Antipodes?*

* Moreover, this revolutionist in Ireland is no revolu- tionist here. Oh, no! Nothing else perhaps could have improved his political status there; but here he would first, like our friend Mr. Ableman (whose next mask will be a godlike moderation) make a spanking fortune in these gold teeming times—and then begin to think of rubbing noses with a Republic. But not before. And if he would have been content, as he says he would, with a Constitution for Ireland such as ours, what a mere halfling must he have all along been? and what wonder that he and his party should have failed?—that the all- in-earnest Irish people would not follow such pseudo leaders? Halflings never succeed: and let Australian Republicans lean upon no such reeds. Nevertheless, let them bide their time wisely. Let them be wholly true to their principles for the nobility of being so, but with- out fretfulness or anxiety as to their sudden formal fulfilment; for there is nothing written in the book of destiny more surely than this,

“We’ll have a brave Republic In this Southern Land of Ours.”

And I who overlook these written words—even I, its prophet—with the same bodily eyes shall see its advent[.]

Meanwhile, however, let us not befool ourselves by looking for any great things from our present so called Responsible Government, though arising out of a Constitution which Mr. Duffy may consider a model. 5b For, in reality, what is it but a pretense based on a pre- tense? What, in its popular bearing, but a poor, tied-up watch-dog sort of a thing that is privileged only to bark? and what, in its ministerial, but a mere rent in the old dead wall of restriction, through which men, half in earnest, may push themselves to play at politics, and gamble for office, and thereby feather, or hope to feather, their individual nests? It should be our part, therefore, to stand, as much as possible, aloof from it, since for us to tinker at it or juggle with it, would be only to waste our public virtue, or deprave our political instincts. For the present, and for some time to come, the intellectual half lights of the country will be quite equal to all the political work absolutely necessary: and let them do it. It will be their natural and predestined vocation; and the results of it will be just the national crab apples and nublins they were born to produce.
