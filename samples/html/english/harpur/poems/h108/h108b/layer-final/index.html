ORIGINAL POETRY.

CONTRIBUTIONS TO AUSTRALIAN LITERATURE.

(Being leaves from Charles Harpur’s Wild Bee of Australia.)

No. 1.

A SIGH FOR THE FORTHCOMING.

The only faithless thing is man, Whose evil plan Excludes man’s brotherhood; All else are trusty to the rod And sireship of Almighty God, But Man in his lordly mood.

The honest moon pays down her light In silver bright; The sun in burning gold; The stars above the hills that rise Like thought-informed and earnest eyes, Keep compact as of old.

The birds their pristine glees prolong— The flowery throng Bloom as they bloomed alway; The beasts amid the fields that won, Are loyal to their mould as on Creation’s earliest day.

‘Tis Man alone—dishonest Man! Whose evil plan Excludes all brotherhood! He only, with disnatured mind, Becomes the butcher of his kind! He! in his lordly mood.

‘Tis Man—’tis recreant Man alone, Who builds a throne, And makes it his belief! Who preaches—chiefly to betray! Who teaches—to enslave or slay! Who joys—at others’ grief!

But shall there not be yet a time When this stern Rhyme Shall be no longer true? When Man shall re-create the mood That guileless kept the brotherhood He erst in Eden knew?

There shall! The very dream that we Might faithful be, Assures us that there shall! When Freedom, to her mission true, Shall commonise the all-grasping few So to ennoble all.

Note.—I sometimes think myself fortunate in the worldly experience of the last five years of my life; but at other times, I can scarcely forbear accusing Providence of something akin to malignity in inflicting it upon me: for it has all but ruined my faith in human nature, giving to its whole moral aspect the furtive and cowardly expression of alie. Pretence—mean, miserable, mouthy pretence—would indeed seem its universal mainstay and only anchor of reliance, whether at sea or in port, at home or abroad! And is there no redemption for it? The cold preachments of mere church systems—the stale ethics and frothy legalities of mere class schools and colleges—the meagre moralities of mere conven- tions, social and political—these cannot redeem it: they have been tried, and approve themselves, each and all, but a part of its pretensiveness.

Well, and what then remains to be done? Must we even heave our humanity overboard, as a radical deformity—a thing incor- rigible? Let us first try another remedy.

The brave unpaltering habitude of perfect, educated, social equality—of absolute, instructed, soul-ennobling individual liberty —that is to say, entire freedom from all false, fashionable, and juggling necessities, would assuredly work out for it, in all truthful regards, a more effectual and abiding redemption: and let us first try that. To shackle it down as hitherto, is but to cramp and straiten it into falsehood. To liberate it thoroughly, were to give it, at all events, its best remaining and most honest chance of escape into truth.

All lies and shuffling pretences are the impositions of a slavery of some kind; and the perfect truthfulness of man to man, can be born only of perfect liberty—of a perfect and conscious freedom of man from man: in fine, such a measure of liberty, in all directions, to the individual, as shall be bounded only by the co-extensive liberties of all other individuals.

But would an individual enfranchisement, thus extensive, tend in any wise to relax our sense of social obligations—such as grow naturally out of our humanity, and are sanctioned both by affection and reason? In no wise would it do so. But it would infinitely exalt our moral willinghood. The righteous discharge of all our true duties would be more than ever the conscious glory of our manhood: because, by being separated from all false and mistify- ing requirements, their essential sacredness would be but the more clearly discernable. They would then act upon us with the direct- ness and healthy energy which are the inevitable attributes of truth, when it is divested of all “baser matter.” But no obediential necessities, imposed by the selfishness of mere authority, could any longer be juggled into the place and dignity of duties. And here lies the prime difference between the moral world as it is, and as it should and might be.

The text was written on my reading certain articles in Black- wood’s Magazine, in which the writer labors to deduce whatever incapacity man has hitherto evinced for a state of democracy, from the doctrine of original sin. To this, it will be seen, I so far assent as to admit that to some great depravation of his moral nature (supposing him to have been originally perfect) is owing the fact of his having fallen away from the capacity of such a state; but I hold, at the same time, that the democratic principle is that alone which can eventually restore him to such a measure of social perfection as may be proper and possible to the present stage of his spiritual abidence. It is the Alpha and Omega—the first and the last condition of human community: the lost birthright of equality, upon the regainment of which depends the moral, social, and political salvation of the world. For man must be denied all adventitious rank and power on the one hand, and educated out of all servile respect for them on the other, before his heart and mind can breathe and act freely, and with perfect social integrity.
