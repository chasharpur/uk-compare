52

Valedictory Stanzas to a Wanton.

To myself can I ever atone For believing a reptile divine? For having once blindly commingled my own With a spirit so earthy as thine? To my breast could I ever have strained— Nay, sung in a rapturous lay, A creature with secretive vileness ingrained, Nor feel as degraded for aye? 53 A creature whose beauty was snake-like! whose smile Was brightened by venom, was sweetened with guile!

Nor boast thou, these verses but tell Of a passion that yearns for thee yet! Could the sinner escaped from the perils of hell, Look back with one sense of regret? But ’tis due to the spirit of song, For having once lauded thy name, That contempt, like thy shadow, should track thee along, And a rhyme, thus recording thy shame, Display thee henceforth, base in body and mind, The scorn of thy sex and tho scoff of mankind!

Note. It is Bulwer, I think, that says: “The love we have lost can never be regained. Whatever feeling succeeds it;—whether it be scorn, or disgust, or hatred, or indifference;—that feeling, whatever it be, will continue to the end last”. This remark is as truthful as it is eloquent. And hence is it, that the differences between those who are supposed to love each other, are so often irreconcilable. Some other feeling, in the breast of one or both of the parties, has succeeded to the throne of a revoked attachment—and that feeling can never be deposed!
