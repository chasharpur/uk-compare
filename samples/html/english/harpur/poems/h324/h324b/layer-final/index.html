THE CREED OF LIBERTY.

BY CHARLES HARPUR.

O Liberty, yet build thee an august And best abode in this so lovely clime! The Old World yet, power-trampled to the dust, Hath never known thee in thy perfect prime, Seeing all Rule which at a given time Expires not, as reposed in public trust. And thence renewable but by Suffrage, must Against thee in its nature be a crime! Seeing that all not privileged to name Their Governors—and thence to govern too, Choosing or chosen, but live unto thy shame; That all are slaves in act who may not do Whate’er is virtuous, and in spirit, who Believing aught, dare not avow the same.

Note.—The matter of this Sonnet is clearly in the teeth of the “wisdom of our ancestors.” But the spirit of the past is only pro- perly venerable in so far as it associates with Truth and Vi[r]tue. The greatest modern sticklers for it in the gross, are proverbially the greatest modern blockheads. What, indeed, can be more philosophically laughable than the wiseacre who is credulous to persuade himself, and others, that he can foresee some deep social decline in the abolition of a mere custom or ritual; and who is pro- foundly astonished, it may be, on finding that certain heads, though not dressed after a certain fashion, are nevertheless the actual repositories, sometimes, of a little veritable wisdom?

But it is only in connection with such trifles that the extreme stickler for the “wisdom of our ancestors” is properly ridiculous. With regard to national instutions he is generally a nuisance of too grave a character for philosophical merriment: seeing that he is ever ready to battle furiously for these,—not because they are in agreement with the necessities of the age, or with the spirit of its progress, but on account of the years they have numbered; the more effete, the more stricken with “dry antiquity,” the more in- violate would he maintain them. In short, he is actuated in this respect by a spirit pestilently similar to that which the mere antiquary anknowledges in his estimate of an ancient coin, offer to scour this of its rust, or of any other filthy accumulation that savours of an olden time, and yon are forthwith admonished that you would thereby render it as valueless as a button.

To a few amongst our ancestors we certainly owe much. But of the mass, that chiefly which should engage our admiration, is the inglorious fact, that so many millions and millions of thinking beings should have dared to travel from the cradle to the grave in so shameless a subjection to the hopes, and fears, and pleasures, and profits of their own petty and passing individualities!—leaving to their posterity, so far as they were concerned,—what? The pa- trimony of a lineal ignorance, and the constitutional curse of an hereditary selfishness!

The “wisdom of the past,” then, may be passable enough as a cant phrase in the mouths of our grandmothers; but be it ours, who are young, to ground our faith more deeply and to build our hopes higher in the wisdom of the present. In all matters of moral and social judgment, the world of now must be wiser, though the very pressure of its progressive necessities, than the 14C world of then could possibly have been. It must be wiser from a fur- ther acquaintance with itself—that is, with the social and political elements which are perpetually modifying its instincts, and being themselves modified by its active condition. In other words, the fulfilling nature of man and the real nature of things, becoming more and more developed in their mutual relations, by virtue of there being a course of time for the prolongation of thought and action, become also more readily perceptible, and are thenceforth matter of fixed or fixing knowledge, out of which a present wisdom is extractable transcending accumulatively and in kind—that is, both in quantity and quality, all the boasted wisdom of the past.

This position may be thus analogically illustrated. As man- hood must be more enlighted, speaking generally, through the pro- longation and enlargement of the mere consciousness of being, than youth or boyhood possibly can be, however otherwise favored; even so must it hold of the race collectively in the maturing tendency of its ages. And as at five and-twenty or thirty, we have often to discard a whole heap of fallacies, of which but a few years before, it is likely, we were the most confident and strenuous upholders: even so, with every century, society is making good riddance of the manifold hallucinations of its minority; and so too, for many centuries to come, the spirit of its progress will have to forget for truth’s sake, even more perhaps than it will have to learn notwith- standing all the “wisdom of our ancestors,” and our own in ad- dition.

Despite of the length to which this note has already gone, I will add a word having particular reference to the principle put forth in the conclusion of the text. The right of private judgment —the right of every man to think and determine for himself in all matters, is at the very root of the Tree of Liberty. It is indeed a glorious privilege; and I pity any one who, from whatever consi- derations, may be deprived or bebarred of it by others, or who can deny it to himself. And it must be as safe, upon the whole, as it is glorious; because, assuming of any truth that it is a thing ne- cessary to be known (and we must assume that much to render it other than a thing a thing indifferent) the universal mind of man, in its perceptive relation thereto, must be progressively equal to this necessity; and because, moreover, such an equality is essen- tial to that harmony in the truth itself which imposes it, and which constitutes, in fact, the peculiar efficacy of any and of all truth. In fine, this right is the very palladium of religious liberty, which, by absolute consequence, can exist intact upon no other basis. And religious and political liberty always grow together, as being equally rooted in intellectual liberty in which the interests of all truth are fundamentally comprehended.

The whole circle of real Freedom—the freedom of the Future, may be thus briefly described. It consists in the right of every man to will, to believe, to do, and to possess, to any extent that may not trench upon the same right in others; the freedom of each individual being thus accurately determined by the equal free- dom of every other individual

And now, reader, strike your compass into the centre of the circle and measure off our Australian portion of it; premising that beef-witted * * *, by virtue of his pelf, and time serving * *, by virtue of his office, and some thirty others similarly qualified, are the creators of the laws by which the political destiny of the present generation is to be shaped, and it may be determined. Well, what is the result? A short scratch with a slight curve in it! A pitiful matter truly; but then it is a veritable guage of the political “wisdom of our ancestors,” and of some value for that reason.
