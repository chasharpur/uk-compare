The Name of Mary

‘What’s in a name?’—you know the rest But this is true as well, Her name by whom we first were blest Will aye with meaning dwell, And breathe, though spoken but in jest, A visionary spell.

Note. It is somewhat singular, that not only the lost love-pleads, but the wives as well, of the greatest modern British poets should have been mostly Marys. Coleridge’s wife I think was a Mary. The wife of Shelley was a Mary; and so also was the wife of Wordsworth. And but for the charge of vanity which it might lay me open to, I might add (seeing that English is very vernacular) ‘And my wife is a Mary’.

Then amongst the Marys who were not the wives of the poetic adorers, (passing over the fact of its being the “one loved name” of the majority of our sonetteers & songwriters) there was Byron’s Mary, his first love, thinking of whom, he afterwards wrote

“I have a passion for the name of Mary For once it was a magic sound to me.”

And lastly there was Burns’s never to be forgotten ‘Highland Mary: she whose unspoiled soul seemed to yearn ..., through the inspirations of her yet mutual lover, like the reflection of some early star in the wild waters of a mountain lake, and whose untimely death and burial suggested this, the most saintly path to the grave in the world
