To a Relative, on being twitted by him with not being sufficiently sensible of some slight benefits.

Indeed I cannot be your slave 146 For favors (what, a little prog!) Even such as I myself, ere this, Have granted to a stranger’s dog.

But still to show you—even you, That yet my gratitude endures, In this you have my note of hand To grant the very same to yours.

A thoroughly generous person will both give and take equally freely. This test, both ways, is almost infallible. Neither does he ever look with the eye of a usurer upon a friendly benefit; and never calculates, in doing a favor, upon a precise return, even in gratitude. If it be paid him; well: if not, it is still will—for he made no special entry of the matter, on the prospective score of a social profit or loss. It was the token of a friend, with him, or the free gift of a benefactor—not the loan of a tradsman, or the humiliating largess of a patron. A niggardly person, on the contrary, is suspicious and over-scrupulous in both regards. Conscious of the effort which the conferring of a favor costs himself, he is uneasily disposed to credit a donor with an equivalent one—and conseq- -uently feels too much obliged to be genuinely grateful, however he may pursuade himself to the contrary. And because forsooth, more generously constituted persons, cannot behold some two-penny benefit through the same mag- -nifying medium as he himself does, he is always and most fulsomely liable to push an unsettled account of this kind, when in his own favor, to extremity, and reproach them either of forgetfulness or ingratitude. And verily, he has his rep reward,—in the megreness of his own heart. But while this shuts in all thorough fraternity of feeling, like an oyster 147 in its shell, it shuts out every thing else that is socially valuable; so that the gain is but little, whatever the loss may be.

C.H.
