The Rose of Abana.

How beautiful was she, whom men once called The Rose of Abana! my early youth’s Enchantment, and my manhood’s promised bride! When my full strength was like a lion’s,—roused Forth from his lair, being menace from without; My fleetness like a roe’s,—when on the hills, In all the wild exultancy of flight, Seen bounding. But my weary head is now, Like Winter, white and bleak—and where the while Is rosy Myrza? where my youth’s delight? The promised glory of my manhood—where? Hark! as if Nature for her lost child yet Were mourning through the wide world unconsoled, An Echo from the mountain with a far And shuddering intonation, answers—Where?

Since in the triump of the Harvest Home Amongst the virgins I beheld her last, Glow, dancing,—like a peerless star amid The sparkling throngs of heaven,—how many years Have joined their shadowy portions to the Past; And all that weary time have I been thus In heart alone! For in her Father’s house Myrza died early. Into the cruel grave In all her maiden happiness she fell!

To-day I called her mine: To-morrow came, And who might then dispute the spoiler Death’s 116 Dread claim to that fair form, or ask the grave’s White worm to spare it—who? Down with her went All my life’s richness, like a sunset, drawn For ever down into the dark of time! Oh! what a vacuum of the world it made— A drear dull-aching emptiness—to know That never her glad-coming step again Might call out rapture from my listening heart, Even as an Echo from her secret cell, In happy wonder, wild a wild throb, leaps,— Meeting the life-stir that comes beating up Out of green pastures wakened by the morn; Or make my soul, through its serenest deepths, To shudder with a pleasure so extreme, That it resembled pain. Yet though long years Have worn me with their cold and constant drip Of joyless day on day, from youth to age, Her memory lyeth warm upon my brain— Her form is pictured on my eyelids yet In all its wondrous beauty: I have but To close them,—and behold, ’tis in the void That then were else around me. In the fresh And sweet-voiced Spring she died—and never more Came Spring to me! Its freshness with her bloom Had faded, and its once-glad melody marked Only the loss of her harmonious breath.

Like clusters of the vine, when in the light They gleam with ripeness, in bright masses hung The curlèd glories of her graceful head: While, under silk-smooth brows, her azure eyes Yearned deep as the blue dome of heaven, unfilmed Even with a summer cloud;—serenely blue, Yet glowing like moist violets, when the sun 117 First steppeth out from some lit eastern steep— Out into space, and while the globy dews, The unbought jewels of the morn, yet flame In all their fleeting beauty. Like two gardens Of roses and lilies mingled were her cheeks— Two gardens over against each other blooming Upon the slope sides of a sunny hill, When Summer flushes with her earliest fruits, And mated wild birds warble. And most like A singing bird of price, within a cage Of ivory, curtained with a crimson blind, To hide it, when ’twas meet, frome eyes profane, Her well-taught tongue discoursed delectable melody In the sweet prison of her gracious mouth. Nor sweeter than her breath itself was, are The fragrant pantings of a wakening breeze, Which, after soft and sunny showers have wept With a bright passing grief, the flowery death And burial of the love-remembered Spring, Out of some bowery maze comes like her soul Effusing from the earth. Her shapely neck Was as a shaft of polished alabaster Having a spirit of warm life within— As smooth-wrought milky marble, with a quick Rich flow of bloody in its blue-branching veins. Her gentle breasts, secrètively beneath Their silken robe, were like a downy pair Of nestling turtle doves,—there, in their soft And innocent concealment, breathing both So warmly. And her ever-agile motion Was as the pacing of a glad gazelle— One of a year old,that hath never looked From out the secret places of the hills Upon the hunter’s form; and thence hath nought, 118 In all its happy attitudes, to hint Infirmity of purpose [unclear] or of will— Nothing betokening a doubt-shadowed life, Or conscious lapse of peace.

Thus richly meet, And beautifully blooming to the eye, Was Myrza! And no guile, no evil, lurked, Like a bowered serpent hid in flowers, beneath Those visible graces: for her young desires Were utterly innocent,—like a troop of lambs, When briskly gathered in a snowy knot Out of the mother flock, it streameth then Along the green bank of some pleasant brook, That spendeth all its cool and murmurous life In dateless joy, as from decline exempt, ‘Mid shades, in Abana. But what availed Her innocence, her tenderness, and all Her wondrous beauty? In her Father’s house Early she died. Into the cruel grave In all her maiden happiness she fell! And I, her chosen one, with time and toil Thus bleached and broken,—am not I the slave Of tasking strangers in an alien land?

In the deep sacred solitude of night, A solemn Vision, like a sudden cloud, Shadowed my soul. Loved voices and far tones Flowed, as if breathing out of the dead Past, Over my heart,—till, with a dim dread joy, I felt a sweet mysterious Presence come And stand in the near gloom beside my bed, Stilly and tall:—and when I thither looked, ‘Twas Myrza! the belovèd of my youth! 119 Even as in her life-time she had stood Beside me of in Abana;—the same! Save that her sweet face now looked wild and wan, And that her dove-like eyes, with moveless lids, Shone fixed and gleamy, as still waters shine Under their palmy fringes. Forth I stretched My arms—but empty on my yearning breast Together fell they, folded!—and again The weary weight of many lonesome years Rolled whelming, like a monstrous wave of pain, Back on my heart.

The mystery of death— The immortality of love—alas! Had these been with me? were they near me still?— In vain I asked.—The white and solemn light Of the cold midnight moon came slanting through The lattice, from a lurid cloud’s lit verge; The mixing murmurs of the wind grew loud Amongst the sycamores beyond the brook, Whose voice rose sobbing, stronglier it seemed, Under that leafy turmoil; while through all A sudden bleating from the hill-side fold Poured troublous down; and at the gate my dog Howled wildly,—howled as if, even over him, As over me, the Spirit of the Past, While it went sorrowing through the hollow dark, Had swept, too, wail-like,—leaving, when it sunk Into some doom-depth infinitely out, A sole sad sense of utter loss behind!

Let me gaze westward, where she keeps afar The sabbath silence of her early grave! Let me gaze westward, where that sacred spot, 120 Haply, as last I saw it, in the warm And verdant Springtime, takes the morning’s glance, And shines all jewelled with the tears of night; Or, like an emerald, in the sunset glows.
