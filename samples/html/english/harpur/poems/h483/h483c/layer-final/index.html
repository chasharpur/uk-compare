The Rose of Abana.

An Oriental Eclogue.

How beautiful was she whom men once called The Rose of Abana! my early youth’s Enchantment, and my manhood’s promised bride, When my full strength was like a lion roused Forth from his lair, being menaced from without, My fleetness like a roe, seen on the hills In all the wild exultancy of flight, High bounding! But my weary head is now, Like Winter, white and bleak—and where the while Is rosy Myrza? where my youth’s delight? The promised glory of my manhood—where? Hark! as if Nature for her lost child yet Were mourning through the wide world unconsoled, An Echo from the mountain with a far And shuddering intonation, answers Where!

Since in the triumph of the Harvest Home Amongst the virgins I beheld her last, Glow, dancing,—like a peerless star amid The sparkling throngs of heaven,—how many years Have joined their shadowy portions to the Past, And all that weary while have I been thus In heart alone! For in her Father’s house Myrza died early. Into the cruel grave In all her maiden happiness she fell.

To-day I called her mine: To-morrow came, And who might then dispute her with the cold Dark grave’s white clammy worm? Down with her went All my life’s richness, like a sunset, drawn For ever down into the dark of time! 163 Oh, what a vacuum of the world it made— A drear dull-aching emptiness—to know That never her glad coming step again Might call out rapture from my listening heart, Even as an Echo from her silent cell, In happy wonder, with a wild throb, leaps, To meet the happy life-waves beating up Out of green pastures in the vale below; Or make my soul, through its serenest depths, To shudder with a pleasure so extreme That it resembled pain. Yet though long years Have worn me with this cold and constant drip, Her memory lieth warm upon my brain, Her form is pictured on my eylids yet In all its wondrous beauty: I have but That then were else around me. In the fresh And sweet-voiced Spring she died—and never more Came Spring to me! Its freshness with her bloom Had faded, and its once glad melody marked Only the loss of her harmonious breath.

Like clusters of the vine, when in the light They gleam with ripeness, in bright masses hung The curlëd glories of her graceful head. And under smoothest brows her azure eyes Yearned deep,—yea, deep as heaven’s dome, unfilmed Even with a summer cloud: serenely blue, Yet glowing, like moist violets, when the sun First steppeth out from some lit eastern steep— Out into heaven, and while the globy dews, The unbought jewels of the morning, yet Flame in their fleeting beauty. Like two gardens Of roses and lilies mingled were her cheeks— Two gardens over against each other blooming Upon the slope sides of a sunny hill, When Summer flushes with her earliest fruits, And mating wild birds warble. And most like A singing bird of price within a cage Of ivory, curtained with a crimson brede, To hide it, when ’twas meet, from eyes profane, 164 Her well-taught tongue melodiously discoursed In the sweet prison of her gracious mouth. And sweeter was her breath itself, than are The odorous pantings of a timid breeze, Which after soft and shiny showers have wept, With a bright passing grief, the flowery death And burial of the love-remembered Spring, Out of some bowery maze comes like her soul Effusing from the earth. Her slender neck Was as a vase of polished alabaster Having a spirit of warm life within— As smooth-wrought milky marble, with a quick Rich flow of blood in its blue branching viens. While Her gentle breasts, secrètively beneath Their silken robe, were like a downy pair Of nestling turtle doves,—there, in their soft And innocent concealment breathing both So warmly. And her ever agile motion Was as the pacing of a glad gazelle— One of a year old that hath never looked From out the secret places of the hills Upon the hunter’s form; and thence hath nought In all its happy attitudes to hint Infirmity of impulse or of will— Nothing betokening a doubt-shadowed life, Or conscious laps of peace.

Thus richly meet And beautifully blooming to the eye Was Myrza! And no guile, no evil lurked Like a bowered serpent hid in flowers, beneath Those visible graces: for her young desires Were innocent as a troop of snowy lambs, Seen sporting by the summer brooks that spend In shady Abana, as if by choice, Their cool and murmurous lives.—But what availed Her innocence, her tenderness, and all Her wondrous beauty? In her Father’s house Early she died! Into the cruel grave 165 Even in her maiden happiness she fell! And I, her chosen one, with time and grief Thus bleached and broken,—am not I the slave Of cruel strangers in an alien Land!

In the deep sacred solitude of night A solemn Vision, like a sudden cloud, Shadowed my soul. Loved voices and far tones Flowed as if breathing out of the dead Past, Over my heart,—till, with a dim dread joy, I felt a sweet mysterious Presence come And stand in the near gloom beside my bed, Stilly and tall:—and when I thither looked, ‘Twas Myrza! the belovëd of my youth, Even as in her life-time she had stood Beside me oft in Abana: the same! Save that her sweet face now looked wildly wan, And that her dove-like eyes, with moveless lids, Shone fixed and gleamy, as still waters shine Under their palmy fringes. Forth I stretched My arms—but empty on my yearning breast Together fell they, folded! and again The weary weight of many lonesome years Rolled whelming, like a monstrous wave of pain, Back on my heart! The white and solemn light Of the cold midnight Moon came slanting through The lattice from a lurid cloud’s lit verge; The mixing murmurs of the wind grew loud Amongst the sycamores beyond the brook, Whose voice rose sobbing, stronglier it seemed, Under that leafy turmoil; while through all A sudden bleating from the hill-side fold Poured troublous down; and at the gate my dog Howled wild and long,—as though even over him, As over me, the Spirit of the Past Had, like a wail heard sorrowing through thr dark, Swept also,—withering, as it went, away Into the silent depths of the wide world, To leave a weary sense of loss behind!

Let me gaze westward, where she keeps afar The sabbath silence of her early grave! Let me gaze westward, where that sacred spot, In the warm Springtime, takes the morning’s glance And shines all jewelled with the tears of night, Or, like an emerald, in the sunset glows!
