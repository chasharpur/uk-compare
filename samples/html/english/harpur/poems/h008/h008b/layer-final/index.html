Allan of the Mill.

An Australian Ballad.

“Come Daughter braid thy glossy hair, And deck thy beauty gay; The Squire has sent a message fair, That he’ll be here to day: And he has gold in countless store, And flocks on many a hill”— Young Fanny heard—to think the more Of Allan of the Mill.

“Why droop thine eyes, my Child? —nay, kiss Thy Mother, though she chide: And think how brave a thing it is To be a rich Man’s bride! To play the queen with full a score Of servants at thy will:”— She heard—but only thought the more Of Allan of the Mill.

She thought how true he was of heart, Of form how fine and free; And how in every generous part The worthiest Youth was he: How fair his front—his songs how sweet, Whilst She their theme was still; And how her heart sprang forth to meet Young Allan of the Mill.

“O Mother, what can gems and gold, Or hoards of worldly store, With hearts dissatisfied and cold, 638 But weigh them down the more? And willing vows, as purely free As dews from Heaven distil, Have wooed me long the bride to be Of Allan of the Mill.”

“Nay Daughter, be no longer loth To yield to our desire! Besides, your Father’s made an oath That you shall wed the Squire! Ere give you to that landless Youth, Your very life I’d spill”— She heard—to think upon the truth Of Allan of the Mill.

The Squire he came—a slave of Self, A Creature stony-cold! And ere he went, those Two for pelf Their Child to him had sold: And soon her heart that late was heard Glad flowing as a rill, Grew mournful, like a widowed bird, For Allan of the Mill.

Her step grew slow, and wan her cheek, Her glowing glances dim, Until they named the hateful week, Should tear her love from him! And then her step waxed firm again, Her eyes beamed stedfast will; And she was heard no more to ‘plain For Allan of the Mill.

But when the fresh and jocund Spring 639 In green and gold attire, Brought on the Day, with breezy wing, That she should wed the Squire; Her father heard, at dawn of light, Her Mother screaming shrill— “Up—up! our Daughter’s taken flight With Allan of the Mill!”

That day, a Horseman passing there, Upon the over night Had met, he said, a youthful pair That seemed in hasty flight: And what the Traveller had shown, As both were absent still, Left none to doubt but she was flown With Allan of the Mill.

x Ten years since then, at Time’s old call, Are gone in weal and woe: And now the Squire, her parents,—all In death are lying low: And when her childhood passed, her name Was all but lost, until Revived there through the wide wealthy fame Of Allan of the Mill.

For where a distant Mansion fair O’ertops the woods so green; A matron beautiful is there— The Angel of the scene! And as six Cherubs in her sight Desport with merry skill; She blesses aye her happy flight With Allan of the Mill.
