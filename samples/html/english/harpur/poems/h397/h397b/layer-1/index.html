Prayer.

Wherever, Youth, thy lot may fall, Whatever be thy moral plan, Pray daily to the God of all— ‘Tis needful to the heart of Man.

That grace shall then attest thy call I cannot say;—but this I can, That Prayer unto the God of all Is sweetening to the heart of Man.

Should evil snares thy steps enthrall, And wouldst thou yet reform thy plan; Pray daily to the God of all— ‘Tis strengthening to the will of Man.

That grace shall meet thy suppliant call I cannot say;—but this I can, That prayer unto the God of all Is victory to the will of Man.

Note. It has been said that I am destitute of Religion. What! because I am not a set frequenter 91 of some particular Church or Chapel? Certainly, my religion may not altogether agree with Doctor Poulding’s, nor with Doctor Broughton’s, nor even with Doctor Lang’s, in so far at least as it is Cal- -vinistic. Notwithstanding all this, however, I would ask the unbigoted reader, whether the man who conceived the above Poem, and composed for himself the ensuing Prayer, amid the profane associations of a Bush life in Australia, can be in any rational sense “destitute of religion”? can be a wanting in a profound feeling of his utter God-dependence, however he may have been occasionally been unfaithful to the light of his own mind, or have fallen under the burthen and imperfection of his [mortality?] humanity?
