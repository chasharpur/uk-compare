Virtueless Persons.

O for a style like figured silk, to dress The safe, sleek seeming of the Virtueless! I cried—but changed my mind, and thought it fitter To strip it stark, and be a little bitter.

The Virtueless!—who are they? They who draw Through Custom’s lungs their breath of life, and deem All right is made and measured by the law, And all men just as good as they can seem.

Note—There is a sort of persons so consciously destitute of all high principle—by whose education and habits of life, these are so utterly ignored—that to insist upon them in their presence, ever so generally, is an implied offence to them. All other persons and all things, that are not pliant and time-serving, like them- selves, they hold to be dangerous, and that it is wise to avoid them. They can believe in no majesty that has not been travelled to by a royal road, and understand no heroic claims that have not been sanctioned by success. In a word, they have no faith in them for anything lying beyond the boundaries of the old material world of custom and trade—no, though it tower heavenward, large and obvious, like a great mountain.

Still, they may be fond of going to church in their best dresses, and never omit their ordinary prayers: for religion is a respectable sort of thing, and they have therefore the habit of thinking there must be something in it. But it must call upon them for no sacrifices. If it do, they are infidels in a trice; the real kernel of all their piety being this, that men are sent upon the earth, by God or the Devil (what matters it which, to the virtueless?), just to sweep property together by any means that do not hang or transport them, or hit a customer on the eye, or overskin a client, to the detri- ment of their business; and to live, at the same time, a life of fleshly comfort and external show. The inward, the essential, the spiritual, have no pedestals in their earthy Pantheon. And this, I say, is their whole duty of man, religious and moral.

And these persons, though virtueless, are seldom criminals, being rather remarkable for a tame con- formity to law. The mere lack of personal nerve and impulsive boldness, keeps them decent and orderly. Or they are thus decent and orderly from social compliance, and for the look of the thing; and not from any genuine love of either decency or order: for they have a pure liking for nothing but their own petty welfare, and for those worldly appliances which the most palpably conduce to it. In a word, if they are moral and orderly, it is because they are passionless and cowardly. I would as lief, myself, be as very a wretch as was ever dragged through a horse pond, as a model man upon their model.

But what is the general social grade of these virtueless persons? They are mostly of the ordinary species of merchants and shopkeepers, clerks and attorneys, and well to do people, with whom gain is godliness, and a small conventional gentility the sole circle and touchstone of merit. The veriest ruffian is often a nobler being than a person of this sort: for a portion of the great unsophisticated heart of humanity may yet be alive in his rude bosom, and ready to glow to the divine warmth of a lofty sentiment, or bow down in homage to a magnanimous principle. But the persons I write of are virtueless in every direction without perhaps being absolutely vicious in any; unless they happen to con- stitute the main mass of a people. Then indeed, cor- ruption sets in upon them with a flood tide (for venality is their public bias) and at once carries them as a com- munity, on a voyage to the dogs.
