The Burthen worst to bear.

Tho Fate should signalise our lot With Glory’s trumpet tone, Yet if some sweet soul mirrored not The sweetness of our own, Even joy to madness were but kin, And hope unborn despair— 33 A weary load of heart within, The burthen worst to bear.

And though we dwelt in Liesure’s cot ‘Mid shades forever green, If Love there too a home had not, To molodise the scene, Even poesy were but a din, And taste itself a care— A weary load of thought within, The burthen worst to bear.

O yes, true harmony of mind Is not beneath the sun, Until we love all humankind The more in loving One. Then say not that it tastes of sin, This fond—this clinging care, To cast the weary load within— The burthen worst to bear.

Love Human and Divine.

Note. Fanatical purists and pietists are 34 grievously prone to impute sin upon the ground of our loving the creature more than the Creator:— thereby evincing much poverty of feeling and vulgarity of conception. But luckily such ground is merely putative, and lies in No Man’s Land: the supposed sin in that case being labored out of a faulty distinction, or rather from an incorrect generalisation of the quality of Love. [The love of God and the love of man or woman, or of any other creature, are essentially different —different in kind. We love the Creator, when we love him purely and properly, through our reason only: we love the creature, when this love is direct and natural, through our feelings as well,—and primarily through our feelings. True, a man and woman may matrimonially ally themselves under motives founded on the mere conclusions or preferences of reason, and fancy such an engagement the result of Love. But Love, properly such, may still be as far away from it as heaven is from earth. At all events, it cannot be present in such a union, until the sanctions of the senses and feelings,—superinduced by habit or otherwise,—shall have given vitality to 35a these preferences of reason, which (in a human direction) are cold and bloodless of themselves as the stones of a dead wall. Love of the creature that is not primarily sensuous, is destitute of the warmth and energy which are proper to it. But to love God sensuously were to love him impiously. For as there may be excess in our love of the creature; so also may there be in our love of the Creator. This assertion may startle, but it is true notwithstanding. The cause of the excess in the first case is the giving ourselves wholly up to the bias of our feelings, to the obvious disparagement of reason; while its cause in the latter, is the allowing the dictates of our reason to descend and mingle with the “baser matter” of our passions: the effect of the first kind of excess being an habitual incontinency of animal appetite, ending in moral emas- -culation; of the latter, a fanatical pruriency, suggestive of obscene rites and irrational mysteries. 37b The former species of excess, and the specific effects of it are very observable in the social world, while All Eccleastical History affords abundant evidence of the fact of the latter species of excess, and of the peculiar effects which are here ascribed to it.

Moreover, I am disposed to question the wholesomeness of that habit of mind amongst over-pious persons, which assumes 36 it necessary /as Hannah Moore did/ to be constantly keeping a cat-and-mouse sort of watch upon the dim impulsive inklings and remote possibilities of sin. It is not the best sign of moral health, depend upon it; nor even of bodily purity. It is even indica- -tive, I think, of something that is very like an envious dispositional proclivity to sensual excesses, and which is only deterred from its objects, if deterred indeed, by the fear of hell and the whip of the hangman. “Goodness thinks no ill, where no ill seems”; niether does genuine purity: and hence the almost unsearchable wis- dom of the saying “to the pure all things are pure.” Can it be other than a species of moral captiousness at the best, which forbids us to enjoy the apparent limpidity of some new fountain of human affection, until it be fully ascertained whether there be or be not any mud at the bottom? Now there may be some mud there, and yet the overflowing liquid be quite pure enough—quite commo- 37a -dious for all the purposes of healthy refreshment.
