Gramachree.

She said her heart was wholly mine, And this to hear her say, Has made mine own to leap for joy, Even like a fawn in play; But now her scorn that once glad heart Is wounding to the core— Ah, gramachree, mo challic nouge, Mo Molly Astore.

And all the wasted feeling I In Passion’s rosy dream, Poured from the Eden of my soul Around her like a stream, Returned upon my heart, o’erwhelms The life it buoyed before— Ah, gramachree, mo challic nouge Mo Molly Astore.

Oh, wayward Love! thy sunny smiles When first they warm the breast, Can bid earth’s drearest region seem A Heaven for the blest:— But why should things so glad e’er make Life’s after pains the more?— Ah, gramachree, mo challic nouge, Mo Molly Astore.

The hunted roe, when reached his rock, May bid his anguish cease; The wounded dove may flee away And be again at peace; But I, where’er I fly, from grief Can shelter find no more— Ah, gramachree, mo challic nouge, Mo Molly Astore. x

x By way of Note to this Song, I will append a third and last extract from my Singleton Speech anent the woes of Love [unclear] versus Debt; which though not strictly in agreement with all the circumstances of the song text, is yet sufficiently illustrative 167 of its general expression for annotative companionship.— ”Terrible also must be the disappointment of one who, by the inev- -itable pressure of an inscrutable sympathy, is compelled into the thrall of some beautiful creature—beautiful to his imagination as Milton’s Eve when gazing into the fountain upon her own new loveliness; but by whom, alas! this his involuntary attachment is resented either from a proud dislike or from a capricious disdain. “He sees her smile on all the rest, But coldly pass him by:” and

his position therefore, is like that of the Peri at the gat

e of Paradise: An Elysium of love is within his ken, radiant with the beams of a surpassing beauty, and wherein happier Spirits may rejoice over its manifold and unparagoned delights—but upon which he can only gaze under the cold consciousness of a hopeless exclusion; till crushed by the cruel contrast, he shall turn away to complain—to weep—to groan in the unspeakable anguish of his soul.”

The reader will have seen ere this, that in thus collecting and ar

ranging my miscellaneous Poems, I have allowed myself no small annotative liberty: and I would explain here, once for all, that this singularity is referable to circumstances of peculiar intellectual isolation. Favoured with the companion- -ship of only one capable man; the possessor of only three books; and of but one hope in the future; I have been mainly living as it were, during the last twelve months, upon my past feelings, fancies, and mental conclusions, as tentatively strained through the alembic of memory. C.H. 1847.
