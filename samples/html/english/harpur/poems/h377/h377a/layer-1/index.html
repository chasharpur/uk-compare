The animal force and feeling of Byron with the mental sensuousness of Keats, the moral beauty of Wordsworth, and the gorgeous ideality of Shelley, in equal proportions and intimately blended, in the constitution of one man, would make him, perhaps, a perfect Poet. I say, perhaps—for it is questionable whether it should be a desirable one: for it would preclude all raciness—all idiosyncasy; and notwithstanding even that, the “fit audience” would be “few” indeed—or rather altogether a wanting. His poems would not tell: they would not hit hard enough upon the coarse materials around them. All the salient points and angles of a comon sympathy with a faulty race would be rounded away from them by the perfection of their author. In a word, they would not cut into common wear; and would never, for that reason, come into popular appliance: for the human can only heartily appreciate the human—not the angelic.
