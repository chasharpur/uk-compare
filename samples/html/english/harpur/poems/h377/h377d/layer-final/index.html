[A Perfect Poet]

“Wish not the Poet perfect: were he so, What part of him were ours, I’d like to know?

The animal force and feeling of Byron, with the mental sensuousness of Keats, the moral depth of Wordsworth, and the gorgeous ideality of Shelley, commensurately blended in the constitution of one man, and curbed in their activities by a consummate judgment, would make him, perhaps, a perfect poet. 3d I say perhaps,—for it would still be questionable whether a perfect poet were a possible character (for what we write with fervour is from our character), without the additional and guardian conditions of a supreme moral culture, based upon an inviolable per- sonal standing, and such an extent of worldly further- ance as no true son of the Muses (not even Words- worth) has hitherto met with. But it is also ques- tionable whether it should be a desirable one; for, as judged from below, or out of its own consummate sphere, (and by spirits not equally free of that sphere, at least potentially,) its very round- ness would be preclusive of all typic affinity and appreciable raciness: and thus from sheer elevation, the “fit audience” would indeed be “few,” if not altogether wanting. His poems would not tell—they would not hit hard enough, nor home enough, upon the coarse materials of which the current public is always composed as a whole. The protuberances—the knobs (so to speak)—fitting them for much sympathetic contact and interjowling with a so widely imperfect brotherhood would be wholly prevented by the faultless mould of their too super- lative author. Or to figure the matter otherwise, and in fine, they would be a cloth of gold that would not cut into common wear, and would never, for that reason come into popular appliance: for the human can only heartily sympathise with and appreciate the human—not the angelic.

Euroma, September, 1866.
