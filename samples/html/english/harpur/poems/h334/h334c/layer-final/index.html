THE NIGHT BIRD.

To cheer my lonely dwelling shed, Upon its humble roof is heard, As duly I retire to bed, A little nightly Bird: Hark! ever there the creature sings Its iterant but melodious things.

Beneath the silent shining stars, And while the moon pays down her light, Although no wiery thraldom bars The freedom of its flight, There nightly thus it ever sings Its iterant but melodious things.

And while I listen, there will come Dim memories of the past, that say Upon my childhood’s happy home A little shrilling lay Was nightly heard, like this that brings Those iterant but melodious things.

I know not if it were in kind The same with yon peculiar bird, But on my Father’s roof I mind, How often we had heard A voice like this without that sings Those iterant but melodious things.

But where is he, the parent sage, Whose Seven then met his nightly call? And he, whose cheek of tenderest age Was fondliest kissed of all? Ask Sorrow—while the creature sings Its iterant but melodious things.

And now too there’s a grave new made ‘Mid scenes to memory unknown, And there cold stranger hands have laid Our manly hearted John! I weep—while still the creature sings Its iterant but melodiouz things.*

* ”We were Seven”—and our family in its earlier years was pre-eminently a happy one. In fact, I have never since seen a greater degree of domestic peace and unity than was presented by my Father’s household for many prosperous years.
