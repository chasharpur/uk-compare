The Night Bird.

To cheer my lonely dwelling shed, Upon its humble roof is heard, As duly I retire to bed, A little nightly Bird: Hark! ever there the creature sings Its iterant but melodious things

Beneath the silent shining stars, Or while the moon pays down her light, Although no wiery thraldom bars The freedom of its flight, There nightly thus it ever sings Its iterant but melodious things.

And while I listen, there will come 36 Dim memories of the Past, that say Upon my childhood’s happy Home A little shrilling lay Was nightly heard, like this which brings Those iterant but melodious things.

I know not if it were in kind The same with you peculiar bird, But on my Father’s roof I mind How often we had heard A voice like this without which sings Those iterant but melodious things.

But where is he, the parent Sage Whose Seven then met his nightly call? Where him, whose cheek of tenderest age Was fondest kissed of all? Asks Sorrow—while the creature sings Its iterant but melodious things.

And now too, there’s a Grave new made ‘Mid scenes to Memory unknown, And there cold stranger hands have laid Our manly-hearted John! I weep—while still the creature sings Its iterant but melodious things.

Yet in the memories that steep Mine eyelids, there’s a gentle lore That brings a balminess to sleep, And gives me all the more To love the Creature thus that sings Its iterant but melodious things.x

x ”We were seven”—and our Family in its earlier years 37 was pre-eminently a happy one. In fact, I have never since seen a greater degree of domestic peace and unity than was presented by my Father’s household for many prosperous years. But Trouble at length made her abode in our midst—and a few evil seasons were enough to break up the foundations of our domestic welfare, and scatter us, some in life and some in death, over half the world; beginning with Henry our youngest, who died a child. Then, after a long but fruit -less struggle in the desolate bye ways of Fortune, our father followed; and latterly our brother John, who died in early manhood at Seymour, a wild district some- -where beyond Melbourne, and was shovelled into his lonely grave (as we learned afterwards) without even a burial service. Of our youngest living brother, William,—if he be living,—who went to sea, a boy, we have heard little for years:—and this is nearly the whole sum of the past and present fortunes of our Family.— Such is life—a fever-dream ending in death.

But should the reader infer from the manner of John Harpur’s burial that he could not have been of any “mark and likelihood”, he would err greviously. He was a man of fine natural parts; was honest, generous, and brave. But he lived and died in the Australian Bush, and amongst those by whom a dead Crichton, if not richly related, whould be given to the dust “without a stone to mark the spot”. I speak advisedly. Such would inevitably have been my own case, had I have died amongst those with whom I was compelled to herd during the most intellectual years of my life. Not that such a social state is aught to the dead. They are happily beyond its low thoughted influence. But it perpetuates the brutal- -ization of the living.

C.H. 1847.
