SONGS, EPIGRAMS, NOTES, AND OPINIONS, &c., BY CHARLES HARPUR.

No. 14.

1.

Expediency.

Great truths are plain but to the few Who look from Freedom’s giddiest height, And thence in word and deed pursue Not the Expedient—but the Right.

Note.—In politics, as in all morals, we should ever be wary of the plea of Expediency. In the hands of a state- sophist, it may be stretched into an apparent justification of any manner of measure; and in those of a tyrant, it is but necessity in disguise. It is expedient, for example, that the franchise should be guarded by intelligence and property: therefore, it is argued, none but men of education and material substance should be entrusted with the franchise. But it is right that every sane citizen, whose unforfeited liberty is to be legislated for, should have a vote in the choice of his senatorial representative. Again: it is expedient, for the sake of unity, that the Ministers of a particular Government should be all of one faith: therefore, it is argued, its offices should be eligible to those only who hold with the religion of the national majority. But it is not right that any man should be excluded from a social or political advantage or position, on account of his born beliefs and speculative convictions, of whatever kind they may happen to be—whether Christian or Jewish, Pagan or Moslem. It is expedient, says even Brougham, that (say) a thousand men inhabiting a thinly-peopled district, should have a Parliamentary Representative, while it should take five times that number of citizens to form an electoral body having the same privilege. But it is not right that any thousand men who, by dwelling in the country, might have a whole member of Parliament to their share, should forfeit four-fifths of such a claim, because they choose to be or to become denizens of a populous city. Men may submit to be so dealt with, but it is only because they cannot help themselves, or for while they cannot. In spite of all the sophistry that can be pumped upon the fact, they still feel that the thing is not right; and what they feel, truly is the logic of Nature. They still say in their hearts, “We have not got our own, but no matter: when we are able, we will claim it with a strong hand[gap]“ And through causes like this, come Revolutions; for Expediency obstinately opposed to Right, is their surest mother. Whenever, then, Expediency and Right may happen thus to fall foul of each other, we should habitually hold fast to the Right, and kick the Expedient to the Devil. Nay more: whenever Expediency is mainly pleaded in support of any measure, that should be enough of itself to warn us to examine it thoroughly from beginning to end; to probe to the very roots both of it and the arguments adduced in its favour; for it is a plea that in its mere speciousness, almost inevitably masks something that is either positively or latently unrighteous.

To go beyond all Expediency—to leave it behind us for ever—we have but to act on the belief, that Right can only be transitorily involved with evil, and cannot possibly displace anything that it ought not to displace. In other words, it can only be charged with evil, during its purgation from evil, as from a foreign and baser matter which has been wrongfully mixed up with it. In its liberated integrity and permanent effect, it must from the pure aboriginal tendency truthward of all honest human interests, bear a fruit after its kind, and work substantially for its own—that is, for Good. If we will not admit this, let us either cancel the word as meaningless, or brand the thing signified, as a delusion. Let us hold, with Paley, that there is no Right apart from Expediency; or that Right is a device, and not an essence[.]
