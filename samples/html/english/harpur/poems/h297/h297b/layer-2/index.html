II

Obituary Lines.

I stand beside my Father’s grave. He died Too late, perhaps, since he endured so much Of corporal anguish sweating from within; But not an hour too soon, for his bruised heart Had long resigned the world, and all his hopes 694a Like faded leaves deciduous, had been blown By blast on blast of sorrow and mischance, From their once vernal hold on what we here Call Nature: he but followed in their wake. Nor was there in the lives of those he loved, Even had he been susceptible of cheer, Enough of fortune to warm into peace A little longer, ere he passed away, The remnant of his chilled humanity. Wet are mine eyes, and my heart akes, to think How much of evil ridged his course of time And mortal pilgrimage; enough, to have slain In most hearts all reliance on the sure Though still reserved supremacy of Good! For few as they, who, on this stormy earth, Can live a long life full of loss and pain, And yet, uplooking through Doubt’s dull clouds, and see In that wide dome which roofs the apparent world Without or seam or flaw, a visible type Of Heaven’s intact infinitude of love! Yet died he a believer in the truth And fatherhood of the Holy One—a God Help-Mighty, nor unmindful of mankind. Yea, in the heavenward reaching light of Faith His soul went forth, as in a sunbeam’s track, Some close caged bird, from a long bondage freed, Goes winging up against the open sky! And Death must be the inheritor of much Transcending this world’s lore, and shrinking all Its boasted wisdom;—must be, at the worst— Even at the worst, a grimly grateful thing, If but for deliverance from doubt, T[he] clinging curse of mortals! In the present [W]hat have we, but the present? All that lies 695 Beyond it lies, or in the iron mould Hath Death borne more,—a measured lot within The gates of the Eternal: for the past Is God’s and only not to reverse no more.
