Obituary Lines.

I stand in thought beside my Father’s Grave: The grave of one who, in his old age, died To late, perhaps, since he endured so much Of corporal anguish, sweating bloody sweat; But not an hour too soon—no, not an hour! 428c Even if, through all his many years, he ne’er Had known another ailment than decay Or felt one bodily pang;—for his bruised heart 429 Had done with fortune! Nor his very tastes, Even the lowliest, had by blast on blast Of sorrow & mischance, been blown like leaves Deciduous, when the year is withering out, From every living hold on what we here Call nature: he but followed in their wake. Nor was there in the lives of those he loved, Even had he been susceptible of cheer, Enough of fortune to warm into peace, A little longer, ere he passed away, The remnant of his chilled humanity. Wet are mine eyes, and my heart akes, to think How much of evil ridged his course of time And earthly pilgrimage;—enough to have slain In most hearts all reliance on the sure Though still reserved supremacy of Good! For few are they, who, on the stormy ball, Can live a long life full of loss & pain, And yet through Doubt’s dull cloud, uplooking, see In that wide dome which roofs the apparant whole Without or seam or flaw, a visible type Of Heaven’s intact infinitude of love! Yet died he a believer in the truth And fatherhood of the Holy One—a God Help-mighty, nor unmindful of mankind! Yea, in the heavenward reaching light of faith His soul went forth, as in a sunbeam’s track Some close-caged bird from a long bondage freed, Goes winging up—up through the open sky! Rejoicing in the widening glow that paths The final victory of its native wings! And whether all was triumph as it went Piercing eternity, or whether cloud 430 Of penal terror gathered in the way, Not less must Death the great inheritor be Of much well worth the having—yea, of much Transcending all Time’s highest lore, and all Life’s boasted values;—must be, at the worst— Even at the worst! a grimly grateful thing, If only through deliverance from doubt, The clinging curse of mortals!—In the flesh, What own we but the present? All that lies Beyond it, lies or in the casual drifts Of embryon need; that, lying open project Tomorrow’s world—or worse, at the wild will Of a demoniac Fortune! But the Dead Have this immunity at least—a known, Fixed lot,—irrevocably fixed, within The gates of the Eternal! For the past Is wholly God’s, and therefore, like himself, Knows no reverse, no change,—but lies for ever Stretched in the sabath of its vast repose.
