ODE TO THE MOON.

Sultan of the stars! now steal O’er clouds like isles beneath thee spread Full-orbed to view; then stand, a seal Of silver on heaven’s mighty lid. Come! round me I would have thy light In one broad burst of glory play! Oh, who while thou thus rul’st the night Could wish the day! Nor feel his yearning spirit fraught With sweetly solemn strains of visionary thought.

Love of my childhood for but when A child I loved thee of all things— Yea, with what extacies I then Did hail thee!—what dear visionings! And when, by high winds wheel’d, beneath Obscuring clouds have wildly throng’d, With searching eye and shortened breath, How have I long’d For wings, that I away might flee, To kiss thy hidden face and dwell awhile with thee.

I sadden! ah, why bringest thou Yet later memories to my mind? Nay, let me gaze upon thee now, As erst, for wonder!—not to find Dim phantoms of each faded dream, That fann’d my heart with pinions dyed In passion, by Old Hawkesb’ry’s stream, Before me glide; And shades of hours all figured o’er With feelings lost, and hopes that know their place no more.

Nor was it thus I looked upon Thy beauty, fewer summers past;— Thus woe-worn, world-distrusting, lone,— And withering in misfortune’s blast: Many that loved me then were by, Of whom now, these I may not trust,— And those are changed; or far; or lie Cold in the dust; Never, alas, to meet again Loving and loved, as then ‘neath thy nocturnal reign.

O Cynthia, it is e’en as though A portion of our spirit fell, As scent from flowers, life’s eras through— And by which web invisible, A gathered after scene of all Affection builded to our loss, Is pass’d thus in dim funeral The mind across: And which, where stained the most with gloom, Uncertain thought is prone to map with spells of doom[.]

But sober reason sager sings; These visioned apparitions are But semblances which former things Imbued our being with, as there 99c Quickened by memory, when is seen Again some mark’d associate sight; And hence it happens, orb serene, Why thou to night Look’st on me from thy native sky, Like an old friend too fond to talk of things gone by.

Let me this night the past forget; For though its dying voices be At times like tones from heaven, yet It bosoms too such change for me,— That when but now my thoughts were given To all I’d suffered; all I’d lost, Turning mine eyes again to heaven, Tear-quencht almost, I started, with a strange despair, To find thee—even thee! smiling unaltered there.

Hence vain regrets of secret pride; My human heart—oh, what wouldst thou? What in the scale of nature tried, Should weigh thy happiness or woe? Numberless millions quick as thee, Have loved—grieved—perished in the light Of this yet youthful moon, since she First claimed the night; And, from the moment of her birth Mature, thus chased, the glooms that swath’d the infant earth.

And be it humbling too, to know That when this pile of haughty clay For ages shall have ceased to glow— Shall into worthless dust decay;— Which, as th’ invading phoughshare drills The unremembered burial-ground, The winds may o’er a hundred hills Scatter around,— That in the midnight heavens thou Shalt hang thy unfaded lamp and smile serene as now.

Nay, more than this;—could even those, The Edenites, who sojourned here Ere Noah’s tilted Ark arose, Or Nimrod chased the bounding deer; Wherever sepulchred, could they, As in the hour of general doom, Now shake awile death’s bonds away, Into their tomb. Solemnly gazing they might find Thee as they dying left thee, watchful Moon behind.

But shall I have these thoughts and not A profitting experience know? Seeing that wither, change, and rot, Must all of earth thine orb below, Should I not turn with this sole aim, In act to shun, in thought control, All that might dim the heavenward flame— Th’ essential Soul I feel within, and which must be A living thing when thou art quencht eternally.

CHARLES HARPUR.
