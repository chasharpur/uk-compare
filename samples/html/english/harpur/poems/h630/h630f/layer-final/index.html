To the Moon.

1.

With silent step behold her steal Over those envious clouds that hid Till now her face, then stand—a seal Of silver on heaven’s mighty lid! So round me would I have her light In one broad sea of beauty lay; And who whilst thus she rules the night Would wish the day?Nor 206 Nor feel his yearning spirit fraught With sweetly solemn strains of visionary thought.

2.

Love of my childhood! for but when A child, I loved thee of all things— Yea, with what happiness I then Would hail thee? what glad visionings? And when between us, up the sky, Obscuring glooms have wildly thronged, With held in breath and searching eye How have I longed For wings, that I away might flee To kiss thy hidden face and ride the dark with thee!

3.

I sadden! Ah, why bringest thou Yet later memories to my mind? I would but gaze upon the now, As erst for wonder;—not to find Dim shades of many a morning joy That erst in Windsor’s sylvan tracks, Glowed round me (a wild Poet Boy) Moving like racks 207 Out in the Past so flecked all o’er With feelings lost and hopes that know their place no more.

4.

Nor was it thus thy beauty shone Upon me fewer summers past— Disheartened, world-distrusting, lone, And shuddering in Misfortune’s blast! Many that loved me then were nigh, Of whom now these I may not trust, And those forget me or they lie Cold in the dust! And never may we meet again Loving and loved as then ‘neath thy nocturnal reign!

5.

Oh Cynthia! it would even seem That portions from our spirits fell, Like scent from flowers, through all Life’s dream, And by which clue invisible, A gathered after-scene of all Affection builded high in vain, Is drawn thus in dim funeral Past us again; 208 And which, where shadowed most with gloom, Uncertain Thought is fain to map with spells of doom.

6.

But sober Reason sagelier sings, These visioned mysteries are but Vague semblances which former things Imbued our being with, as put In act by memory when is seen Again some marked associate sight, And thence it happens, Orb serene, Why thou, to night, Lookst on me from thy native sky Like an old friend too fond to talk of days gone by.

7.

Let me this night the Past forget! For though its dying voices be At times like tones from Eden, yet It bosoms too such change for me, That when but now my thoughts were given To all I had suffered—loved—and lost! Turning again mine eyes to heaven, Tear-quencht almost, 209 I started with a strange despair To find thee—even thee, smiling unaltered there!

8.

Hence vain regrets of secret pride! My human heart, what irks thee so? What in the scale of Nature tried Should weigh thy happiness or wo? Pale millions, so by Fortune curst, Have loved for sorrow in the light Of this yet youthful Moon, since first She claimed the night, And thus mature even from her birth, Chased with pale beam the glooms that swathed the infant Earth.

9.

And be it humbling too, to know That when this pile of haughty clay For ages shall have ceased to glow— Shrunk to a line of ashes grey! Which, as the invading ploughshare drills The unrememberd burial sward, The wild winds o’er a hundred hills May whirl abroad— 210 That in the midnight heavens thou Shalt hang thy unfaded lamp and smile serene as now.

10.

Nay, more than this, could even those, The Edenites, who sorrowed here Ere Noah’s tilted ark arose, Or Nimrod chased the bounding deer— Wherever sepulchered, could they The rigid bonds of death and doom, Now, for a moment, shake away,— Down on their tomb Thy watchful face they still might see, Just as they dying left it, gazing solemnly!

11.

But shall my thoughts thus widely range And I therein no profit know? Seeing that wither, waste, and change Must every form that earth can grow, Shall I not ever henceforth aim To shun in act, in thought control, Whatever dims the heaven-born flame— The essential Soul 211 I feel within, and which must be A living Light when thine is quenched eternally?
