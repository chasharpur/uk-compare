To the Moon.

With silent steps I watch thee steal Over those envious clouds that hid Till now thy face, then stand—a seal Of silver on heaven’s mighty lid! So round me would I have thy light In one broad sea of beauty lay; And who while thou thus rul’st the night, Would wish the day? Nor feel his yearning spirit fraught With sweetly solemn strains of visionary thought?

Love of my childhood! for but when A child I loved thee of all things— Yea, with what happiness I then 179 Would hail thee? what glad visionings? And when between us, up the sky Obscuring glooms have wildly thronged, With held-in breath and searching eye, How have I longed For wings, that I might heavenward flee, To find thy hidden face, and ride the dark with thee!

And hence it was that ever forth My fancy doated more and more, Upon the wild poetic worth Of that old tale in Grecian lore, Which to the head of Latmos gave Supernal glories, passion-won By him who in the mystic Cave,— Endymion,— Was wont to meet thee night by night, And drink into his soul the spirit of thy light.

I sadden! Ah, why bringest thou Yet later memories to my mind? I would but gaze upon thee now, As erst, for wonder;—not to find Dim shades of every morning joy That once in Windsor’s sylvan tracks Glowed round me (a wild Poet Boy), Moving like racks Out [unclear] the Past, so flecked all o’er With feelings lost, and hopes that know their place no more.

Nor was it thus thy beauty shone Upon me fewer summers past— Disheartened, world-distrusting, lone, And shuddering in Misfortune’s blast! 180 Many that loved me then were nigh, Of whom now these I may not trust, And those forget me—or they lie Dark in the dust! And never can we meet again, Loving and loved as then, ‘neath thy nocturnal reign.

O Cynthia! it would even seem That portions from our spirits fell, Like scents from flowers, throughout Life’s dream, And by which clue invisible, A gathered after-scene of all Affection builded high in vain, Is drawn thus in dim funeral Past us again; And which, where shadowed most with gloom, Uncertain Thought is fain to map with spells of doom.

But sober Reason sager sings: These vision-mysteries are but Vague semblances which former things Have long left with us, even as put In act by Memory when is seen Again some marked associate sight; And thence it happens, Orb serene, Why thou, to night, Look’st on me from thy native sky, Like an old friend too fond to talk of things gone by.

Let me this night the Past forget! For though its dying voices be At times like tones from Eden, yet It bosoms too such change for me, That but now my thoughts were given 181 To all I’d suffered—loved—and lost! Turning mine eyes again to heaven, Tear-quenched almost, I started with impatience strange, To find thee—even thee, smiling untouched by change!

O vain display of secret pride! My human heart, what irks thee so? What in the scale of Being tried, Should weigh thy happiness or woe? Pale millions, so by Fortune curst, Have loved for sorrow in the light Of this yet youthful Moon, since first She claimed the night, And thus mature even from herbirth, With pale beam chased the glooms that swathed the infant Earth.

And be it humbling too, to know That when this pile of haughty clay, For ages shall have ceased to glow— Shrunk to a line of ashes grey! Which, as the invasive ploughshare drills The unremembered burial ground, The wild winds o’er a hundred hills May whirl abroad— That in the midnight heavens thou Shalt hang thy unfaded lamp and smile serene as now.

Nay more than this: could even those, The Edenites, who sorrowed here Ere Noah’s tilted ark arose, Or Nimrod chased the bounding deer— Wherever sepulchered, could they The rigid bonds of death and doom, 182 Now, for a moment, shake away— Down on their tomb Thy watchful face they still might see, Just as they dying left it, gazing solemnly.

But shall my thoughts thus widely range, And I therein no profit know? Seeing that wither, waste, and change, Must every form that earth can grow, Shall I not ever henceforth aim To shun in act, in thought control, Whatever dims the heaven-born flame— The essential soul I feel within, and which must be A living Light when thine is quenched eternally?
