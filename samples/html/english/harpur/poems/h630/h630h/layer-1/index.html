To the Moon.

With silent steps behold her steal Over those envious clouds that hid Till now her face, then stand—a seal Of silver on heaven’s mighty lid! So round me would I have her light In one broad sea of beauty lay; And who whilst thus she rule’s’t the night, Would wish the day? Nor feel his yearning spirit fraught With sweetly solemn strains of visionary thought?

Love of my childhood! for but when A child, I loved thee of all things— Yea, with what happiness I then Would hail thee? what glad visionings? And when between us, up the sky, 20 Obscuring glooms have wildly thronged, With held in breath and searching eye How have I longed For wings, that I might heaveward flee, To kiss thy hidden face and ride the dark with thee!

And hence it was that ever forth My fancy doated more and more, Upon the wild poetic worth Of that old tale in Grecian lore, Which to the head of Latmos gave Supernal glories, passion-won By him who in the mystic Cave,— Endymion,— Was wont to meet thee night by night, And drink into his soul the spirit of thy light.

I sadden! Ah, why bringest thou Yet later memories to my mind? I would but gaze upon thee now, As erst, for wonder;—not to find Dim shades of every morning joy That once in Windsor’s sylvan tracks Glowed round me (a wild Poet Boy) Strewn like dun racks Out in the Past, so flecked all o’er With feelings lost, and hopes that know their place no more.

Nor was it thus thy beauty shone Upon me fewer summers past— Disheartened, world-distrusting, lone, And shuddering in Misfortune’s blast! Many that loved me then were nigh, Of whom now these I may not trust, 21 And those forget me—or they lie Dark in the dust! And never can we meet again, Loving and loved as then, ‘neath thy nocturnal reign

O Cynthia! it would even seem That portions from our spirits fell, Like scents from flowers, throughout Life’s dream, And by which clue invisible, A gathered after-scene of all Affection builded high in vain, Is drawn thus in dim funeral Past us again; And which, where shadowed most with gloom, Uncertain Thought is fain to map with spells of doom.

But sober Reason sagelier sings: These vision-mysteries are but Vague semblances which former things Hath long imbued us with, as put In act by Memory when again is seen Again some marked associate sight; And thence it happens, Orb serene, Why thou, to night, Look’st on me from thy native sky Like an old friend too fond to talk of things gone by.

Let me this night the Past forget! For though its dying voices be At times like tones from Eden, yet Its bosoms too such change for me, That when but now my thoughts were given To all I had suffered—loved—and lost! Turning again mine eyes to heaven, 22 Year-quenched almost, I started with a strange despair To find thee—even thee, smiling unaltered there!

O vain regrets of secret pride! My human heart, what irks thee so? What in the scale of Being tried, Should weigh thy happiness or woe? Pale millions, so by Fortune curst, Have loved for sorrow in the light Of this yet youthful Moon, since first She clamed the night, And thus mature even from her birth, Chased with pale beam the glooms that swathed the infant Earth.

And be it humbling too to know That when this pile of haughty clay, For ages shall have ceased to glow— Shrunk to a line of ashes grey! Which, as the invading ploughshare drills The unrembered burial sward, The wild winds o’er a hundred hills May whirl abroad— That in the midnight heavens thou Shalt hang thy unfaded lamp and smile serene as now.

Nay, more than this; could even th[unclear] The Edenites, who sorrowed here Ere Noah’s tilted ark arose, Or Nimrod chased the bounding deer— Wherever sepulchered, could they The rigid bonds of death and doom, Now, for amoment, shake away— Down on their tomb 23 Thy watchful face they still might see, Just as they dying lift it, gazing solemnly.

But shall my thoughts thus wit range And I therein no profit know? Seeing that wither, waste, and change Must every form that earth can grow, Shall I not ever henceforth aim To shun in act, in thought control, Whatever dims the heaven-born flame— The essential Soul I feel within, and which must be A living Light when thine is quenched eternally.
