205b From noon until the setting of the sun, The Rabbi Joseph on his knees had prayed, When from the Synagogue a messenger 205c In haste Approached him, and besought that he 205d Would instantly go forth with him, and pray 205e For one—a Woman—grieviously tormented.

The Rabbi, ever to the call of sorrow, Open as heaven, arose; and girding straight His robe about him, with the man went forth; Who swiftly led him deep into the woods That hung, heap over heap, like broken clouds, Along the southern terraces of Hebron: When through a broad glade suddenly beheld, A wondrous structure, like a palace, showed Its gleaming front and many-pillared porch, Fretted with sculptured,—vinage, flowers and fruit, Or rough with figures exquisitely wrought, As by the hand of Magic. While interposed 140c And still, at pleasant intervals, were growths Of higher verdure, gemm’d with golden pips, Or luminous with odors, glassed below In tiny fountains raying to the light, 140d Each like a shield of pearl.

With pictured walls, and columns gleaming white As Carmel’s snow, or blue-veined as with life, And from whose capitals suspended being Sidonian tissues all with threaded gold Embroidered, or all colored through with dyes Of puce and purple,—rich as sunset clouds. And letting off from these were chambers, filled With tables in mosaic, and on which Were lucent vases of American mould, In christal, and in alabaster, mixed With ewers whose luminous transparency Were stained with inward splendors,—caskets clamped With ivory edges, and all spregged with pearls, And jewelled cups, and lamps with globes of gold, Or feathery branches bearing pine-like cones, [Clasps], viols, mirrors, of unspeakable cost— A pomp of orient wealth, till thus beheld, Incredible!—While in the halls disposed, And in each chamber, all in readiness, Stood trains of servitors, of every clime, Greeks, Persians, Indians, Ethiopeans,—all In richest, though in various, raiment clad. 207 Short time for wonder had the Rabbi, ere A page approached and led him to a still And distant chamber, where the sick one lay. Which entering soon, such brilliance met his gaze As shrank the costliness of all that he Had erewhile seen, though wondrous. Small indeed This chamber was as with the rest compared, Yet obviously contrived to fit a nice And individual privacy. But all Within it showed a sumptuousness extreme, And told of lavish habits,—glowing all With purple & gold, or interblazing quick With royal jewels of a nameless price: While in the midst of all this orient pomp, On profuse pillows of the swan’s new down, Encased in the soft silk of Shiraz,—lo, A queen-like Woman lay:—so beautiful, That though upon her moon-like vizage, pain And languor, like eclipsing shadows, lay, The ancient Rabbi’s pious heart was thrilled With singular tremours as he looked upon her. Young was she—but alas! from time to time, Oer her fair face a hetic flush would break, To leave that utter pallor which portends The manner of the tomb.

From youth to age, Much had the Rabbi Joseph sought in herbs And minerals, the virtues they possess, Soothing or pungent; and, by mixing these, And so uniting them in their effects, With absolute integrity of art, Had, for all ailmemts, fit medicaments. Of these he offered her what to him seemed Then needful. But the dying Woman said: Alas! I want not these, nor did I send To implore thy presence, thinking thou couldst cure A malady as mine,—so fell and fatal— Yea, fatal as the funeral steps of Time! But simply that I might unload my mind Of a dread secret that has been to [unclear] me An ever-haunting and most ominous fear, Hanging and darkening like a thunder cloud O’er all the ways and prospects of my life, And which must quench it ere the moon arise.

The Rabbi answered: If indeed it stand Within my power to serve thee, pour at once Thy sorrow out before me. But, if not, And if thy secret have a deadly taint, 209 Forbear, O Woman to acquaint my soul With aught that it would seek not if itself advised, Or aught that only could for horror hang Upon the memory of a man of Israel.

“I am, said she, the daughter of thy friend Rabbi Ben Bachai—be his memory blessed! But now the widow of a Prince, though one Of Ishmael’s race. Behold you this abode! This Did ever yet the tent-born Arab build Thus for his pride or pleasure? See you all These riches! Tell me, when were such amassed By the grey Desert’s wild & wandering son? Riches he could not even hoard in dreams, As not by him conceivable. Ah, no! Deadly and desperate was the game that gained them! And with a burning bitterness at best Have I enjoyed them ever. And at last How vainly now do I repent them gainless And mend with a repentant hand my peace In abject poverty. Their very taste, Though to my sense but as a thing foregone, How vainly now do I abhor—too late!

Thus having spoken, calmly for awhile In her pale loveliness she lay, with looks Dreamily self-involved,—as if her mind Were vaguely roaming in the fatal Past: When suddenly her beauteous countenance grew Bedimmed and dreary, like a moon eclipsed; Then darkened more, and writhed with horrid pangs; Till in her burning agony she tosses about About convulsed, and from her foaming lips Words so weird a meaning smite the ear Of Rabbi Joseph, that he bowed his head And cast his eyes in terror on the ground!

And thus a space, bowed rigidly, he stood As one transfixed—though pitying, and with prayer Strengthening his soul. And when again his looks He lifted to the Woman—lo, that face Which erewhile showed so ghastly-wild and weird, Was cloudless as a summer heaven! The late Dark brow was bright; the late pale cheek suffused As with inevitable roseate bloom; And wondrous more than all, her eyes that seemed Before so sunken and so weary, now Were big with splendor, and around her shot 211 Electric influence! while her lips that erst Were thin & faded as autumnal leaves, Now full and crimson, curled with stormy pride, Or quivered with a fierce voluptous power.

Well might the Rabbi stand in disbelief Of all that now he saw. Could thus be she Beside whom, dying, he so late had knelt? This bold and beauteous being that now looked So lovely-fierce—yea, terrible, in all The bright audacity of passionate power? But seeing his amazement, thus she spake.

“These are the common changes of the full And fatal ailment, that with torments strange And varied, as thou seest, is tearing up The dark foundations of my mortal state, And sinking all its structures, hour by hour, Into the dust of Death. But nothing now Is left me, but to meet the my coming doom As best I may, and suffer.”

Hearing which, The Rabbi in his wisdom knew that she 212 Was troubled thus by the indwelling power Of some strong Spirit of Evil, and on this Sent straightway for an unction, wherewith erst The famous ansestor whose name he bore,— Herod the Great’s chief hakim,—had expelled The Demon haunter of the dying king. ‘Twas brought him, and therewith did he at once Annoint her forehead, and her eyes, and touch The tips of all her fingers. Then he made Within a consecrated crucible, A fire of citron-wood and cinnamon; And splashed the flames with insense,—every rite Inspiriting with customary prayers. Then as the smoke arose, he bowed her head Into its coils, that so she might imbibe The salutary odour, till the Fiend That dwelt within her should be thence expelled.

Again a change came over her. Her face Once more grew pale with pain; once more she writhed In burning torment, uttering many words Of most unhallowed meaning! Yet her eyes Were fixed the while, and motionless her lips! Whereby the Rabbi knew certainly 213 ‘Twas not the Woman of herself that spake— But the dread Spirit that ruled over her: And thus it cried aloud:

Part II

“Why am I here, in this my last resort, Purturbed with incense and annointings? Why Compelled to listen to the sound of prayers Which smite me through as with the fire of God, Reddened with wrath? What of this Chamber be So full of us—for Legion is our name— Yet look around! Is it not also full Of our immitigable punishers? Full of avenging Angels, holding each A scourge of thunder in his potent hand, Ready to lighten forth! and who, then armed, For ever chase and wound us as we fly! Nor end with this—but in each wound they make Pour poison sweltered from that tree Asgard, Whose mortal shadow in its blackness falls Over the lake of everlasting doom! Why thus constrained! What have I to confess? —Five hundred years ago, I who thus speak, Was an Egyptian, living at the Court Of Ptolemy Philadelphus. To the height 214 Of mounatainous power, though roughened with unrest, And girt with dangers as with thunder-clouds, Had I by any means, resolved to climb, And I succeeded. In m I crowned with its impunity my lust For Beauty,—sowing, broadcast as it were, Such sensual baits around me as might win The fairest daughters of the land—and lo, Their lustrous eyes surcharged with passionate light The chambers of my harem! But at length, Jaded with lust,—I longed for riches, such As never any one man’s hoard again, before or since, Might equal; and to gain them, steeped my life In every public, every private wrong, [Frauds, lies, theft and secret murders.] But at last A favored minion I had trusted most, And highest raised, betrayed me to the king. Yet dearly did he rue it; nor till now Guessed I how deadly grateful is revenge! I stole into his chamber as he slept, And with a sword whose double for hours I had ground and ground, to fit it for the deed, I staked him to his pallet. Morning came And showed the murder: meanwhile I had fled. 215a But first I sent, as oft I had done before, A present to the mistress of the man By whom he had been prompted to impeach me, Of rich-spiced wine and scented cakes. On these Both feasted, and both fell together down Rolling and raging in their dying pangs, Like poisoned desert dogs!
