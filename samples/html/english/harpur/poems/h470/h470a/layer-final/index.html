No. 5.

BYRON.

A daring Intellect and fervent Soul, Whate’er he wrote made mighty as a Whole. Yet Admiration’s self dare not exempt Too much in Byron from entire contempt! That sneering, ’mid familiars, at each Feeling Wherewith to the World he seemed all wildly reeling Making us feel, as with an Actor’s heart And not a Poet’s, he but played a part! That blending of the Pure with the Impure So vilely in his Woman portraiture! That scorn-masked Envy of the luckier Great! That littleness which hurled a Rival’s hate At Thought’s own oracles in Wordsworth’s strain! That nursing cherishment of foul disdain For what in Others was but weak, or vain, Or simple; flowing from their caste, or creed, Or social mode;—with much besides, indeed, That, howe’er masterly on the World’s mere plan, Was lamentably ignorant of Man!

But let me leave to the censorious ease Of the grave Moralists (my betters) these Defections—(such they are, even to the shame And detriment of all Poetic Fame!) And only, as becomes me, tilt the while At some unsightly peccancies of Style.

Here must admit the most admiring one That there is much to censure and to shun. The oft-paraded Mannerism quaint; The big No-meaning and theatric Feint: The pinning word to word a Line to make; The affected Snip-snap; the mysterious break So doated over by each rhyme sick ass, As—”Well—I might have been—but—let it pass!” Even when he’s most felicitous and terse, Too oft recurs the bald and make-weight Verse: Great Homer’s Style hath moles, ’tis said, but these Are fistulous, and indicate Disease.

Yet, I repeat, his Fervency of soul Whate’er he wrote made mighty as a Whole. Not more resistless in its downward will, The Rhone pours thundering from its native hill, Than from his solitary spirit burst Its gathered load, by stormy passions nurst, To broaden on eternally in Song, So wildly naked—so supremely strong!

CHARLES HARPUR.
