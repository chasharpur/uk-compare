X.

Byron.

A swift Imagination, wildly free, Was Byron’s Muse;—yet wanting subtlety Of taste and touch, and innocent delight, To astonish was the end of all her might. And hence his verse, so vividly it glows, 331 Lacks richness, and the beauty of repose. The beamy strength of polished steel behold, But where’s the quiet pleasantness of gold?

Darkly magnanimous of soul, but proud And desperate of heart, he spake aloud Those terrible mistrusts that from within Blurr’d life with a fatality of sin And mental wretchedness, as if the light Of Truth itself were swarthy with despite! And thus insure of moral faith, the strength Of his whole nature fell in twain at length, Opposing so, as in a balance nice, The love of virtue and the lust of vice, And oft in thought confounding them—until, From a strange insincerity of will, His better tendencies, subjected, waned Even as his worse a growing mastery gained.

And though a daring fervency of soul Made what he wrote most noble as a whole, Even Admiration’s self dare not exempt Too much in Byron from entire contempt! That sneering ’mid familiars at each feeling 332 Wherewith to the world he seemed all wildly reeling; Making us doubt that with an Actor’s art, And not a Poet’s, he but played a part: That blending of the pure with the impure So vilely in his Woman portraiture: That unheroic railing at his fate, And scorn-masked envy of the luckier great: That littleness which hurled a rival’s rage A Thought’s own oracles in Wordsworth’s page: That nursing cherishment of foul disdain For what in others was but weak or vain, Not wicked; flowing from their caste or creed;— With much besides that was, in very deed, As mischievously ignorant of Man As it was knowing on the world’s mere plan.

His Conrads, Laras, Alps, and pallid Giaours— What is there like them in this world of ours? For the dire ravin of the tiger kind In hunger there’s sufficient cause assigned; But if, for gainless rapine, men could be Like Byron’s—verily, a gallows tree Might symbolise Humanity for evil In the most classic short-hand of the Devil.

333

Now let such airs as prompt the opera dance In sensual Italy or shameless France, Announce his Women! all with large long eyes Darkly resplendent, like the midnight skies; All ripe, and ready too, for Love to dish them, And warmly wishful even as Lust could wish them! Yes, maugre the poetic draperies So prodigally mantled about these His Parasinas, Dudus, and Hadees, How naked in their passions are they all, How full of earth, how merely animal!

But wronged in Julia most hath Woman been: Portrayed at first as lovely in her mien As the new-risen Moon; piously pure, Fervently gentle, thoughtfully demure: And all for what?—to show how soon she can Put on the shameless scolding harridan! If this be true to Woman’s nature—then, Not Truth, but Folly, guided Shakespeare’s pen! But ’tis not true to Womanhood, no more Than to her better self the abandoned whore! No; ’tis true only to the strumpet Muse That weds a Rouè and frequents the stews!

334

But let me leave to the censorious ease Of well-paid moralists (my betters) these Defections—such they are, even to the blame And detriment of all poetic fame! And only, as becomes me, tilt the while At some unsightly peccancies of style.

And here must he who most admires admit There’s much unclassic, and yet more unfit. A style so tending into verbiage, Where dashes domino full half the page, Though to the vulgar it look bold and free Can not be classical whate’er it be: Can not be even good, so much it wars With closeness, and all flowing finish mars.

Then who that hath a bravely simple care For what is straightly beautiful, can bear The mannerism that might vex a saint, ‘Tis worn so threadbare?—the theatric feint? That linear dashery which pins word on word As cooks spit pigeons, skewering bird to bird? Those mere lake-level surfaces of sound Where all the music in the rhyme is found? 335 Those pauseless fluencies (blank-verse mistakes)? With those insufferably affected breaks So imitated by each rhyme-sick ass— As—”well—I might have been—but—let it pass”? Even when he’s most felicitous and terse, We catch too frequently the make-weight verse: Great Homer’s style hath moles, ’tis said; but these Defects are tetters, showing mere disease.

But with what mastery his satire pours A caustic that yet burns into the sores Of Folly and Pretense. Now like a snake Sweltering with venom in some Indian brake, It hurls its vengeance (fated not to miss) And strikes its victim with a deadly hiss! Yet it lacks polish, and that edge humane Which lops away, not tears a limb in twain. Hence ’tis unlike the sword that Pope could draw, But serratedly trenchant, like a saw. Nor is it such as gives a ponderous hurt— For that it is too spiteful or too pert; And thence unlike the herculean club that once John Dryden wielded against knave and dunce.

336

Yet, I repeat, his fervency of soul Made what he wrote right noble as a whole. Not more resistless in its downward will The Rhone pours thundering from its native hill, Than from his solitary spirit burst Its gathered load, by stormy passions nurst, To broaden on eternally in song, So wildly naked,—arrowy, and strong.

Note.

I have sometimes, since this criticism was written, repented somewhat of its severity—especially when thinking of the desolate death-bed at Missilonghi, of one who, with all his failings, was yet so worthy to have yielded up his mighty ghost in the land of his nativity, and in the midst of his kindred. Still, as I believe its judgments are upon the whole just, they must remain as they are written. Of the vulgar offender, from whose character, when he is dead, nothing profittable to the wisdom of the present or the future may be extracted, it were needlessly cruel perhaps to judge rigidly—perhaps to judge at all. Even a Watt or a Fulton exists to posterity only through an engine—of prime practical 337 use no doubt, but without personal prestige; and the manner of life of the foregone man himself, may be held therefore to be fairly out of court. But with great authors it is different. They are the “living dead”, and their demerits as well as merits should be rigidly investigated. They are the model influences of the race, and their recorded thoughts the indelible impresses of the great spirit of humanity, for good or for evil, upon the rude mass of our moral existence; and it is a duty therefore which we owe to the sacred cause of spiritual progress, that in our final judgments of them, we should “extenuate nothing”, being careful, however, in a commensurate degree, to “set down nought in malice.”

Carlyle has said somewhere, that Byron conceived his striving after the Infinite and Eternal, was only like mounting to the housetop to reach the stars: and there is much characteristic truth couched in the figure. It stikes, indeed, as with psychical lightning, in the damning inconsistency of his nature:—a mighty consciousness of the riches and grandeur of his spiritual attributes, for ever perversely backsliding into a dissatisfaction at the littleness and poverty of their material conditions. He could not abide God’s appointed time, in faith and hope. On the strong wings of his soul he could ascend as it were into the purlieus of 338 of heaven; but because he could not also drag his perishing body into its immortalising light, he allowed himself to regard the disparity between this soul and this body as a fatal mockery and a wrong—a sort of twofold subterfuge in the destiny of man. Nor in this habit did he only occasionally indulge; he frowardly cherished it into a mental disease: so that at length all sincerity of spiritual reliance became broken up in him, and he sat down under the lessening shaddow of the Material, to lament its hourly loss, with the abandon- -ment of a miser weeping for his gold—nay, with the weakness of a faded woman, repining at the mortality of her beauty.

I have censured Byron in the text for that monstrous inter- change of purity and impurity which is so patent in his female characters, and I have done so upon the principle, that however such a mixture may sometimes obtain in actual life, the poet is nevertheless a recreant in his art, who carries this bodily, as he has done, into the Ideal. An unsightly wart upon the face of the original is not necessary to the truthfulness of a portrait—the spirit of the countenance being the grand essential; and through this, though the blemish be omitted, we are, and should be, best satisfied. 339 So in poetry, a picture of life should be a cleansing of the actual subject. In this lies its morality. The picture is a purification of the reality, in order that the world may grow purer by the picture.

Besides all this, the poetry of Byron is saturated almost throughout with an eternally morbid self-villification—a sort of introverted mysanthropy. It is also pervaded in about the same degree with a pining hopelessness of humanity. To so great an extent are these injurious qualities prevalent in it, that they will inevitably nauseate, in the end, a thoroughly healthy and enjoying mind, and as surely detach, if they do not disgust, a sanely progressive one.

Nevertheless, Byron, though not nearly so popular as he once was, is still very popular. Even the anti-poetical profess to understand his writings, and to like some of them. But these are not the best signs of his poetic worth,—nay, one of them is rather a bad sign of the tone and temper of his teachings. With his widest class of readers he is thus popular, because his appeals are mostly to the propensities, and through these to the Lower sentiments; and he is partially liked by by the cold-blooded and misbelieving, because of the shrewd worldly cast of many of his opinions, and the cynical drift of much of his speculation.
