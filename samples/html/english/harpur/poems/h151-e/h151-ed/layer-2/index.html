The Book.

Away fond Book! thou talkst of mirth and joy, And love and good;—what mokery to me! O it is irksome to the child of want To hear a full blown scribe—one sitting soft On easy chair, with cate in hand, and wine Mantling before him in its crystal font, And raying inward, like a wealth of gems Smelted and run all into one red mass Of introverting flames;—to hear him, thus Complacently mellifluous, numbering o’er 251 In his nice-paring and most formal rhymes, As on his fingers, the so many good, And lovely, pleasant, and adorning things To which Man’s life is hier! What mockery To him who finds with Misery his account! What peace, what joy have I? What recompence For all the felt capacity of a young And social heart, ever intent to plan Schemes of unutterable kindness, yet Doomed only to inhabit solitude And languish thus alone!

The stormy petrel A thousand leagues from land amid the sprays Of wild seas that no voyager hath yet Scarr’d with his keel, is less alone than I Am in the crowded mart, or sabbath walk At even,—nay, in the familiar paths Of daily life! The bann’d and vagabond fox,— Even he hath portions that in contrast scoff At my life’s poverty! Yon eagle, though He saileth now along, the only speck On the wide round of heaven, hath yet a mate Poised on some distant rock to watch his coming And welcome the wild wanderer back:—but who Regards my passages about the world? And if I reap a feeling of delight From the rich influence of arising, or Of setting suns; or from the melody Of morning birds, or from the vernal sweets Of evening flowers, and smile,—who smiles with me? I turn, and sit me down to weep Over my broken fortunes, and the dimm’d And vanishing rainbows of my earlier hopes, Whose loveliness Time heeded not,—there’s none 252 Will mix a tear with mine!—Away, fond Book! Thou prat’st of mirth and joy, and peace and love,— What mockery! Go.

Here rather be it mine With objects to converse that hold through ruin A kind of fellowship, reflecting back Through many a mystic process of the Past My fate’s drear tendency. ‘Twould seem indeed That some such relevance of association, With fixed pre-influence, in secret, had Hither attached my wandering feet; for fast By yon old willows that incline their growth Over the stream’s abrupt and mossy bank, And,—their redundant tresses, downward all, Massing impervious,—solid shadows cast Upon its waters where with darkling gleams They deepen into stillness, even as shades Of horror gloom the sullen soul of Guilt— And near to where is heard the mournful note Of some lone marish-fowl, and low, at times, A solitary splash as with its weight The seeded flags sway downward—even there That scared Rosetree stands which, once, in mood Industrious, and elate with boyish will, I planted: and long cherished—till, at last, It withered like my hopes. And O, ’tis strange, And yet most true, that I did come to have A vague but haunting notion that as it Did flourish in the future, so should they!

How freshly yet in Memory’s glass I see Reflected, even through the stain of tears, Each richly happy circumstance that rose And as slant sun-glows, in succession, flush A cloudlet in the west, went brightening through My novel Being on that summer eve— 253 One of those placid eves whose cool delight Doth seem distilled from heaven’s serenest depths, As though the peace of the eternal world Were passing into ours, and stilling down Its turmoil to unwonted adoration! I see myself, a child, as then I bent Busily mute, and with important hand Mounding the crumbled mould of richest sort About my leafy client. All the while, Weighed on their burnished pinions overhead, The passing birds, observant, trilled abroad Their happiest notes, and round about, as charmed By their smooth-warbled eloquence, the flowers, Sweet prodigals! thick fumed their balmiest smells.

And when at length, my pleasant labor done, Clapping my hands for perfectness of joy I bounded up exultant,—in the Day’s Last gleamy glances, the proportions huge Of all this mighty prospect swam at once, Enormous, on the wide dilating eyes Which spake that moment’s rapture!—yon rude hills Uppiled against the south like rugged banks Of earth-based cloud, then listed all along Their ridge-lines with a lambent fringe of fire; Yon slopes o’erbuilt with villages, and dark Beneath the plough, or bright with cereal gold; With these the stream that, lapsing westward, flashed Through yon high woods with massive turrets topt, And spires, grey rising into the calm air, Or flaming up against the reddening clouds That isle-like hung beyond the western edge Of yon bare down, powdered with flocks, as now, And drawing, with a semicircling sweep, Its dun and rolling outline, like a sea, 254 Against the dusky north;—all these at once Swam in their various aspects into one Vast picture, on the wide dilated eyes Which spoke that moment’s rapture! and I cried Be thou, fair Tree, of this my favorite haunt The future elegance! And with my growth It grew, luxuriant,—but to fade thus soon. Ah, blasted witness of departed joys, No more shalt thou, what time the cuckoo’s heard, Breathe an imparadising pèrfume round, And with thy ruddy family compose A manifold gem conspicuous in the fresh And lovely crown of Spring! nor he who then Fostered thy beauty, be so glad again: For Time who brought the blight to thee, hath cast A canker on his being, scaring all The blooms of fancy and each smile of heart.

Thus, in the morning of my life, I have No happiness rooted in the earth, to hold My spirit to the actual. All my hopes Are blown away by adverse worldly winds, Blown sheer away,—out of the world, to seek Such solace as may be derived from far And lonely flights of Faith. Yet even these Only divert, not satisfy, my soul. Still when her wings refuse them, wearied out By so wild will’d an aeronaut as I, Having no nearer comfort, even as now, Their foregone influence do I meditate, Tracing them upward in their heavenward track Through the similitude of many things.

As through an ocean of uprolling mist Amid the morning Alps, a morning bird 255 Keeps soaring, trustful of the risen sun, Who then is turning all the mountain tops To diamond islands washed by waves of gold That shatter as they surge,—keeps soaring, till Its shoots at length into the cloudless light, And gleams a bird of fire: So Faith upmounts Through the earth’s misty tribulations—up Into the clear of the eternal world; Unfainting, fervent—till with happy wings Dispreading full amid the rays of God, It glories, gleaming like the Alpine bird. But wearying in her flight, even Faith returns, As does the bird,—returns into the mist That shutteth down all less adventurous life: But stronger for the mighty vision left, And livelier for the warmth upon her wings.
