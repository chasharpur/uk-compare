II.

Blindness to Merit.

Dogs and cats will after kind, And so will donkey after donkey; And vain it were to seek the mind That measures Merit in a monkey.

Note.—There are two main causes, amongst many minor ones, of that blindness to merit which is so obser- vable in the world. The first is the want of a corres- ponding intelligence to that upon which it is based, or out of which it arises, as a ground and measure of judgment. The other is even worse, being to the social, what ungodliness is to the religious life; for it is the contemporaneous absence or negation of merit, and consequently of all sympathy with it.

I once heard a misanthropical individual hold forth on

this matter, somewhat as follows: There is, Sir, a Donkeydom in every community, with a large popu- lation; so that whenever three mortals are gathered together, it is more than likely that two of them are of that guild—perhaps the whole three. And the worst of it is, that these donkey-men can be brought to value nothing that is not current in Donkeydom. They have brotherly hearts and eyes only for brother donkeys, with just the average endowments and perceptions of their most ancient but stolid order. And as they have thus no natural measure for the claims of any one, in the man-part of their own image and likeness, who happens to be higher than a donkey, so they have no spiritual capacity for believing in anything that does not either pertain to, or directly apply to Donkeydom. Now there is much truth in this queer apologue, though

too contemptuously put. For how can men who have drawn every breath of their life in the air of the most petty and selfish interests, be lifted into fellowship with one who, looking from the towers of Science, or from the heights of Philosophy, is wont to regard all such interests as but light obscuring fogs? Or what ladder of experience can such men have, whereby to ascend into the nature, and so estimate the worth of any one who is inspired by a noble ambition? Above all, what insight of sympathy can they have, whereby to appreciate truly the godlike endurance of some pure soul, lonely and unsuccored, and yet forever struggling onward towards the Mecca of its faith? amids. And not only is it old, but useless, if it be not suggestive of somewhat tending to a remedy. It should, however, both suggest and provoke somewhat tending to that end. It should stir and incite all men who are conscious of belonging to the higher orders of merit—whether scholars, or statesmen, or poets, or artists—to be more ready than they now are, not only to acknowledge, but to aid and encourage to the utmost every related spirit. They at least can understand one another, in virtue of being peers, and must either accept the duties of mutual aid and comfort—or dodge them[.] From this dilemma there is no escape for them, save in a decline from their order of merit. But is this exacting too much? Then let them be only as ready to befriend one another, as brother Moneygrub is to further the worldly interests of brother Catchpenny—and that itself will be a great improvement upon the past.
