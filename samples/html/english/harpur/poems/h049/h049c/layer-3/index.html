Blindness to Merit.

Blindness to Merit! ’tis an ugly fact That meets us in the dull world’s every act! While of its causes, two in chief, like drones, Fly large and lumpish ‘mongst the minor ones: The first is want of judgment, as a ground And measure of the rule whereby tis found, In that attainment, of whatever kind, Wherein is founded its appeal to mind; The other’s worse, and works for social scath As in religion does a want of faith, 231 Namely, a poverty of worth, and thence Of sympathy with it, as a consequence.

There once was one—a shrewd sarcastic rogue, Who used to sneer forth this queer apologue. —There is in every shire of every nation A Donkeydom, with a large population, So that where five are met, full three are from (Or four it may be) the same Donkeydom! Prone creatures! having hearts, eyes, ears for none In the man-portion of their likeness done, Who may outmeasure some renowned long Tom, The standard hero of their Donkeydom: For they are clannish, and delight to call Their fellows fondly—’brother donkeys all!’ Yea, they are clannish, and, like divels, hate Whatever is undonkeyishly great, Or even small, if haply it appears In one unsanctioned by due length of ears. With them all noble sentiment’s a flam; All unfeed effort an insulting sham; Nor could an angel make them understand How bravely genius doth enrich a land. In fine, they scout all influence that tends 232 To universal, and not local ends, And aid in nought that may not issue from The one great project born in Donkeydom, Whose moral end is to prevent or ban All loftiness of character in Man; Even their giant (the aforesaid Tom) Being but a pigmy out of Donkeydom!

ow there is truth here, even though it be Evinced, perhaps, somewhat too scornfully. For how can men whose every breath of life Is drawn in the hot air and ’mid the strife Of pettiest interests, have a kindred heart With him who hath built heavenward and apart The structures of his mind, and looking thence Over this world-thronged universe immense, Is wont all such embroilments to deplore As light-obstructing vapors—nothing more? What ladder of experience can they build, To mount with,—up, into a nature filled With beauty, or by mighty truths inspired; Or one even with a bold ambition fired; So to appraise it rightly, and disclose The reason of its godlike overflows? 233 But least of all in such men can there be Devotions chiming into sympathy With some pure soul, unsuccored and alone, Struggling in weariness unwearied on— Unwearied, day and night, and night and day, Tow’rds the far Mecca of its faith alway!

ut ’tis an old complaint, whose age forbids The grey antiquity of the pyramids To reach so far back in the waste of Time! And also useless, though thus edged with rhyme, If tow’rds some remedy it do not tend, Nor touch us like a warning from a friend. But this it should: yea, (flowing from the vast Up heaping miseries of the faithless Past, Into one world wide spirit-force at last!) With trumpet tone, it should at once demand Of Nature’s sovereign men in every land, Whate’er the Province of their Merit be— Arms, Science, Morals, Art, or Poetry,— That they do claim and aid, wherever known, All spirits truly kindred to their own; Nor can they shirk this duty, if they would Decline not from all noble Brotherhood.

Well, let them then Look forth amongst the common herd of men, And gather thence their rule: Yea, let them be As prompt and true in their fraternity, As honest brother Bullion is to rub The world’s path smooth for brother Moneygrub; And this—ay, even this, will be a vast Improvement on the tenor of the past.
