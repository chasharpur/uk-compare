War.

I.

Its General Effects.

He who in battle slays his fellow man, Without first having duly weighed the cause Of quarrel, and derived the approval thence Of his own heart, as to the part therein By him to be sustained,—he is, no doubt, Strictly a murderer! And though this stern truth Should sound distastefully [to many—those Especially who trade in national madness And brutal glory; magnifying so A rag inscribed with some stale jugglery Heraldic, far more than they honor God! 125 Yet what then? Is not this great world grown grey In woe if not in weal? and surely now ‘Tis time the voice of Truth, in all things, should Be lifted clearly, and sent ringing forth Even like the startling spirit of a trumpet! For herein had her simple test been taught From the beginning, what lagoons of blood Had so been spared mankind! what desolations, What infamies, prevented!

In the roll Of History,—who that hath a mind to think, And heart to feel, can read her constant tale Of royal foolery to unholy wars Still leading—wholesale human butchery! 126 Nor curse the moral blindness—or, at best, The horn-eyed self-abasement that hath given The Nations hitherto to bow them down And deify their Scourges! Till, alas! Evil became their good, and each a school Merely of rival Inhumanities Called heroes, and great captains of their age, And “heaven-born ministers”, proud hirelings, who Besought no heaven, perhaps acknowledged none, Save courtly power.

Oh, monstrous! and yet such Bad seminaries only have the Nations Been hitherto,—yea, through all time, and down Even to the days wherein we breathe; and such The scholars of their glory, king-made lords, 127 And fame-taught conquerors; schooled to that extreme, Wherein the identities of Right and Wrong Are elbowed from the conscience by fast crowds Of kindless schemes, established on the fact, That national mischief is the epic road To national distinction.

Hence they grow Disnatured, and prepared to wade, with War And Conquest, shouting their blood-drunken rage, Into such depths of murder, that the lets Of natural piety, with the grosser shapes Of horror and remorse, that set some bound To vulgar violence, are swallowed up In the dark scope of their perverted lives, By one all-blanking flood of crime at last: 128 Even as the landmarks of the earth were lost In the blind growth of the prevailing deluge! And hence it is, their terrible careers Completed, that such blood-damned monsters die In a benumbed incertitude of heart That looks like peace. The military fiend, Napoleon, hence,—a wave-bound prisoner, Perishing peicemeal on a desolate rock,— Escaped the sense of blood, and witnessed Death Come shadowing onward calmly as a saint.

II.

Its particular Effects.

It is a Battle-field! From sights severe, Many and thick, of human suffering here, 129 Select we one.—Behold a soldier bow Over his brother, lying slain below. Wringing his hands in agony, he cries Ah me, my brother, wilt thou ne’er arise From this so cold and blood-polluted bed, Unmeet for thee—unmeet even for the dead? Hark! a triumphant roar, like thunder, splits Heaven’s concave, and proclaims that victory sits Upon the banners under which they fought!— He heeds it not! Compulsion hither brought Him and that murdered one—his only brother; The masculine image of a buried mother— The last thing that he loved beneath the sky; What most he cared to live for,—or to die.

will extend this Note with a prose piece which was suggested by an idea I had on the subject of War: namely, that a highly benevolent 135 man could not slay a fellow creature even in battle, without being occasionally troubled afterwards with the memory of his deed—especially whenever his remembrance of it might be strongly excited by real or fancied resemblances between the Living and the Dead. And to embody this idea as strikingly as possible—to stamp it with as much Crusoe-like verisimilitude as I could, I gave to it the medium of a direct personal anecdote.

M

ajor H., of the — Dragoons, had the tallest forehead I have ever seen, with Benevolence coning up at the top of it, like the larger end of an egg; and the following ancedote is strikingly confirmative of the predications of Phrenology as to the probable manifestations of so excessive a developement of that organ. At the time of which I write,—certainly,—the poor Major was in a state of bodily and mental morbidity, he having just partially recovered from a severe fit of sickness;— 136 still the predominant action of the faculty was in conclusive accordance with the Phrenological theory. He was taking a quiet evening stroll with myself upon the parade, and feeling at length that he had rather overtasked his strength, was about leaving me, to return to his quarters, when a young trooper of another Regiment came towards us from the Brigade Office, having a message for myself. As he approached and saluted us, it struck me that the motions and manner of the Major were unaccountably singular. Advancing rapidly two or three paces, he stared earnestly and even wildly at the face and form of the trooper, and then turning away with a suppressed yet perfectly audible groan, abruptly bad me good evening, and departed for his quarters; ere I could make any enquiry as to the cause of his apparent pain and distress, though much and anxiously concerned on his account. But to be brief: so soon as I had dispatched the Regimental business of which 137 the young trooper had been sent to remind me, I hurried to my friend, the Major’s, to enquire after his condition. I found him lying at full length upon a sofa, with a settled and almost despairing expression of pain and sorrow dispread, like a cloud, over his naturally amiable and paternal countenance. He nodded to me as I entered, and held out his hand in silence; taking which, I enquired with infinite solicitude,—for I loved the man,—whether there was any serious occasion for apprehending a relapse of his complaint?—what was the nature of the attack which had affected him so suddenly, an hour previously, on the Parade?— and so forth. “No—no”, said he, “that was not it. I will tell you the cause.” Having thus expressed himself, he again became silent for nearly a minute, during which he had that peculiar fixedness of eye which the world-wearied invalid is so wont to have when painfully thinking back through a long lapse of time; then turning on his side, he looked up somewhat studiously 138 into my face, and proceeded in his confidential intention, as I then thought, rather incoherently. “War is a bad trade”, said he, shaking his tall grey head in the dusky twilight that fell through the window immediately above the sofa, and while he did so, the solemn purpose of his thoughts deepened the centre of his pale high forehead into palpable shadow,—”It is a bad trade at the best. Were I the father of twenty sons, not one of them, if by any means I could prevent it, should ever traffic in war.” I was about to reply, by some general remark in defence of legitimate warfare, when he suddenly sat up, and continued: “The young trooper I saw this evening upon the Parade, was a living counterpart both in face and figure of a young Frenchman whom, twenty years ago, I sabred at Salamanca, and the mere sight of him made me feel again—yes, again, like a murderer—like another Cain!” “Well but,” said I, “I am sure Major, from all 139 I know or could believe of your nature and habits, that you slew the Frenchman fairly.” “Oh yes, I slew him fairly enough, according to the sanctions of the War”, replied the invalid, with a dreary touch of irony in the tone of his voice; “that is, I did not deal him an unnessary blow—not a blow I mean that could be spared from the defence of my own life: and yet (the emphasis of the yet, as he expressed it, cannot be written)—and yet, when he fell headlong from his horse, and lay dying and dead upon the ground beneath me, I felt, I tell you, like a murderer: I felt that I had drunk, and that deeply, of the blood-reaming cup of Cain! I was the only sur= -viving son of my mother, and it struck me, that I once had a brother resembling the youth I had slain. There was a cry of blood coming up against me! And had I been at that moment the possessor of a universe, I could have given it as freely as ever I gave a penny to a beggar, to have been able 140 to have said effectually to that slaughtered son of man, Arise! and be as you were but now, before this our bloody meeting in the horrible traffic of war! And yesterday again,—twenty years after, at the mere sight of a youth who resembled him, I felt again, I say, like a murderer—like another Cain!”
