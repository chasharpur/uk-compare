ORIGINAL POETRY.

We have received a very angry and in- sulting letter from Mr Charles Harpur, as to some alterations we made in his poetical lines on Robert Emmitt, in the Chronicle of the 11th instant, and which piece, in the shape we gave it, he says, “I positively disclaim.”

We admit the fact—we plead guilty to the crime alleged of alteration—we even changed the spelling of poor Emmet‘s name to the Irish orthography. But it was at most an error in judgment. We did it, not from a wish to injure the Parnassian fame of Mr. H., but the contrary; most assuredly not from any poetic pre- tensions of our own, to which we have no claim. Mr. H. declares he would “as soon be visited with a slash of a stock whip, as that we should have bestowed any, even the smallest of these same im- provements upon his poem.”

Having had no previous experience of the wrath of angry poets—the genus irri- tabile vatum—we will naïvely own, that having read Milton, whose flights of fancy are boundless—Pope—Dryden—Shake- speare—Goldsmith—Byron, and others, we do not remember, though they have talked of “fiery souls”—”dark spirits”— “tame souls”—”perturbed spirits,” &c. &c., that they ever gave a hue to the human spirit—an incorporeal essence which human eye has not seen, nor human mind conceived to have a colour;—we do not know what hue Mr. Harpur attributes to it. We had also the “cold chain of silence.” We have read of the “mantle of oblivion,” but the “chain of silence thrown round a name,” was too fine for us, as was the enwrapping “big feelings“ with a “word“—and we believe “minions of death“ is an entirely new idea in po- etry. These and other passages, may be, for aught we know, not only highly poetical, but sublime; however the obtuse- ness of our intellect, could not ascend to such lofty heights, and wishing to oblige the writer, and at the same time exclude from the Chronicle what in our humble judgment seemed harsh and strained meta- phors, at variance with the laws of criticism, and injurious to some pretty lines in the piece.—We own, we say, to having com- mitted the mortal sin of striking out the”hue of his spirit“ stanza altogether, and altering a few phrases. This is the head and front of our offending. But we had not the most distant idea of evil to the writer, or that we were impeding the march of his fame to immortality. Sed diis aliter visum!

Here is the great original itself, which we so profanely dared to touch. Under- neath it is the carcase of the murdered poetry; with regard to the merits of which our opinion does not greatly differ from Mr. Harpur’s—and having a hand in its composition, we hope we may, as applied to it, quote without offence, the well known lines:—

Mediocribus esse poetis, Non Dii, non homines, non concessere columnæ.

ROBERT EMMITT.
