‘Tis late—for it is midnight when the Moon Thus through yon casement shows her dream-pale face. No wonder that they worshipped her of old, So mystic in its beauty is her look. How wild and lonely must his heart have been, Even as mine, who erst in vision brought Her nightly down, Love’s Visitant, to him Whose dreams she impassioned in the mountain cave, The Carian boy, Endymion?

All so still At first the scene, that even Silence there Did tingle audibly, and ake around, Intenser every moment,—yea, until So keen the sense of her still presence grew It cut into my being: when, wondrous change! Through Heaven’s black concave a tremendous storm Hath bellowed in its strength, and I, as though Befated with a wizard’s insight, saw Its guiding Demons from the foremost clouds Unmask, and dart, with bare blue arms, abroad Ten thousand thunders—globes of writhing fire! 62 Till harrowed to daring by the sight, I smiled With a proud grief, to know I bore the brunt Of such wild work—alone! I smiled, and lo From a deep fissure in the cloudy mass That hung aloft its lurid canopy, The blue-eyed Stranger’s semblance, bodying forth, Hath hovered o’er me smiling! Suddenly The fiends were laid, the thunders hushed, and all The clouds erewhile so foul, receding, shone Like pendant lakes of liquid gold, embossed With purple isles; and, to complete the change, Profluent rills mingled their murmurs round, Flowers sprung beneath, and, in as instant growth, The clasping tendrils of embowering vines Rustled above to songs of blithesome birds! Then, at her nearer presence, overcome With perturbations of a joy so rich— So honey-thick, that even its own wings Were clogged with its own sweetness;—thus o’ercome, 63 Methought I could not but—though restless— sink Into Sleep’s drowsy arms—and I have slept.
