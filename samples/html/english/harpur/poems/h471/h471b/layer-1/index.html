XI.

Moore.

Bright, sparkling Moore! when first I heard him sing, How daring seemed his Muse! how sweetly wildering! ‘Gods! what strong ecstasy my soul did seize When first I read his matchless Melodies! But thus luxuriant and bold and wild, His genius yet is the peculiar child Of Fancy’, as with Imagination she Divides the world of Ideality.

Much hath he sung of Love (being such a pet Amongst the Graces of high life)—and yet His Lays, or gay or sorrowful of mood, Do Love’s divinity but little good. What though his amourously-happy line Be honied with a melody divine? Where is the heart-warm music—(am I wrong?) The flower-like nativeness of Burns’s song? What though the dashing style in which he hits The wooing lyre, enchant our exquisites? With all Love’s zeal, most passionate, most intense, He wants its clear affection, faith immense, 341 And quiet fulness of benevolence. Or should these qualities at times confess A growing being in his earnestness, With meretricious dews they’re straight besprent And chilled to death,—or else, unwilling, sent To lose themselves in search of ornament. So much his Muse hath in her spirit more Of rhetoric, than of true poetic ore; So much more do his sympathies aspire To copy foreign art than trust in native fire.

Even when our admiration’s rapt away On the bright current of some better lay, Just when the yearning soul would have no sense But for sweet Human Love’s own eloquence, An interrupting bird is sure to be Sent warbling by on wings of simile: Or just as we begin to overflow With all the luxury of some artless woe, Then, when the Muse but genuine tears should shed, Behold a shower of metaphors instead! Or for some Humanity’s true sighs, we hear Some exiled Zephyr mourning for Cashmere.

This may seem vastly natural to the creature That’s fashionably learned in human nature; Divinely sentimental unto those Who never sympathise—but with the woes Of Eastern heroines and Corsaire-like beaux; Quite killing to such lady-birds as pule Within the Arcadia of some boarding school: But to the strong of heart and sound of head ‘Tis poetry only as sweet cake is bread.

Yet Moore, if not a monarch Bard, is still A royal prince of bright Apollo’s hill, Whose filial hand Muse hath thrown with affluent hand A robe of Indian beauty o’er his native Land! And ever dear unto the brave shall be His high and spirited praise of Liberty! And to the generous his contempt of slaves, His hate of traitors and his scorn of knaves!

Note.

I shall not wonder if many dissent from this criticism. I was myself, when somewhat of a literary novice, an extravagant admirer of Moore. But I cannot now read 343 him with any continuous relish. He has no repose—nothing of the calm consciousness of natural uncompelled power. His eternal beating about for similitudes would alone do away (as noticed in the text) with any concentration of heart-felt interest upon the themes of his poetry. Besides, even when these extraneous helps are most readily hit upon, there is almost always some marked artificiality in the mere manner of their introduction—an air of the closet even in the verbal demonstratives:

Like that bird (or) like that flower, which—

and so he goes off; thus intimating at the very outset, that the things themselves are either fabulous or distant—to be far sought and flown after;—that the Poet himself is stuck at a desk or doing the refined in a drawning-room, and not taking his fire from the sun-kindled vitality of Nature, in her absolute presence (as he should at least seem to be), with all his birds and flowers rejoicing and blooming about him. His poetic enthusiasm is not inginite—not in him, but carried about with him, tied up as it were in a bag, like laughing gass, and to be inhaled only upon special occasions. He first arrays himself in his singing robes, with great care and method, and then takes a pull at it,—long or short, hearty or otherwise, or according to the size and importance of the intended effort.

Hence it is that we are so apt to be provoked into an un- -seasonable sort of merriment, even when reading some of his most serious and meant-to-be-pathetic passages. We can- -not help thinking what a fussy strain the little fellow must have put upon all his poetic tackle, to ere he had succeeded in lifting himself to the given altitude. In short, everything he handles—no matter what—is made to assume a restless, blazy prominence that wearies while it dazzles; or to exhale a forced odor that is too fashionably compounded to be either refreshing or wholesome; or it is twisted and filigreed into a super-romantic unreality that one cannot away with, and is sure to dislike in the end.

Nor do I now There is nothing which deters us from a second perusal of any work, whether in prose or rhyme, like an extreme floridness of diction. We care not to be refresh the unmittigated glare it has left in our memory, or venture again into the super- -sunny dazzal that has heretofore oppressed us. Like one who has jaded himself by wandering around the garish aisles of a christal palace, we would rather repose our “wearied virtue” amid the quiet of a common green field, or under the familiar shelter of a shadowy hill. Hence who cares to read Lalla Rook a second time? or having done so, who is he that would not gladly take refuge from the effects of such a feat in the comparative quietude of Goldsmith or 345 Cowper, or amid the ungaudy mountain nooks and homely green pastures that impicture the pages of Wordsworth?

Nor do I now think th Moore’s versification so sweet and musical as I did at the time the text was written. With all its glibness, it is very often patchy, and eked out by the necessities of the rhymes. Its general character is gushiness, not profluency, and it is never truly easy but by an effort to appear so. And to disguise this want in it, of a certain sweet natural continuity of flow, he is constantly resorting, as if under the goad of a self-convicting instinct, to rhetorical turns and exclamatory interlardations.

I speak in the opening passage of the text of Moore’s genius as being more to Fancy than to Imagination. Now there is no doubt a broad general distinction between Fancy (so called) and Imagination, however hard it may be to draw it in a few words. And yet so intimate is their relation, however obtaining, that there is often an apparent interchange of juncture between them, and even a displacement of the one by the other. Still, perhaps, what we specifically call Fancy, is but Wit playing with the outlying properties of Imagination; for however mixed their operations may sometimes appear, this mixture obtains only in material and sensible regards —never reaching, in any event, to such con- 346 conceptions as agitate those vaster depths of the soul, which may be properly designated as the regions of pure Ideality.
