No. 2.

MOORE.

Bright sparkling Moore! when first I heard him sing, How daring seemed his Lays! how sweetly wildering! ‘Gods! what strong ecstasy my soul did seize When first I read his matchless Melodies! But thus luxuriant, and bold, and wild, Yet is his Genius the peculiar Child Of Fancy, as with Imagination she Divides the World of Ideality.

Much has he sung of Love (being such a pet Amongst the Graces of High Life), and yet His Lays, or gay or sorrowful of mood, Does Love’s Divinity but little good! What though his amourously happy line Be honeyed with a melody divine? Where is the heart warm music—(am I wrong?) The flower-like Nativeness of Burns[’]s Song? What though the dashing style in which he hits The wooing Lyre enchant our Exquisites? With all Love’s passion-burning zeal intense, He wants the moral faith of it,—and thence, Its calm, clear fulness of Benevolence! Or should these Qualities at times confess A growing Being in his Earnestness, With meretricious dews they’re straight besprent And chilled to death,—or else, unwilling, sent To lose themselves in search of Ornament! Even when our Admiration’s rapt away On the bright current of some better Lay; Just as the yearning Soul would have no sense But for sweet Human Love’s own eloquence, An interrupting Bird is sure to be Sent warbling by on wings of Simile! Or just as we begin to overflow With all the luxury of some artless woe, Then, when the Muse but genuine Tears should shed, Behold a shower of Metaphors instead! Or for Humanity’s true Sighs, we hear Some exiled Zephyr mourning for Cashmere.

This may seem vastly natural to the Creature That’s fashionably learn’d in Human Nature; Divinely sentimental unto those Who never sympathise but with the woes Of Eastern Heroines and Corsair-like Beaux; Quite killing to such Lady-birds as pule Within the Arcadia of some Boarding School; But to the strong of heart and sound of head, ‘Tis Poetry only as sweet cake is bread.

Yet Moore, if not a Monarch Bard, is still A Royal Prince of bright Apollo s Hill; Whose filial Muse hath thrown, with affluent hand, A robe of Indian beauty o’er his Native Land! And ever dear unto the Brave shall be His high and spirited praise of Liberty! And to the Generous his contempt for Slaves, His hate of Traitors, and his scorn of Knaves.

CHARLES HARPUR
