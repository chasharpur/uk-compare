Sonnets Inspired by Love and Beauty.

Note. That portion of the Prefatory Remarks to the “Personal and other Sonnets,” which treats of the rhyme-arrangements of the various forms of the Sonnet, and which claims for the majority of them a peculiar construction in that regard, of my own invention or devisement, was not written as applying to those which follow: which are here given as forming a distinct Series, and as being written for the most part on the pure Italian model.

C.H.

The Confirmer.

How beautifully doth the Morning rise Over the hills, as from her bower a Bride Comes brightened—blushing with the shame-faced pride Of Love, that now consùmmated, supplies All her full heart can wish; and to the eyes Dear are the flowers then, in their green haunts spied, Glist’ning with dew: pleasant at noon the side Of shadowy mountains ridging to the skies: At eve ’tis sweet to hear the breeze advance Through the responding forest dense and tall; And sweeter, in the moonlight, is the dance And natural music of the waterfal: Yet, to confirm and fix the charm of all, Love must be near us with its magic glance.
