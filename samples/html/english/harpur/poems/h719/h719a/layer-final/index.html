DESPAIR.

Terrible is thy grandeur, O Despair! And great, though sad, thy privilege. For he Whose horrible Mate thou art, can dare to laugh At all the fortunate most dread. What thought Need he take of the Earthquake that upheaves The continent beneath him, with a sway Likest the boundless impulse of the main, What time the challenging Spirit of the poles Drives his black herd of bellowing Tempests forth? He hath no more to do with Place. The Storm That makes the hall of Heaven one chaos dire Of dreadful glooms and rending sounds, deals down No bolt that might inflict a harm on him. The wild remorseless Deep, when rushing loud Through all its shaken confines, with a wrath Which isles submerged, and stranded navies, long And terribly memorise, no more alarm Can pour upon his dark and widowed spirit Than might yon babbling brook. When Armies rush In thunder to the fight, the unholy scene Bears not upon its blasted breast, a sight Fearful to him. Or did he for wild sport, Upon his throne defy the Russian Bear— Or him who lords it in that Moslem hold, The city once of Constantine the Great, Yet were he safe from aught of further evil As though he filled his grave. He hath forgot The taste of fear; the faculty hath lost To feel vicissitude;—events to him, Whether by Fortune brightened or begrimmed, Being hueless as the noiseless tide of Time On which they hurry past. Even such art thou, Despair! and such I felt thee at my heart For many a day and night.—But to arise With a new sense of strength, from Visions new Of Love and Truth and Freedom, and through these A promise that Humanity, so much By Error now deformed, shall yet become A Creature as divinely-browed again As the first Father, Adam;—to arise With a fresh-swelling vigour thus derived In solitary thought, and shake thee off! Even as Shakspeare’s lion, in the Bard’s Rampant conception of the act, did shake The dew-drop from his mane—into “thin air.”
