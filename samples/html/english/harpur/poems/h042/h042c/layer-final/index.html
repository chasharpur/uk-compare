(THIRD PAPER.) VIII.

That species of Beauty which results specifically from lines, curves, angles, &c., however variously manifested, is more obvious, and ultimately more simple, perhaps, than any other. It is also, in its relation to the human mind, more dependent upon certain of its preconcep- tions, than any other charactery of the Beautiful. In some cases, indeed, it would seem to be wholly origin- ated by mental c[o]mbinations. Every well-lined dia- gram displays it in some degree. Numbers also possess and exhibit it, dealing, as they do, in the exact parts and constituent values, the unities and progressions, of a symmetrical whole. And when this species of Beauty applies in things of use or utility, it is raised and brought nearer to us. Hence it may be supremely resi- dent in the fashion of a common car, and finely obvious even in a well-ploughed field. Nevertheless, it has a higher law in the constitution of things than any which can be derived from their mere utility. A ship which has been constructed expressly for speed, and another with a view chiefly to its capacity for burden, are both equally useful in their way; but the lines of the one shall instantly strike the eye of taste as being more agreeable, more elegant,—more in accordance with some spiritual ideal that lies deeply though vaguely within us, than those of the other. The pillared porch of a tem- ple exhibits in a high degree this beauty arising out of harmonising contours and fitly-lined proportions,— enhanced, no doubt, by pre-conceptions of service or utility, but more enhanced by a reference, however dimly obtaining, to its prototype in Nature—namely, the foremost boles and leafy domes of the glades and avenues that intimate, and conduct us into, the Druidic sanctuaries of a wood. The following are the only detached verses of my own, which have been drawn out of this particular treasure-chamber of the Beautiful:—

[THE BEAUTIFUL VIII]

There is no curve of sea or sky, No turn of hill-top far defined, Without some fitness for the eye, Some meaning for the mind.

There is no checkered play of light In tree tops by the sunshine wrought, Without its pleasure for our sight— Its beauty for our thought.

No time-hewn arch of rock is found, ‘Neath which some torrent’s waters 9999roll, But hath its import and its bound In measures of the soul.

All straight lines meeting hint the way How like things best to union run; All circles type the Perfect—yea, The all-enclosing One.

All fancied points produced, that flow From isle to isle or star to star,— All carry with them as they go The Beautiful afar.
