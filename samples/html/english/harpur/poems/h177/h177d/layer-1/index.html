Hope On.

Gold’s a cheat, there’s no denying, Love too hath a faithless wing; Still ’tis useless, sighing useless sighing— Rather list to Hope replying: “Flowers will come again with Spring; And in the desert way we’re going, There’s yet one stream of bounty flowing— Hark! I hear it murmuring.”

Fame’s a liar, Beauty fading, Hope itself may plant a sting; Still forbear, forbear upbraiding— Rather list to its persuading: “Flowers will come again with Spring.”

Friendship turns, itself denying,— Yea, itself! the heart to wring; Still, ev’n though all faith be dying, Ever list to Hope replying: “Flowers will come again with Spring; And in the desert way we’re going, There’s yet one stream of bounty flowing— Hark! I hear it murmuring.”

The above ois an attempted lyrical embodiment of the philosophy of Hope, with full admission of its mundane vanity. We may know its deceitfulness but too will; but we should know also, the utter necessity of its beguiling companionship throughout the wayward and up-hill journey of a life so precariously provided for as Man’s. Hence, however it may have broken promise with is in the past, we should still cherish it with regard to the future, in a forgiving spirit: for though it may again befool us, and most likely will do so, after precisely the same fashion, it will yet keep us alive, and even in health, up to the next pitfall in the disastrous highway of fate – and even then take beating as obsequisously as an erring pointer, and be still as ready as ever to search onward after our better fortunes; ay, every whit as imaginatively as before. Or that is, if forgivingly indulged. But it should be progressively corrected. Well; let us disciplin as judiciously as we can; – but never think of perishing or parting company with it. Let continuous misfortunes and disappointment, if it needs must be so, must away from us anything, everything also; but let our hold upon Hope be forever an unbreak- able one. For there is a sort of compassionate faith in its very flattery, or rather in the result of it: seeing that human doings upon the whole, are but a universal heap of accumulating failures, and that only for their being touched, in the prospect, with the delusive enchantment of Hope’s promissory light, we should be at once smitten with “such a misbelief of all in the world” – such a mortal cold consciousness of the utter vanity of every thing earthly, as would for ever see us into the torpor of that “death in life” which is everyway more deplorable than are the dreary and darkness that house within the grave – for there the end, at the worst (morally speaking), is both definitive and natural. Let us hope on then, and live – ay, live to get well: - and to continue so.

(Methinks the actual Genius of Hope, in its own proper spiritual personality, must have split my 466 full stop in that last sentence, into a colon, thereby hinging on to it the concluding clause. Certainly those four final words don’t exactly feel as though they had just slipped through fingers of mine. Certain I am also that I have no such wholesale trust (when sober) in the tender mercies of the Forthcoming, as they would father upon me. I may wish I had, however; and for that reason, I will even let them abide where they are, however they came there.)
