[HOPE ON]

[...] Friendship turns, itself denying— Yea, itself! the heart to wring: Still, even though all faith be dying, Ever list to Hope replying— “Flowers will come again, with Spring And in the desert way we’re going, There’s yet one stream of bounty flowing— Hark! I hear it murmuring.”

The above is an attempted lyrical embodiment of the philosophy of Hope, with full admission of its vanity. We may know its deceitfulness but too well; but we should know also the utter necessity of its beguiling companionship throughout the weary pilgrimage of a life so precariously provided for as man’s. Hence, however it may have disappointed us in the past, we should still cherish it with regard to the future in a forgiving spirit: for however it may again befool us, it will keep us alive, at all events, up to that point —and even then take beating as obsequiously as an erring pointer, and still continue to search for our better fortunes a[s] before—. Let us correct it then as judiciously as we can, but never think of parting company with it. There is a sort of compassionate truth in its very falshood, or rather in the result of it: seeing that human doings are upon the whole but a world-wide heap of accumulating failures, and that but for these being touched when in the prospect with the enchantment of its promissory light, we should be at once smitten

“With such a misbelief of all the world”— with such a mortal cold consciousness of the vanity of every thing earthly as would freeze us into the torpor of that death out of the grave which is every way more deplorable than are the decay and darkness that house within it—for there the end at the worst, is both definitive and natural. We will Hope on then and live.

C.H.
