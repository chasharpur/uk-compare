IV.

The Rose Tree.

[The alimucta is the “vegitable wonder” alluded to in the Poem. It is the most gigantic of all vines; and when it meets with nothing higher than itself to clasp, it is said to assume the form of a sturdy tree, the topmost branches indicating, however, as they contort and tress outward in the air, their natural inclination to climb. A vine- -tree of this kind will sometimes festoon (it is said) the whole face of a vast precipice; it being peculiar to the mountain regions of India.—Vide Asiatic Researches.]

A matchless Rose Tree! From the shaded mould 497 Banked tow’rds the Garden wall, its bole twists up, Massive and knarled in miniature, and whence, As it ascends, a hundred lesser stems Branch off, and so, through many a loop, are led Upward and spreadingly against the stones; And thus, in somewise, ’tis resemblant,—yea, A fairy adumbration as it were, Of that vast vegitable wonder which Is seen alone, in mighty growth, to climb The fronts precipitous of Indian alps. —Then, midway up the wall, no farther stayed, Its wide spread boughs lop outward in a mass, And by their weight of leaves, as still the sprays Keep tapering, are all curved into a dome: A leafy Heaven! from whose vernal skies A thousand glowingly consummate blooms, Pensile and round, look sweetly smiling forth, Even like the faces of so many Cherubs, Flushed with celestial health.

Euroma, 16. Dec: 1865 (
