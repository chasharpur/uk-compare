III.

The following is a less sensuous picture of feminine beauty than that which is given in the preceding section. It is more raised and tempered by sentiment; and while it has less passion, the want of it is well compensated by the purer breathings of a fraternal, though admiring affection. Its passion is of that kind which seeketh no return, and which shields itself under the saying, “If I love thy beauty, what is that to thee?

A LADY BY MOONLIGHT.

A splendid Vision of delight, How she swims upon the night, And in her virgin beauty glows More rosy than the virgin rose!

Her dew-gemm’d feet, on twinkling, seem Two fairies of the lunar beam, For there were almost room for them On lilies of the slenderest stem.

Her happy eyes, befringed with jet, Glow,—each a sapphire fitly set, And in them softly blends with mind A splendour not to be defined, As fountains in the twilight play And radiate with a nameless ray.

Her dark locks, parting from her brow, Redundant down her shoulders flow, Which through them shine, like Alpine snow Between the splitting volumes gleaming Of a dark cataract downward streaming. Or as more bounding in her tread, Those glossy riches round her head Float rippling, as the sprays in motion Crown-curl a billow of the Ocean.

O Earth, with so much beauty clad, Look up to heaven, and be glad! Gaze earthward from your golden cars, And be ye happier, O ye stars! Out shining from thy heavenly noon, Smile yet more, thou smiling Moon!
