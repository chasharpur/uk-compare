Rhymes, Humourous Poems, Epigrams &c.

Dr. Johnson.

A man-lump of Learning in scholarly garments, Who thought all live languages absolute varmints; But his Latin, to polish his English, came pat in, (He was full to the throat, was the Doctor, of Latin) And so he rubbed down, with much erudite pother, The bark of the one with the rust of the other, And saw in the twain, when in tumid verse blended, A classical English poetic style splendid. He had wit, with scant measure of nature or mirth in’t, With a great solemn soul—with a great deal of earth in ‘t. But though I should mouth at him thus till my jaw crack, I love the old fellow in spite of each drawback! And because of his spirit, so nobly unpliant, When the slight of proud greatness had made him defiant; And because of his kindness, so mellowly human, Though rough in its husk, I account him a true man.

N.B. I think I must mean (in that line at the head there) By the epithet “scholarly” garments quite threadbare: Or greasy, or rusty, or fusty, or dusty From contact with bookshelves whose tomes have grown musty, And the drip of the lamp, as their wearer sat poring O’er grey-headed myths while his neighbors were snoring. And indeed, though your college-bred scamp may impassion With his glossy array the fond eyes of dame Fashion, Or your dandy-divine, who cut learning when primed with 396 Just the lore (and no more) that a benefice rhymed with; Was there yet seen a Scholar—a true one— Encased in a garb that did look like a new one?
