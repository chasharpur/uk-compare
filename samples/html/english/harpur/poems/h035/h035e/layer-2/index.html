THE BEAUTIFUL. BY CHARLES HARPUR.

I.

BEAUTY.

First in the human form and aspect fair ‘Tis seen, and in the shapes of animal being, And things inanimate if new and rare; In blending colours next and sounds agreeing, Till Thought grows lightened with that inward seeing Which finds it in the Mind beyond compare, And in the infinite combinations there Of memory or knowledge, fixed or fleeing. The inmost spirit of all truth, it hues The countenance of Science; throbs like thought In every star of heaven, and embues All the moon looks on; to the heart is brought Alive in every sunbeam, if, love fraught, Such bright communion it would not refuse.

But the subtlest considerations of Beauty carry us farther than this. Very comprehensively we might define it as being, in its physical import, the expression of persons and the light of things; and we might as comprehensively say of its moral nature, that it is the outward play or opaline lustre of the soul universal—the visible token of its occult value, and the prophetic affirmation of its transcendental supremacy. But whether does it come to us through the mind alone, or through its objects alone, when in union with it in some sufficing manner? Or is it really resident in both, as an aboriginal essence, pre-existing in Nature in a manner analogous to light, and, like it, coincident with percep- tion? These questions are extremely difficult, perhaps insoluble; but they are not impertinent. Without Mind to perceive and compare outward objects, there would (humanly speaking) be no outward beauty; but Mind itself would also be barren of it, if it were not for the objects thus perceived and compared. And seems it not somewhat, therefore, of which both are receptive, and which acts transfusively between them, as a flowing virtue in the constitution of the world? x this being itself , the outward and secular garment of God, and thence not only p[unclear]ly or apparently beautiful, but essentially so.Or effluence of deity, and thence not merely apparently, but essentially existent? But what, then, is deformity— ugliness, whether partial or total? It is simply its privation, from a less degree to the greatest, as gloom and darkness are the gradational privations of light. Or rather shall we say of Beauty, that it is a certain joint result of the relation subsisting between Mind and Matter, whenever that relation is harmonious and happy? That in the physical world, it is the glow of truth, or fitness, in fruition with a perceiving intelligence; and that in the ideal world, or in the mind itself, it is the desirable, or the possible in perfection, as bodied forth and naturalised by a com- bining, and so far creative, Imagination?
