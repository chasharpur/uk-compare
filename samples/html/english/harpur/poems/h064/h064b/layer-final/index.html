Original Poetry.

SATIRICAL EFFUSIONS, By Charles Harpur.

I.

A CHARACTERISTIC EPITAPH FOR A LATELY DECEASED ABLE DEALER.

Here out of memory is stealing, A wight who throve by able dealing. He went, well dressed, to church each Sunday, To mart as duly every Monday. He thought all poorer men were sinners, And never deigned to share his dinners With those who might not have as good At home, or better, if they would. He never gave a mite to want, And would have hanged a mendicant, Because all outcasts, to his sense, Were plainly bann’d by Providence. For theft he would have caged his father, But thought a cheat a genius rather; And that a boy whose wit could span A trick of gain was quite a man; And that a girl adroit in trade Was more than chaste—yea, twice a maid! He held a lie of business good Judgmatically understood; And that the truth, with equal reason, Was sometimes something worse than treason. Held too that even a parson’s grace Was measured by the pounds his place Might yield him in his holy race, That being God’s own way of giving The better priest his special living. With him all creditors were civil, All debtors frontless as the devil; All rights of capital most just, All rights of labor vile as dust! And thus he lived till he was hoary, Then died:—So ends his earthly story. And where to, next, ’twas his to go, Sir, There’s not a meerer Jew there: But granting ’twas to Heaven, the poser Is,—what he finds to do there! And even if he went to hell, The puzzle keeps its edge as well, For devil to damn’d soul being brother, They never double chalk each other; But jointly guard the infernal portals, Against the social greed of mortals. In Heaven they neither buy nor sell, (This is the Moral—mark it well!) And thence can have no place of merit, For the mere Dealer to inherit: In Hell, the damn’d for all duration, Are rather bettered by damnation; So kindless is all human badness, So super-devilish mundane madness.
