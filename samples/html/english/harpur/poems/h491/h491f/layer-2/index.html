The Scamper of Life.

By Charles Harpur.

Avast! I say, ye restless things! Where are you all thus running to? To Nothing? at at such a pace As if you all were funning too! And with a giddy gabbling noise That really is quite stunning too!

Crack! there goes bold squire Jehu with His tandam blood-hacks both in hand; And here the Flying Pieman skelters Ever on, as loth to stand: Which shall be foremost found, I wonder, By and by, in Nothing-Land?

A frolic beauty scampers past— A ripe and rosy wonder rare! And haply all her charms may buy, (If she can keep them fond and fair) A lift o’er that last ugly stile— But not a worm beyond it there!

A poet follows, listening but To his own bedlam bletherin’! Follows how wildly fast, though pulled By prosy Need’s strong tether in! Fool! yon grim Pit’s no cozy place For stringing rhymes together in.

A saint sends next—sends, making even A more loud and frantic din!Flashing 456 Flashing aloft his jewelled fist As one who’d, like a man, lick sin! Zounds! yon red Gulph’s no place, I say, For playing pious antics in!

A rush of civil service men Go next, all more in less in fear, As horsed on slippery slides that jut Sheer over yon dread Prec’pice near! Gad! what is even a first class lead In such a race? I guess ’tis queer!

A motley group as wild ensues, With care and scheming pale and bit, Wool-samples in their shaky hands, All eager to make sale of it— I wonder what King Death out there Will offer by the bale for it?

And Pinchem, after all the deals Which thy smart hand has caught by th’snout, What shalt thou have to do at all When life’s poor job thou’st wrought right out, There where no sugar‘s ever stored, And tea is never thought about?

And what shall Wantworth’s weighbill say When he too has done sharking here? “Good people all, down there below, Don’t let this blade come carking near! He bites; and every one he bit Went, like Bill Blether, barking here.

And what shall Windy’s passport plead For all his froth-mad cavilling 457a And patriotic fume, when he Shall also here have had his fling? “The bearer did his best—and worst— Being only up to badgering

And eke when that Bashanie Bull With neck and hide so thick—Bill’s son! Must follow his own watery pills In patients long since sick and done, Shall Wisdom then, or mere Pretence, Have cut her final stick and run?

And what shall Truth declare, when High Shall, nolens volens, draw first near The zoisty schemers of the past? “So then, he’s off—that Law-nurst seer! Who hated me almost as much As he feared fighting Broadhurst here!”

And when brave burly Broadhurst too No more in our sun’s light shall stare With all a hero’s mighty joy On pops with barrels bright and bare, It may be, even he‘ll meet what Shall make him wink to fight with there!

And when prince Stuart bolts a-head, So far, like all too clever men, 458 That his so redly royal phiz This world again may never ken— Will strutting Turkey cocks, I wonder, Be as proud as ever then?

And what if Rodd, and the Gold Field squad when they at last (a donkey trio) trot away? Three biped-bodied Things (liken men) Would go, no doubt, to pot that day, But as to any mind as well Our world would miss them—not a ray!

And is it then, ye silly Worms, You rush with such grimaces still, Tow’rds yonder awful Brink, all straining For the foremost places still? Yea, though your abject Fears thereat Make the most horrid faces still!

“Then what is Life?” despairing cried Poor Shelley in the vapors all: And what is Life? I also ask, If such like are its capers all, And its first fame-struck fiddlers only— Folly’s cat-gut scrapers all?
