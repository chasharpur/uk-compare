POEMS, ORIGINAL AND SELECT.

(By Charles Harpur.)

THE BALLAD OF SIR GILBERT.

Sir Gilbert is a happy Knight, and happier is he aye, To think his sole Child’s lovesome eyes grow love- lier every day: Two violets bathed in morning dew, So tenderly serene:— Two well springs hued with heaven, as through Their willowy fringes seen! And so it hath been ever forth, since first his heart could say: A lily of the valley is born to me this day.

Sir Gilbert is a gladsome man.—a gladsome and a free! But why hath he provoked to day this storm of minstrelsy? It is as though his heart had run Stag wild with mirth and wit, And he would have old Time’s grey one Go bounding on with it! But did you ask the reason why, the happy man would say: My lily of the valley is seven years old to day.

Sir Gilbert was a gladsome man.—a gladsome and a free; But silent in his hall is now the voice of minstrelsy: It may be, he hath felt his prime A-wearing from him fast, And so would use the present time More staidly than the past. But did you ask the reason why, the altered man would say: My lily of the valley is withering away.

Sir Gilbert was a stalwart Knight, and sturdy as a tree, Though genial as the warmth of wine in pleasant company; And can it be, that he hath so Misused a year of time, That few his face again would know Who saw him in his prime? Ah! that is not the reason why his head is turned so grey: His lily of the valley has witherëd away!
