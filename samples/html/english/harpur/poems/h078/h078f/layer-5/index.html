VIII.

Sir Gilbert Blount

1.

Sir Gilbert Blount is a merry Knight, And merrier is he aye, To think his sole Child’s lovesome eyes Grow lovelier day by day: Two violets in a lonely dell Where two are seen alone— Two Diamonds of the Desert,—well Their willowy fringes shown.

And so it hath been ever forth Since first his heart could say: A lily of Love’s green valley Is born to me this day.

Sir Gilbert Blount is a gladsome man— A gladsome and a free! But why hath he provoked to day This storm of minstrelsy?It 69 It is as if his heart had run Stag-wild with mirth and wit, And he would have old Time’s grey one Go bounding on with it!

But did you ask the reason why, The happy man would say: My lily of Love’s green valley Is seven years old to day.

2.

Sir Gilbert Blount was a gladsome man— A gladsome and a free! But silent in his hall is now The voice of minstrelsy: It may be he hath felt his prime A-wearing from him fast, And so would make the coming time Redeem the giddier past.

But did you ask the reason why, The altered man would say: My lily of Love’s green valley Is withering away!

Sir Gilbert was a stalwart Knight, And sturdy as a tree, Though genial as the warmth of wine In pleasant company; And can it be, that he hath so Misused a year of time, That few his face again would know Who saw him in his prime?

Ah! 70

Ah! that is not the reason why His head is turned so grey: His lily of Love’s green valley Has witherèd away.

+ In allusion to a fountain in Arabia called the Diamond of the Desert
