Sir Gilbert.

Sir Gilbert is a happy knight, And happier is he aye To think his sole child’s lovesome eyes Grow lovelier every day: Two violets bathed in morning dew, Ethereally serene! Two well-springs hued with heaven, as through Their willowy fringes seen! And so it hath been ever, since first his heart could say: A lily of the valley is born to me this day.

Sir Gilbert is a gladsome man, A gladsome and a free; But why hath he provoked to-day This storm of minstrelsy? It is as though his heart had run Stag-wild with mirth and wit, And he would have old Time’s grey one Go bounding on with it! But did you ask him wherefore, right fatherly he’d say: My lily of the valley is seven years old to-day.

Sir Gilbert was a gladsome man, A gladsome and a free; But si[l]ent in his hall is now The voice of minstrelsy. It may be he hath felt his prime A-wearing from him fast, And so would use the future time More staidly than the past. But did you ask him wherefore, the altered man would say: My lily of the valley is withering away.

Sir Gilbert was a stalwart knight, And sturdy as a tree, Though genial as the warmth of wine In pleasant company; And can it be that he hath so Misused a year of time, That few again his face would know Who saw him in his prime? Ah! that is not the reason his head is turned so grey: His lily of the valley has withered away.
