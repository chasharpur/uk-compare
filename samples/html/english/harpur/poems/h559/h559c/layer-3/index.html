VI.

Early Summer.

24

Chasing cold Winter’s dull sadness, The Springtime has come in its gladness! The sky how serene, And the mountains how green; And look you, my beautiful Mary, ‘Neath the vigorous beams Of the sun, how the streams Are Dazzling on all hands like luminous dreams That garbling abroad through some Land of the Faery. The echoing valleys and rocks How they ring with the life of the flocks! And how lusty and sportful the rout and the noise Of you gambolling herds! And hark how those holliday boys An all Gleeing together as shrilly as birds While forward and backward they dodge and they run, And tumble and roll the green grass in their fun.

And the flowers;—so many and choice, How they star the fresh Landscape, and all but rejoice Aloud in their blooming delight! While the sprout-tender trees, And the shrubs all in blossom, red, purp, and white, With the vines amid these Dressing down on the sight, As their features converse in the glad-coming breeze, Seem laughing with all their might.

And the birds, as they’d never have done, Are carolling over their happy employ That in the first flush of the morning begun! And the bees are abroad in their murmurous mirth Wherever a balm-breathing blossom has birth, To plunder but not to destroy! And the beautiful lizards how deftly they run 25 Over the pathway! and see, in the sun, How the grasshoppers beat their bright wings for joy.*

x The first two of the foregoing poems were the spontaneous overflowings of that uncontainable gladness with which, in early youth, the coming of Summer always inspired me. In my boyhood, I par- ticularly remember, that my very life at such seasons was a plastic centre as it were for all that was exhillerating in surrounding nature — the fountain-home of a thousand currents of joy, bright and gushing as the mountain rills, fresh and pleasant as the breath of heaven.

x And should not a sensibility to the large and beautiful beneficences of the outward universe, be promoted as much as possible in all, not merely as a medium of healthy pleasure, but also as one of the strongholds of rational religion? (I would have said natural religion had I not wished to imply more.) But to answer the question: I think it should. For though from an instinctive fear of degenerating into cant, God as their author may not be directly re- ferred to in our expression of the delight of which they are the ministers: still in the very secret of the heart’s gladness there resides an acknowledgement of his bounty; because the Beautiful and the Good are ultimately traceable to that source alone. To feel the one is to admit the consequence, however tacitly; and to become conscious, even this mediately, of the Divine Presence, is to become better.—

C.H.
