THE MURDER OF THE LAMB:

A LEGEND OF THE SHEEP-FOLD.

BY CHARLES HARPUR.

I.

O’er the heavenmost verge of a mountain high Through a gate of burning gold, The Sun, even like a great red eye, Looks down against the Fold; While the stirring sheep out staring keep, Their Shepherd to behold.

Forth from his well-slabbed Lodge thereby Singing a merry rhyme, He comes—for his old heart runs high For joy in his morning’s prime: And with him comes a Shepherd Boy, For it is lambing time.*

And at their side with social mien And glossy waving hair, A well-kept, well-taught Colley’s seen Right knowingly to fare: A sage and faithful dog, I ween, And full of happy care.

148ba

And oft the lambs were left astray, To danger lying stark, But for the heedful and wary way He’ll round them up, or mark From his knowing stand on some vantage height, Whenever the Flock’s too straggled plight Suggests the warning bark.

And often through the live-long day The mountain nooks among, The echoes hid in the caverns grey With tangled brakes o’erhung, Shall merrily mock from rock to rock The peal of his mellow tongue.

Hark! ’tis the grate of the Fold’s lath-gate Thrown wide with a swinging shock; And then one huddling countless bleat From the fast out pouring flock Spreads onward, with a spreading beat Of many-pattering tiny feet: Likest of all things to the fall Of rain upon a rock.

And tinkle, tinkle, as they go, The wedder-bell is heard; Tinkle, tinkle, sweet and low, Like the voice of the dingle-bird,† By which, where hidden waters flow, The crag-pent air is stirred.

And merrily still their dams before As pastureward they go, The lambs crowd out, to scamper o’er Some known bank to and fro, Which then doth seem as they over it stream A drifting bank of snow.§

149aa

And ever as some one will start Out with a wilder joy. Yet gladlier skips the old Shepherd’s heart, Grown old in such employ— And to the sound of their swarming feet Showering along like falling sleet, As a gleeful bird might its pinions beat, So claps his hands the Boy.

But why doth a swift pang now wring His eager face—to see One lamb in the midst of that glad crowd bring Less mirth than there in its midst should be— A slender and broken-spirited thing Snatching a fearful glee?

And lo, its eye, abashed and meek, Sends oft a furtive glance The presence of the Boy to seek, As he views it there askance: Then, strange to say, amid its play It cowers and turns about, And slinks at once to the Flock away From the thick of that merry rout.

What can there be in that Urchin’s mood To cloud that lambkin’s fate? “The boy is brave at heart and good Though given at times to hate, And somewhat will-wild from the rude Inbreathings of the lonesome wood:” So saith his ancient Mate.

And sooth, there is a forest cast About his meagre face, As into it the moods had passed Of many a lonely place, And, settling there, infixed the air Of some half Indian race.

And he is paler than a Lad So free of limb should be, And in his eye there lurks some sad And singular mystery: And that yon Lamb he worketh wrong Whose look, even, scares it from the throng, Is a matter plain to see.

149b

Alas! his mortal hate hath curst A Creature that he loved at first— Loved in a gentle sort. Day by day this passion grew To see it in its sport, And many a fond thing would be do Its mute regard to court.

And ever his keen love to feed, When forward the old Man should speed To head the Flock,—alone Leaving him there behind, to keep The lambs all strown about in sleep— In snowy patches strown, On this particular one he’d creep, And sometimes seize it at a leap, And sometimes chace it down.

And when the little trembler so Was taken, he would place Its foremost feet his chin below And gaze into its face: But only fear was living there— Not what he yearned to trace.

O! had it been a thing with speech Tow’rds which his love did glow, Some sympathy within their reach Sweet words had given to flow: And through the might of his strange delight To have waked a kindred mood, Had kept its fountain brimming bright And welling forth for good.

But in the turned and timid face He loved so well, to find Of love and joy no answering trace, Whilst fear had there a constant place, So shocked at length his mind And shamed his heart,—that in his grief He turned to hatred for relief! Of such a bitter kind Is the inward smart of a young lone heart Whose love is cast behind.

And daily then this hatred grew, Till it ere long became A load his very soul did rue! And the mystery of the same, After it still, like a scorpion, drew A burning sting of shame.

A hateful shame! And then he’d take The lambkin as before, But not, as once, the love to slake That glowed in his life’s red core; But aye to beat and evil entreat The Creature more and more.

II.

At last a murderous intent Did fasten on his will, And keep him pondering as he went How he might safely kill The hated little Innocent! But so as not to shed Its blood, for he of kindless Cain Had by the lodge-fire read, And now the blood of Abel slain Did cry to God from the conscious sod Where it lay in a pool so red.

And thus he feared to shed its blood Though fully bent to slay: And wrung by this same murderous mood Was he upon the day Whereon we saw the Flock outrun At the uprising of the sun, And all the lambs in play.

And outward still as the Flock did hie Upon that morning bright, A rushy plat, as he followed by, Did take the Urchin’s evil eye— 200b And here, hid from the old man’s sight, As the sheep feed home in the evening gloom, I’ll wreak, thought he, my spite! Tied by the neck in the rushes here And left deserted, surely Fear Will kill it in the night!

The Morn hath died in its mountain pride, And yet the Flock feeds out— Till his sun-robe, flaming at noon world-wide, Old Time, for haste, hath half cast aside, Tending west with a shadowy stride— And then ’tis fetched about, And taught to keep with a well-spread sweep Upon the homeward route.

And now the golden Eventide Is glowing in the west— Glowing like a Summer bride Upon old Winter’s breast! O how can Hate in all the wide And lovely world, thus beautified, Dare harbour unconfest— Nor casting off its hellish scroff, Transform to a Spirit blest?

201a

So feels even that rude-nurtured child If his heart aright I read— And yet as through the forest wide The fleecy breeders feed Wide-whitening homeward,—should they pass Near yonder nook, even there, alas! Will he do a hateful deed.

And the ground is moist, and the spear grass high, And the herbage greenest there, And thither still, as the rest graze by, The elder dams repair, And after each one her lamb doth run To frisk in so fresh a lair.

And thither too, at their pattering heels, His hate the Boy hath brought: And now on the doomed Lamb he steals— And pounces—and ’tis caught! Then stooping, he his form conceals, And ponders, pale with thought.

But suddenly a darksome glee Glooms outward from his mind, And with a fierce impatience he Around its neck doth bind And knot the rushes, so as there What time the Flock shall onward fare, To leave it tied behind.

And the dark like a pall down over all Is duskening through the air; And only its own little self so white To behold in the boundless gloom of night, 201b How shall it madden with affright In that so lonesome lair? Alas! far on the Flock is gone, And onward still doth go: That terrible Boy, in his cruel joy, Ah! can he leave it so?

He can! he will! and homeward still He driveth on and on: And now his ancient Mate before Their halting charge is gone, The dams into the fold to score— And thereof lacking none, Come Lad, saith he, to our ease will we, For a good day’s work is done.

But from the settling Flock, I guess, Full soon he ‘gan to mind That a long bleat oft,—full of distress, Poured out into the hollow wind: For shaking his head, the old Man said We have left a Lamb behind!

And then the wrong the Boy had done Did sorely against him plead! Darkly he supped apart, to shun The thoughtful Shepherd’s heed: For at each long bleat, the block-raised slab He supped on, jarred, as it fain would blab The tale of his wicked deed!

III.

All through the forewatch of the night That mournful Mother’s wail Did thus reproach his cruel sprite; Until he thought he also caught Her Lamb’s voice in the gale Homeward desolately brought! And his conscience then like a Fury wrought, And his wilful heart did quail.

And so, all in the dead midnight Unto himself he said, I’ll fetch it home!—His heart grew bright, For Mercy is ever a spirit of light, And he leaped from his thorny bed— But to stand aghast as if stricken fast Into cold stone with dread!

For sees he not in the lightless room The Victim of his spite! But never might gleam in the solid gloom A living Lamb so white! And there is the power of some terrible doom In the glow of its eyes so bright!

Breathless, pulseless, long he stared! Long, ghostly stiff, he stood! When from the hearth, on a sudden, flared From the covered and smoulding wood, A wild flash of light on that spectre so white— And lo, from its side ran blood!

Blindly staggering back to bed, On the old Man then he cried, Who, starting from his calm sleep, sped In wonder to his side, And there learned all as it did befall, And where the Lamb was tied.

Straightway his clothes he hurried on, And hurried at once away, That good old Man: and as alone The guilty Urchin lay, An age of fear, it seemed, had fled In darkness by, ere again the tread Of the sad Shepherd made his step heard Loud on the homeward way.

And then as past the Fold in sight He came, thus spake the ancient Wight, Alas, for thee, poor dam! Some evil creature of the night Hath slain thy pretty lamb! All torn I found it where ’twas tied— Oh God! the listening Boy outcried, The murderer that I am!

245b

Yes, thou has done a fearful deed, The old Man made reply; And to the Lord of all hast need With a contrite heart to cry, Till sorrow true and penance due Win pardon from on high.

And suddenly the Boy’s heart did break Into a wonderous prayer, That God the Father of all would take Sweet pity upon him for sorrow’s sake— Sorrow unfeigned, and spare To let the evil he had done, Being tempted of the Evil One, Some deadlier fruit yet bear!

And when he had prayed, he felt, he said, Some mystic Presence bright Burn downward, as from heaven, on spread And healing wings of light: And now for me, I know, said he, That Mercy herself shall plead, Till sorrow true and penance due Annul my sinful deed.

And first this burden did he lay Upon himself that night, That when a lamb should chance to stray, Chance often as it might, He’d neither rest nor quit the quest Until the morrow’s light.

Yet sometimes o’er his conscious ear At midnight still would come, A long bleat, sorrowful and drear, Announcing through the gloom That Spectre Lamb so deathly white And wondrous to behold, As first it met his fated sight And reproached his soul with its eyes so bright In the Lodge at the old Sheep-fold!

And then with a most contrite heart And fervent would he pray— To see the Phantom at once dispart, Smit through with a golden ray, As a mist wreath rare is shred into air By the first shotten glance of the day! And ever as it were wasting fast Paler and fainter it came and past At each recurring time: Till sorrow true and penance due Had cleansed him of his crime.

§ The snowy whiteness which in this Poem is attributed to the appearance of the lambs, is refer- able to impressions made upon the author’s memory in his boyhood, when tending, with his elder brother Joseph, a flock of sheep belonging 149ab to their father. At that time the Colonial lambs, having the Cape mixture in them, were much whiter than they are at present. Nay, even long after, when bred in a large degree into the Spanish and Merino types, they were so—the major part of them having a snowy flue or floss upon the extremity of the staple, which did not fall away from it until they were several weeks old. And it is also worthy of remark perhaps, that the wool of the lambs having this flue or floss upon them, was finer and less harsh than the wool of those that were then born without it: notwithstanding that it evidently resulted from a lingering strain in them of the coarse Cape mixture. At present, our Colonial lambs are a sort of drab color, and can only be called white by a poetic license.
