Miscellaneous Poems.

The Murder of the Lamb.

Part I.

O’er the hunch of a mountain piled to the sky, Through a cloud-gate of burning gold, The sun looks, like a great red eye, Sheer down on the smoking Fold: Whence the stirring sheep forth staring keep, Their Shepherd to behold.

Out from his well-slabbed Lodge therby, Singing a merry rhyme, He comes—for the tides of his heart run high For joy in the bracing prime; And with him a Lad comes ready and glad— His help in the lambing time.x

And at their side with social mien And glossy waving hair, A well-kept, well-taught Colley is seen Right knowingly on to fare: A sage and faithful dog I ween, And full of a happy care.

179a

And oft the lambs were left astray, To danger lying stark, But for the heedful and wary way He’ll round them up, or mark Whenever the Flock’s too straggled plight (As seen from his stand on some vantage height) Suggests the warning bark.

And often through the live-long day, The mountain nooks among, The echoes hid in their caverns grey With tangled brakes o’erhung, Shall merrily mock from rock to rock The peal of his mellow tongue.

Hark! ’tis the grate of the Fold’s lath-gate Thrown wide with a swinging shock: And then one huddling, countless bleat From the fast outpouring Flock, Spreads onward, with a spreading beat Of many-pattering tiny feet: Likest of all things to the fall Of rain upon a rock.

And tinkle, tinkle, as they go, The wedder-bell is heard; Tinkle, tinkle, sweet and low, Like the voice of the dingle-bird, 180a By which, where hidden waters flow The crag-pent air is stirred.

And merrily still their dams before As pastureward they go, The lambs crowd out to scamper o’er Some known bank to and fro, Which then doth seem as they over it stream A drifting bank of snow.x

And ever as some one will start A-head with a wilder joy, Yet gladlier skips the old Shepherd’s heart, Grown old in such employ— And to the sound of their swarming feet, Showering along like falling sleet, As a gleeful bird might its pinions beat So claps his hands the Boy.

But why doth a swift pang now wring His eager face—to see One Lamb in the midst of that glad crowd bring Less mirth than there in its midst should be— 181a A slender and broken-spirited thing Snatching a fearful glee?

And lo, its eye, abashed and meek, Sends oft a furtive glance The presence of the Boy to seek, As he views it there askance: Then, strange to say, amid its play, It cowers and turns about, And flockward slinks at once away From the thick of that merry rout.

How comes it that yon Urchin’s mood Thus clouds that lambkin’s fate? “The Boy is brave at heart and good, Though given at times to hate, And somewhat will-wild from the rude Inbreathings of the lonesome wood:” So saith his ancient Mate

And truly, there’s a forest cast About his meagre face, As into its mould the spirit had passed Of many a lonesome place, And settling there, infixed the air Of some half Indian race.

182

And he is paler than a Lad So free of limb should be, While in his eye there lurks some sad Life-irking mystery: And that yon Lamb he worketh wrong Whose look, even, scares it from its throng Of mates while still they bound along, Is very plain to me.

Alas! his mortal hate hath curst A Creature that he loved at first After a gentle sort:— Day by day this passion grew To see it in its sport, While many a fond thing would do Its mute regard to court.

And ever his keen love to feed, When forward the old Man should speed To head the Flock—alone Leaving him then, behind to keep The lambs all strown about in sleep— In snowy patches strown, On this particular one he’d creep And sometimes sieze it at a leap, And sometimes chase it down.

And when the little trembler so Was taken, he would place Its foremost feet his chin below, And gaze into its face: But only fear was living there— Not what he yearned to trace.

183

Oh! had it been a thing with speech Tow’rds which his love did glow, Some sympathy within their reach Fond words had taught to flow; And through the might of his strange delight To have wakened a kindred mood, Had kept its fountain pure and bright And welling forth for good.

But in the turned and timid face He loved so well, to find Of love and joy no answering trace, While fear had there a constant place, So shocked at length his mind And shamed his heart,—that in his grief He turned to hatred for relief! Of so extreme a kind Is the bitter smart of a young lone heart Whose love is cast behind.

“Wild wretch!” he cried, “Why this dismay— This struggling to be free? Can nothing that I do or say Avail to win from thee Or love or fellowship?—Away! And now beware of me.”

And daily then this hatred grew, Till it ere long became A load his very soul did rue: While the mystery of the same, Still after it, like a scorpian, drew A burning sting of shame.

184

A hateful shame! And then he’d take The Lambkin as before, But not, as once, the love to slake That glowed in his life’s red core; But aye to beat and evil entreat The Creature more and more.

Part II.

At last a murderous intent Did fasten upon his will, And keep him pondering as he went How he might safely kill The hated little Innocent, And yet its blood not spill!

For, by the Lodge-fire, he of Cain Had erewhile heard with dread, And how the blood of Abel slain, Although in secret shed, Did cry to God from the conscious sod Where it lay in a pool so red.

And thus he feared to shed its blood, Though fully bent to slay: And wrung by this same murderous mood Was he upon that day Whereon we saw the Flock outrun At the red uprise of the sun, And all the lambs in play.

And outward still as the Flock did hie Upon that morning bright, 185 A rushy plot, as he followed by, Did take the Urchin’s evil eye, And he muttered self-communingly: “Here, crouching out of sight, As the sheep feed home in thr evening gloom On that Lamb will I wreak my spite! Tied by the neck in the rushes here, And left deserted,—surely Fear Will kill it in the night!”

The Morn lies dead on yon mountain’s head No longer a-glow with its cloud-fires red, While still the Flock feeds out: Out till his sun-robe, at noon world-wide, Old Time, for haste, hath half cast aside, Tending west with a shadowy stride— And then ’tis fetched about, And taught to keep with a well-spread sweep Upon the homeward route.

And now the golden Eventide Is glowing in the west— Glowing like a Summer bride Upon old Winter’s breast! O how can Hate in all the wide And beautiful world thus beautified, Dare harbour unconfest— Nor casting off its hellish scroff, Transform to a Spirit blest?

So feels even that rude-nurtured Child If his heart aright I read— And yet, as through the woody wild The fleecy breeders feed, 186 Wide-whitening homeward—should they pass Near yonder nook, even there, alas! Will he do a hateful deed!

And the ground is moist, and the spear-grass high, And the herbage greenest, there; And thither still, as the rest graze by, The older dams repair; While after each one her lamb doth run To frisk in so fresh a lair.

And there too, at their pattering heels, His hate the Boy hath brought: And now on the doomed Lamb he steals— He pounces—and ’tis caught! Then stooping, he his form conceals, Pale pondering o’er his thought.

Till suddenly a cruel glee Glares outward from his mind, And with a fierce impatience he Around its neck doth bind And knot the rushes—so as there What time the Flock shall onward fare, To leave it tied behind.

And the dark like a pall down over all Is duskening through the air; And only its own small self so white To behold in the boundless gloom of night, How shall it madden it with affright In that so lonesome lair! That terrible Boy, in his savage joy, Ah! can he leave it there?

187

The dams have left it every one, At first forth-stringing slow— But soon far on the whole have run As faster the head-sheep go: That cruel Lad, however bad, Ah! can he leave it so?

He can! he will! and homeward still Keeps driving, on and on: Till now his ancient Mate before Their halting charge hath gone, The dams into the Fold to score— And thereof lacking none, Come Lad, saith he, to our ease will we, For a good day’s work is done.

But from the settling Flock, ere long, Did he begin to mind, That oft one strong bleat—sorrow-strong, Went out in the hollow wind: And shaking his head, the old Man said We have left a lamb behind

And then the wrong the Boy had done Did sorely against him plead!— Darkly he supped apart, to shun The careful Shepherd’s heed; For all that day, he had thought and done Since the strong red uprise of the sun, Kept chasing him now with a phantom speed; And at each long bleat, the block-raised slab That he supp’d on—jarred, as it fain would blab The tale of his singular deed.Part

Part III.

All through the forewatch of the night That mournful Mother’s wail Did thus reproach his cruel spite— Till in the dying gale, Out desolately far, he thought Her Lamb’s faint voice he also caught, And his conscience then like a Fury wrought, And his wilful heart did quail.

And so, all in the dead midnight, Within himself he said, I’ll fatch it home!—His heart grew bright, For Pity is ever a spirit of light, And he leaped from his thorny bed— But to stand aghast, as if stricken fast Into cold stone with dread.

For sees he not in the lightless room The Victim of his spite? But never might gleam in the solid gloom A living Lamb as white! And there is the power of some terrible doom In the glow of its eyes so bright!

Breathless, pulseless, long he stared; Long, ghostly stiff, he stood! When from the hearth, on a sudden, flared From the covered and smouldering wood, A wild flash of light on that Spectre so white— And lo, from its side ran blood!

189

While in the dark, out o’er the roof, Was heard a low wild moan, As of some Spirit there aloof In suffering and alone: A hollow sound that, wasting, went Away into the firmament: And the Boy shut fast his eyes at last, And heaved a fearful groan.

For he thought of his dead Mother then— And could it her spirit be? And he thought of the prayers she had taught him when A little child was he And how oft she had said that of all things dread The worst was cruelty

Then blindly staggering back to bed, On the old Man loud he cried; Who, starting from his calm sleep, sped In wonder to his side, And there learned all as it did befall, And where the Lamb was tied.

Straightway his clothes he hurried on, To hurry at once away, That good old Man: and as alone The guilty Urchin lay, Long ages seemed to have o’er him sped In darkness, ere the Shepherd’s tread Was heard again to louden plain Upon the homeward way

And then as past the Fold in sight He came, thus spake the ancient wight: “Alas, for thee, poor dam! Some evil creature of the night Hath slain thy pretty Lamb! All torn I found it where ’twas tied—” O God! the listening Boy outcried, The Murderer that I am!

190

Yes, thou hast done a deadly deed, The old Man made reply; And to the Father of all hast need With a contrite heart to cry, Till sorrow true and penance due Win pardon from on high.

Yet lie not moaning there, my son, But up and get thee drest, And rouse the Flock with the rousing sun; For of all ways, the best For righting the wrong thou hast done to one, Is justice to the rest.

But he had sickened—and his bed Kept many a day and night: And oft—See, there again! he said, It comes so ghostly white! There in the depth of the lightless room, Piercing the damp and settled gloom With the glow of its eyes so bright.

Thus lay he—till his heart did break Into a wondrous prayer, That God, the Father of all, would take Sweet pity upon him for sorrow’s sake, Thus lowly lying there! Nor let the wrong that he had done, Being tempted of the Evil One, Some deadlier fruitage bear.

And when he had prayed, he felt he said, Some mystic Influence bright, Burn downward as from heaven, on spread 191 On healing wings of light, And turn to a holy hope, the dread That hitherto ever about his bed Had come with the darksome night.

And thereupon, rightly promptly he Arose, from sickness freed, And shouted—Now I know for me That Mercy herself shall plead, Till sorrow true and penance due Annul my sinful deed.

And first this burden did he lay Upon himself that night: Whenever a lamb should chance to stray, Chance often as it might, He’d neither rest nor quit the quest Until the morrow’s light.

Yet sometimes o’er his conscious ear Long after would there come, A long bleat, sorrowful and drear, Announcing through the gloom That Spectre Lamb so ghostly white And wondrous to behold, As first it met his fated sight, And reproached his soul with its eyes fire-bright In the Lodge at the old Sheep-fold.

But then with a most contrite heart And fervent, he would pray— To see the Phantom at once dispart, Shot through with a golden ray;Even 192 Even as a thin white mist is burst, Shred up and shrunken, by the first Red flash of the perfect day.

Thus came, thus vanished, to the last, The Spectre of this wild rhyme: But ever, as it were wasting fast, Paler and fainter it came and passed At each recurring time; Till sorrow true and penance due From his young life had purged the hue And slime-stain of a crime.

x The snowy whiteness which is all along in the Poems attributed to the lambs, is referable to impressions made upon the Author’s memory in his boyhood, when tending with an elder brother, upon the banks of the South Creek, and in the primative woods lying out from them, a large flock of mixed sheep belonging to their father. At that time, the Colonial lambs, having the Cape mixture yet in them, were much whiter than they are at present. Nay, even long after, when bred in a large degree into the Spanish and Merino types, they were so,—the major part of them having a snowy flue or floss upon the extremity of the staple of their wool, 181b which did not fall away from it until they were several weeks old. And it is also worthy of remark perhaps, that the wool of the lambs having this flue or floss upon them, was less harsh than the wool of those that were then born without it: notwithstanding that it very evidently resulted from a strain of the coarse Cape mixture that was yet lingering in their blood. At present our Colonial lambs are a sort of drab color, and can only be called white by a poetic license.
