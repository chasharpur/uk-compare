THE MURDER OF THE LAMB.

A LEGEND OF THE SHEEP-FOLD.

BY CHARLES HARPUR, ESQ.

[I dedicate this Poem to my juvenile country- men.—C. H]

Part I.

Just o’er the dewy mountains high All domed with clouds of gold, The Sun, with his big round red eye, Looks down against the Fold; And the stirring Flock bleats out, to spy Come forth its Shepherd old.

Out of his well-slabbed Lodge thereby, Singing a merry rhyme, He comes, for his old heart leaps high For joy in the Morning’s prime; And with him comes a Shepherd-boy, For it is Lambing-Time.

Hark!—’tis the gate of the Fold, I weet, Thrown wide with a swinging shock; And then one huddling, countless bleat From the out-pouring Flock, Spreads onward,—with a spreading bleat Of many-pattering tiny feet; Likest of all things to the fall Of rain upon a rock.

And merrily still, their Dams before, As pastureward they go, The Lambs crowd out, to scamper o’er Some known Bank to and fro; Which then doth seem, ‘neath their white stream, A living bank of snow.

And ever as some one will start Out with a wilder joy, Yet gladlier skips the old Shepherd’s heart, Grown old in such employ;— And to the sound of those small feet, As gleeful birds their pinions beat, So claps his hands the Boy.

But why doth a swift pang now wring His eager face, to see Amongst the rest one Lamb there bring Less mirth than there should be?— A broken-spirited little Thing Snatching a fearful glee!

And lo, its eye abashed and meek, Sends oft a furtive glance The presence of the Boy to seek, As he eyes there askance; And strange to say, amid its play, Into the grass its face It droppeth then, and slinks away, To join no more the race.

What is there in that Urchin’s mood To cloud that Lambkin’s fate? “The Boy hath a brave heart and good, Though all too passionate, And something rudened by the rude Inbreathings of the lonesome Wood:—” So saith his ancient Mate.

But he is paler than a Lad So free of limb should be, And in his eye there lurks some sad And burning Mystery! And that yon Lamb he worketh wrong Whose look so scares it from the throng, ‘Tis very plain to see.

Alas! his mortal hate hath curst A creature that he loved at first, But after a strange sort.— Day by day this feeling grew To see it in its sport, And many fond things would he do Its mute regard to court.

And ever, his keen love to feed, When forward the old Man should speed To head the Flock, alone Leaving him so behind, to keep The Lambs, all strown about in sleep, In snowy patches strown,— On this particular one he’d creep, And sometimes seize it at a leap, And sometimes run it down.

And when the little Trembler so Was taken, he would place Its foremost feet his chin below, And gaze into its face: But only Fear was living there— Not what he yearned to trace!

O, had it been a Thing of Speech Tow’rds which he yeared so, Some sympathy within their reach Sweet words had taught to flow; And back through the gleam of this strange Love- dream, A kindred light to feel, Had kept the fountain of its stream Still welling forth for weal.

But in the timid-turning Face He loved so, ne’er to find Of mututal joy a single trace, Whilst pain had there a constant place, So shocked at length his mind. And shamed his heart,—that in his grief, He turned to Hatred for relief! Of such a bitter kind Is the knawing smart of a young lone heart, Whose love is cast behind.

And daily then this hatred grew, Till it ere long became A load his very soul did rue! And the mystery of the same After it still, like a Scorpion drew A burning sting of shame!

A hateful shame! and then he’d take The Lambkin as before; But not, as once, the love to slake That glowed in his heart’s core; But aye to beat and evil treat The Creature more and more!

Part II.

At last a murderous intent Did fasten on his will, And keep him pondering as he went How he might safely kill The hated little Innocent! But so as not to spill Its blood, for he had read of Cain, And how the blood of Abel slain Did cry against him still.

And thus he feared to shed its blood, Though fully bent to slay: And wrung by this same murderous mood Was he upon the day Whereon we saw the Flock out run, At the uprising of the Sun, And all the Lambs in play.

And outward still as the Flock did hie Upon that morning bright, A rushy Plat, as he followed by, Did take the Urchin’s evil eye,— And here, hid from the old Man’s sight, As the Sheep feed home in the evening gloom, I’ll wreak, thought he, my spite! Tied by the neck in the rushes here, And left behind so, surely Fear Will kill it in the night!

The Morn hath died in his mountain pride, And yet the Flook feeds out;— Out, till his sun-robe all so wide The Noon hath partly cast aside, Stealing east with a shadowy stride,— And then ’tis wheeled about, And taught to keep, with a rounding sweep, Upon the homeward route.

And now the golden Eventide Is glowing in the west, Glowing like a Summer-bride 135b Upon old Winter’s breast! O, how can Hate in all the wide And beautiful World, so beautified, Dare harbour unconfest? Nor casting off its hellish scroff, Become a Creature blest?

So feels the spirit of that child— That child of Mystery! As yet, as through the Forest wild, The Flock, so fair to see, Feeds whitening homeward—should it feed Through yonder Rushes, on his deed Of hate still bent is he.

And the ground is moist, and the rushes are high, And the herbage is greenest there; And thither still, as the rest graze by, The elder Dams repair; And after each one her Lamb doth run To frisk in so fresh a lair.

And thither too, at their pattering heels, His hate the Boy hath brought: And there on the doomed Lamb he steals— He pounces—and ’tis caught! Then stooping, he his form conceals, And horrid is the glee he feels, Around its neck to bind And knot the rushes there—and then He rises and drives on again; And ah!—’tis left behind!

Left behind!—and the long black Night Shall settle o’er it there! And only its own little Self so white To behold in the boundless gloom! O spite! How shall it madden with affright? How sink then to despair? Alas! far on the Fock is gone, And onward still doth go! That terrible Boy, in his savage joy, Ah! can he leave it so?

He can!—he will! and homeward still He driveth on, and on: And now his ancient Mate before Their halting charge is gone, The Dams into the Fold to score;— And thereof missing none, Come Lad, saith he, to our ease will we, For a good day’s work is done.

But from the settling Flock, I weet, Full soon he ‘gan to mind, That oft a long distressful bleat Poured out upon the mind; And shaking his head, the old Man said, We have left a Lamb behind!

And then the evil he had done Did irk the boy indeed! Darkly he supped apart, to shun The Shepherd’s thoughtful heed; For at each bleat the very seat Seemed conscious of his deed!

Part III.

All through the forewatch of the night That mournful Mother’s wail Did thus reproach his cruel spite! Until he thought he also caught, Homeward desolately brought, Her Lamb’s voice in the gale! And then his mind like a Fury wrought, And his wilful heart did quail!

144a

And so, all in the dead midnight, Unto himself he said, I’ll fetch it home!—His heart grew bright, For mercy is a Thing of Light! And he leapt out of bed— To stand aghast, as rooted fast, And breathless as the dead!

For lo! he sees in the lightless room, Lightless even as the tomb, The Victim of his spite! But never might gleam in the solid gloom A living Lamb so white! And there is the power of some terrible doom In the glow of its eyes so bright!

At last, recoiling to his bed, Upon the old Man he cried; Who, starting from his calm sleep, sped In wonder to his side; And there learned all as it did befal, And where the Lamb was tied.

Straightway his clothes he hurried on, And hurried straight away, That good old Man; and as alone The guilty Urchin lay, An age of fear, it seemed, had fled In blackness by, before the tread Of the sad Shepherd made his step heard On the homeward way.

And then, as past the Fold in sight He came, thus spake the ancient Wight: Alas for thee, poor Dam: Some evil Creature of the night Hath slain thy pretty Lamb! All torn I found it where ’twas tied— O God! the listening Boy out-cried, A murderer I am!

Yes, thou hast done a fearful deed, The old Man made reply, And to the God of all hast need With a contrite heart to cry, Till sorrow true and penance due Win pardon from on High!

And suddenly the Boy’s heart did break Into a wondrous prayer, That the Great God of all would take Sweet pity on him there: Lest that the murder he had done, Being tempted of the Evil One, Should damn him to despair!

And when that he had prayed, he felt, He said, a living ray Come downward, as from Heaven, and melt Into his soul straightway! And now, said he, I know, for me Even Mercy’s self shall pray, That sorrow true and penance due May take my Sin away!

So felt he:—but for many a year, Sometimes o’er his conscious ear At midnight there would come, 144b A bleating sorrowful and drear, Announcing through the gloom, That spectre Lamb so deadly white, As first it met his doomed sight In the old Shepherd’s room.

And though with a most contrite heart And fervent then he’d pray, The Phantom would not all depart Until the dawn of day; Yet faintlier through the gloom of night, And faintlier, came it on his sight At each recurring time; Till sorrow true and penance due Had blotted out his crime.

CHARLES HARPUR.
