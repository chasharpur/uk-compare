The Spectre of the Cattle Flat.

Part I.

‘Twas night—and where a watery sound Came moaning up the Flat, Six rude and bearded Stockmen round Their blazing hut-fire sat: Laughing, as on some starting hound The cracking fuel spat.

And merrier still the log-fire cracks As night the darker falls, While not a noisy tongue their lacks To tell of drunken brawls: But most of battle with the Blacks Some bloody tale appals.

One tells how, after a fierce fray, A wounded Chief he found Dark-lying, log-like, on his way, Sore gashed with many a wound, And how, as there alive he lay, He staked him to the ground.

Or how, in the elbow of a creek That ran ‘neath crag-walls grey, A tribe entire, with many a shriek, Was pent, and held at bay, Till there, like sheep, in one close heap, Their slaughtered bodies lay.

Amongst 12

Amongst them then Ned Connor spoke, And up his form he drew: What is there in an open stroke To boast of? Ye but slew Those who’d have done, each hell-black one, The same or worse to you.

But lost amongst the hills one day, Which then was well-nigh shut, I met a Black upon my way And thus the matter put Unto him—See! this knife’s for thee— Come guide me to my Hut.

His savage eyes grew huge with joy As on the prize they bent, Then leading, even like a boy, He capered as he went: But think you, men, to give the toy Ned Connor ever meant?

An hour had brought us many a mile, And then, as closed the day, The Savage pointed with a smile To where my Station lay: There! give to me the knife, said he, And let me go my way.

‘Tis long since, but how well even yet My memory can retrace His features; for the sun was set, And through an open space, The long red gleams of its last beams Fell full upon his face.

13

But I ne’er meant that he should touch The knife as I have said, And when he stretched his hand to clutch, A thought came in my head: I raised my gun, as if in fun— I fired!—and he was dead!

Dead as a door-nail! There he lay In the forest, that duskier grew While out in the blood-red West away My Hut loomed full in view:— But what, my boys, keeps down your noise— Do ye think I ate him too?

The ruffian laughed in a ruthless mood When ended had thus his tale; But all the rest, though men of blood, With horror seemed to quail, And saw, though he stood boastfully, That Connor too was pale.

For through the moaning of the trees He seemed to hear the sound Of his own laughter in the breeze Keep roaming out—till drowned In wild and bitter mockeries Up answering from the ground.

Now what to hear had made them fear Had also made them dry: But strange! the water-pail that late Brimm’d in a corner nigh, Was empty! In amazement great, There’s not a drop! they cry.

14

The pail has leaked! cried one: and yet The ground is dry below! What next, I wonder? Here ’twas set Fresh filled an hour ago— Here, where it stands, by these my hands: The Devil drinks deep, I trow!

It cowed them! this so strange affair, However it came about: ‘Tis surely empty, and yet where Hath ever a drop leaked out? Another said, and shook his head: There’s something up I doubt!

It cowed them! Though they were indeed A bold and reckless crew, While each saw in his Mate’s pale heed His own;—yet since they knew The utmost measure of their need The thirstier they grew.

At last in bitterness they said Come this will never do! ‘Tis your next turn for water, Ned, Then why not go? He drew Full hard his breath, and from his head There dripped a sudden dew.

But shaming to be taxed with fear, He seized the pail, and said, What care I though the night be drear— Who ever saw the dead? And if I fail to fill this pail, The Devil shall instead!

15

Thus on the ominous word that he Who drew it last had said, He played in scorn—but bitterly, Because of a haggard dread That shivered within his heart of sin, Till it shrunk as it were dead.

Part II.

But see how mad a thing is pride In a sinful heart of stone! Far rather would he then have died Of the fear he felt, than own How deep within him Horror sighed At going forth alone.

Yet forth he strode. A sudden blast Went sobbing by the door, Through which they heard his footsteps fast Beat out—and when no more They heard them, round the fire aghast They gathered as before.

I would not go alone to night The way that he has gone, Said one, for all the gold so bright That ever was fairly won: To slay that Creature was not right— I’d say’t were he my son!

And now, impatient all and wild, They wondered at his stay;Till 16 Till one outspake: A weanling child Could not make more delay! If longer slack in coming back, He’ll bring with him the day.

But while they thus were wondering—hark! They heard a frantic shriek; Then nearing footsteps through the dark Came staggering and weak: And while in a crowd the dogs howled loud, Not one of them durst speak!

Not one of them but stared amazed, In silence, ghostly pale, While the dogs still howled as they were crazed, And snuffed the moaning gale: Till ’mid their rout those steps without Seemed all at once to fail:

Seemed to have failed a space without As the earth had swallowed them in! Then from the thick of that huddling rout, Loud over its howling din, Rose such a shriek as seemed to speak Of inexpiable sin!

And ‘gainst the door that to had swung One rushed, and in twain ’twas split! ‘Twas Connor! Through it had he sprung And fallen into a fit! And long that night in a ghastly plight There struggled he in it.

And 17

And when his sense returned—again The day was breaking bright; But shuddering as in mental pain, He turned from the happy light, And pointing said, I would to bed, For Death is in my sight.

They bore him to his bed straightway, Those horror-stricken men, And questioned him as there he lay, Of what had met his ken: Within himself he seemed to pray— And thus spake to them then.

I went (you heard) with impious boast For water to the brook; But when the threshold I had crossed All strength my heart forsook: I rued each forward step—but most I feared behind to look.

Long murky clouds were hurrying fast Across the moonless sky; Strange sounds came moaning up the blast That whistled by fits so high: A winding gleam—and lo, the stream Went wildly moaning by.

As never before, it struck me sore With a dull damp sense of awe, For crawling dank out o’er the bank, Crude Shapes methought I saw: Yet I must not fail to fill this pail, I said,—but down and draw.

18

Down went I—but those banks I ween, Seemed never so deep before: While up in the woodless gap between I saw great night-birds soar; And looming black in the scudding rack The Flying Fox swept o’er.

Alas! but every sound and sight Seemed full of boding blame; So that I now had turned in flight, But for as dread a shame, As o’er the stream a curlew’s scream In sudden challenge came!

But stumbling on to the water’s edge, Mine eyes thereon I threw, To see distinctly through the sedge Outstretching full in view— Not mine own Shadow from the ledge, But Him!—the Black I slew!

Part III.

So having said, his whole frame shook, His whole soul seemed to quail: Till water in his palm he took And sluiced his forehead pale: And then with earnest voice and look, He thus resumed his Tale.

‘Twas no delusion! Full of fear I was, but in my brain, 19 My thoughts, I say, ran quick and clear, Though terrible in their strain: And even now, thus lying here, I feel that I am sane.

Yes, there he was, outstretching dread, Within the gleaming brook! The same as when his blood I shed, In figure and in look! Even to the last accusing cast His dying aspect took.

While there, too, even as then it stood, My Hut loomed in the view; And in the mocky-sky of the flood, (I tell you, Mates, ’tis true!) That day’s red sunset—red as blood, Came burning back anew!

With a mighty bound I spun sheer round, And up the high bank sped; But ah! as swiftly in my track Bare footsteps seemed to tread! Step, step, for mine, close at my back, Still following as I fled.

It was a horrible thing to hear Behind me still that sound! I could not bear to have it there, And madly faced me round! When through the dark a sudden spark Shot upward from the ground.

Will-stunned 20

Will-stunn’d as with a heavy stroke, I could not turn again; But saw, whence came the spark, a smoke Arise from the clammy plain, While thence an earthy odor broke That stung into my brain.

At first I saw it bloating out, In size not o’er a span: Then as it slowly coiled about To heighten it began, Until it took in bulk and look The Likeness of a man.

The Likeness of a man, I say, As visioned to the mind, But to the eye an Image grey, To shapeless change inclined, Like columned mist at break of day When reached by the rising wind.

Yet was it dreadful to the eye, Though more so to the thought, When standing vaguely up, man-high, And gathered as from nought, It had through change so weird and strange To a human mockery wrought.

No stir was near: I might but hear The beating of my blood! And there, within my reach almost The horrid Phantom stood! I stared till fear in fear was lost And awe alone ensued.

21

I spoke—I know not what—and lo, The diabolic birth ‘Gan wildly writhing to and fro, As if in ghostly mirth— Then sidled nearer—smelling so Decayedly of the earth!

Then suddenly it wheeled, and went Right round me—round and round; Till, in its hellish sport, it bent And at me seemed to bound! A bound—a rush: then came a crush That beat me to the ground!

Mine eyes splashed out with sputtering flame— The ground kept swimming fast— And frightful laughters huddling came In a rush of the moaning blast! A moment more—and all was o’er, As I had breathed my last.

At length my brain began to swim As consciousness re-grew: But when with eyeballs strained and dim I looked again—I knew A Form stood o’er me—even Him! The Savage that I slew.

I shrieked—and bounding to my feet I fled,—but, as before, Bare footsteps tracked me, beat for beat With mine even to the door: What then befell I cannot tell— I know of nothing more.

22

He ceased, and turning in his bed Aloud for mercy cried, And for three days and nights, ’tis said, Nought uttered he beside: When raving out with sudden dread The haunted Murderer died.

The fearful men around him then Each one of them did say; It was a damnéd wrong in Ned That Savage so to slay: And where the path to the streamlet led They buried him next day.

There did they bury him, to ease A crudely pious dread, That sought, so choosing, to appease Some Influence from the dead, Which there had brought for his crime, they thought God’s doom on a Murderer’s head.

Note. No doubt the reader’s interest in the foregoing Tale will be somewhat increased, when he is informed that it is substantially true. To whatever source the Spectral terrors of it are to be referred,—whether to some of the darker agencies of a spiritual world, or to the formative vapours of a diseased imagination only,—yet the guilty Hero of it was frightened to death much in the manner described.
