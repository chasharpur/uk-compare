Ned Connor.

‘Twas night—and where a watery sound Came moaning up the Flat, Six rude and bearded Stockmen round Their blazing hut-fire sat, And laughed, as on some starting hound The cracking fuel spat.

And merrier still the log-fire cracks As night the darker falls, While not a noisy tongue there lacks To tell of drunken brawls, But most, of battle with the Blacks, Some bloody tale appals.

x One tells, how after a fierce fray, A wounded chief he found Dark-lying, loglike, on his way Sore gashed with many a wound: And how, as there alive he lay He staked him to the ground!

or how in the elbow of a creek That ran ‘neath crag-walls grey, A Tribe entire, with many a shriek, Were pent, and held at bay: Till there, like sheep, in one close heap Their slaughtered trunks all lay.

Amongst them then Ned Connor spoke, And up his form he drew: “What is there in an open stroke To boast of? Ye but slew Those who’d have done, each hell-black one, The same or worse to you.

But lost among the hills one day, Which then was well-nigh shut, I met a Black upon my way, And thus the matter put Unto him:—See! this knife’s for thee; Come, guide me to my Hut.

His savage eyes grew huge with joy As on the prize they bent, And leading, even like a boy He capered as he went: But think you, men, to give the toy Ned Connor ever meant?

168

An hour had brought us many a mile, And then, as closed the day, The Savage pointed with a smile To where my Station lay: There! give to me the knife, said he, And let me go my way.

I never meant that he should touch’t, As I before have said, And when he stretched his hand to clutch’t, A thought came in my head: I raised my gun, as if in fun— I fired—and he was dead!”

The ruffian laughed in a ruthless mood, When ended thus his tale; But all the rest, though men of blood, With horror seemed to quail, And saw though he stood boastfully, That Connor too was pale.

For through the moaning of the trees He seemed to hear the sound Of his own laughter in the breeze Keep roaming out—till drowned In wild and bitter mockeries, Up-answering from the ground!

Now what to hear had made them fear Had also made them dry: But strange! the water-pail that late Brimmed in a corner nigh, Was empty! In amazement great, “There’s not a drop!” they cry.

“The pail has leaked!” said one; “and yet The ground is dry below! What next, I wonder? Here ’twas set Fresh filled an hour ago— 169 Here where it stands—by these my hands! The devil drinks deep, I trow!”

It cowed them! this so strange affair, Howe’er it came about: “‘Tis surely empty, and yet where Hath any drop leaked out?” Another said, and shook his head: “There’s something up, I doubt!”

It cowed them! though they were indeed A bold and sturdy crew! And each saw hued in the rest’s pale heed His own:—yet for they knew The utter measure of their need, The thirstier they grew.

At last in bitterness they said Come this will never do! ‘Tis your next turn for water, Ned, Then why not go? He drew His breath full hard, and from his head There dripped a sudden dew.

But shaming to be taxed with fear, He siezed the pail, and said “What care I? Though the night be drear, Whoever saw the dead? And if I fail to fill this pail, The devil shall instead!”

Thus on the ominous word that he Who drew it last had said, He played in scorn—but bitterly, Because of a wild dread, That shivered within his heart of sin Till it shrunk as it were dead.

He sallied forth. A sudden blast Went sobbing by the door, 170 Through which they heard his footsteps fast Beat out—and when no more They heard them, round the fire aghast They gathered as before.

“I would not go alone to night The way that he is gone,” Said one, “for all the gold so bright That ever was fairly won: To slay that creature was not right— I’d say’t were he my son.”

And now impatient all and wild, They wondered at his stay, Till one outspake: “A weanling child Could not make more delay! If longer slack in coming back, He’ll bring with him the day.”

But while they thus were wondering—hark! They hear a frantic shriek; Then nearing footsteps through the dark Come waywardly and weak: And while in a crowd the dogs howled loud, They stared, but feared to speak.

Against the door that to had swung One rushed then, and ’twas split! ‘Twas Connor! Through it had he sprung And fall’n into a fit: And long that night, in a ghastly plight There struggled he in it.

24

And when his sense returned—again The day was breaking bright, But shuddering as in mental pain, He turned him from the light, And pointing said—”I would to bed! For Death is in my sight.”

They bore him to his bed straightway, Those horror-stricken men, And questioned him, as there he lay, Of what had met his ken: Within himself he seeemed to pray— And thus bespake them then.

26

“I went (you heard) with impious boast, For water to the brook, But when the threshold I had crossed, All strength my heart forsook: Each forward step seemed fate—but most I feared behind to look.

27

Long murky clouds were hurrying past Across the starless sky; Strange sounds came drowning up the blast That piped by fits so high: A winding gleam, and lo! the stream Went wildly moaning by.

28

As never before, it struck me sore With a dull damp sense of awe, And crawling dank, and o’er the bank, Crude shapes methought I saw! Yet I must not fail, to fill this pail! I said,—But down and draw.

Down went I—but those banks I ween Seemed never before so high: While up in the woodless gap between Some night hawk wined his cry And in black dragon-outlines seen A vampire bat went by

29

Then stumbl[in]g to the water’s edge, Mine eyes thereon I threw, To see distinctly through thr sedge, Outstretching full in view— Not mine own shadow from the ledge, But him!—the Black I slew.

30

‘Twas no delusion! There he stood Within the gleaming brook, The same as when I shed his blood His stature and his look, 172 Even to the last accusing cast, His dying aspect took!

31

With backward bound I started round, And up the bank did flee! But ah! as swiftly in my track Bare footsteps seemed to be! Step, step, for mine, close at my back, I heard, but nought could see!

32

It was a horrible thing to hear Behind me still the sound! I could not bear to have it there, And, desperate, faced me round; When through the dark a sudden spark Shot upward from the ground!

33

Staggered as with a sudden stroke, I could not turn again, But saw, whence came the spark, a smoke Arise from a clammy plain, While thence an earthy odour broke, That bit me to the brain!

34

At first I saw it bloating out, In size not o’er a span; Then as it slowly coiled about, To heighten it began, Until it took in bulk and look The Likeness of a man!

35

The Likeness of a man, I say, As visioned by the mind, But to the eye, an Image grey, To shapeless change inclined, Like columned mist at break of day When reached by the rising wind.

36

Yet was it dreadful to the eye, Though dreadful more to thought, When standing vaguely up, man-high, And gathered as from nought, It had through change so weird and strange To a human mockery wrought!

37

No stir was near, I might but hear The beating of my blood, And there, within my reach almost, The horrid Phantom stood! I stared till fear in fear was lost, So awful was my mood.

38

I spoke—I know not what—and lo, The diabolic birth, ‘Gan wildly writhing to and fro, As if in horrid mirth— And then against me rushing so, It dashed me to the earth!

39

Mine eyes splashed out with sputtering flame,— The ground kept swimming fast— And frightful laughters huddling came In a rush of the roaming blast! A moment more—and all was o’er, As I had breathed my last.

40

At length my brain began to swim As consciousness regrew; But when with eyeballs strained and dim I looked again—I knew A Form stood o’er me: it was him! The Savage that I slew!

41

I shrieked—and bounding to my feet, I fled,—but, as before, Bare footsteps tracked me, beat for beat With mine even to the door: 174 What then befell I cannot tell— I know of nothing more.”

42

He ceased, and turning in his bed Aloud for mercy cried, And for three days & nights, ’tis said, He uttered nought beside: When raving out with sudden dread, The haunted murderer died.

43

The fearful men around him then, Each one of them did say, It was a damnëd wrong in Ned That savage so to slay: And where the path to the streamlet led They buried him next day.

44

There did they bury him, to ease A crudely pious dread, That sought, so choosing, to appease Some Influence from the dead, Which there had brought for his crime, they thought, God’s doom on a Murderer’s head.
