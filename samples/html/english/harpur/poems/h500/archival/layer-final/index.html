II.

Shortness of Life.

Mourn not Life’s shortness—life on earth Hath in its brevity its worth.

Note.—It is certainly questionable whether man could be [s]afely entrusted with many more years than his three score and ten. What, for instance,—what gigantic schemes of mischief could not a Napoleon devise and execute, with a constitution equal to the wear and tear of some five hundred years? Again, even the best men—even dreamers of Utopias, like More— grow more selfish and unprogressive as their days accumulate; grossly selfish sometimes, and insanely conservative. And even were this life lengthened to hundreds, instead of tens, of years, and should Providence at the same time, so order it as to make the results above spoken of, work themselves out with a slowness proportionable to the extension—its then duration (so far at least) would be only essentially equivalent to its present. Nothing would be gained by the alteration, whatever might be lost. Here then, in at least two great national and social regards, the shortness of human life is a positive benefit to the race.
