To Kossuth. (written in 1850)

Thou Man of moral majesty and might, Now that thy Country, bound and bleeding, cries For help in vain, and regnant Austria’s spite Can cloud thy starry fame with dastard lies, Not to despair alone bespeaks the wise, Bespeaks the true, thou pleader for the right! For Liberty, an essence born of light, May swoon—may slumber—but she never dies! Thy sacred purpose evermore shall swell The Magyar’s heart till through his war-trump given Victorious! thy great eloquence so well Doth clothe it, and project it up to Heaven, Wounding God’s peace, as the still sky is riven By some great mountain in the Land of Tell! ※

1850 (Addressed to him in 1850)

※ Of Kossuth’s vast power of patriotic appeal, even when clothed in a foreign language—the English for instance—Mr Cobden has well remarked, in a noble speech of his own, that “he never in his experience had met with one who ascended to such heart and soul-stirring eloquence; and when such was its effect in a language not his own, he could not help wishing he had witnessed the effect of Louis Kossuth’s patriotic appeals to his countrymen on the plains of Hungary.”
