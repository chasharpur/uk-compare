[GENIUS LOST. THE SORROWS PART EIGHT: WHITHER?]

How terrible is thy grandeur O Despair! How great is thy grim priviledge! No sleep! no rest!—’Tis now the dead of night, Sightless and solemn. One continuous cloud Of thickest texture bloats from east to west, 92 Like some huge breathing miscreation, charged With slumbrous will—a latent potency Ready to burst in thunder o’er the world! The Moon is in the ocean. Not a star Studs the vast cope of heaven—for that black Cloud Hangs from it now, in circuit, like a pall Funereal: its innumerable host Of sometime shining mysteries seeming all Quencht, and apparently as beingless, As do those countless myriads of mankind, Whose living souls out of the disc of Timepopulous campaigns 93 Looked glowing,—clustered over populous plains, And thronged in caravans, and martial camps, And the grey towery cities of the earth,— Long centuries ago. Full Silence broods With universal night-black wings outspread, Over the sleeping city—broods unruffled; Save while some steeple clock with sullen stroke Records the midnight hour.

Now is the time Supposed, when from the dreary vaults of Death Pale spectres rise, and striding on the blast, Lift in deep fitful moans and breathless shrieks Their fatal voices. Now’s the time too, when Those living ghosts, the broken hearted,—pale And desolate on the neglected couch Where sleep comes seldom, make their desperate plaint Secure from casual ears; and while they list The ghostly din without, feel at their heart A wild desire to join the viewless troop— 94 To be that shadowy incorporeal Thing The happy fear.

The night is drear indeed! But not to me—as being all devoid Of moment, such as once it might have held, When the mute slumbrous dome of Darkness gave A meet though solemn shelter to a soul Drows’d with sweet thought; for then I could rejoice In the return of light: but now the chords Of glad or strange surprise on which the change Of days and seasons struck, within that soul Lie broken all, and voiceless. Yea, to me What hath this night of dreariness or dread? To-morrow, let the sun shine ne’er so bright, The birds sing ne’er so blithely, and the flowers Bathe gladlier in the dews, and breathe abroad Unusual sweetness; let the freshest winds And greenest woods that ever tangled sounds 95 Into all harmonies, commingle near Star drops suspending, let the daintiest notes E’er flung by Eölus into the lap Of Silence, hover over,—seeming well Celestial anthems filtering from the skies;— And let the Spirit of the seasons too, On every sunny hill and valley, write In characters less mystic than his wont The promise of abundance; yet, Tomorrow Would be as drear to me!

For I have felt Out of the light and dark hours of the world An Influence pass—a Presence that was wont To robe me with a shadowy trust in days And nights to come; and ’mid the wants and cares Of this unyielding present, used to bring From out those future depths a smile wherewith To freshen this pale cheek,—a dream of love To still the ineffable yearnings of this heart Love-withering in its loneness! But ’tis gone 96a Forever! Could I fail even now to feel,— Now, through the medium of the thin dry air— The suffocating emptiness of each gust That hits my aching brow as I look out Into the dark of this so desolate night, Its absence? Or in the instinctive dread To meet the morrow in the stern cold face Of the so unfriendly world, which even now Presses around me like a tangible gloom, A deadly proclamation fail to read Of its departure from the pathless wild Of all my future life? And all in vain In the swift bark of retrospective thought, Far up the gleaming reaches of my soul I voyage—inland, where the mountains rise Of mystic wonder, holding springs that melt 154 Down From the white snows of the dateless Past, Voiceless and cold;—no comfort cometh thence, For Faith, as smitten through my mortal need, Is starving too, and the keen quietitude And cold-bright countenance of the Inane, But pinches her the more.

96b

Why should I live? Is not my portion servitude and rags? My doom, to slink about this excellent earth, Unknown, uncheered, untrusted and unloved? Whilst others, oft the vilest of her sons, Entrenched behind hereditary state And more hereditary impudence, Play the God o’er me!—yea, even while they stink 97 Within the nostrils of all temperate men, Worse than a pauper’s corpse of their abused And swoll’n mortality! while to the Angels And Eons of the spiritual world, The evil stench of their vice-sweltered lives Must reach beyond the moon!

But these extort My scorn, no more! and I, perhaps, by living, Could somewhat in my generation do To beat their impositions into dust, Or in the fiery crucible of a mind Inflamed beyond all mitigative means, Resolve them into air!

But ah! even they Who bear my name, as bound to me by ties Of kindred,—do I even to them confide My bolder thoughts, or my ideal draughts Of Man’s true magnitude; or some great hope Of what the wronged World yet may be, when roused By Truth, as by a trumpet, she shall start 98 Even like a wakened lion from the dark And nightmare ridden trance that she had known Of ages, shaking her Oppressions all Like hornets from her mane! when all that now Is darkly sacred held—the pious fraud That blinds with terror the bewildered minds Of millions, till they lick, like beaten hounds, The hand that fastens on their bended necks The chains of Superstition; chains at first Forged from old rhapsodies by her craftiest scribes The vulgar to enthrall, and strengthened since By the subjected to subject in turn— All this shall she laugh at, or desire no more As but the babble of her babyhood, And bury them with the Past!–but when to these ++ (Silly but mischevous, and thence full of wrong!) I thus confide my thoughts even they the while They seem to admire, half shudder; or they turn Looks of such strange conjecture hard upon me, Or of trust shadowed with such dubious heed As bringeth o’er my brain a wilder cloud Of lonely sorrow Of mental isolation.

Then to know Cold change in these, the daily falling off From the frank fond love of old times; to mark Their growing recklessness, by selfish deed And careless word displayed, of whether good Or ill betide one whom they yet will call A relative, and who loves them passing well— Ah, this bad knowledge like a loathsome snake Lies coiled about my heart!

And thus of some I loved forsaken, and in deep disgust Others forsaking,—desolate in soul I turn to ponder over what may be, Till my imagination drifts away In its own loneliness—far, far away Into a wide and melancholy vast Of wasteful wonder, as some storm-struck ship Abandoned of her crew, doth blindly roam O’er wide world of waves

They too,—they 100 At whose suggestion first, I strictly gave My fortunes to the thankless Muse, and whom The more to please, I struck with bolder hand Into the mightier meanings of the Lyre, Condemn its tones as far too thundrous now For their nice ears; the spirit it would start As all too stormy wing’d for their calm minds To follow in its flight! And there are some Who sought me when I cared not to be found And flattered forth my friendship—but to fall Coldly away—how soon! And why was this? I wanted but their friendship, not their aid! x I scorn the charity that can spend itself In bloodless succors such as beggars crave And never did aught vile that they should plead Dishonor, from the contact—yet they shun me, Or meet me with a frost upon their looks That freezes to my bones!

O be it told From thy unfathomable darkness, Night, Why were these burthens, huge as mountains, piled 101 Horribly up, and crushed upon my soul? And ye, mysterious voices of the blast, Why doth the ministry of Fate thus turn All sympathy away?

Oft have I seen A flower, as forewarned by the stilly touch Of Nature, fold against some coming blast Charged with the vapours of a blighting change, Its tender leaves; and can it be that thus Some instinct, or some fatal prescience, shuts Men’s hearts ‘gainst mine—open to all but it? Some feeling that within me there resides A direful influence on their household ties, Their worldly welfare, or those high-sown hopes That burn as fixt stars in the heaven of God? Could it be worse, even did my presence give A death-damp flavor to the ambient air? Funereal accents such as wail abroad From haunted caverns, to the passing breeze?—

102

Alas! these thoughts are storming all my soul With madness—yea, the madness of despair! And though my reason, lifting up its strength, As desperately confronts them, just as well Might he—the lonely cast away who stands, On some wild sea rock, dripping, seek to preach Obedience to the breakes surging round That perilous point, as I (in this wild gloom) Strive to subdue them:—and why should I strive? No: rather let them come and howl like wolves Within my failing brain! and knaw, and tug At my sick heart, their bitter food! for they Will help me sooner to my one desire, Which,—having nought to live for,—is to Die!

No dread prevents that wish! For I have made This many a day the bone-built door of Death 103 In prospect so familiar, that I feel Stung, as it were by some progres[s]ive law In nature, or by some occult ghostly lore, Despite of all its terror!

Yea, to pass At once into that shadowy aisle which leads To the great dome of Knowledge! Time and place With this soul-clogging, this corporeal frame, Could fool me then no more!—

And have I not The means at hand? What interposes?—Ah! ‘Twas madness!—but ’tis past! And now, whilst yet Life’s painful breath remains me, I will sing— Here, on this desolate death bed, will sing My proper Dirge, if but to shaddow forth The weary mystery that hath slain me thus, And give my being, through its heat, to slip More easy down the icy steep of Death!

I was weary, I was weary Of the day and of the night; They had swept to darkness and to death Each vision of delight: The day was but a charnel grown Where Love and Fancy shrouded lay; In the still night my soul had heard Young Hope arise and flee away.

I was weary, of all around me And weary of all above; Of the rising and the setting sun, And the moon’s wild look of love: The sunny stream—the wild bird’s song— The breezy voice of the moonlit tree, But swell’d me haunting stave of Fate,— “The wide world hath no place for thee!”

106

I was weary, of hope, and weary of joy And weary Ev’n of woe; Of every decency of life— Of every friend and foe: Much less My body alone shall be Among the white worms of the tomb, Than my wild spirit was ever Even in my childhood’s home.

O so weary! O so weary Of all beneath the sun! So weary, weary, weary! My race of life is run. O for a grave some brook beside, Or under a weeping willow tree: O for a grave on some wild cliff That hangeth o’er the sounding sea.
