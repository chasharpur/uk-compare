Whither?

How terrible is thy grandeur, O Despair! How great is thy grim privilege! For he Who knows thee, in his gloomy heart can laugh At all the happy dread. What thought need he Acknowledge of the Earthquake that upheaves The continent beneath him, with a sway Likest the boundless impulse of the main, What time the inclement spirit of the poles Drives his black herd of bellowing Tempests forth? No more hath he to do with place. The storm That makes the hall of heaven one chaos dire Of dreadful glooms & rending sounds, deals down No bolt that might inflict a harm on him. The wild remorseless deep, when rushing loud Through all its shaken confines, with a wrath Which isles submerged, and stranded navies crunched Within its hungry and wide foaming jaws, Memorise many an age, no more alarm Can challenge in his self-abandoned soul Than might some babbling brook. When armies meet In thunder, littering all the field around With dead and dying men, the unholy scene May bear not on its blasted breast a sight 301 Fearful to him. Or did he for wild sport, Upon his throne defy the Russian Czar— Or him who lords it in that Moslem hold, The city once of Constantine the Great, Still were he safe from aught of farther ill As if he filled his grave. He hath forgot All taste of fear—the faculty hath lost To feel vicissitude; events to him, Whether by Fortune brightened or begrimed, Being hueless as the tide of Time itself On which they hurry past. Even such art thou, Despair, and such I feel thee at my heart, World-vast and dread!

‘Tis now the dead of night, Lightless and solemn. One continuous cloud Of thickest texture bloats from east to west, Like some huge breathing miscreation, charged With slumbrous will—a latent potency Ready to burst in thunder o’er the world. The moon is in the ocean. Not a star Studs the vast cope of heaven—for that black Cloud Hangs from it now, in circuit, like a pall Funereal: its innumerable host Of sometime shining mysteries seeming all Quenched, and apparently as beingless, As those death-clouded myriads of mankind, Whose living souls out of the disc of Time Looked glowing,—clustered over populous plains, And thronged in caravans, and martial camps, And the grey towery cities of the world, Long centuries ago. Full silence broods Over the sleeping city—broods unruffled; Save while some steeple clock with sullen stroke Records the midnight hour.

Now is the time Supposed, when from the dreary vaults of death Pale spectres rise, and striding on the blast, Lift in deep fitful moans and breathless shrieks Their fatal voices. Now’s the time too, when Those living ghosts, the broken-hearted,—pale And desolate on the neglected couch Where sleep comes seldom, make their desperate plaint Secure from casual ears; and while they list The ghostly din without, feel thrilling forth A wild desire to join the viewless troop— To be that shadowy incorporeal thing The happy fear.

The night is drear indeed! But not to me—I mean, it is devoid Of moment, such as once it might have held, When the mute slumbrous dome of darkness gave A meet though solemn shelter to a soul Drowsed with sweet thought; for then I could rejoice In the return of light. But now the chords Of glad or strange surprise on which the change Of days and seasons struck, within that soul Lie broken all, and voiceless. Yea, to me What hath this night, in aspect though as grim Even as it might be if it were indeed The last of Time, enshrouding a dead world Drifting unsphered—yet, what to me, I say, Hath it of singular dreariness or dread? To morrow, let the sun shine ne’er so bright, The birds sing ne’er so blithely, and the flowers Bathe gladlier in the dews than ever yet Since Paradise at the first touch of Morn Woke smiling, and up breathed in its bright face Unparagoned sweetness; let the freshest winds 303 And greenest woods that ever tangled sounds Into wild harmony, commingle near; And like a shower of tiny stars—or like Celestial anthems filtering from the skies, In the winds’ lulls, let the most dainty notes Ere flung by Eölus into the lap Of Silence, greet me, though but heard in thought; And let the Spirit of the season, too, On every hill and every valley write In characters less dubious than his wont, A promise of abundance;—yet, To-morrow Would be as drear to me! For I have felt Out of the light and dark hours of the world An Influence pass—a Presense that was wont To robe me with a shadowy trust in days And nights to come; and ’mid the wants and cares Of the unyielding present,—as the dove Of promise, brought, returning to the Ark, A fresh-plucked olive branch,—did use to bring From out those future depths some hope wherewith To color this pale cheek;—some dream of love To still the ineffable yearnings of a heart Else withering in its loneness! But ’tis gone For ever! Could I fail even now to feel— Now, through the medium of the thin dry air— The suffocating emptiness of each gust That hits my aching brow as I look out Into the dark of this so desolate night, Its absence? Or, in the instructive dread To meet the morrow in the stern cold face Of the so unfriendly world, that even now Presses around me like a tangible gloom, A deadly proclamation fail to read Of its departure from the pathless wild Of all my future life?

And all in vain In the swift bark of introspective thought, Far up the gleaming reaches of my soul I voyäge—inland, where the mountains rise Of mystic wonder, holding springs that melt Down from the white snows of the dateless Past, Voiceless and chill;—no comfort cometh thence, For Faith, as smitten through my mortal need, Is starving too, and the keen quietude And cold-bright countenance of the Inane, But pinches her the more.

Why should I live? Is not my portion servitude and rags? My doom, to slink about this excellent earth Unknown, uncheered, untrusted and unloved? Whilst others, oft the vilest of her sons, Entrenched behind hereditary state, And more hereditary impudence, Play the God o’er me!—yea, even while they stink Worse than a pauper’s corpse of their diseased And swollen mortality! while to the Angels And Eons of the spiritual world The evil stench of their polluted lives, With deadly odours of the soul that fume Like the miasma of a charnal forth, Must reach beyond the moon!

But these extort My scorn,—no more! and I, perhaps, by living, Could somewhat in my generation do To beat their impositions into dust, Or in the fiery crucible of a mind Inflamed beyond all tolerance of the arts Of stablished Pomp,—all mercy,—all remorse,— Resolve them into air!

But ah! even those Who know me best, and thence to me were bound Once by a sympathy of spirit, yet More deeply fixed, and stronger, than the tie Of kindred,—do I even to them confide My bolder thoughts, or my ideal drafts Of Man’s true magnitude; or some great hope Of what this wrong world yet may be, when roused By Truth, as by a trumpet, she shall start Even like a wakened lion from the dark And nightmare-ridden trance that she hath known Even from the youth of Time, and with a bound And mighty roar, her vile oppressions shake Like vermin from her mane! When all that now Is darkly sacred held—the pious fraud That blinds with terror the bewildered minds Of myriads, till they lick, like beaten hounds, The hand that fastens on their bended necks The chains of superstition; chains at first Forged from old rapsodies by some crafty scribe The vulgar to enthrall, and strengthened since By the subjected to subject in turn— All this,—the babble of her drunken dream, Silly, but mischievous, and thence full of wrong,— Shall be her laughter, while her aim shall be To bury it with the Past!—But when to these I thus confide my thoughts,—even they, the while They seem to admire, half shudder; or they turn Looks of such strange conjecture hard upon me, Or of trust shadowed with such dubious heed, As bringeth o’er my soul a wilder cloud Of wintry sorrow, and a keener sense Of mental isolation.

Ah! to know 306 The heart’s cold change, the daily falling off From the fond frank love of old times; to mark A growing recklessness, in selfish deed And careless word displayed, of whether good Or ill betide one whom men yet will call A brother, and who loves them passing well— Ah, this bad knowledge like a loathsome snake Lies coiled about my heart!

Yes; even they At whose suggestion first, I strictly gave My fortunes to the Muse, and whom the more To please, I struck with an embolden’d hand Into the mightier meanings of the lyre, Condemn its tones as far too thundrous now For their nice ears; the spirit it would start As all too stormy wing’d for their calm minds To follow in its flight! And there are some Who sought me when I cared not to be found And flattered forth my friendship—but to fail Coldly away—how soon! And why was this? I wanted but their counsel, not their aid! I scorn the charity that spends itself In bloodless succors such as beggars crave, And never did aught vile even to the vile Such as might yield them the poor right to plead Dishonor from the contact:—yet they shun me, Or meet me with a frost upon their looks That freezes to my bones; or else evokes A pang within me of unspeakable scorn That burns my vitals, like a deadly poison Running through every vein,—or, if I know Another feeling, it is shame for them, Not for myself—or at the very worst Shame for our common kind.

And thus of some I loved, forsaken, and in deep disgust Others forsaking,—desolate in soul I ponder what hath been, as leading on To some finality—some last result; 307 It yet shall largely profit me that thus Matters evolve; seeing my habits thence Are shaken from their once tenacious hold Of time and sensual things.—And come thou meek Religious trust, that sometimes to my soul Fliest friendly, like a heaven-descended dove With wings that whisper of the peace of God; Come, and assure it now, that all thus seen Of evil, by the patience of the One Almighty Master of the universe, Is but allowed to dash our vain repose On Time’s foundations, and all mad belief In human consequence; that finally, Amid the death of expectations fond, Discoveries diurnal that the pomps And pleasures of the world, are but bright mists Concealing ’mid its heights of glory and fame Its depths of degradation;—that all weal, And beauty and peace, even in their permanence, Are but the florid riches of a soil That crusts the cone of some yet masked volcano, Whose darkling fires but wait the dread command: “Up to the work appointed!”—we at length, Even thus admonished, thus in hope and heart Subdued and chastened, might be so constrained To look between the thunder-bearing clouds That darken over this mysterious ball’s Blind face, for surer, better things beyond Its flying scenes of doubtful good, commixed With evident evil: Yea, conclude at last That whereso in the universe of God Our better home may be, it is not here: Then here why build we?

Yes, thus let me learn 308 To school my stricken heart; and with an eye Towärds contentment in this troublous life, From by-gone disappointment strive to draw A rigid satisfaction, while I deem These loftier moods of thought, for all the waste Of uninformëd Hope, and all the dreams Of a too wild and venturous Fancy, blenched By the stern strictures of unsparing Time, Meet compensations—yea, their proper fruit, Though forced, and moral end.

O then farewel Fancy and Hope, twin Angels of the Past! Thee, Fancy, chiefly, as of my younger life The spiritual spouse,—farewel! with all Thy pictured equipage: the shapes sublime Of universal liberty and right, Dethroning tyrants and investing worth Alone with power and honor; and with these Those visions that come shining to the heart Like evening stars from a serener air, Of generosity in rapture high At rival excellence; of charity Living in secret for her own sweet sake; Of mercy lifting up a fallen foe; Of pity yearning o’er the child of shame; Unselfish love, and resolute friendship,—all, Even to common trust,—farewel! These lights May never burn in the grey dome of Time Or constellate for me the world again! No more! no, never more!

Ah! I am sad: Yet, if my spirit I explore aright, Gainfully sad; for I do feel succeed 309a Some freshness of the heart—some breezy freshness Breathed from the memory of my earlier days, Albeit Regret moves like a ghost within Their twilight borders, beckoning me with looks That glisten through their visionary tears. Thus a lone traveller over barren hills, When wearily resting on some time-worn bank By the wayside, with retrospective gaze Calls up the flowery valleys he hath passed; And catching from the gale thence flowing, some Memorial sense of fragrance, he at last Arises heartened to pursue his way, Though still regretting that each onward step But casts between him and their green delight A lengthened desolation.—I am sad.
