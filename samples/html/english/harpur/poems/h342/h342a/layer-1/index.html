The Olden Warrior.

Note.—The above was the Title of my earliest attempt at story telling in verse. On completing the poem, and being somewhat disatisfied with the subject, I threw the [unclear] manuscript by, amongst other neglected papers—where, having remained uninspected for several years, it became greatly tattered and de- -faced by insects. On a reperusal, however, of the legible remains of this old performance, many of them appeared to me to possess considerable merit:— still, had I even leisure (which I have not) to re-write what is missing, I am doubtful whether I could now bring myself to consider the subject worthy of the effort. And such passages, therefore, as are entire, or nearly so, are given simply as fragments in the consecutive order in which I find them; in the belief that, upon the whole, they are worthy of preservation; and to show moreover, that I have been anything but an idler, from the first, in the thankless service of the Muse. C.H.

Fragment I.—Canto I.

Now shall the bold Invaders bleed! For Egbert hath mounted his night-black steed, Whose nostrils are stretching and quivering wide, As bursting they were with his tameless pride: Pride headlong as the mood, The rapture of a torrent flood, When snorting up with gathered force On some ruining dam that hath vexed its course— High into whirling ridges thrown, A moment—then it thunders on.

‘Tis well the hand is both skilful and strong, That now graspeth his reins as he leaps along! For lo, his wild eyes in their stormy ire Seem struggling to emit their fire: Then softened, in heed of his rider they turn; 22 Then timidly shrink, and then madly burn; Till mingled in them seem to be Rage, terror, rapture, and agony.

As under the tightened rein He plunges on with unslackening strain, The efforts of that curbëd pace Implies a speed that never lay In courser of mortal race: Thus, riding in some open bay, The anchored galleon’s lunging sway,— Whilst the wind is sweeping o’er it, And the waves burst up before it,— Seems to boast, if loosed, a might To cleave the deep with the speed of light.

Or as a burst of harmony Just as it begins to be, Seems strengthening in a wild intent To overleap all government, And thus the listening ear doth teach To dream the bliss it may not reach: So, who in curbed career yon steed Might view, nor measureless fancy his speed?

And fair his rider is As aught beneath the boundless sky, Save the infant’s smile of bliss, And the love of woman’s eye. And he is hier to many a name That was with glory—is with fame. Concentred in his noble face Beam the bright honors of his race; And what a nice chivalric grace The elegance of his spirit lends To his tapering bust and plumëd head, As sideward now he bends, Moodily, as ‘twere to read The sable volume of his steed.

23

And doth not in his eye appear The dewy sheen of a thoughtful tear? And with that look o’er his lofty brow The shade of a painful memory now, Pass? as a wandering cloud is seen The sun and some harvest field between, Throwing o’er its valued cheer A passing gloom unseemly there: And doth not the wave of a sigh inflate The jointed scales of his bright breast-plate?

Even so: for that steed bore his sire on the day, When like a spent meteor dissolving away, He blazed his last in the whirl of the fray; And then, although madding with wounds and unreined, Even then to desert his fallen lord he disdained: But while to the groans of the wounded behind, The wild glee of Conflict came back on the wind— While succouring columns, in ruinous might, And insensate as torrents, rushed by to the fight, He stood sternly at guard by the dying brave, And pawed out in part his battle-field grave: But the hours of three seasons since then have o’errun, And forgetting the sire, he is strode by the son.

II.

Far along yon woody height The Vassals are grouping in warlike plight. Brightly brown their weapons gleam In the young sun’s slanting beam As he redly rises still Over the highest eastern hill; To cast at length a steadier glare Down on the mustering movements there Of many Warriors—old and young, Yeoman and serf; and these among, Many a rugged mountaineer, Nameless, or bearing names of fear: W[unclear] Wild dwellers of the secret cave Where storms are brewed and snow floods rave; 24 And spectres shriek in the fog-shroud grey, And wizards banish half the day; While on each moon-frequented height Lean wolves howl through the lengthened night.

As eagles suddenly appear What time the chase hath slain a deer; As boding ravens, one by one, Post for some scene of murder done, These ever from their mountain home Against a rumoured battle come; Oft richly clad, as some aver, In garb of missing traveller: But what their calling, whence they are, None question on the edge of war, For they have souls that love to know The presence of a costly foe; And promissed but free chance of spoil, They’ll rush into the deadliest broil: Then brandished blade and high tossed targe Suddenly signal their howling charge; As lightnings in the louring cloud Foreflash the thunder, following loud: And faultless conflict they must wage Who turn the tempest of their rage.

III.

How strongly in that sudden camp, The various characters there thrown Are in their several actions shown. The old in earnest speech appear, The reckless rudely jesting near; The saturnine affect command; The cruel are whetting the flashing brand; The ardent, with impatient heed, Stand ready, each one near his steed, Who snorteth forth the morning air Like smoke, and gazes from the crowd With mad conjecture in his stare; The gentle, in abstracted mood, Are sauntering through the underwood That in many a tangled lair, Fantastic, clouds the hillocks, where 25 Mingles with the bristly broom Each musing Warrior’s nodding plume, And in distressful tone is heard The timid cry of the startled bird: But boon with timeless glee, and loud In word though impotent in deed, The trifling form a special crowd;— By gaudy garb or restive steed Hither and thither swayed, they breed A coil; and turned with prep nice To vague impertinent advice, In vain you’d baulk them, even with jeers; For still they’ll turn to lay a slice Upon more passive ears.

IV.

But what doeth Egbert now away From his warriors all in prompt array? He should not seek at a time like this The Bower in yonder vale, I wis.

Goeth he not to the trysting place Of a Chief’s daughter—the Lady Grace: There to part in hope and love, And soothe the fears of that gentle dove, Who waits him in the myrtle Bower Trellised round yon bathing tower That glooming o’er the garden stream Its guardian Genius well may seem, As it ever flashes by Like lightning in a vernal sky, And, taught to gush in mazes rare, Pours its song of murmurs there.

Never the breeze, like a sweet-breatht lover, A fairer Bower hath lingered over! And never was there costlier gem In an Eastern diadem— Never did one more radiant gleam In the sun’s mine-searching beam, Than now that Bower’s the casket of— The breathing gem of Beauty’s love.

Its fruitful festoons, moving, play Like blushes o’er the cheek of day. And the path which leads to its recess A stranger’s feet were swift to miss, It being so wayward, and so green 26 With velvet grass, though shaven clean; And rarest flowers so thickly gem Its borders and hang over them. How blissfully and frequent glows The dew-anointed rose, Where, bending downward, it is met By the dreaming violet; And oft, with these, the pink, and bell So bashful, seem a sisterhood Of many-featured Maids, that dwell Together for their good: Or gaudier flowers, like stars, are strewed Brightly along—or onward still, Cups and lilies mingled, [unclear] ray Like a little milky way.

V.

And wot ye not that fair Lady hath long Been loved by the Chief of yon armëd throng; And that now for the last time they sever Ere their young lives shall be mingled forever In one bright current, like a river; So Peace, returning, shall attest She takes no coward to her breast? But were lord Egbert one, disgrace Now first had stained his ancient race! Even Envy herself dares not opine Such curse might cross its lofty line; For ‘twere as though some planet’s ray Could as a meteor’s waste away, And leave upon its once-bright track The night of horror, doubly black! And woe were his, whatever his name Who but hinted the chance of such dastardly blame; For who can poise the lance, or wield Like Egbert the brand in the listed field? And who may list to his lofty sense, Nor carry a lay of valor thence? And who may study his stately air, Nor find his father’s spirit there?

But hath not her sire, Sir Harold, sworn That deeds of the battle field only shall earn His blue-eyed Dove with the light-like air, And lips than the rarest of rubies more rare, And tempting as cherries new-pluckt from the bough And moist with the health of the morning air; The pensive alabaster brow, The cheek of rose and neck of snow; 27 The peerless passion-breathing charms That swell o’er the fold of her ivory arms; The nymphic waist—the fairy foot So elegantly mute, That by its fall on hardest grain Silence never lost her reign.

VI.

Like the Moon in a palace of cloud, Shining through the leafy shroud Of that fair Bower, sweet Grace is seen To welcome now her Lover, proud Of boon so rich I ween. Like the strongly coming Sun, As now he meets that moon-like one, His beamy armour glints and gleams In checquers through the viny tresses That wanton there in all the excesses Of Nature’s solitary dreams, When first her earnest soul doth start To the thrilling and passionate kiss of Art, And weds the order which, in part, She yet for Beauty’s sake transgresses.

VII.

“Before I knew thee, Egbert, when First venturing in the ways of men, Being a lone and only child, Fond hopes and wishes, many and wild, Desturbed me, yet uncaptured dwelt Apart from those who to my beauty knelt. Yes; wilder than a sister‘s could, Mine longed,—or heart more practised should, For One in whom it might confide The hidden treasure of its love And all its visionery pride— One whose full-measuring worth might move Reason to smile on Fancy’s blindness, And help to ope each source of kindness: But turning from each Suitor rude, It sickened o’er its solitude, And mourned its wasted feelings, cast Like perfumes on the barren blast.

“Then Egbert, thee, without a peer, I met, and thou becam’st thus dear! 28 The dearer for the loveless way My heart, until that happy day, Had come in its so lonely dream: As to the bird all night astray Between a dim and dreary sky And the great sea’s dull-gleaming eye, The day-star’s shore-revealing beam Is dearer for the late distress Of its lengthened hopelessness.

“Yet would I hold this love my shame— Yea, make some cloister its cold tomb, Ere it should wile thee from thy fame, Or bind my Warrior to his home, When boastful foes are in the field, And every noble Youth should wield A sword, his Father Land to save, Or on its frontier find a grave!

“Then go—and if a Maiden’s pr, Insphiring prayer, be heard on high, Fortune’s own hand shall grave thy name Upon the storied shield of Fame, Nor shalt thou, Egbert, die.”

VIII.

At last he hath bidden adieu to his love, Who hath prayed for him to the Power above; And a severe yet grateful sorrow Dims his eye and clouds his brow, As he moves away from that Lady now. His unmail’d hand is playing thorough The glossy folds of his charger’s mane, And he bows his head in gentle pain.

“Why doth he thus inactive stand? Surely enquire his restless band. But should he have left so quickly one So loving and loved—who all undone Must be if fates adverse ordain Them never to meet again? Should he not return and take One more adieu for pity’s sake?”

More time than seemly might he lose Dallying with such amorous muse, Were not—hark! to his ear conveyed By the breezes hurrying by, Bursts of martial minstrelsy, 29 From where within the oaken shade The Bards are met—Enough! his eye Beams but heroic ardour now! Strong purpose only sterns his brow! His charger, mounted unaware, With an unsteady, astonished air, Plunges under his gleaming form; As some tall vessel, black as night, Staggers on beneath the might Of a terribly beautiful storm.

IX. Call of the Bards.

The Foe hath bared his brand, And stretched it out o’er native Land! His banners beat our native air! The print of his foot is in our sand! His tents on our hills appear! Then son of the valley and child of the hill, Hither in yon vengence throng! Hither every valiant will, Every arm that’s strong!

Rise as the waves of Ocean rise, To the challenging tempest’s greeting! Gather as torrents gather, In the valley of waters meeting!

X.

All along that woody height Late straggling groups together rush: Even as instant lightning crouds In fragments from the broken clouds, From every clump of brush Late hidden warriors spring With furbished weapons glittering.

Hither and thither those armëd men Still hurry; till all are mounted—then, With rapid wheel the outer groups Rush into close and ordered troops;— These simultaneously are thrown Into the main and central one, Which staggereth upon the eye Into regularity.

30

As from their clouded fountains The waters of the mountains, With gathered swell at length o’erflow The marges of some lake below, Then burst, with oceanic song, Irresistibly along: So with a thronging din, at first, Of arms and iron shodden feet, And then one forward-sweeping [unclear] burst, Like thunder, that chivalric call Those banded warriors meet.

XI.

Hark, the earth doth shudder and grate Under the closing columns’ weight! Louder and louder is heard afar The Battle’s ebbing rush and meeting jar! Mingled shrieks and threatenings high, And the horrid mirth of the riderless courser, Mix in the dun depths of the sky Hoarser and hoarser! The everlasting hills Ring as before they never rang! Their hollow rocky eaves And solitary caves A thunderous murmur fills! Where the grey precipices overhang, The eagle, sitting far and lone, Affrighted hears—from the worn stone Redoubling—the tremendous clang— The shout—the yell—the groan!

XII.

But too long ‘twere to tell in this passing lay All Egberts glorious feats that day— How many a knight of mightiest deed Was left to die in the track of his steed! For still, the louder the turmoil rung— The thicker the arrows and javelins sung— The fiercer the foe and the stouter his blow More daringly proud did that Warrior grow!

But worthy the theme be y my song, Its numbers full-flowing and free, 31 Whenever to tell my glad task it may be The wished return of the Hero ere long: When sung to the Harp shall be his name, As a word from the mouth of victorious Fame

Fragment XIII.—Canto II. Song of Triumph.

Throned in his Sire’s triumphant car, We sing our glorious Hero home! He cometh—Victory’s morning star Arisen from the Battle’s gloom: Where Valor urged so well The thunderbolt of war, That strongest foes might not repel Its ruin—they fled or fell!

Glory bent her iris o’er him, Terror stalked in gloom before him, As he charged the Foe! So, o’erarched with skiey flashes, Led by the storm, some torrent dashes From its mountain source, and crashes All it meets below.

The Invaders looked—and saw him come, Then heard Destruction yell their doom! The base but thought of how to fly— The noble how they best might fly; Since naught was left their broken might But a stern death or shameful flight! And peace be to the foeman brave Whose choice was then—a grave.

XIV.

By mighty Love impelled, At once from the triumphal car Sprang he, when his Lady love afar. His eager eyes beheld! Gazing she was from the Castle tower, Expectant of their meeting hour. Arrayed in robes of lustrous white, She glistened in the sunbeam, proud 32 Of the beauty its own warm beauty avowed, Like some lone and holy Spright Beckoning happy souls that way; Or like the Moon, as on the grey Hill top she sits in a shining cloud.

XV.

On Love’s re-union only bent, Away on his night-black steed he went, Fixt on the Castle tower his eye! Away from Victory’s pageantry Faster and faster [unclear] doth he ride! And though many a steep may lie, Between With many a perilous break, between, And though the ford is rapid and wide, Few drops of time shall fall, I ween, Before he alights at his Charmer’s side.

XVI.

“He comes—he comes at last!” she cries, “Then why do tears thus fill mine eyes? All my thoughts are such as please— My heart is happy—why not at ease? He comes—the deep ravine is past! He comes—but I thought his steed more fast! Say, Maidens, what is it irks me so? And why does my sight thus come and go? Yet mind not the tears that are in mine eye— Ask not the reason—I know not why.”

XVII.

The Castle hall is crowded now, And the tones of the harp are swelling high! But clouds are on each Warrior’s brow, And tears in every Warrior’s eye! For first, each fallen Clansman’s name The Bards would shrine in songs of flame: Such wildly earnest rhyme, As sounding forward, fiery-free, 33 So reaches o’er the lapse of Time The echoes of posterity! Telling of those, their Country’s pride, Who for her weal had nobly died. But thus, with longest labored strain, They sing the mightiest of the slain.

“In the misty ear Of the Angel of War, His Shade ascending from the battle-grave, Now in the region of mid air First viewing, stretched in prospect fair, The Land of the departed brave, May turn awhile well pleased, to hear The Dirge that we are pouring here.

“Think, that on yon sheeny cloud Which, like a silvery shroud, Hovers in the gale high blowing, His passing Spirit may be knowing Delight even from the grateful stave We now are lifting to its brave Departed merit! Who thirsteth not such worth to have? Who would not Danger’s worst defy, Like him, although like him to die, Such fame forever to inherit? Hark! Echo starting from her grot, Questions round her—Who would not?”

XVIII.

The wine is poured in the festive bowl— Gloom hath forsaken every soul— Mirth is bright in every ey— Glad are the strains of the minstrelsy— And softly sitting, side by side, Behold the Bridegroom and the Bride.

All that amorous Bards have taught— All romantic fancies, fraught With passion, have of Love conceived, Or virgin hoped, or wife believed, Was in that Bridegroom’s tender look When by her side his seat he took. And though her dove-like eyes appear Struggling to withhold a tear; And though her timid breast is panting ‘Neath her gemm’d array, 34 Like a gentle billow, mantling Under the sunny robe of day; What is there in those eyes exprest But earnest love—its source that breast? Else in her looks one may but read A delicate attempt to plead In bland excuse for her meek distress, A fond excess of happiness.

Hark, they whisper through the throng. “Poorly hath praised her every song! For who may turn their eyes away From hers, nor find a darkened ray Radiate from each Maiden, who, Till then, seemed Beauty’s brightest hue? Of all that’s elegantly light— All that’s beautifully bright— All that’s excellently rare, And inevitably fair And sweet in Woman, is not she An exquisite epitome: As Paradise is understood From ancient story to have been A living abstract of each good Else strewn o’er this now mortal scene?” Thus talked of her the festive throng, Till high arose the Bridal Song.

XIX.

Night sits supreme in the broad sky, And the stars are beginning their dance on high The Moon from her accustomed height, Swells large and round upon the eye, And throws a faint light On the skirt of the night, And a gleam on the leaf as you pass it by: Or by some driving cloud Obscured as with a gauzy shroud, Her outline, there detected still Seems a spectral wheel on the lonely hill, Imparting to the darkened scene A wild mysterious mien.

35

But turn thee and behold; fast by The lighted Hall glows blazefully. From each arched casement, opened wide, Flows music in a thrilling tide; And a thousand tapers strike the eye, And the flame of the jewel, goes [unclear]ing by On Beauty’s And through the which is seen to bloom The nuptial wreath—to nod the plume; And a thousand tapers strike the eye, And the flame of the jewel, dancing by On Beauty’s brow as she swims along In the wayward whirl of the tripping throng.

Such scene is beautifully rife With memories that last for life. Flitting form, and strain, and beam Are there, as in some fairy-dream. And to the distant one, between Darking weaving wood, May seem, thus blazing on his sight, A lantern huge in the hand of Night.

XX.

But before that planet bright Had flecked the east with drops of light, In a faint and fainter key The minstrelsy had died away; By slow decrease the guests had gone, The lights had faded one by one, See such as glad As bedward reeled the bacchanals; And darkness, as a curtain falls, Had gathered round those ancient walls; And bliss had crowned the Warrior tried, In the ivory arms of his deed-won Bride.

Postscript.

In the prefatory Note, I speak of the subject of the foregoing Fragments, as somewhat unworthy of the effort it would now cost me to re-con- -nect them as a whole, by reproducing, as nearly as possible, what is missing; and certainly, the celebration of war preparations and battle deeds, irrespectively of that which should alone sanction them, a just cause, were both thoughtless and immoral in any poet of the nineteenth century, having the slightest pretentions to philosophy. True, such a cause seems to have been the favored one of the poem; and yet, judging from remains of it, the spirit of the whole must have been, withal, too merely warlike. The cause, which should have been its main argument, seems only to have been touched upon incidentally, and for the sole and extrin- -sic purpose of making the personal prowess and individual fortunes of its Hero the more interesting to the reader. And it is of this, as a philosopher, that I now disapprove.

Still I am aware of the propriety of qualifying any disapproval of the kind, in its personal bearing, considered nationally. We should be sedulous to strip War of its factitious glories, but we should be careful, 37 at the same time, not to cut too emascu- -latively into the combattive instincts of our nature. We must not “go-a-head” too fast even in this direction. These instincts are only to be corrected with reference to the past, and more wisely restricted as to the future—not prematurely obliterated; seeing that so long as the national systems of the world continue as rivalously constituted as at present, so long at least will there be occasional clashes of interests amongst them, involving finally an appeal to the sword; and that, in the event of such an appeal having to be made, any very great disparity of personal courage and warlike prowess among the belligerants, however superinduced, were clearly such a political deficiency as nothing else could supply, with a promptitude requisite to the national integrity of one or more of them—and consequentially, perhaps of all. And this state-system of things, subject of course to many progressive modifications,—but in the main the same, will no doubt continue for several centuries to come.

I have as little doubt, however, but that there will be a stage in human progress, at which War will have become morally impossible. This may be easily made 38 to appear feasible. Suppose a period when all men shall be educated to that degree (only) which at present gives ordinary weight to any individual as an authority in morals and politics —Thus educated, all men will be thinkers; and in that case, the judgment of the many tending, as it always does towards the truth, will sufficiently negative or correct the perversions and misconceptions of the few. Hence, upon any national question arising which may involve the [unclear] possibility of War, the idea that the sword can only establish the fact, that it may be more dextriously wielded by one man (the agressor perhaps) than by another, will have become so universal as inevitably to deter the popular interests on all hands from consenting to its arbitrament; and the several Governments therefore, will be necessitated to devise together, upon all such occasions, some legal process for the international adjustment of their differences—unless indeed, upon their being thus left in the lurch by the moral sense of the people, they shall choose rather to turn their cabinet ministers into “bold dragoons”, and get up amongst themselves and 39 on their own account, a bloody cam- -paign in little, to the infinite pity and wonderment of those up Utopian times.

The “Olden Warrior”,—being the work of a mere boy,—was obnoxious, I doubt not, to many objections besides that alleged against it above—which indeed is a moral and not a critical one. Whether the conduct of the story was well managed or otherwise, cannot now be ascertained; but it strikes me, from what I recollect of it, to have been rather a loose affair in that respect. Neither was the story referable, it would seem, to any age or country otherwise than through the general character of its circumstances—a defect in itself of magnitude enough to have been fatal to its artistical pretentions had it appeared before the reader as an original whole. In its fragmental state then, it is luckier; seeing that it will therefore be arraignable at the bar of Criticism upon purely natural grounds only. That is, if its pictures, thus rendered unamenable as to subject, are yet found to possess in themselves sufficient verisimlitude 40 as to effect, they will thereupon be entitled to a poetical acquittal—if to nothing besides.

Of the Versification I would say a word or two. It is, I think, almost the best thing about it. Its irregu- larities are designed, not involuntary. They are slidden into and out of without abruptness. In short, its character is similar to that of my next earliest production of any length—the Kangaroo Hunt: and its peculiarities once mastered or gotten familiar to, it will be found, I flatter myself, both consonant to its subject and agreeable in itself. Such, at all events, is my own opinion of its merit, on thus tenta- -tively reviewing it by the additional light which a prolonged course of study has thrown over the subject of English Versification in all its varieties.

C.H.
