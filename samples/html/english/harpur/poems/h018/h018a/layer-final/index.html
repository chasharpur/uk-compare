AN AUSTRALIAN SONG, BY C. H.

I.

Australia, how rapt can I wind by thy fountains, Or view thy wild scenes that spread far on my sight, And rise in my love as I rise on thy mountains, Where the proud noble eagle doth love to alight? And where’s the Australian, who, without emotion, Can view thy wild scenes—grandest isle in the ocean— But feel that he loves thee,—Australia, huzza!

II.

And oft as I muse on thy surge-sounding shores, Thro’ the night of the future my fancy will roam— I see the sun’s beams light a day far before us— And walk in the blaze of thy glory to come. And then from thy dark cliffs, that seduceth the ocean, I pay thee a visit of heartfelt devotion, And sing as I wander—Australia, huzza!

III.

Be proud of thy sons; for should war e’er invade thee, Mark!—the nations shall know them as dreadful to foes; And thy daughters—for they with bright charms have arrayed thee— As the rose is the pride of the bough when it grows. Not a flower in thy garden of beauty is wanting, For aught that could render the female enchanting May be found in thy fair ones—Australia, huzza!
