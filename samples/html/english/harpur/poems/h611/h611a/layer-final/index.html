II.

To Henry Parkes on re-reading his Sonnet beginning “Who would not be a Poet?”

“Who would not be a Poet?”—thus I read In thy proud Sonnet, my harmonious Friend; And then the streamy lines still led My utterance on—but at the end The question made me ponder, and let pass The Poet; seeing that for aye, alas! A Man of Sorrows he is doomed to be. But thus far my assent was given: There is not under the dome of heaven Aught happier in itself than the witch Poesie.

What is not spell-touched by her winged Powers? See those happy creatures, Flowers, 12 Even where they sweetest be Blest into beauty by the genial Hours— There as they breathe in bliss away Their soul of fragrance, should but we In being verse, are not even they Then sweeter for the breath of rapturous Poesie?

Yea, Love itself is earthy, till betimes, Upon the bosom of her mystery We lay it, like a wayward child, Hushing its nature wild With fancy-honied but truth-tempered rhymes,, Distilled from all things sweet that be, Wrought from all that most sublimes In Nature, whom herself, we see Happiest Smile when her music chimes With the music of passionate poesie

But “who’d not be a Poet?”—there I pause Forebodingly; because, To see “all beauty with his gifted sight,” To love like him with all the soul, To be when life is morning-bright The very Creature of Delight— Delight beyond control! Is still to be In a like degree, To sensitive of Misery apart And loss and slight and all the weeping shapes of dole.

There was an earnest-thoughted Boy, that still Had to himself proposed thy question, till His swelling heart and throbbing head Flowed with the tunefulness he coveted. He loved to stray alone, this studious Creature, Yet was not solitary, but apart; For to his eye and ear and heart, Each Aspect, tone, and tenet of Nature, Was still a grand, or sweet, or lovely feature; And, temperingly with these, 13 Her veriest simplicities, Even with his body’s growth, nourished his spirit’s stature.

And thus accomplished only, to the fight Of Life’s dishonest battle did he come, To meet instead of Valor mean Despite; To see unblushing Fortune doom To some repute unholy, Or to some miserable estate, All such as would not trample on the lowly And glorify the falsely great; Or for a hireling’s sordid pay Defend the oppressor and belie the oppressed: And so at last he turned from all away, Even those he loved the best, To brood with a proud bitterness apart O’er dead yet lovely Hopes—a quick yet broken Heart. x Even where they gladdest grow— Even where they deepest glow

or many years I was being obliged to associate daily with persons whose natures could value nothing in mine that I valued myself; whose souls indeed had no faith for anything in God’s glorious Universe that was not, in their own vile phrase, “money’s worth.” Thus circumstances, when I reflected what “miserable men” almost all our Poets had ever been through the unfitness of similar compulsory associations, I had nearly armed myself in the resolutions of destroying as far as I could, all poetical record of my being: and of living mentally apart for the remainder of my life, dying eventually, like the wolf, in silence. But as time slid on, I gradually thought and felt myself out of this bitterness of love—for such it was; love of the Beautiful, crushed into bitterness by the burthen of its own incommunicable wealth. This analysis of feeling may be sneered at by the prosaic, but a “fit audiance though few” will understand it better, by virtue of a lovelier experience. C.H.
