ORIGINAL POETRY.

A JOLLY GOOD FELLOW.

BY CHARLES HARPUR.

Who ne’er treats a friend with proud rudeness Because he has less of mere pelf; Nor thinks himself wise when his shrewdness Puts benefit by on a shelf; And who has a good word for all goodness, Though not very saintly himself: Though he fuddle sometimes till he’s mellow, And delight in a rollicking glee, Still, he is a jolly good fellow— A jolly good fellow and free: Yes; he is a jolly good fellow, And shall ever be held one by me.

Who never a true breath abateth Through dread of a myrmidon’s pike, Or, like Dodge, for a safer time waiteth When his honor’s in peril to strike; And who ever and openly hateth All wrong and the devil alike: Though he fuddle sometimes, &c.

Who taketh advantage of no man Because he is lowly in life, And never makes light of a woman Because she is weak—or his wife: Yet has no more fear of Lord Bo, than He has of—the back of a knife! Though he fuddle sometimes, &c

Who never denies a poor sinner A dole he in reason can spare, Or helps a poor maiden to win her To wrong, by so baiting his snare; Or is greedily glad o’er his dinner, Because it is more than his share: Though he fuddle sometimes, &c

Who being made drouthy by nature, Will sit o’er his cups till he wink. And yet, ere one dear kindred creature Thereby into ruin should sink, Or even be grief-pinched in feature, Would sooner be—lost, than he’d drink! Though he fuddle sometimes, &c.

With fellows of this stamp—a rare lot! Established on every hand, It would grieve me but little to spare what To raise model folk is now planned, Nor should I much care if there were not A temperance man in the land! For though they whiles fuddled till mellow, And rejoiced in a rollicking glee, Yet each were a jolly good fellow— A jolly good fellow and free: Yes; each were a jolly good fellow, And a good enough fellow for me.

THE POINT.

But since there’s no jolly good fellow That right to this standard comes up, I hold that no one should get mellow With a less perfect warrant to sup; While to sing of mere sots, were to bellow Pig-praises—so down with the Cup!
