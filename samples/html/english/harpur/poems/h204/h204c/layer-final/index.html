BOTH SIDES OF THE MEDAL.

BY CHARLES HARPUR.

I.

THE OBVERSE.

Who ne’er treats a friend with proud rudeness, Because he has less of mere pelf, Or thinks himself wise when his shrewdness Puts benefit by on the shelf, And who has a good word for all goodness Though not very saintly himself: Though he fuddle sometimes till he’s mellow And delight in a rollicking glee, Still he is a jolly good fellow— A jolly good fellow and free; Yes; he is a jolly go[o]d fellow, And shall ever be held one by me.

Who never a true breath abateth Through dread of a myrmidon’s pike, Or, like Dodge, for a safer time waiteth, When his honour’s in peril, to strike, And who ever and openly hateth, All wrong and the devil alike: Though he fuddle sometimes, &c.

Who taketh advantage of no man Because he is lowly in life, And never makes light of a woman Because she is weak—or his Wife, Yet has no more fear of Lord Bo than He has of the back of a knife: Though he fuddle sometimes, &c.

Who never denies a poor sinner A dole he in reason can spare, Or helps a poor maiden to win her To wrong, by so baiting his snare, Or is greedily glad o’er his dinner Because it is more than his share: Though he fuddle sometimes, &c.

Who being made drougthy by nature Will sit o’er his cues till he wink, And yet ere one dear kindred creature Thereby into ruin should sink, Or even be grief pinched in feature, Would sooner be damned than he’d drink: Though he fuddle sometimes, &c.

With fellows of this stamp [gap] a rare lot! Established on every hand, It would grieve me but little to spare what To raise model folk is now planned, Nor should I much care if there were not A temperance man in the land: For though they whiles fuddled till mellow And rejoiced in a rollicking glee, Yet each were a jolly good fellow— A jolly good fellow and free; Yes; each were a jolly good fellow, And a good enough fellow for me.
