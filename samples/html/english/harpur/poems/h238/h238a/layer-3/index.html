Under the Wild-fig Tree.

Ah, many and many a day is gone, Since face to face were we, Gra Machree! Under the shadowy Wild-fig Tree; And yet,—how my distant heart is thrilled, Like a harp that is swept by the breeze alone, Whenever I think of Thee Gramachree! And my head with a strange dim stir is filled, Like the sound remote of a sooming sea.

‘Tis a sense of other and happier days, /When face to face were we Under the shadowy wild-fig Tree That thrills and sweetens within my breast, Like a breeze that far from its Eden strays, Whenever I think of Thee, Gramachree! And this dim brain-stir is the wild unrest Of a moaning and homeless memory.

Ah! that the beautiful Spirit of Love, That, when a-met were we, Under &c Drew into itself all our sympathies, Should be wandering now like the arkless dove— 143 Should only have been, to be Thus re-felt in the stir of thought’s mystic breeze, And the shell-like murmuring of memory.

Note. When conceiving some of my Poem, that especially which referred to the Past, I distinctly remember to have wished myself for the occasion a Musician instead of a Poet. I have felt that Music alone had sufficient sublety of expression for the burthen of my spirit, as it lay in the disturbing presence of a mass of dimly recurring thoughts, and distantly recollected sensations,‒ ‒ painfully pleasurable. Far-off memories of affection, and the pale phantoms of an ancient and a still haunting sorrow, elusively intermingled, and absorbing intermittantly, all present consciousness — what but the voice of Euterpe could give meet utterance to such a condition of being? So many thoughts have we, and particularly, so many sensations, which the subtle reach and wand-power of Music can alone seize in passing, or reproductively symbolise when past.

Yet I once thought Music wholly subordinate to and dependent upon Poetry in the expression of absolute purpose. But my feeling of the nature and scope of the former Art has altered, or rather developed considerably since then; from my having drunk more deeply into the divine effluences of some of its great Masters. I feel now /in 1849/ that language could no more arrest and embody such conceptions of the Beautiful 144a in its eternity, as are lapped forever in some of the bursts of Mozart, for instance, than a golden [unclear] [unclear], however exquisitely woven, could encompass and hold in the [unclear] spirit of a rose. They are instinct with revelations of the soul’s immortality; and all who have heard them have “drunk of the milk of Paradise.”

And seeing that the foregoing remarks will operate as a set-off to some things contained in it, I will annex a prose piece which originally formed the concluding part of a speech I once delivered at the Singleton Debating Society, on the question: Which has the superior Influence, Music or Poetry?

To contradistinguish as strikingly as possible the operations of Poetry and Music, as separated by the Question, for discussion to night; let us imagine in our presence four representatives of the entire race of Man. One of these shall be a savage; one a semi-savage, or half-civilised; and two highly civilised— one of these two being of the temperament of genius, and the other a mere worldling—a man shelled all over, like an armadillo, with material limitations, and a profound believer in roast beef and pudding.

First then, in the hearing of these four, let us suppose that a musical composition of accredited excellence is in the course of performance;— 147b Now warbling underward, like a solemn & lonely, like the voice of a hidden brook, or shrilling up heavenward, wild and inexpressible, like the unpremeditative carol of a lark,—and anon 144c deepening and doubling into a kind of harmonious thunder. Such is the quality of the music; and of what kind may we suppose the results? Why, the savage has arisen at a bound, whooping in horrid glee, with every feature in his harsh visage distending 145 with preposterous excitement. The semi-savage is either capering with far more of vigor than of grace, or spinning his hat on the top of his finger and thumb in unmeaning and ludicrous merriment. Of the remaining two, the worldling has settled himself, with an air of much method, for a comfortable snoose, and the other is rapt into a delirium like that produced by opium-smoking, through the perception of a beauty almost as extreme as death, but so far, so mystical and shadowy withal, as to shade every effort of the mind to collect it into anything like a deffinite and a[unclear]ing existence. But the performance is at length ended—and now for the permancy of its influence. With the dying cadences of the last vibrations, that too, is fading into nothingness; and the next set of circumstances, be they of what character soever they may, shall obliterate every trace of its effects upon the minds of the hearers: just as the print of footsteps in the sand of the sea shore is dashed out by the undulations of the returning tide. So much then for mere Music.

“Now in the hearing of these same four, supposing them for the occasion to be all of one speech, let us next imagine that a Poem of supreme excellence is in the course of recital: not one singing of war, “once the only argument heroic deemed”, but discoursing in solemn measures of the march of intellect and the eternity of truth, and occasionally descending from the Andes of the 146 soul into the vale of an improved daily life, to celebrate in softened strains the hallowing influences of its domestic charities, and of those ambrosial sentiments which bind together, as with a golden chain, two young and trusting beings, with whom

“Thought meets thought, ere from the lips it part And each warm wish springs mutual from the heart:”

and finally ascending again in its theme, to announce in prophetic tones, the ever-strengthening light of Knowledge, the evil atmosphere of social and political error, which has hitherto rendered the capable spirit of humanity so pale in its beauty, so dim in its glory, shall depart—shall break away like a great gathering of gloom before the strengthening splendour of the noontide sun. Such a poem is in the course of recital—and of what Kind may we suppose the results. The savage, as a matter of course, has settled down into brutish indifference. The semi-savage is listening with a face expressive of a vague puzzlement and is occasionally heard to mutter something about the “wonderful works of Nature”! Of the remaining two, the worldling the man of [unclear]like facts has all along been performing a solo on the nasal bassoon, thereby manifesting perhaps the self- congratulation which is going on his dream of having found, or safely stolen, a huge heap of money. But there is yet one, on whose spirit its sublime monitions are falling like seraphic fire from heaven; in whose bosom its sure inspiration shall ever more be treasured as a 147a religion,—a solace and a purification: whether he fill a dungeon or sit upon a throne: whether he toil in the streets of a dissolute city, or, like Burns,

“——————————in glory and joy, Behind his plough upon the mountain side.”

Here then, in this one individual, the influence exercised by Poetry, is greater in actual amount and value, than that which is effected by Music upon the whole four. The purpose-power of the latter is indefinite and transitory upon all; of the former definite and permanent upon one. The influence of Music, and as it is, may be more generally and immediately effective, than that of Poetry; but it is a mere surface: and like, in this respect, to the waters of one of our summer lagoons; occupying a wide extent of prospect, but so shallow withal, as to be evaporated by the heat of a single day: while, on the contrary, the influence of Poetry may be likened to a mighty river; of little comparative view in breadth, and eluding the eye as it winds away amongst the green woods and hanging mountains, but at the same time, deep, rapid, and eternal.
