Similes from Homer.

[In rendering the following bits, the line I prescribed to myself was this: To comprehend thoroughly the poetic drift or intention of the passage before me—and then give the English reader that, though with as little departure as possible from the cast of the original. For Homer’s similes, however good as wholes, are always much involved in their parts, and thence defectively articulated. And hence, in translating them, the true manner is, not to give exactly what he does say, but what he evidently would say,—or in other words, what he is only hindered from saying, either by want of method, or by a certain imma- -turity of power, which is mainly attributable, no doubt, to the mental crudity of his age.

But is not this to speak somewhat irreverently of Homer? Not if, as I believe, the truth is more to be revered than anything else. Not if, in the fields of Literature, as in all other fields, we should speak of things exactly as we find them. A mere Pedant, perhaps, who would have outsiders believe, that he holds in his Greek an exclusive key as it were to all literary perfection, (and thence to a glory which can reflect only upon his privileged fraternity),— may pronounce differently. But I challenge any honest researcher, who can spell through Homer in the original, to say whether what is above written of his similes—to push the criticism no farther—is other than the truth.

Nay, in partial proof of this, let the English reader look over a literal rendering of the first simile of the ten which are given below.

“As when a wave of the sea is driven on the loud- -sounding shore in constant succession under the north- -west which wind which has set it in motion; at first indeed it rears itself high up in the deep—but then, 528 dashing against the coast, it makes a mighty roar; and swelling, rises high about the jutting prominences, spitting out from it the foam of the sea: thus the thick phalanxes of the Greeks moved on incessantly to the fight.”]

I.

As when upon the loud-resounding shore The long sea-swells, beneath the north-west wind Which sets them first in motion, are impelled In mountainous successions, wave ridge on ridge, With a continuous bursting: from afar First come they, rolling in their bulk aloft, Till, dashing full upon the quaking coast, They break with a dread roar; nor yet retire, But swell and eddy round the jutting crags, Spitting their white foam wide about the while: So thick the Grecian phalanxes moved on Rank after rank, incessant, to the fight.
