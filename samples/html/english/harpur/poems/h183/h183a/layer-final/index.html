SONGS, EPIGRAMS, NOTES, AND OPINIONS, &c., BY CHARLES HARPUR.

No. 9.

1.

Political Contradiction.

Freedom that’s half a fact and half a fiction, What is it but a sorry contradiction? Pure Despotism’s honest at the least, Nor hides the hoof that indicates the Beast; But sees in Fortune’s man-compelling star, The one sole sanction of its Jove-like Czar.

Note.—The English nation is one great political con- tradiction. Call it free and self-governed, or fettered and class-ridden, and you hardly know which phrase comes nearest the truth. Call it powerful or feeble, rich or poor, sturdy or vacillating, generous or stingy, merciful or cruel, and some phase of its being will justify every epithet. But the strangest anomaly of all, comes out from between the people and the government: standing, as they really do, in the broadest contra- distinction. This anomaly is more or less observable through all English history; but since the accession of the present Royal Family, it has become fixed and glaring—namely: a People loving and lauding liberty, with a Government hating and abusing it—a People always demanding political reform, with a Government everlastingly at dodge and baulk with all political pro- gress[.] And as the Satanic spirit effuses from its centre in hell, into the remotest corners of the world, so the obstructive influence of the Imperial Government, circulates outward to inform and actuate its most distant offshoots. But wherein lies the whole mystery of the evil? Why, the whole hog of it—snout and tail, guts and bristles—is a Governing Aristocracy, with absolutism at his heart, and expediency for its mainsail.
