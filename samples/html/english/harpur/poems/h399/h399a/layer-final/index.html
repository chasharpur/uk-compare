II.

Pressure from Without.

The surest means to solve A Senatorial doubt, Or cure a personal resolve, Is pressure from without.

Note.—I have a beef-steak story exactly in point. Mrs. Mealimouth was a refined little body. And if she cherished a superlative distaste for any one set of things more than another, it was for all such hard little words in English as have been soiled by vulgar use and association—at least, so far as her own pretty mouth might be imperilled by their utterance; but she had no such super-refined scruples, when the leathern lips of a servant-girl or errand-boy were to be the mediums of some vulgar command involving their appliance. But it so happened that she had lost her only indoor help, nor could she for some days provide herself with another —and the butcher was at the door, waiting for “orders.”

Now it was Mrs. Mealimouth’s opinion (as it is mine) that a good eatable beef-steak is only to be derived from that part of the bovine kind which is vulgarly called the rump. But how could she ever mention such a word in her own proper person—with her own ever-delicate mouth? Such a dreadful impropriety was not to be thought of! And so she bespoke so many pounds of steaks off the best part.

“Yes mum,” quoth the butcher, whisking off and back again with miraculous celerity: “Here they are, mum.” There they were, sure enough—but off the round.

Though Mrs. Mealimouth said nothing at the moment and pocketed her disappointment with exemplary (apparent) good breeding, I may venture to assert, I think, that it “got her steam up” considerably: for she vowed afterwards that she would never again deal with any nasty fellow who could mistake so plain a com-mand.

Well, in the full spirit of resolved refinement (having still no servant) she, the next day, ordered the opposition butcher, “over the way,” to send her so many pounds of beef-steaks from the most proper part for steaks. “Yes mum”—and with the butcherly quickness of the craft, they were home before herself. But “confusion worse confounded!” they were actually off some part of the beast, near the shoulder.

“This would vex a saint!” exclaimed Mrs. Meali- mouth, and appealing to Mr. M., she wished to be infor- med if it was not altogether too bad to have had com- mands so explicit as hers had been, thus egregiously mistaken in both instances? “But did you say off the rump? my dear,” was the crusty rejoinder of Mr. Meali- mouth. “No: I did’nt say that exactly—I said off the most proper part.” “Well then, in future, say off the rump, and you’ll get them off the rump,” persisted Mr. M., who, as the reader will perceive, had no great sympathy with the verbal delicacy of his better half,

Now her previous disappointments had doubly 2e whetted Mrs. M.’s appetite for a steak of the right sort, and the butcher was again at the door, waiting for orders. It required an effort to give the word, certainly, but the pressure from without had had its due effect even upon her guarded organisation;—and so she said to the butcher—with her own mouth—”Send me so many pounds of steaks—not off the round, mind you; but higher up—in short, off the rump! And off the rump she got them.
