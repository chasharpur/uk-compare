The Losses of the Past.

The world is emptied of that mystic gladness Which, dwelling round my childhood, o’er it came In every murmurous game 83 Played by the winds!—and always in my sadness, I look out over its unfreendly face In vain for some fresh place, Which now should seem a possible habitation For that rich light of love wherewith afar My soul rayed, like a star Beaming sweet influence from its heavenly station— Rayed tow’rds all Beauty, through the boundless joy Of the then happy Boy!

‘Tis gone too—that frank trust is gone for ever Wherewith I afterwards essayed the world, And first the flag unfurled Of my own individual endeavour! Youth’s unworn bravery I strive in vain To gather back again, If but to taste once more the heroic feeling That went then from my heart, as some bold rill Leaps from its native hill! But ah! instead, a sense of loss comes pealing Out of the sunless Past—like a long wail Heard in the midnight gale! A sense of vain intents, and hopes yet vainer, And rich affections wasted, as bestowed Where Love had no abode! He only of young life can be a gainer, Who carries with him its unperished bloom Into an early tomb.

And yet, though coming to this sick conclusion, My weary soul, de methinks, derives some gain Of strength even from the pain Attendant upon Memory’s transfusion Over a life so lossful,—strength to know 84 The personal end of woe: That gauge of all past suffering to a tittle, Which leaves us in our central self assured, As to its brunt inured; And thence to feel, the future can bring little That was not typed in what time brought before— And to expect no more.
