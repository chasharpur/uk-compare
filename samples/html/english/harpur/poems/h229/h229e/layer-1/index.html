The Losses of the Past.

The world is emptied of that mystic gladness Which, dwelling round my childhood, o’er it came In every murmurous game Played by the winds!—and ever in my sadness I look out over its unfriendly face In vain for some fresh place, Which should to Hope seem a fit habitation For that rich light of love wherewith afar My soul rayed, like a star Beaming sweet influences from its heavenly station— Rayed tow’rds all beauty, through the boundless joy Of the once happy Boy.

‘Tis gone too—that frank trust is gone for ever, Wherewith I afterwards essayed the world, And first the flag unfurled Of my own individual endeavour! Youth’s unworn bravery I strive in vain To gather back again, If but to know once more the heroic feeling That went then from my heart, as some bold rill Leaps from its native hill! But ah! instead, a sense of loss comes pealing Out of the sunless Past—like a long wail Heard in the midnight gale! 13 A sense of vain regrets, and hopes yet vainer, And rich affections wasted, as bestowed Where Love had no abode! He only of young life’s an absolute gainer, Who carries with him its unperished bloom Into an early tomb.

And yet, though coming to this sick conclusion, My soul, methinks, derives some gain Of strength even from the pain Attendant upon Memory’s transfusion Over a life so lossful,—strength to know The personal end of woe: That gauge of all past suffering to a tittle, Which leaves us in our central self assured, As to its brunt inured; And thence to feel, the future can bring little That was not typed in what time brought before— And to expect no more.
