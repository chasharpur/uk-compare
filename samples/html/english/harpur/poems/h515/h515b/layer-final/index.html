THE SNOW CHILD.

A Swiss Legend.

BY CHARLES HARPUR

Beware child, in your gambols, of the fatal rift below:— Thus warned his Boy, a Switzer, amid the mountain snow. But the snow will never harm me, replied the way- ward child, As back into his Father’s face he gayly looked and smiled.

And forward then he started like a spirit through the drift, And many a wild bound ventured unmindful of the rift; Till in his heart the father, with a feeling strange and wild, Said, canst thou be my own Herman—so like a Phantom child?

And then he looked a moment, out to where, with thundrous jar, An avalanche had bounded from a glittering Alp afar— He had looked away,—a moment did hardly inter- vene; When lo! the little Herman’s form was nowhere to be seen!

With a great ineffable horror crushing in upon his heart, He stood;—then shook the glaciers with an upward shuddering start, As o’er the snow he shouted the vanished Herman’s name— But from the dreary echoes—hark! the only answer came!

Answer me! Come to me! he cried in wild search to and fro, Unmindful of the fatal rift beneath the piles of snow; Still ‘Herman!’ shrieked the Father, as he madly rushed about— But only some wild cavern responded to the shout!

And when that childless Switzer sought his kindred in the vale, His late-dark locks hung snow-like o’er a forehead icy-pale; Dread tokens of a sorrow that had bleached him, though so brief, Like the breath of many winters, in a single blast of grief.

Then his kindred left him lying in the serpent-coils of woe, And up into the mountains went, amid the piles of snow; But in vain their search was heedful, day after day,—in vain— The wayward little Herman was never seen again!

And never on the mountains did his Sire again rejoice, Filling their icy gorges with his bold and manly voice; For the vanished Herman’s Image, there snow- snatched from his side, Came freezing to his blasted heart until the day he died!

No; never on the mountains did that Sire again rejoice, Filling their icy gorges with his bold and manly voice; Though evermore his child he sought, Spring after Spring,—in vain— The body of little Herman was never seen again!

But often since the mountaineer, amid the snow- storm wild, Sees through the white drift bounding a lovely Phantom child; There bounding through the white drift, as with a frantic joy, And straightway he bethinks him of the hunter’s wayward Boy.
