IV.

Waller.

A prim, complacently poetic wight, Smooth in his style, and in his matter light. He lived in Milton’s day, more lauded far And cherished, than that bright peculiar star. With principles as sliding as his rhymes, His pitch-pipe was the tenor of the times: These changed, of paltry Charles without a pang Of shame he sings, as he of Cromwell sang. ‘Midst [unclear] titled jades proud was Sir Bard to sit, Though waxing grey, a fashionable wit; And while spiced wine, his better Muse, he’d sip, Dish up heroics on each strumpet’s lip; Or o’er a girdle, in high-doting rhyme, Strain—amourously strain at the sublime.

Even such a bard was Waller: yet, ’tis true, Some credit was his literary due: With rugged models, studious to refine The pause and cadence of the the Heroic Line, He gave its rhymes a periodic tone To the then popular poetry unknown. x

x This is hardly giving him all his due. He wrote many bright things. Some of his Lyrics are exquisitely finished and truly beautiful. Still I believe the above estimate of his genius and character is correct in the main—as far as it goes. His fashionable tact alone, would be conclusive to my mind of 61 his secondary order as a poet. No great poet is ever the possessor of this characteristic; for if he were, it would be fatal to his greatness, because it would inevitably lead him to affect the modial, to the neglect of the primary, both in Man and Nature. It is eminent in Rogers and Moore, and indicates their order as conclusively as it did Waller’s: while it commands in their regards the same compensations. Like him, they will enjoy a prime rank with the Present, and a secondary one with the Future: while Wordsworth and Leigh Hunt, like Milton in the prior parallel, will have to wait in the grave for their compensations in the juster verdict of Posterity.—With reference also to Waller’s having improved upon the heroic rhymes of some of the poets then popular (see Donne’s &c.), it should be remarked, that in avoiding their faults, he slided into an opposite extreme, and became too smooth and even—so much so, that in reading him, we would often exchange his slipping monotony for their variety of cadence, even though accompanied with all their linear ruggedness. C.H.
