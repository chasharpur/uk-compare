92

To S.P. Hill Esqr

Author of “Tarquin the Proud” and other equally splendacious and never-to-be-forgotten pieces.

Like Cowper, when he first read Milton’s verses, I danced for joy, thine reading. O, so prime are they, That Homer ev’n, with all the Nine for nurses, Did lisp so early nothing like thy rhyme I say! What though the critics daily cut thee sharper, (I wonder where the villains think they’ll go to?) Preferring Hallaran and Parkes and Harpur— Nay, the rhymed nincompoopery of Lowe too? Alas! not therefore our Parnassian Hill Should give his Castaly to flow no more: Original at least must be the quill That writes as goose-quill never wrote before: Besides, a heighten[e]d miracle’s disclosed In thee—the ass of Balaam only prosed.

It is likely that I never should have gone to the trouble of hanging the above ironical calf-skin on the recreant limbs of this witling, had I not heard that he was in the habit of solacing his own intellectual abortiveness by attempting to disparage my national poems. Now I can allow no one, great or little, to kick with impunity against that class of my productions. It is a matter of conscience with me, in fact, to sink all who do so utterly if I can. Hill’s yoke-fellow, Ewing, also attempted to make himself conspicuous at my expense in a 93 lecture on Poetry at the School of Arts, of which Institution the former is Secretary—and of Master Ewing himself, something more in another place. But what a vain thing do these insigni- -ficants imagine, if they suppose their virdicts can discourage my bardic consciousness even for a moment. I am altogether out of their reach both by nature and art. Nay, what if even Lowe and Elwin, men of some real literary standing, were to sanction their small doggery by begetting pups of a like colour? Why, I should just squib Lowe and Elwin out of my way as contemptuously as I did Hill and Ewing before them: beli[e]ving still, every whit as solidly as hitherto, that I am doing more, and am missioned to do more, for the intellectual future of my country than any other writer it possesses, either by birth or adoption. And this consciousness has been the sole fee or reward by which I have been moved through all my literary labors; and if my patience has at any time given way under them, it has been simply from feeling that if my writings were as surely bread to me while I lived as they were to become the foundation stones of my country’s literature after I am dead (though hastened perhaps by hunger), I should in that case have nothing else to care for but to encrease and multiply them. On this head then, henceforth, “let no man trouble me.”

March, 1848

C.H.
