But two steeds and a Moslem in death lay still, And a kite of the desert that came flop flop In heavy flight from a pyramid’s top, In a merry mood At the scent of blood, Was circling o’er and screaming shrill.*

* I found the subject of these rhymes in some account (I know not whose) of the French achieve- ments in Egypt, under the mighty murderer— NNNapoleon. I thought the incident capable of being elaborated into a powerful poetical picture, and seized upon it accordingly: but having done so, I must record against it the facts, that I consider Bona- parte’s invasion of the East was not simply but monstrously unwarrantable; and that I have but little admiration, under any circumstances, for mere military heroism. What have truth and human good to do with the sword? What can it prove, if used offensively, but that men are some- times worse than wild beasts? and what, even when employed in the defensive, but that murder, violence, and robbery are sometimes only to be repelled by their own inhuman means? What is there in mere bravery worthy of engaging our admiration? The bull-dog is brave. What is there in mere destruc- tiveness worthy of commanding our emulation? The tiger is eminently destructive. If we must needs glorify our individualities, let it, in the name of sanity, be for such things as lift us above, not such as ally us to the brutes. Still I would not be misunderstood. I esteem rational valor a prime social virtue; and think that they who would not uphold their country’s liberties at the cost of life, were unworthy even of that base existence for which, in such case, these would be forfeited. All I would be understood to mean, is, that if in the national systems of the world now obtaining, war is occa- sionally inevitable, still woe be to them by whom war cometh.

What most astonishes one with regard to war, is, that so few or rather none of all the ous who have hitherto [gap]ded in it, should have thus expostulated with themselves, while plying its devillish enquiery against their fellow creatures: “Verily, if this 445 our worth be not Satanic, what is there in the whole Universe worthy of damnation? Or rather, is there, even in the profound of Hell, aught that can match us for madness and impiety?—a completer set of deadly and desperate devils?” C. H.
