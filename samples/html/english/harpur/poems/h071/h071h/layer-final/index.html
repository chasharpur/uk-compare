A Combat.

A Trooper of France, in desperate need, Had struggled from under his dying steed Where Egypt’s pyramids appeared In giant bulk, as if giant-reared; While on his black war-horse, the Turk Who had borne him down, to finish his work Back wheelingly careered.

Taking his resolute stand Foot-deep in his Charger’s blood, The soldier of Christendom sword in hand (A trusty blade and good That glared as it moved, like the lightning-tongue Of a settling cloud that had just been flung By the rending rush of a whirlblast, far Out in the front of the storm of War) His foe awaiting stood.

Urged into uttermost speed, On—on with a scattering tail, Like the hurrying thunder-bolt’s smoky trail! On—on with a streaming main, 104 Like the foremost racks of a hurricane! On came the Tartar steed.

Then passed, with a vengeful clash Of shivering blades, and a sludgy splash, And lastly a lunging downward crash, The imminent shock! And when its cloud of dust upthrown Cleared off, there stood that Trooper alone, Firm as stands the topmost stone Of a midsea rock, Over which, all in vain, a black Storm had just blown!

But two steeds and a Moslem in death lay still— And a kite of the Desert that came with a flop In heavy flight from a pyramid’s top, In a hunger-glad mood At the scent of blood, Was circling over and screaming shrill— Screaming with hunger, and screaming for joy As she thought of her famishing brood, For that Man the destroyer so well could destroy And glut them so bravely with food.
