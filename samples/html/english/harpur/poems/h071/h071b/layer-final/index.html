THE COMBAT.

(By Charles Harpur.)

A Trooper of France, in desperate need, Had struggled from under his dying steed, Where Egypt’s pyramids appeared; While on his black war-horse, the Turk Who had borne him down, to finish his work, Back wheelingly careered.

Taking his resolute stand Foot deep in his charger’s blood, The Soldier of Christendom, sword in hand, That gleamily out pointing, showed Like the lightning tongue of a settling cloud, His Foe awaiting stood.

Urged into whirlwind speed, On—on with a scattering tail Like the hurrying thunder-bolt’s smoky trail! On—on with a streaming mane Like the foremost racks of a hurricane! On came the Arab steed!

Then passed with a vengeful clash Of blades, and a lunging downward crash, The imminent shock! And when its cloud of dust upthrown Cleared off, there stood that Trooper alone, Firm as a rock! But two steeds and a Moslem in death lay still, And a kite of the desert that came flop flop In heavy flight from a pyramid’s top, In a merry mood At the scent of blood, Was circling o’er and screaming shrill.*

* I found the subject of these rhymes in some account (I know not whose) of the French achieve- ments in Egypt, under the mighty murderer— NNNapoleon. I thought the incident capable of being elaborated into a powerful poetical picture, and seized upon it accordingly: but having done so, I mustrecord against it the facts, that I consider Bona- parte’s invasion of the East was not simply butmonstrously unwarrantable; and that I have but little admiration, under any circumstances, formere military heroism. What have truth and human good to do with the sword? What can itprove, if used offensively, but that men are some- times worse than wild beasts? and what, even whenemployed in the defensive, but that murder, violence, and robbery are sometimes only to be repelled bytheir own inhuman means? What is there in mere bravery worthy of engaging our admiration? Thebull-dog is brave. What is there in mere destruc- tiveness worthy of commanding our emulation?The tiger is eminently destructive. If we must needs glorify our individualities, let it, in the nameof sanity, be for such things as lift us above, not such as ally us to the brutes. Still I would not bemisunderstood. I esteem rational valor a prime social virtue; and think that they who would not uphold their country’s liberties at the cost of life, were unworthy even of that base existence for which, in such case, these would be forfeited. All I would be understood to mean, is, that if in the nationalsystems of the world now obtaining, war is occa- sionally inevitable, still woe be to them by whom war cometh.

C. H.
