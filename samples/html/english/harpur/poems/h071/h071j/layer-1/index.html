A Combat.

A Trooper of France, in desperate need, Had struggled from under his dying Steed, Where Egypt’s pyramids appeared In giant bulk, as if giant-reared; While on his black war-horse, the Turk Who had borne him down, to finish his work, Wheeled back, and at him again careered.

Taking his resolute stand Foot-deep in his Charger’s blood, The Soldier of Christendom sword in hand, A trusty blade and good, (That glared as it moved, like the lightning tongue Of a settling cloud that had just been flung 157 By the rending rush of a whirl-blast, far Out in the front of the storm of War) His Foe awaiting stood!

Urged into uttermost speed, On—on with a scattering tail, Like the hurrying thunder-bolt’s smoky trail! On—on with a streaming mane, Like the foremost racks of a hurricane! On came the Tartar Steed.

Then passed, with a vengeful clash Of blades shivering blades, and lunging crash Down rushing, and raising a sludgy splash, The terrible shock! And when its cloud of dust upthrown Cleared off, there stood that Trooper alone, Firm as might stand the topmost stone Of a mid-sea rock, Over which, all in vain, a black Storm had just blown.

But two Steeds and a Moslem in death lay still— And a Kite of the Desert that came with a flop In heavy flight from a Pyramid’s top, In a hunger-glad mood At the scent of blood; Was circling over, and screaming shrill!
