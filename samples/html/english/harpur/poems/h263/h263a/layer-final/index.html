A GARLAND OF WILD FLOWERS. By Charles Harpur.

I.

THE STILL HOUR OF LOVE.

O come to the stream-illumed grove, Lady! O come—’tis the still hour of love, Lady! For the voice of the day now is hushed, and at rest Is the beast on its couch and the bird in its nest.

The grasshopper’s gone to his bed in the green, And all chirping things, save the cricket, are still; And the risen Moon comes, like an armour-clad queen, Through the pines on the top of the hill.

The last-lagging plover’s form dimly is given As over he sails for some wave-beaten isle; And the stars cluster forth from the chambers of heaven To dance in their coming Queen’s smile, And with golden ray’d glances, As still she advances, To look back the pleasure it gives them the while.

Bright—brightly flame the dwarf waves of the stream now As deftly they leap to the night breeze’s tune, For up in their motion the imaged stars beam now,— As spirit-lights rise From the depths of thine eyes, When they flash in the light of the Moon.

O then to the stream-illumined grove—come! In the still sweet spirit of love—come! For the voice of the day now is hushed, and at rest Is the beast on its couch and the bird in its nest.
