[GENIUS LOST. THE SORROWS PART SEVEN: THE CEMETERY]

(make this the seventh)

I love to muse—too much now love to muse Amid the tombs, while thus the shades of Eve Come lengthening over.—Silence keeps the place, But interrupted by the murmurous toil Of provident bees, settling in busy troops, At intervals, upon the wild thyme and wild vines Bushed round yon well-known Grave.—Such is the hum They make, that even Death might sounder sleep Under their lullaby, and Spectres lose Their lean dissatisfaction, and become Enamoured of a spot so visited.

40

Coming and going—even thus, in sooth, The generations of mankind appear And buzz, in solemn business, on the Earth, A mighty Grave, and all within it, one Vast Death! and as these flies, departing, leave No stain in the pure air where now they glanced, So the world’s generations pass away, And not a shred remains of all they were, In actual being!—

I knew the gentle One Here folded in the cold arms of dumb death: 2 ‡ A fair haired Girl, with eyes as wildly sweet As those which look forth from the purple depths Of life’s first dreams, while in some flowery dell We slumber, lull’d by a cool gurgling brook: Look mystically forth into our hearts To search them to the core, and be to them thenceforth Soul-stars forever, shining from the past, Pregnant with inoffensive fire, they flashed Their sweetness like ignited perfumes round. But now, one drop of all their precious light Remains not! and her Mother vainly looks For hours upon her vacant seat, as there 41 To catch some gleam by clammy death unquencht— Some lonely lingering gleam!

Her matchless voice Was as the voice of birds and bubbling brooks Remotely modulating more and more With breathing instruments, and whispering joys Of green boughs clasping in the sighing gale, All confluent in one deep harmonious draught Sweetening and thickening with emotion,—till It ravished every sense, and dowered the soul With wild wings woven from the bliss of dreams! Such was the various spirit of her voice, As over her work at eventide she sung Before her Father’s door. But now—ah, me! What of that charmful voice? Lost—ever lost Out in the dim dead Past! As well we might Hope in the air to find a yellow trace Of yon departing bee, as hope again Of it to hear, under the sun and moon, A tone—a passing murmur!

42

What is Life? When Beings thus gifted with divinest charms Fade ere the flowers, and in their beauty go Down to their bridal in the Grave, behind In the dark world they brightened, leaving nought But its dull daily tedium;—even as fleets A pleasant vision from the hopeless heart Of one who wakes but to the cares and toils Of Poverty!

Nay, what is Life? when Beings Instinct with thoughts and longings in their spheres Even vaster than the star-emblazoned belt Of this immeasurable Universe! Whose arts give birth to wingëd truths that soar And pass aline about the regal Sun; Accompany the rolling Moon through all Her changes, like revealing lightnings;—yea, That voilate the dim and pathless glades Of farthest space, there wakening voices, which The Earth, through the emotion of the Sage 43 Gazing at midnight from his lonely tower, Responds to;—that in bold discovery sit In the bright bowers of the sidereal Heavens, Drinking their harmonies, to return at last Laden, like bees, with knowledge to their age! O what is Life? when Beings thus robed with spirit, Yet pass away like unrecorded dreams— Yet wander over the subjected earth More perishing than its grass!

But let me pause: This very power, wedded to suffering dust, Were clinging misery, if pitying Heaven Not offered to time-wearied hearts death’s cool Oblivious cup.

Thus sanction I the wish That now I homed me in this silent City, Or shared its rest apart, in some lone spot Of woods and waters, where the gentle Echo Is seldom summoned from her mossy cell, And then but to attend the wandering steps 44 Of musing Melancholy. Ye would laugh, Doubtless, ye sons of pelf, at wish so wild! What could you else? Wrapt up in selfissh craft, Or wallowing besotted in the sty Of solitary gust, ye never knew To feel the burthen of a human heart!

And ye, cold moralists, who discourse of Man, Man the mysterious! whose spirit’s depths Are as unsearchable as Ocean’s caves, As of some stringëd instrument whose tones, However eloquent of sorrow and joy, Glory and love, are yet elicited By the appulses of apparent touch, And tested by a scale,—ye would declaim: Such wish is but the pining of a heart Surcharged with wild ambition, from the aims Of which, at length, Experience hath dispelled Hope’s aggrandising hues; or of a heart Jaded with animal indulgence, sought 45 Through lawless Passion!

Oh, how wise are ye! At least so think the Cliques, and partly in that Resides your recompense; the residue, Weakness of sages! condescends to take The shape of pence hot from the unwilling palms Of the fat magnates of the Land, who bolt The moral physic with their daily soup; And thus full-blown with righteousness and food, And it may be from indigestion, too, More holily severe,—how promptly they Will then, from their judicial eminence, Thunder down judgment on some ragged wretch For daring to be hungry!

Oh, away! Ye, all complacent in the iron pride Of forms and dogmas, and those worldly rules Which centre all the decency of life In being passionless, and bearing hearts Barr’d like a miser’s door ‘gainst all assault 46 Of sympathy,—how should ye apprehend In what guise recommended comes the wish That makes Death beautiful, to a young Poet Loving all pleasant and all excellent things,— All loveliness, and yet unloved!—to one Hungry for joy, yet clung with misery! How recommended it may come to him, Whose candour only renders him the more Suspected of his kind, and thence, indeed, Incompetent to bridge his daily way With safety, or with credit, o’er the deep And secret pits, and broad obstructive moats, Wherewith Melevolence while his fellows sleep Dreaming of brotherhood, or godless Power Triumphant in the face of trembling crowds, And with such warranty arm’d as oaths and bribes Win from the wrested Laws,—do incommode The roadways of the world! Nay, how to him It comes, who daily wages hopeless war 47 With savage want, yet cannot stoop to act The parasite, or as a menial serve Whom the mind’s independence bids despise! That moral self assertion from within Sallying unbid, to which even Labor’s son Pretends not, but with perfect right may hold His birthright in the midst of crownëd kings, And in its innate rectitude even dare Despise them, if they dare be vile!—Away!

Away! for such or worse is every phase Of my existence,—even at the best. Nay, when my hopes are liveliest and would live, Though surely dying, and with resolute heed I strive once more to take a stable hold Of nature and of life, my efforts still Are but like hungry roots that blindly grope For moisture through a hard and barren soil That dooms them to the famine of my fate!

48

Then be thou mine, thou sad yet soothing Wish That makes Death beautiful to the weary one: The Wish foretasting, if no more, the long Inviolate sabbath of its voiceless rest. Come, utter to my burning heart thy cool And brook-like murmers! Come; and with thee bring A spirit of philosophy, to chase Those spectral glooms with which wan Fear deforms Time’s dim glen leading to the Grave;—thus warbling Unto the listening motions of my soul: What though the desolate death bed of Want Be thorny, while the invisible cords of life By gaunt Disease, there only slothful, one By one, to the attenuating last, Are gnawed in twain—yet, this once past, how thrice Desirable to Misery’s haggard child, Is that repose which breathes about the bier A holy tiding of unbroken rest!

49

And here sleeps soundly one who in the world Had little rest; and one whom well I loved For knowing well, and more, the more I knew him. A Youth as freely bounding in his spirit Once as a wave: but he grew dark of thought— And died! I one day asked him as we strolled Together, of his ailment, and he answered “The vanity of Hope.” And now ’tis mine To know what tracts of desolate truth that phrase Doth volume! As I muse, out of some dim And secret cavern of my past existence A response, drearier than a knell, seems drawn To verify its import; as it were The soul-heard doom tone, lingering even yet Over some dateless epoch of my life, Whenceforth my heart insensibly began To lose the measure of its hopefulness— The melody of its first love, that sought To mingle with and swell the harmony Of universal Good; and from which time 50 My Being, graduating in despair, Hath day by day given up, even to the last Bright drop, its human faith: albeit I yet Believe in Good’s existence; it indeed Hath an oasis in this desert World, But Evil, like the sand, is everywhere! And hence, our youthful tendencees to love And friendship, all the sooner they are pushed Into the antagonistic crush of Life, But give us so much earlier to know The vanity of Hope! Alas! that thus The warm and fertile heart of Youth itself Should be but as it were the living grave Of all its loveliest aspirations, slain By the sharp swords of evil yesterdays.

And who so wounded and weary of heart as I, Could much frequent these tombs, and oft observe This unfenced Grave respected by the foot Even of the lightest of the sabbath throng, 51a Nor wish for such a home? Who thus forlorn, Might fancy that the very gale, first heard So loudly boisterous in the neighbouring wood, As touched by decent awe, in passing here Humbled itself, and sighing, gently moved The meek heads of memorial flowers to bend 50a With pensive heed, as if they also felt That somewhat mourned by unforgetting Love Was lying in the cold dark where they grow From some lone mound,—nor wish that he were laid With all his sorrows there?

51c

Here—only here In the dark dwellings of this silent city, Is rest for the world-abandoned. Obloquy here, Disease and Poverty, forego their victim. The fox of envy and the wolf of scorn snarl not within these gates. The enemy, Who comes to triumph o’er the powerless bones That once he feared, still hates,—even as he comes, By the dismaying silence smitten, stops, As listening to some far reproachful voice Heard only through the mystery of his soul, And, shuddering, asks forgiveness. Here I would sleep, 52 And should an enemy so plead, and might My injured spirit, hovering over, hear,— The boon were granted. O that here, even now, Were frozen into forgetfulness the sense— That I, on this so populous star of God, This Earth that I was born to and have loved Am utterly uncared for and alone!
