The Cemetery.

I love to muse—too much now love to muse Amid the tombs while thus the shades of eve Come lengthening over. Silence keeps the place, But interrupted by the murmurous toil Of provident bees, settling in busy troops, At intervals, upon the wild thyme and wild vines Bushed round yon well known Grave. Such is the hum 291 They make, that even Death might sounder sleep Under their lullaby, and Spectres lose Their lean dissatisfaction and become Enamoured of a spot so visited.

Coming and going—even thus, in sooth, The generations of mankind appear And buzz, in solemn business, on the Earth, A mighty Grave! and all within it, one Vast Death! And as these flies, departing, leave No stain in the pure air where now they glanced, Even so our human generations pass Away for ever through the void of Time, And not a shred remains of what they were In actual being!—

I knew the gentle One Here folded in the cold arms of dumb death: A fair-haired Girl, with eyes as wildly sweet As those which look forth from the purple depths Of life’s first dreams, when in some flowery dell We slumber, lulled by a cool gurgling brook— Look mystically forth into our hearts To search them, and to be to them thenceforth As stars for ever, shining from the soul: And to be matched but once in all the world! Pregnant with inoffensive fire, they flashed Their sweetness like ignited perfume round. But now, one drop of all their precious light Remains not! and her Mother vainly looks For hours upon her vacant seat, as there To catch some gleam by clammy death unquencht— Some lonely lingering gleam!

Her matchless voice 292 Was (unto them who through their spirits hear Those special utterances that soul inspires) Even as the voice of birds and bubbling brooks Remotely modulating more and more With lowly whisperings—the primeval joys Of green boughs clasping in the buxom gale, And breathing instruments, and chiming chords All ultimated—all sublimed, and thence All confluent in one deep harmonious flood Sweetening and thickening with emotion,—till It ravished every sense, and dowered the heart With wild wings woven from the bliss of dreams! Such was the various spirit of her voice, As over her work at eventide she sang Within her Father’s hall. But now—ah, me! What of that charmful voice? Lost—ever lost Out in the dim dead Past! As well we might Hope in the air to find a yellow trace Of yon departing bee, as hope again Of it to hear, under the sun and moon, A tone—a passing murmur!

What is Life? When Beings thus gifted with divinest charms Fade ere the flowers, and in their beauty go Down to their bridal in the Grave, behind In the dark world they brightened, leaving nought But its dull daily tedium;—even as fleets A pleasing vision from the hopeless mind Of one who wakes but to the cares and toil Of poverty!

Nay, what is Life? when Beings Instinct with thoughts and longings, in their spheres Even vaster than the star-emblazoned belt 293 Of this immeasurable Universe! Whose arts give birth to wingëd truths that soar And pass a line about the regal Sun! Accompany the rolling Moon through all Her changes, like revealing lightnings!—yea, That violate the dim and pathless glades Of farthest space, there wakening voices, which The Earth, through the emotion of the sage Gazing at midnight from his lonely tower, Responds to!—that in bold discovery sit In the bright bowers of the sidereal heavens Drinking God’s harmonies, to return at last Laden, like bees, with knowledge to their age! Oh, what is Life? when Beings thus robed with spirit Yet pass away like unrecorded dreams— Yet wander over the subjected earth More perishing than its grass!

But let me pause: This very power, wedded to suffering dust, Were clinging misery, if pitying Heaven Not offered to time-wearied hearts the cool Oblivious cup of Death—the sleep of Sleep.

Thus sanction I the ungrateful seeming wish That I were homed now in this silent City, Or shared its rest apart, in some lone spot Of woods and waters, where the gentle Echo Is seldom summoned from her mossy cell, And then but to attend the wandering steps Of musing Melancholy. Ye would laugh, Doubtless, ye sons of pelf, at wish so wild! What could ye else? Wrapt up in selfish craft, Or wallowing besotted in the sty Of solitary gust, ye never knew 294 To feel the burthen of a human heart.

And ye, cold moralists, who discourse of Man— Man the mysterious! whose spirit’s depths Are as unsearchable as Ocean’s caves, As of some chorded instrument whose tones, However eloquent of sorrow and joy, And glory and love, are yet elicited By set appulses of apparent touch, And tested by a scale,—ye would declaim, Such wish is but the pining of a heart Surcharged with wild ambition, from the aims Of which, at length, Experience hath dispelled The sun-rise hues of Hope; or of a heart Jaded with animal indulgence, sought Through lawless passion.

O how wise are ye! At least so think your Cliques, and partly in that Resides your recompense; the residue, Weakness of Sages! condescends to take The shape of pence hot from the oily palms Of Earth’s fat squires, who, for example, bolt The moral physic with their daily soup; And thus full-blown with righteousness and food, And it may be from indigestion, too, More holily severe,—how promptly they Will then, from their judicial eminence, Thunder down judgment on some ragged wretch For daring to be hungry!

Oh! away! Ye, all complacent in the iron pride Of forms and dogmas, and those worldly rules Which centre all the decency of life 295 In being passionless, and bearing hearts Barred like a miser’s door, ‘gainst all assault Of sympathy,—how should ye apprehend In what guise recommended comes the wish That maketh Death so beautiful, to me— To me—a Poet, full of glowing power, Loving all pleasant and all excellent things— All loveliness, and yet unloved!—to one Hungry for joy, yet clung with misery! How recommended it may come to him Whose candour, and entire belief in Truth’s Divinity, but rendered him the more Suspected of his kind, and thence, indeed, Incompetent to bridge his daily way With safety, or with credit, o’er the deep And deadly pits, and broad obstructive moats, Wherewith Melevolence while his fellows sleep Dreaming of brotherhood, or godless Power Triumphant in the face of trembling crowds, And with such warrants armed as oaths and bribes Win from the wrested Laws,—do incommode The roadways of the world! Nay, how to him It comes, who daily wages hopeless war With savage wants, yet cannot stoop to act The parasite, or as a menial serve Whom the mere consciousness of being a man, And sofar independent, bids despise;— That moral self-assertion from within Sallying unbid, to which even Labor’s son Pretends not, but, with perfect right, may hold His born immunity in the midst of kings, And in its innate rectitude even dare Despise them, if they dare be vile!—Away!

Away! for such or worse is every phase Of my existence—even at the best. 296 Nay, when my hopes are liveliest and would live, Though surely dying, and with resolute heed I strive once more to take a stable hold Of nature and the world, my efforts still Avail not, and may only well compare With dry and hungry roots that blindly grope For moisture through a hard and barren soil That dooms them to the famine sweltering round!

Then be thou mine, thou sad but soothing Wish That makes Death beautiful to the weary one: The Wish foretasting, if no more, the long Inviolate sabbath of its voiceless rest. Come, utter to my burning heart thy cool And brook-like murmurs! Come; and with thee bring A spirit of philosophy, to chase Those spectral glooms wherewith wan Fear deforms Time’s dim glen leading to the grave;—thus warbling Unto the listening motions of my soul: What though the desolate death-bed of Want Be thorny, while the invisible cords of life By gaunt Disease, there only slothful, one By one, to the attenuating last, Are knawn in twain,—yet, this once passed, how thrice Desirable to Misery’s haggard child, Is that repose which breathes about the bier A holy tiding of unbroken rest?

And here sleeps soundly one who in the world Had little rest; and one whom well I loved For knowing well, and more, the more I knew him. A Youth as freely bounding in his spirit Once as a wave: but he grew dark of thought— And died! I one day asked him as we strolled Together, of his ailment, and he answered “The vanity of Hope!” And now ’tis mine 297 To know what tracts of desolate truth that phrase Doth volume! As I muse, out of some dim And secret cavern of past destiny A response, shuddering through the leaves of thought— Shuddering vibratory, like a knell,—seems drawn To verify its import; as it were The soul-heard doom-tone, lingering even yet Over some dateless epoch of my life, Whenceforth my heart insensibly began To lose the measure of its hopefulness— The melody of its earlier love, that sought To mingle with and swell the harmony Of universal good; and from which time My Being, graduating in despair, Hath day by day given up, even to the last Bright drop, its human faith: albeit I yet Believe in Good’s existense; it indeed Hath an oasis in this desert world, But Evil, like the sand, is everywhere! And hence, our youthful tendencies to love And friendship, all the sooner they are pushed Into the antagonistic crush of life, But give us so much earlier to know The vanity of Hope! Alas! that thus The warm and fertile heart of Youth itself Should, as it were, be but the living grave Of All its loveliest aspirations—all Its purest faiths,—all buried up,—all slain By the sharp swords of evil yesterdays!

And who so wounded and weary of heart as I, Could much frequent these tombs, and oft observe The unfenced grave respected by the foot Even of the lightest of the sabbath throng, Nor wish for such a home? Who thus forlorn, 298 Migh[t] fancy that the very gale, first heard So loudly boisterous in the neighboring wood, When passing here, as it were touched with awe, Humbled itself, and, sighing, gently moved The meek heads of memorial flowers to bend With pensive heed, as if they also felt That somewhat mourned by unforgetting Love, Was lying in the cold dark where they grow Pranking yon mound,—nor wish that he were laid With all his sorrows there?

Here—only here, In the dark dwellings of this silent City Is rest for the world-abandoned. Obloquy here, Desease and Poverty, forego their victim. The fox of envy and the wolf of scorn Snarl not within these gates. The enemy Who comes to triumph o’er the powerless bones That once he feared, still hates,—even as he comes, By the dismaying silence smitten, stops, As listening to some far reproachful voice Heard only through the mystery of his soul, And, shuddering, asks forgiveness. Here would I sleep, And should an enemy so plead, and might My injured spirit, hovering over, hear,— The boon were granted. O that here, even now, Were frozen into forgetfulness the sense That I, on this so populous star of God, This Earth that I was born to, and have loved, Am utterly uncared for and alone!
