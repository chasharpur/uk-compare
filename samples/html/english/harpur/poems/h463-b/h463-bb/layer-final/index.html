Milton.

[His Minor Poems.]

As through some stream’s most christal purity, The virgin gold is seen below, we see Through the immortal Mask as by it flows, In lengthening music to its happy close,— That drossless chastity of heart and thought Which stored the youthful Poet as he wrought; With all the visionary affluence That tempered the stern grandeur of his sense.

Then in his Carol for the Virgin’s Son, Toned like a trumpet for some victory won Amid the wondering Shepherds placed, we mark Heaven’s high heraldry descend—and hark! While burning choirs, in star-like clusters, gem 58 The midnight gloom that shroudeth Bethlehem, How swelling up in utterance divine, Their tidings peal through each triumphant line! The theme is changed: wild lamentation loads The ear of Doom; for now the Infernal Gods From their pontific thrones suddenly hurled, Forego, despairing, the re-conquered world! And hellward all, in the tumultuous verse, Like far-off thunders heavily disperse.

With Melancholy then, how sweet to rove, Whilst the wild moon seems wandering lost above, ‘Mid clouds that like drear woods and mountains grey Blind up the track of her accustomed way; And hear in spirit our footstep’s solemn beat Just reach the echo of that still retreat, Whence streaming out, from Thought’s mysterious tower, The lamp’s long blade divides the midnight hour: Or thrown in fancy on the shaded grass, To mourn the ocean-whelmëd Lycidas, Under the “opening eyelids” of the day, And till the sun “drops in the western bay.”

Then, for relief, let Fancy woo such dreams As to his youth were known, by haunted streams Sleeping,—till music, like the soul of Love, Came breathing from some Nymph-concealing grove;— Or joyously upstarting, she is free To dance o’er the green earth with glad Euphrosyne! Jest in her train with him who Care derides, And laugh with Laughter, “holding both his sides.” x

x If I were asked to give in three words the quality which I think most distinguishes Milton’s poetry 59 from that of all other British writers, I should say, pict- -uresque and ideal suggestiveness. He seems to have had a fuller sense and to have enjoyed a more perfect possession of the imaginative presence of the persons and things of his subjects than any other of our poets; and hence it is that a single word in him, by the intense force of its ideal selectness, becomes so often the key to a completeness of effect that strikes like magic; as in the following examples

The floods stood still, like walls of brass, While the Hebrew bands did pass, But full soon they did devour The tawny king with all his power.

How real and present does this complexional attribute of the king render the whole picture.

And as in this:

Tells how the drudging Goblin sweat To earn his cream-bowl duly set, When in one night, ere glimpse of morn, His shadowy flail had threshed the corn.

Here the shadowy flail may be said actually to substantiate the spiritual operation of the Goblin

And as in this:

I hear the far off Curfue sound, Over some wide-watered shore, Swinging slow with sullen roar.

Here by the magic of a single word we have the curfew both + a hand as well, while we are listening, at the rope that swings it: and thus as oft and near at the same moment—we hear it with the Poet far away in the watery distance, and give the ringer a hand as well at the rope: and thus its reality is instantly established.

And this:

Let my lamp at midnight hour, Be seen in some high lonely tower

Not only do the woods in italics identify the object but they give a lid of eye as it were to our very imaginations wherewith to behold it the more definitely

And again in this:

The air was calm, and on the level brine Sleek Panope with all her sisters played.

What a key to the whole calm is this in the word line, and how the sleekness of Panope harmonises with and insensifies the wateriness of every thing else.

Like instances might be given to the extent of a volume from his entire writings. And without this kind of excellence in a prime degree no port can be epical. Its frequency is the spell that can alone reattract us again and again within the sea-like and self-shrinking vastitude of a long heroic Poem. It is that too, which can alone give sufficient reproductive scope to profound criticism. Still, as in Milton, a chastity of judgment bordering on abstemiousness is essential to its judicious exercise. The evoked Idealism must in nowise be disrobed of the dimness of distance, however largely. 60 it be made to loom into the subject. The suggestiveness of the effect re- -sides in the choiceness it may be of a single word—and there it must be left entire.
