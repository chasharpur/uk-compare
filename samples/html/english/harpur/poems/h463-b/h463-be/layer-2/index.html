As through some virgin stream’s pellucid flow The pebbled gold is seen to shine below, So see we through his Masque, as by it flows In lengthening music to its happy close, That native worth of feeling and of thought Which stored his youthful spirit as he wrought, With all the visionary affluence That tempered the stern grandeurs of his sense.

Then in his Carol for the Virgin’s Son— When, like a trumpet for some victory won, ‘Tis sounding,—with the wondering hinds, we see Round us descending heaven’s bright heraldry! And while their Choirs, in star-like clusters, gem The midnight gloom enshrouding Bethlehem, Hark! swelling forth in utterance divine, How their glad tidings peal through each triumphant line! The theme is changed:—wild lamentation loads The ear of Doom! For lo, the infernal Gods, Down from their shrines and thrones pontific hurled Forego, despairing, the re-conquered world, 311 And hellward all, through the tumultuous verse, Like outward wasting thunders heavily disperse!

Now with Il Penseroso let us rove Whilst the wild Moon seems wandering lost above, Through clouds that like drear woods and mountains grey Blind up the track of her accustomed way, And hear, in spirit, our footfall’s solemn beat Just reach the echo of that still retreat, Whence streaming forth from Thought’s mysterious tower, The lamp’s long list of light divides the midnight hour.

Or thrown in fancy on the shaded grass, Mourn we the ocean-whelméd Lycidas In the new glory of the morn, or while The sun, in setting, crowns some western isle, And, with her visioned train, “sleek Panopé” Figures the shining levels of the sea, And from the “guarded mount” Thought sees aspire The Archangel’s presence like a tower of fire! So shall the countenance of Nature borrow Such life and depth from our melodious sorrow, That ever thenceforth, even the loss we rue Shall make each field fresh, every pasture new.

Or, for relief, let Fancy woo such dreams As to his youth were known, by haunted streams Sleeping,—till Music, like the soul of Love, Came breathing from some Nymph-concealing grove: Or joyously up starting, she is free To dance o’er the green earth with glad Euphrosyné! Jest in her train with him who Care derides, And laugh with Laughter “holding both his sides.”

Note.

Were I asked to give in the fewest words possible, the quality which I think most characterises the poetry of Milton, I should say, a certain picturesque and ideal suggestiveness, itself the unforecasted product of intensity of conception. He seems to have had a fuller sense, and to have enjoyed a more perfect possession, of the imaginative presence of the persons and things of his subjects, than any other of our great Poets, with the exception perhaps of Spencer. And hence it is, that a single word in him, by the intense force of its ideal selectness, or of its picturesque propriety, becomes so often the key, as it were,—or the Aladdin’s lamp,—to an effect that strikes upon us like absolute magic; revealing, by disclouding, a whole world of imagination, and alike unlocking the golden gates of Heaven, or the grim and ghastly portals of Hell.—Take the following examples of this peculiar and distinguishing happiness.

The floods stood still, like walls of brass, While the Hewbrew bands did pass, But full soon they did devour, The tawny king with all his power.

How real and present does that complexional attribute of the Egyptian monarch make the whole picture? Again:

Tells how the drudging Goblin sweat To earn his cream bowl duly set, When in one night, ere glimpse of morn, His shadowy flail had threshed the corn.

313

Here the “shadowy flail” actually substantiates (so to speak) the spiritual operation of the Goblin. In other words, its vagueness is so appropriately in keeping with, and significant of, the nature of the operation referred to, that it gives to an imagination somewhat of the body, and all the force of a fact. Again:

I hear the far off curfew sound Over some wide watered shore, Swinging slow with sullen roar.

Here, by the magical selectness of a single word, we have the curfew both afar off and near at the same moment of time. We hear it, with the Poet, sounding far away in the watery distance, and yet lend the ringer a hand as well at the rope which swings it; tingling even as he does, with its immediate vibrations: and thus the imaginative validity of the thing, and of every associated circumstance, is most sufficingly authenticated.

Let my lamp at midnight hour Be seen in some high lonely tower

Not only do the italicised words in these lines identify the inside of the tower, as the abode of the lonely student, but they also give a second sight, as it were, to our imagination, wherewith to define it objectively, as seen from without, and at a distance.

The air was calm, and on the level brine Sleek Panopé with all her sisters played.

What a key to the whole calm of the season is there 314 in that one word level, and how the sleekness attributed to Panopé harmonies with and intensifies the wateriness of everything else.

Again: speaking of the breaking up of the Satanic council in the great hall of Pandemonium:

Their rising all at once was as the sound Of thunder heard remote.

We are absolutely present, for the moment, at this dread council: for who has not been one of a large congregation under the sounding roof of a church, when it has all at once risen at the requirements of the service? Or of a great seated meeting in some public hall, when it has suddenly started to its feet in the act of breaking up?

Finally: observe in the following example, how audibly confirmed is the imaginative place and presence of the thing described, by the one word echoing. We are inducted by it, on the instant, into the sounding aisles of the banian.

There they chose The fig-tree; not that kind for fruit renowned, But such as at this day, to Indian’s known, In Malabar or Decan spreads her arms Branching so broad and long, that daughters grow About the mother-tree,—a pillared shade High over-arched, with echoing walks between.

Like instances might be given, to the extent of a volume, from the whole body of his writings. And without this kind of excellence in a prime degree, no poetry can be epical. 315 Its prevalence is the spell which can alone re-attract us, again and again, within the ocean-like and self-shrinking vastitude of such a poem as the Paradise Lost. It is, moreover, that peculiar quality which affords the most ample scope to profound criticism. Still, as in Milton, a chastity of judgment bordering upon abstemeousness, is requisite to the judicious exercise of the faculty in which it originates; for without a correspondency of reserve and tact, it may lead to mere penciling, or degenerate, as it sometimes does in Shelley, into a laboriously vicious creativeness. The evoked idealism must be in nowise disrobed of either the misty dimness of distance, or the dusky viel of partial concealment, however largely it may be made to loom upward into the general subject of the picture. Or the suggestiveness of the effect may be resident (as in most of the foregoing examples) in the marvellous choiceness of a single word—and there it must be left entire. Add aught, however little, and all is instantly overcharged, or brought so far forward as to become familiar: our wonder is glutted, and consequently endamaged.

There is another remarkably spellable quality in Milton’s poetry—if it be another, for it is infused into it no doubt by the same ideal intensity:—I mean the singular impressiveness of his poetical geography,—that is to say, of his imaginative mention of ancient and far off localities. When, for example, he speaks of Satan’s ranging over the orb of the earth,

“Up beyond the river Ob”;

or when he makes mention of

”Abana and Pharphar, lucid streams”;

not only do we seem to be carried into the presence of those limpid celebrities, but to become one in spirit with all the thousand generations that have lived and died upon their banks and drank of their waters. The localised being of the Past, indefinite of form, but ideally granted, seems at once to overshadow the living verge of the Present, till both are commingled into a kindred consistence;—and thence, for the time, we live as it were in the breath of both—as having dwelt of old, and as still sojourning in the pilgrimage of our thoughts,

“Up beyond the river Ob,”

or by

“Abana and Phasphor, lucid streams.”

In the midst, however, of all this superinduced dreaminess, we possess of course a pleasing consciousness of the remoteness of these places in absolute fact. Nevertheless, they become thenceforth endowed in our sense of them with a sort of twofold existence—a remotely ideal as well as a material and actual one: being incorporated into the map of our physical relations, and blending, at the same time, like the dimly recurring scenes of a wanderer’s childhood, with the reverted prevalence of our souls.

One of the general charges made by poetical readers against the poetry of Milton is, first, that it wants tenderness, and secondly, that it exhibits too little of a sensibility to the charms of female beauty. Both these charges, however, are not merely untenable, but flagrantly indiscriminate. That Milton “with his singing robes about him”, could not descend to those effiminate airs and languishments of desire out of which our modern pictures of love and beauty are mostly elaborated, may be very well admitted, for it is well that he could not. And it is well also that his sage spirit was above indulging in that steamy sort of admiration for mere personal perfection in the female sex, which is only excusable in the undiciplined natures of the very young, and which should be cured even in them, as no doubt it would be, by frequent fasting. But enough of this. Was that poetic soul untender, or insensible to feminine loveliness, in whose lonely recesses a Vision like the ensuing could grow up and flourish like the one palm-tree of an oasis, even when the body enclosing it was blind and old—”in darkness, and with dangers compassed round”? The passages is long, but I must give it nearly entire. It has all sorts of merit;—beauty of conception with precision of language, while the melodious variety of the parts evolve throughout into a perfect harmonic uniformity. But above all things, let the reader observe in it, the exquisite indirect, unconscious compliment which Eve is made to render to her own personal loveliness, and the artless emphasis of every expression in the 318 lines conveying it. They impicture the imagination with an idea (just as the lake was before impictured with an image) of supreme feminine beauty; and thus our rapturous assent to the meritoriousness of the compliment is inevitably secured; while the satisfaction at which we arrive when uttering the last three words —”smooth watery Image,” has nothing beyond it. It is an imaginative enjoyment having a “measureful content” —a finality within itself.

The minor poems which most exemplify the peculiarly sublimative character of Milton’s genius, are Lycidas and the Hymn on the Nativity. Almost every stanza in the last mentioned production is the ideal quintessence of some ivied legend, some twilight myth, or some time-shadowed tradition— things that have hung so long in the sunset spaces of Imagination, as to have become saturated, like evening clouds, with the last hues of its nature. Almost every line is a picture, coloring as it were the dim and lifeless regions of the recorded Past with the reflected immortality of thought and passion—as these ray forth everlastingly from the poetic spirit of “one Milton”, as from some early star that shineth out of a cloudless heaven, upon the grey desolation of a Babylon, or of a Tadmor in the wilderness. Certainly, in no other poem in the language of equal length, is there anything like the same amount of prime poetry. Then on the mere score of versification how unique and vigorous it is—especially in the profluent sweep and volume of its alexandrines: as in the following examples:

“While birds of calm sit brooding on the charmèd wave.”

“Swinges the scaly horror of his folded tail”.

Where else may we look for alexandrines like these, 320 except occasionally—few and far between—in Spencer, and once or twice in Shelley’s Ode to a Skylark?

I will add somewhat upon the style and conduct of Milton’s main production—the Paradise Lost. Its prime characteristics, as to style, are, as was noticed by his friend Marvel,—”gravity and ease”. The phraseology is everywhere as far removed from anything strained and mouthy as from aught that is bald and frivolous. Neither is there anything merely curious or starched in the general texture of the composition, or tangled and eked out in the evolution of the measure.

Yes: thus ends the mightiest Epic in the world, with a quietude and easiness of manner that might have dissatisfied a Poet less epical in his art, but which breathe of the very nature and august presence of the highest epical propriety.

The critical prate at one time so much in vogue about a falling off in the latter Books of the poem was captious in the extreme, to my thinking, and even silly. But you could not once mention its title, without being met with, “Oh, but how it falls off as you get far into it.” Now I who have read it many times can detect no such declension in any part of it. On the contrary, the treatment of the 321 subject throughout, appears to me to be equally epical. Are not the seventh and eighth Books full of marvellous beauty, world-wide and various as that of Nature herself? And what falling off is there in the ninth? while for sublimity of invention, for grandeur of illustration, and the wonderfully diversified modulations of the verse, the tenth Book is certainly quite equal if not in some capital respects superior, to any that precedes it. Then observe that portion (perfect in every way) of the eleventh Book, be- -ginning with the 141st. line, and ending with the 250th; and subsequently, in the same Book, the whole of the vision- -pictures, commencing with the 429th line, and thence running all through it: in one of which—the death of Abel— that ghastliest of all recorded facts,— the fact, namely, that the first man who tasted of death, was murdered! and by his brother! is given with a hearty force that is unexpectedly terrific.
