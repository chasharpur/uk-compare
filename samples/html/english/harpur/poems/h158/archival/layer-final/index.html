POET’S CORNER.

THE GRAVE OF CLEMENTS. *

[I had travelled the most of the day over rugged mountains, which lay range after range around me, like a sea of billows, but some held sway, as it were, over the rest, peering like rival em- perors above them, and I had now descended into a deep hollow glen, in the midst of which I observed carved, upon a tree, “Clements.” I had heard of his fate, which induced me to turn out of the road to see his grave: it was a calm evening; the sun was going down over the western summits, leaving his glory behind him, as a prince casts off his robes on retiring to his slum- bers: the evening breeze had ceased to whisper, and all was peace. I leaned over the grave, and involuntarily sighed, “poor Clements;” for I felt a strange sympathy for the dead;—I imagined him on that fatal day just like myself—young, healthful, and buoyant—feeling all the wildness of the scene, or perhaps contrasting them with those happier ones, where all he loved was—far away —and indulging in the still hope, that on some future day he should again meet a mother’s wel- coming tear, or a father’s smile. But it was not thy lot, said I, and it may not be mine. I next turned my thoughts to his parents; I pictured them, old and grey, mourning for a beloved son, whom they should see no more; but, said I, his fate may be yet unknown to them; they may not have yet given up the hope of again meeting him; but did they but know, that even now, the sum- mer flower rears its head upon his lonely grave, far in a wilderness, where a civilized being seldom treads—where the wild dog holds his nightly revels—where the savage, halting for a moment, points out to his children the “white man’s grave”—they would weep—and I wept. The night was setting darkly in the glen—and I dropt a parting tear upon the grave, and hurried on.]

O, sympathy! so gratifying thou art, To his friends, if he had such, thy nectar impart; For thy tears e’en are gleams on the wint’ry fate— E’en are charms to the fast-breaking heart. Like moon beams shed on the gloomy wild— Like wreaths of flowers to the dying child.

Without thee, the stranger, would mourn; Unheeded, the invalid smart; As dews to the floweret, as showers to the corn, Is sympathy sweet to the heart.

She’s a pensive calm to the troubled mind; A returning ray of light to the blind; A pillow of down to the withered brave— And a glittering tear on the lonely grave.

Sydney, April 15, 1833. C.H.

* An emigrant murdered on the road to Hunter’s River, by the notorious Donohoe, and his gang.
