My Love.

She was not beautiful, they said, The cold of heart and stern of head, Who measure by some given line The meet, the graceful, and the fine: But there was something round her being That gifted me with rarer seeing; And there was something in her voice That made my very soul rejoice With an exceeding joy—for she Was more than beautiful to me.

I asked not, are her glances bright? I felt them lovely in their light: I asked not, is her visage fair? I felt a soul of sweetness there: It was my fancy’s dearest dwelling: I asked not, is her form complete? My heart was kneeling at its feet, To feel,—and ’twas enough,—that she Was more than beautiful to me.

Note—A person to whom I read this Poem, objected to the expression, “my heart was kneeling at her feet”. How, said he, can a heart be said to kneel? I made him no answer. I could make him none. It was criticism with a vengeance—stupidly profound: a disheartening revelation of the Great Deep that impassably divided our individual perceptions. And there is there is nothing more drearily isolating, than for the moment, than to find that a mystery to another, which is sufficiently / however ideally/ evident to one’s self.
