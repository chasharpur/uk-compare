“Yes”

My soul is raying like a star— My heart is happier than a bird— And all to hear, through Fortune’s jar, One promissory Word.

A sound as simple as the low Quick sliding gurgle of a rill, And yet of power to overflow A world with blissful will.

I feel as though the very air Was breathen from the heart of Love; 168 As Pleasure in the sun’s bright lair Sat brooding like a dove.

A billow of the sunny sea— A cloudlet of the summer sky— How wide is their felicity! So widely blest am I.

O Beauty, through one little word What boundless powers are thine, to bless! O Love, a seraph’s voice is heard In thy confiding Yes. x

x What Boethius says of Friendship may be applied with equal propriety to Love: “it has its origin in heaven, is a sacred felicity, and ought not to be numbered with the gifts of Fortune”: and in this spirit I have placed in the text, the promise of the one in direct opposition to the threat of the other. No doubt the mere Economist will declare this to be altogether wrong, upon the strength of a profound pinch of mathematical snuff; and when the human heart can be worked to a given result as mechanically as a sum in the rule of three, he will be altogether right—not till then. But he is a philosopher and I am only a poet. He knows he is right upon the infallible showing of a moral Gunter which he carries in his pocket; but Nature feels that he is wrong from an innate dislike of all procrustean processes. He has no faith but in social parellelograms, and she abhors them. He would eschew all risks; and there is a paramount conviction at her motherly heart, that these are the grand suggestives of all that is devoted in feeling and heroic in action; and that upon his mere plan, even if practicable up to the painless perfection of its theory, her very divinity would die out of man in half a dozen generations—and hence she will ever signalize his attempts to intrude beyond the vestibule of her temple with “its all very well, Mr. Ferguson, but you don’t live here.”x But he is not yet done with me—not till he has given me a hair to split. Is not his consciousness a key to Nature as well as another’s? Yes; but with a ward wanting; that one through which the spirit of Shakspeare yearned into kindred with the whole world, and which has its sentient type in a live heart—and hence, I repeat, her inner temple will ever be a “far countree” to the mere Economist. Still he is commendable fellow enough, 169 so long as he modestly restricts himself to the tabular utilities of his vocation.

C.H.
