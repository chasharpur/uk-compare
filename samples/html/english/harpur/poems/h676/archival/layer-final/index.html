IV.

What’s a Boon?

“Our Constitution is a Boon,” say noodles, As men born hers were under some strange ban, And to be petted into loyal poodles, With but a dog’s right to the Rights of Man!

Note.—Even that knowing fellow and cunning (only cunning) statesman, Mr. E. Deas Thomson, talks glibly about the rights which the almightily beneficent Imperial Government has just “bestowed” upon us. But I can tell him, that the days are fast coming, when an Australian will be apt to spit in the face of any one who shall dare so to talk in his presence. Our feeling then (as it should be now) in Constitutional matters,will be this: “If England will not rule us with a full recognition of all the rights proper to us as men, she shall not rule us at all: we will take the matter into our own hands.” At least, thirty thousand Australians, by birth or adoption, think as I do in this matter, even now, and what if they also felt as I do? Why, no hybrid, no pretensive, no bungled system of Government, could exist in the land for a single year. And in the event of our tumbling such a Government upon the heads of its minions, what power, on earth, could attempt to coerce us, with any other result than the provocation of our contempt? But then our trade would be perilled by such a course of action! What of that? Half a dozen great national rights asserted and established, would outweigh in value, our whole clip of wool for half a century, with all the immediate architectural splendour of our sea- port, pitched into the scale with it; for the country would then begin to grow men as well as wool, and alchemise golden characters as well as golden nuggetts. Wrong Governments will always exist, so long as men are afraid to uproot them at once. But when the con- viction becomes general, that only what is right and equal, can be productive of political good, and that all wrong, to be effectively throttled, must be dealt with summarily, they will cease for ever—they will become impossible. No doubt, Mr. Thomson will sneer at this, as so much republican fume, for such a man as he, is too cunning, ever to be wise.
