Point in Poetry.

The Poet’s work, that should at least seem free, By too much point becomes mere carpentry. The highest and the noblest growths of wit Are never, or but seldom, touched with it: For of the Ideal ’tis but truly born When made the apex of a burst of scorn, Or irony, or hate, all torture-torn; Not to improve the passion, but to make The wave, full surging, on its object break. No doubt, to verse didactic it is free, But verse didactic is not Poetry. The Muse should be a teacher, but refuse To be a mere schoolmistress should the Muse: She should instruct us, but her methods never Be set ones, academically clever. Her morals, like great Nature’s morals, aye 322 Should work themselves out in an unforced way, And not so patly, as to hint the while At cryptic ingenuities of style. Whate’er the theme, her ethic lights should shine Full forth as from a central heat divine, Or heat inherent to the passion wrought Into the chastened harmony of thought, And not be like extraneous coals of fire Blown for the nonce into unusual ire. —But I have wandered from the point, I see, Which first I aimed at—Point in Poetry.
