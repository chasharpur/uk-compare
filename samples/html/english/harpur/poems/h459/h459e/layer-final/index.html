A Rhym[e]

BY CHARLES HA[RPUR]

A Rhyme of Love in loneliness— But sound and sweet. though lonely; And eke of Hope in unsuccess— But thence the braver only.

1.

Blissless am I, except in this That I delight to sing of bliss; And loveless, save that oft and long I love to sing of Love—a loving Child of Song.

2.

And thus beguiled, behold How all my Years, from youth to manhood bold, (Like roes that on lone Hermon skip Fast by the pilgrim, in a careless train) Have I let pastward trip, Ungathering aught that men call gain; Though deeply laden as a full-fraught ship With many a costly thoughted strain, And shearing oft a golden clip From dream-flocks folded in the procreant brain, And fed in Fable-Land—that bowery strip Yet left of Paradise—that one domain Whose blossomy beauty no frore winters nip;— Wherefore the kind world thinks—and even I Myself think sometimes, with a faithless sigh, That I have almost lived in vain, Thus letting Fortune slip: And most it moves me, does this self-disdain, When some snob-Crœsus, monkey-mean in feature— Some Mammon-glorified inglorious creature, That, like a gaudy upstart butterfly, Was yesterday a grub,—comes flaunting by With purse-pride in his eye! For then, in sooth, it seemeth a disgrace To have let so very a lump of earth Out run me (though the prize were nothing worth) In any race!

3.

But soon again my heart fills—and I sing Triumphantly again Some rich-rhymed Orphic strain! Yea, so much stronger than my will Is ever the love of Song—that still I only have my natural fling, Only am happy,—caroling! For then joy-flashing eyes and love-dewed lips, And things that company best with these,— Gleeful birds on splendid wings, 12 sh flowers, and shady trees with golden pips Orient minstrel sings,) my thoughts all thought of gain, As ‘twere a thing most vain! Joy-flashing eyes and love-dewed lips! For ever and aye of these My Fancy, like a honey-sucker, sips, Arbored round with Eden trees All starred with golden pips; Or, upon a silk-soft cheek Lying sleek, She suns contentedly in those Her little, lazy, loving self, Evën like some fairy elf Pillowed on a blushing rose. Yes, thus, with me, the world by goes, Whilst other—duller men enjoy Its real loaves and fishes— Each rosy substance of my rosiest wishes, Ev’n till they cloy.

4.

But if a thought like this, Will mar at times my tuneful bliss, By so much stronger than my will Is still the spirit of Song—that still Turning to the Muse’s spring, At once therein my heart I steep, at once again to sing Of eloquent eyes That are of sunny thoughts the ever-sunny skies! And gracious lips Dashed with such honey-dew as young Passion sips! And what associate best with these,— Birds and streams and flowers and trees All starred with golden pips.

5.

But sometimes quitting Themes so sweetly—sweetly song-befitting; Luminous fancies of luminous eyes Whence Love looks, thought-veiled, as from sunset skies The light glows richer for the half eclipse It taketh so divinely wise From Heaven aërial draperies; Sweet dreams that swarm round honied lips Like honey bees; Glad birds, fresh streams and flowers, and trees All starry with golden pips;— Sometimes quitting such delectable themes, Tho’ seeming loth,—ev’n as a breeze That long hath dallied amid summer leas, A perfume-laden loiterer seems,— I clap to the yearnings of my Lyre Loud forth rushing wings of fire, Or more to keep the sensual under Wrap them in soul-shaking thunder, While for the noble and the wise, The valiant and the free, I sing of that great popular emprise 13 Of hardy progress. which, well willed, to be Tendeth the sooner in Man’s destiny— Willed by the noble and the wise, Willed by the valiant and the free! When Truth, long watching, from her Alpine chair Shall read in the lore of the reddening skies The advent of her jubilee; And all true men shall seek her there Where the prophet peaks arise Out of the shattering mist—the phantom sea Of old iniquity,— And thence high up, where bloom in heaven’s sublime The sun-flowers of the prime, God’s herald glories of the golden time About to be;— Through dense and rare shall seek her there, Breathing with lion lungs the strenuous mountain air Of a supreme, up-climbing liberty!

6.

And thus employed, my purpose is most clinging: For though unto the present I Am nothing, I would be no less Unlike to those who, Mammon’s torch enringing Like moths, in their vain worship fly Into its blaze at last—and, blasted, die The prey of a splendid wretchedness; And something too for my harmonious singing, To those diviner times My Country yet shall know, and wisely knowing, bless! Yea, something for the silver pealing chimes, And for the beauty that fades not by Of soul-enriching rhymes! A star of Poesy in the future’s sky Throbbing with deathless thought, and to the praise That honied lips shall breathe and eloquent eyes shall raise.

7.

With moods and aims thus lovely and strong, Albeit in lowliest station, And nameless to the passing generation, I labor early—labor late and long, Even like the Bards of old, To fix each rich dream in as rich a song; As one should set some priceless gem In many figured gold: The gold of a glorious diadem, Which, on the mighty heads of a long line Of monarch Poets (each from his own mine Of values, adding to the heritage A round of lustres,) is, from age to age, Foredoomed to sway The heart of a great People, great and sage,— Yea, growing more and more divine From birth and nurture in the holier day Shed forth on every hand— Shed with a supersensual shine, Flood-like, o’er the whole broad Land, From Truth’s for evermore disclouded shrine.
