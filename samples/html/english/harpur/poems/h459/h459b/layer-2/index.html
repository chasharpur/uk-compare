Rhymes.

I.

The Poet.

Blissless am I, except in this That I delight to sing of Bliss; And loveless, save that oft and long I love to sing of Love—a loving Child of Song. Thusly, like roes that on their mountains skip, Hitherto, have I let my Years all trip Ungathering aught that men call gain; Wherefore the kind world thinks—and I Do sometimes think, even while I sing,— That I have lived in vain, For letting Fortune give me so the slip: Most when some station-gilded thing That, like a gaudy butterfly, Was yesterday a grub, comes flaunting, fluttering by.

But soon I turn again and sing. By so much stronger than my will Is this strong love of Song—that still Joy-flashing eyes and love-dewed lips, And things that garland best with these, Happy birds on splendid wing, 9 Clear streams, fresh flowers, and shady trees All starred with golden pips, Drive from my thoughts all thought of pelf! Joy-flashing eyes and love-dewed lips! Forever still of these My Fancy, like a honeysucker, sips; Or suns contentedly in those Her little lazy loving self, Even like some Fairy Elf Pillowed the while upon a blushing rose. Still thus, with me, the world by goes, Whilst other—duller men enjoy Its real loaves and fishes— Each rosy substance of my rosiest wishes, Even till they cloy.

But if a thought like this Will mar at times my blissless bliss; By so much stronger than my will Is still the love of Song—that still I turn me to the Muses’ spring To dip my heart therein, and sing Of eloquent eyes That are of sunny thoughts the sunny skies; And gracious lips Dashed with such honey as young Passion sips; And what associate best with these, Birds, and streams, and flowers, and trees All starred with golden pips.

And thus employed, my purpose is most clinging: The more so, that as to the Present, I Am nothing, I would be Yet unlike those who, Mammon’s torch enringing Like moths, live only in its blaze to die; And something to better—wiser times My Country yet shall see— 10a Something for my sweet singing, And for the wealth that fades not by Of soul-enriching Rhymes! A star of poesie in the Future’s sky Throbbing with deathless thought, and to the praise That honeyed lips shall breathe and eloquent eyes shall gaze.

Yes, with this aim in lowliest station, And nameless to the passing generation, I labor—labor heartily and long, Even like the Bards of Old, To set each rich dream in as rich a song; As one should bed some priceless gem In many-fingered gold: The gold of a splendid diadem, Which on the heads of a mighty line Of Monarch Bards is doomed to sway A Chosen People, grown divine From birth and nurture in the better day Shed over th[e] broad land by Truths disclouded ray.

The above Poem is perhaps the richest thing in the vein and spirit of its imagery that I have written; and I notice this particularly, because at the time of writing it, I was poorer in my outward circumstances than at any other period of my life:—out of employment, out of friends, out at elbows, out of hope. So desperate, both immediately and prospectively, was my worldly condition, that I began to revolve the subject of suicide in my mind, to consider whether, under such circumstances, it might not be an allowable thing. But, fortunately, my moral conclu= -sions were so far against it, that I have lived to see com= -paratively better days. One amongst many reasons dissuad= ing me 11 from it, I may as well mention. It was lest the fact should tell against poetry and poets: lest the low thoughted sneerer should have it to say: Such is the moral fruit of the Tree of poetical knowledge. And I was the more sensitive on this head, from feeling, that so far from being weakened in mind by my poetical studies and exercises, I had thence derived that very reflectional strength which was even then lighting me on to a great moral victory over the shame and pain of poverty:—From feeling, in short, that I had always been most faithful to every duty when most studious of the Beautiful, which is also the Good; and least observant of any, when most neglectful of the Muse.—I would add to the remark with which this Note accidentally made, as to a certain richness in the imagery of the text, that I apprehend it to be, at the same time, of a character only to be heartily appreciated by thorough poets—or by persons who are nearly such, through what Leigh Hunt calls the “grace of books”.
