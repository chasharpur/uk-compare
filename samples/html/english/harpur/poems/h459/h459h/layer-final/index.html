Miscellaneous Poems continued.

A Rhyme.

A Rhyme of Love in loneliness,— But sound and sweet, though lonely; And eke of Hope in unsuccess,— But thence the braver only.

Blissless am I, except in this That I delight to sing of Bliss; And joyless too, but for my bent To rhyme of Pleasure’s innocent; And loveless, save that oft and long I love to sing of love—a loving Child of Song

And thus beguiled, behold How all my years from youth to manhood bold,— Even like roes that on mount Hermon skip Fast by the pilgrim,—in a careless train Have I let pastward trip, Ungathering aught that men call gain: Howe’er the Gods in their divine Appraisements, may regard this seeming want of mine.

At all events, even so Thus hath it fortuned with me,—though Ever the while, even like a ship Of Ophir, laden low With many a golden strain: Shorn have I also many a costly clip From dream-flock folded in the procreant brain, And feel in Fable-Land—that bowery strip Yet left of Eden, that one sole domain Whose blossomy beauty no frore winters nip:— 70 Wherefore, I say, the world thinks—and even I Myself think sometimes, with a faithless sigh, That I have almost lived in vain, Thus letting Fortune through my fingers slip, With all that men call gain.

Yes, almost lived in vain! And most it moves me, does this self disdain, When some snob-Crœsus, monkey-mean in feature,— Some Mammon-glorified inglorious creature, That like an upstart butterfly, Was yesterday a grub,—flaunts flaring past With purse-pride in his scoundrell eye When me-ward it is cast! For then, in sooth, it seemeth a disgrace To have let so very a lump earth Outrun me (though the prize were nothing worth) In any kind of race.

But soon again my heart frills—and I sing Triumphantly again Some rich-rhymed Orphic strain: Gliding from the lyric string Swiftly and freely as a dove’s wing slips Forth from its bough perch in the shining Spring When no cold influence nips. Yea, so much stronger than my will Is ever the Spirit of Song—that still I only have my natural fling Am only happy—caroling.

Only am happy so, Caroling as I go, Happy and careless as a wood-born breeze 71 That does but gather as it onward slips A wealth of sylvan melodies. Yes; joy-lit eyes and love-dewed lips, And things that company best with these— Gleeful birds on wings of splendor, Fresh-blown flowers divinely tender, Streams in whose clear beauty dips Many a bough of bordering trees Loaded thick with golden pips Or murmurous with bees— Drive from my thoughts all thought of gain As ‘twere indeed a thing most vain, And much unfit to house within a Poet’s brain.

Thus, whether for my worldly weal or woe, From musing cometh all the gust I know, From singing all the fond heart-ease. Joy-flashing eyes and love-dewed lips! Ever, I say, of these That honey-bird, my Fancy, sips, Arbored round with Eden trees; Or upon a silk-smooth cheek Lying softly, lizard sleek She suns contentedly in those Her little lazy loving self, Even like some fairy elf Pillowed on a blushing rose: And still, to perfect their success, I mould These wonts of Fancy into songs of gold.

Still as I stand apart In social loneliness of mind and heart, Can I sometimes choose but sigh That thus the world with me has gone ungarnered by? 72 Whilst other—duller men enjoyed Its real loaves and fishes— Each rosy substance of my rosiest wishes, Even till its sweetness cloyed.

Yet, if a thought like this Will mar at times my tuneful bliss, By so much stronger than my will Is still the Spirit of Song—that still, Turning to the Muses’ spring, At once therein my heart I steep, at once again to sing Of eloquent eyes, That are of sunny thoughts the ever sunny skies! And gracious lips, Dashed with such honey dew as young Passion sips! And what associate best with these— Birds, and streams, and flowers, and trees All starred with golden pips, And populous with bees.

But sometimes quitting Themes so sweetly song-befitting: Luminous fancies of luminous eyes When Love looks though-veiled, as from sunset skies The light shows richer for the half eclipse It taketh so divinely-wise From heaven’s aerial draperies; Sweet dreams that swarm round honeyed lips Like honey-loving bees; Glad birds, fresh flowers, clear streams, and trees All starry with golden pips;— Yea, sometimes quitting such delectable themes, Though loth at first,—even as a breeze That long hath dallied amid Summer leas 73 A perfume-laden, would-be loiterer seems,— I clap to the yearnings of my Lyre Loud forth rushing wings of fire; Or more to keep the sensual under, Wrap them in soul-shaking thunder; While for all spirits ingenitely free I sing of that great popular emprise Of hardy progress, which, well-willed, to be Tendeth the sooner in Man’s destiny— Willed by the bravely wise!

Or with as loud and bold a chime I sing of that yet braver time, When world-wide Justice from her alpine chair Shall read at length in the rich-reddening skies The gospel of her advent, and declare The sacred sign of her epiphany there Amid the purple dyes; While all true men—the bravely wise Shall seek her there with fearless feet and free, Where the prophet peaks arise Out of the shattering mist—the phantom sea Of old iniquity! Through dense and rare shall seek her there, Breathing with lion lungs the rigid mountain air Of a supreme, up-climbing, God-great Liberty!

And thus employed, my purpose is most clinging: For though unto the mole-eyed Present I Am almost nothing, I would be no less Diverse from those who, Mammon’s torch enringing Like moths, in their vain worship fly Into its blaze at last—and blasted, die The prey of a splendid wretchedness! 74 And something too for my harmonious singing To those diviner spiritual times My Country yet shall know, and wisely knowing, bless! Yea, something for the silver-pealing chimes, And for the charm that never fadeth by, Of soul-enrhiching Rhymes! A star of Poesy in the future’s sky Throbbing with deathless thought, and to the praise That honeyed lips shall breathe and eloquent eyes shall raise.

With moods and aims thus rare and strong, Albeit in lowliest station, And nameless to the passing generation, What wonder that, despite neglect and wrong, I labor early—labor late and long, Even like the Bards of Old, To fix each rich dream in as rich a song? As one should set some priceless gem In many-figured gold:— In the gold of a glorious diadem! Which on the mighty heads of a long line Of Monarch Poets—each from his own mine Of mental values, to the heritage Adding a round of lustres in his day— Is thence foredoomed from storied age to age To temper and to sway The heart of a great People, great and sage— Yea, ever growing more sublime and bright From birth and nurture in the holier light Shed forth on every hand— Shed with a supersensual shine, Flood-like, o’er the whole broad Land, From absolute Truth’s at last disclouded shrine!
