A Friend in Need

I was needing—not needy, but just at a rise In my aims, when a lift would secure me a prize, And so I at once asked a Friend who of old Had vowed that he treasured my friendship like gold, And that to establish me, he, for his part, Would hold even cheap the red blood of his heart: “Will you lend me a hundred, to save the twice ten Per cent they exact in the Usurer’s den? At the same time I’d have you, my best Friend alive, Not to lose by the loan—but to charge me say five.

Well what did this excellent son of his mother, Who loved me (these words were his own) like a brother

Why, after a sigh full of exquisite feeling, And a look at the floor, and then one at the ceiling, He replied: “Will I lend you a hundred you say? Alas! my expenditure’s just that per day, In completing the wheel, as I yet hope to do, Of an income like that of old Rothschild the Jew. Besides, I’ve just given a hundred at least To a Church I deny, and the War in East; For my charity’s none of the home-keeping sort, But begins far abroad, where the other falls short. And then there’s a purse to be raised very soon, Towards releiving that lonely old man in the moon. And moreover, I’m bound to give fifty or so (As rosin) to Hauser’s divine fiddle bow; For he is our just now on the stage, And one cannot, you know, keep quite out of the rage. 2 Nay more, my dear Friend, (but of this be you mum) I am pledged to contribute a pretty round sum Tow’rds building a monument carved in relief To that Housemaid heroic, who strove with the thief For a bundle of small clothes till podged in the side (Right under the stays) with a knife—and so died But dying, sir, showed, would but Flunkeydom learn, What glory (with wages) even Housemaids may earn! My heart at the thought gets so vast in my breast, It will crack, if I pause not, and hush it to rest: For I—as you very well know,—even I Have slaved as a hind, till I thought I should die! Yea, till from my fingers blood dript to the ground, And the ache in my bones made me howl like a hound.

[A Pause

My Friend kept his countenance well for a while, But looked tickld at last, and then said with a smile This is bunkum, you’ll say: so it is, I admit, But the thrivers by bunkum must pet it a bit In short, I cant help you—I would if I could: I’m a trump—but I’m not in the cash lending mood. Yet be you not downcast, my very dear fellow, Since your health is so good, and your pipe is so mellow And the Vision of Genius, all lovely and rare, Are with you, no matter how badly you fare! And your appetite also, I doubt not, is fine, While a girl of sixteen might think meanly of mine. Ah my Friend in the health of your sylvan abode, Think of me in my counting-house pent like a toad, With marrowless bones,—a system quite shaken,— And a skin like the rind of an old flitch of bacon! Hence, though you’re so needy, and I am so rich Which is luckiest?—ah, there is no knowing which! 3 So good by—and (aside) go and die in a ditch! For what do I care how I was or how I have heretofore flattered him,—then isn’t now.

This muttered, my Friend made a hasty removal, With a grim covet smile of intense self approval. He had money, it seems, or to give or to lend To anyone—anything—save to the friend He had vowed that he prized at a world-full of gain, Aforetime when more in the brotherly vein! Had I begged of the man, with a List in my hand, Whereon his vile dole might have glared like a brand, Perhaps his five pounds he’d have bade me put down For the liberal look of the thing to the Town. Or to publish his rise through anothers decline, And sauce his purse pride with the torture of mine!

In the teeth of Belief he this bitter fact hurled There’s a natureless spirit abroad in the world! For they would lend only who have nothing to lend, They only be friendly who cannot befriend! While he who reviles them, whose fingers most itch To add a new heap to the hoards of the rich; And the beggar on horseback is he, be you sure, Who in riding to hell would ride over the poor! And yet there must here and there flourish a true man Or God would abolish a race so inhuman!

For all I experience—all that I see— Some fatal conviction comes ever to me; And the story I tell is beginning to hiss With the head of a Fury with one:—it is this: My old heartless Friend were he tougher than steel, Shall never succeed in completing the wheel 4 Of the mighty concern (as he yet hopes to do) That’s to make him as rich as old Rothschild the Jew! For the man who is false to a Friend in such need, As relieved, would but give him to prosper with speed Must club with the Devil of Ruin that day, And shock by the deed his good Angel away!

P.S. My old heartless friend, with a system quite shaken, And a skin like the rind of an old flitch of bacon,” Went of course, like a ninepin, erelong to the ground,— Leaving what to his creditor’s?—nix in the pound! And thus bankrupt—thus done for—thus character-stained And seen-through, for ever would have remained, Had not some then in power, for political ends, Sent him forth on a tour to his Brummagem friends. And so in effect, without meaning that same Did white wash and help him to play a new game! For once more, like a grub that has turned to a fly, With the help of the Devil, he’s buzzing on high, And again making wormy the moral belief Of all who much heed the success of the theif! Yet be patient! A man of pretense so profound Can lean upon nothing that’s lastingly sound, But only as seemingly risen once more, To turn out the failure he turned out before And fall as did Lucifer, never on high To be lifted again—unless hanged up to dry! While I who write this, still a Prophet shall stand—! One born & baptised in the light of the Land.

Finis
