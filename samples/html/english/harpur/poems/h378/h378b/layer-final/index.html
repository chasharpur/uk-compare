II.

Petty Pride.

If you would in state bide, At Fizgig’s festive hall, Never mind his great pride, But be tender of his small. And if your heart is some bit In widow Blaze’s purse, Hint rather she’s a strumpet, Than that once she was a nurse.

Note.—I have hitherto alienated, from time to time, in my career of life, many of my professed well-wishers— especially those of the ‘Pon-my-honour gentility genus— by unwittingly treading upon the bunions of their small pride, which I mostly found to be infinitely more sensitive than their great—if, indeed, of the latter sort, they had any at all. Now, having none about myself of the small conventional stuff I speak of (though not deficient in that higher self-valuation which constitutes our great pride), I am heniously liable to overlook the possible possession of it by others[gap] And in the effect of this habitual neglect of it in others—namely, a certain straightforward and unmincing avowal of social impressions, lies the whole secret of my by-past want of success in general society: one of the million of matters I have since found out in my solitary inquiries into causes. But men will yet know me better—and then the characteristic to which I refer, as having hitherto stood in my way, will become an admitted merit, and a social recommendation; and instead of being voted a fellow with too little veneration for “pastors and masters,” and all that, I shall be universally coun- tenanced, as a very instructive and tolerable bruin. Hitherto, however, it has stood in my way: for what is so pestilently prevalent in the world, as petty pride?— a tremblingly sensitive petty pride? Your lady Flamingo, who may be a publicaness, as well as a marchioness, is “all alive” with it. Opine that she cheats at cards, or double-chalks in the reckoning, and she may pass it off with a petulant “Well I’m sure!” or “You are very complimentary!”, but innocently suppose that her silken finery has been scoured, or that she has a sister a washerwoman, and the offence is deadly. Your genuine Snob, too—and his name is Legion, and his grade of every sort, and his place everywhere—he is “all compact” of it. Pull his nose, and in process of time, he may forgive you; but tell him, in the honesty of your belief, that it bears a striking family resemblance to the pieman’s, and he is your enemy for life.

P.S.—The social significance of the term “Snob,” in the peculiar sense in which it is used above, has never been defined, perhaps, so tersely as it deserves to be. It indicates, figuratively, an ass that would look like a lion; and means, literally, any poor human starveling, that would seem to be cutting a dash, without the means, or the soul, for cutting anything but a sorry figure, and the most ridiculous capers: in which sense, a lord may be a snob, and his shoemaker not[gap] A genuine nobody of this kind, having to announce, in the obituary of his news- paper, the awfully accidental death of his only child, will make even it the occasion for tracing his descent with painful minuteness, up to an apparent somebody, though his family tree shall have borne but one such apple—and that, too, in its native quality, shall have been little better than a wilding.
