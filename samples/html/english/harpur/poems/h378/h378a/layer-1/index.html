Small Pride.

If you’d long in state bide At Fizgig’s festive hall, Never mind his great pride, But be tender of his small.

And if you’d hope a plump bit From Madam Blaze’s purse, Hint rather she’s a strumpet, Than that once she was a Nurse.

Note. I have hitherto alienated, from time to time, in my career of life, many of my professed well-wishers, —especially those of the ‘pon-my-honor [unclear] gentility genus, by unwittingly wounding their small pride; which I mostly found to be infinitely more sensitive than their great;—if indeed, of the latter sort, they had any at all. Now having none about myself of the small Conventional stuff I speak of, /though not in the least deficient, I believe, in that reasonable and manly self-valuation which constitutes our great pride/, I am heinously apt to overlook the possible possession of it by others. And in the effect of this habitual neglect of it in others,—namely, a certain straightforward and unmincing avowal 70a avowal of social impressions, resides one of the prime secrets of my want of success in general society: 70b for what is so pestilently prevalent in world as petty pride? Your Lady Flamingo,—and she may be a publicaness as well as a marchioness,—is “all alive” with it. Opine that she cheats at cards, or double chalks in the reckoning, and she may pass it off with a petulant “Well I’m sure!” but suspects that she patches her petticoat, or has a sister a washerwoman,—and the offense is deadly. Your genuine Snob too,—and his name is Legion, and his grade of every sort, and his place everywhere;—he is ”all compact” of it. Pull his nose, and in process of time he may forgive you; but tell him, in the honesty of your belief, that it bears a striking family resemblance to the Pieman’s,—and he is your enemy for life.

P.S. The social significancy of the term Snob, in the peculiar sense in which it is used above may not be so generally known as it deserves to be. Figuratively, then, it means an ass that would look like a lion literally, any paltry human thing that would seem to be cutting a dash without the means or the soul for cutting anythings but contemptible capers: in which sense a lord may be a Snob, and his shoemaker not. A genuine Nobody of this kind, having to announce in the obituary of his Newspaper, the awfully accidental death of his overly only child, will make even it the occasion for tracing his descent, with painful minuteness, up to an apparent Somebody; though his family tree should have afforded but one such apple,—and though that too, in its native quality, should have been little better than a crab.
