Part VI.

The fog is all dispelled at length, And dazzlingly the day’s young strength Reflects from each moist rock, and through Their foliage fretting, drinks the dew In every gleaming tree; For up in the vast of heaven anew The sun hangs clear of each craggy height, Like a burning buckler burned with bright And nimble shooting rays of light, A glorious sight to see! (1.) But now the flyer hath lost his pride Of speed, and, on the mountain’s side Bounds shorter, stumbling as he goes: While, as through the echoey throes Along that hollow and cavernous way, Yearning into an audible being The woodland Spirit might seem to say:

“Faint not thus, from ruin fleeing, Child of the Forest, renew thy speed! So mortal sore is Sore is thy need! For oft amid these wild recesses The creature that now so near thee presses, Is seen to drive his lengthy shape— But when did ever his quarry escape? Then speed thee—child of the Forest, speed! Sore is thy need!”

The desperate wretch with mustered force, In hope of foiling, shifts his course— But hark, that yelp! short, thick, and hoarse— A hurried yelp of savage pride, Tells back, that Lightning’s at his side. Yet on, together, hip to hip,— On spring they, in dread fellowship; For the grim-jawed hound hath yet to seek 135 The ghasted forester’s outstretched neck, And there alone he’ll grip! Like some dim demon of the wind, Towler, half seen, sweeps hard behind; And Whip, whose ardent sobs confess His unrelenting savageness, Maintains with constant strain his place; Then Lady and her yellow twain, With all the rest, pour on amain: While from the left apace, Verging obliquely, lo, the train Of foremost Hunters how they gain Upon the closing Chase.

But mark yon copse of tough marroo (2.) With ashen saplings pillared through, And interlaced with thorns and briars, And shot (3.) with creepers that like wires Wind up the higher wood— Upstretching even from the rill That down yon gorge so wildly rude, ‘Neath vines that flaunt in wanton will Goes darkling with a lowly trill, See how it vestures, like a hood, The brow of the lonesome hill. Snapped full oft hath such a Copse The brightest link of the hunter’s hopes; For thick within its savage regions, Rank on rank, in woven legions, Lavaish growths of runners rise, Through which the hound his way must bore, Even while his prey, with practised eyes Detecting where the game-track lies, Bounds on, with Danger at his heels no more.

Is it hope gives sudden speed To the jaded Flyer’s need? Yea, he flattereth his care With the show of refuge there. 136 His tramping waketh into voice Within it many an obscure bird— Hark! the scolding yellow-bill’s noise And Jehu’s urging note (3.) are heard, With Tel sounding bell, And the dog-bird’s (4.) sudden bark as well, As starting af to the higher sprays, Alarmed, they view the comming Chase! While harder yet its victim strains, For in the cries of that noisy throng A promissory earnest reigns, To hearten him along! To him, as near their clamors ring, Seem they not even thus to sing? (5.)

“Fellow child of the forest primeval, Hitherward haste thee! Gain our wild hold, and those creatures of evil In vain shall have chased thee! Look! our guardian thorns shall meet Their coming, our woodvines entangle their feet.

Here secure doth the wallaroo (6.) feed, And the jumbuc, though wanting both cunning and speed; And here, with the jungle-bird, fearless and free As the eagles that fly In the clouds of the sky, Alone is there is Sh for thee! Haste then! haste with a resolute will— Haste! that the Forest may know the still.”

Right well the Flyer hath onward stretched— A moment’s grace, and the Copse were reached! Even now its spreading skirts (out tossed In strips and clumps that stand alone, As squadrons round a camping host In lines and clum are thrown) Part hide him: now is he more near: And now,—as about to disappear, 137 The Hunters shout at once their fear— “Lightning take heed! our hopes are vain If once the Flyer that jungle gain— Thy strength were nothing there!” Thus they—and in the dells about, One to the other as it were, “Nothing there!” the Echoes shout— “Nothing there!”

But shouted they in vain? I trow not!—well their timely hint That conscious hound hath ta’en! For lo, by the energetic dint Of one long leap, Quick as thought His quarry is caught, And, backward hauled, adown the steep Wheels with him fastened, round and round, With sideward tug and upward bound— Till Towler, with a headlong aim Throws in his aid and weighs the game With lunging downfal to the ground. Arriving in succession fast The rest pour in—and to the last All keen to grip, with bootless haste Their yet remaining vigor waste.

Up, too, the foremost Hunters run In silence, briefly touched each one By pity,—a swift inward sting! But soon the flaying is begun, Soon ended—and the quartering As deftly also done. Then harken how the mountains vast Through all their echoey caverns ring With calls that tell the slaughter’s past.

Like a glory-full monarch, the Lord of the day Hath passed from the world, in a wide Flushing pomp of right royal display, And the west is no longer with crimson dyed, And the glens gloom away In the twilight so grey, And the Bard that sang thus on the mountain side Hath ended his sylvan Lay.

And blame ye not, though his theme be uncouth, Nor look ye much that for his rhyme to be smooth, Or that his flight should be lofty and strong, Since he is both unschooled and young: Yea, one whose aptest years were passed In primal solitude wild and vast.

Thus nurtured, self urged, first he knew Australia’s virgin Muse to woo, And of Song’s bright mysteries ‘gan to guess With a lone self-cherished studiousness: While toiling along the plough’s first tracks, Or ‘tween the strokes of the felling axe, Or while upon the prairie wide Where the savage whooped and the wild dog cried, As round the squandered herd he hied.

Then be it enough that he hath told o’er Sports that never were sung before: And let not the cavils of critic display In thankless return for the sylvan Lay Which he hath framed in the joy of his heart And poured on the winds of the world to day, Give his spirit in lonely abashment to smart; And as homeward he wends with glad Whip at his side, Humble the step of his bardic pride.

Notes to Part VI.

(1.) The reader will observe here, through the medium of the images introduced, the notation of time. At starting the game kangaroo, the sun was just risen as it were on the mountains: it now appears hanging clear of them in the sky. This will give—say, ten minutes or so; the pursuit being thus unusually prolonged, as before observed, by two circumstances— namely, an extraordinary power of endurance in the particular animal pursued, and an error which the hounds had met with a little before the time noted. In general, either gets clear of the hounds altogether, or is overtaken and killed by the time here given, or in less time. Still, I believe, the license thus taken is perfectly allowable—the object if it being, to comprise as many characteristic circumstances as possible with description of a single Hunt.

These remarks are not so much offered with any view of instructing the judgment of the intelligent reader, (in which regard they may be needless) as for the purpose of forestalling in popular opinion, the obtrusive cavils of that petulent tribe of self-installed reviewers—the “small deer” of Literature —who have been sedulous to acquaint themselves with nothing of the critical art beyond its cant and captiousness; or who, on the other hand, are disposed to look for true mastery no where but in the rush-light performances of emanating from their own petty cliques, the sole things they are wont to praise. But such critics, even when they do praise, always indicate very sufficiently the sort theu belong to: for they praise with a vengeance! that is, with a vehemence that would be only laughable, if it were only foolish, and not amazingly reckless at the same time.

(2.) Marroo is an aboriginal name of a sort of brush ironwood. The word is a perfect iambus—the accent being hard upon the last syllable.

(3.) Shot, as it is here used, is a weaver’s term. When the general texture of a piece of clorth is pierced at intervals with a stronger or more costly thread, it is said to be shot with it.

(4.) For the character of this bird’s note, see a passage of Part I., and note to same.

(5.) So much do the notes of this curious bird resemble the barking 140 of a diminutive dog, that even one well acquainted with bush matters, is at times so thoroughly deceived by them, as to look about him for the where= -about of the little four-footed companion of some fellow peragrator—until, perhaps, he detects the sounds as coming from the tree tops, and is so reminded of the bird written of.

(6.) On the approach of any strange animal, the gregarious and other birds peculiar to those dense, isolated brushes which often cloud up and render all but impassable the lower recesses of our mountains, will, on some occasions, appear to gather in the direction of the threatened invasion of their commonwealth, pretending all the while to a most valorous determination of defending it to the utmost. On such occasions, the pertinacity with which some kinds of them keep scolding away, and the fussy defiance of their attitudes, are curiously amusing. But of the kangaroo and other familiar beasts, they evince little or none of this provincial jealousy. And having observed further, that a hunted kangaroo will suddenly turn, as if by instinct, in whatever direction it may happen to hear the cries of certain habitual brush- -birds, I fancy that I have in nowise overstepped the legitimate license of Poetry, in having imaginatively construed their clamors into the incentive welcome which follows in the text.

(7.) Walloroo is the aboriginal name of a species of kangaroo which is peculiar to the higher and more difficult mountains. A wallaroo, when disturbed, will descend the sides of the steepest ranges ranges with facility, by leaping from crag to crag, although these may stand many yards apart, on the edges of the clefts, or below each other in a step-like series. Hence he soon gets into a place of safety amongst caverns and ledges that are unapproachable to man or dog, and is seldom taken. But on a level this kind of kangaroo is far less fleet than the forester. It is also less elegant and tapered in form, having shorter and clumsier legs and longer arms: so centered no doubt by Nature, to adapt its powers of locomotion to the ruggedness of the mountains upon which dwells. It also differs from the ordinary forester in color—the male being much darker (almost black), and the female, comparatively, as much lighter: (or almost white—or, more exactly, a sort of cream color.)

(8.) An aboriginal name of a little shag-haired species of kangaroo peculiar to mountain copses. It may be called the mountain 141 wallaby, being to the wallaroo what the common wallaby is to the kangaroo proper. The jumbuc is the least elegant in its form, and the dullest in its nature, of all the kangaroo kinds—of all such at least as I happen to be acquainted with. The Blacks of the Hunter call the sheep jumbuc, from a resemblance, however remote, arising out of the hairy shagginess of the one and the woolliness of the other. But this, like most other of our indegenous animals, is named variously by the aboriginal tribes of different localities: and is known amongst the Hawksbury mountains by the native name of whirring.
