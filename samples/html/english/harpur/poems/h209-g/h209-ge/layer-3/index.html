Part VI.

I.

The fog is all dispelled at length, And dazzlingly the day’s young strength Refracts from each moist rock, and through Their foliage fretting, drinks the dew In every gleaming tree; For up in the vast of heaven anew, A glorious sight to see, The sun hangs clear of each craggy height, Like a burning buckler burred with bright And nimble shooting rays of light— Even so aloft hangs he! (1.) But now the Flyer hath lost his pride Of speed, and on the mountain’s side Bounds shorter, stumbling as he goes: While, as through the echoey throes Along that hollow and cavernous way Yearning forth into audible being, 118 The woodland Spirit might seem to say:

II.

“Faint not thus, from ruin fleeing, Child of the Forest, renew thy speed— Sore is thy need! For oft amid these wild recesses The creature that now so near thee presses, Is seen to drive his long gaunt shape— But when did ever his quarry escape? Then speed thee—Child of the Forest, speed— Sore is thy need!”

III.

The desperate wretch with mustered force, In hope of foiling, shifts his course— But hark, that yelp! short, thick and hoarse— A hurried yelp of savage pride, Tells back, that Lightning’s at his side. Yet on, together, hip to hip,— On spring they, in dread fellowship; For the grim-jawed hound hath yet to seek The ghasted forester’s outstretched neck, 119 And there alone he’ll grip! Like some dim demon of the wind, Towler, half seen, sweeps hard behind; And Whip, whose ardent sobs confess His unrelenting savageness, Maintains with constant strain his place; Then Lady and her yellow twain, With all the rest, pour on amain: While from the left apace, Verging obliquely, lo, the train Of foremost hunters how they gain Upon the closing Chase!

IV.

But mark yon copse of tough maroo (2.) With ashen saplings pillared through, And interlaced with thorns and briers, And shot (3.) with creepers that like wires Wind up the higher wood;— Upstretching even from the rill That down yon gorge so wildly rude, ‘Neath vines that flaunt in wanton will 120 Goes darkling with a lowly trill,— That dense copse vestures, like a hood, The brow of the lonesome hill. And Snapt full oft hath such a Copse The brightest link of the hunter’s hopes; For thick within its savage regions, Rank upon rank, in woven legions Lavish growths of runners rise, Through which his way the hound must bore, Even while his prey, with practised eyes Detecting where the game-track lies, Bounds on, with Danger at his heels no more.

V.

Is it hope gives sudden speed To the jaded Flyer’s need? Yea, he flattereth his care With the show of refuge there. His tramping waketh into voice Within it many an obscure bird— Hark! the scolding yellow-bill’s noise And Jehu’s urging note are heard, 121 With Teleltella’s sounding bell, And the dog-bird’s sudden bark as well, (5.) As starting aloft to the topmost sprays, Alarmed, they view the coming Chase! While harder yet its victim strains, For in the cries of that noisy throng A promissory earnest reigns To hearten him along: To him, as near their clamours ring, Seem they not even thus to sing? (6.)

VI.

“Fellow child of the forest primeval, Hitherward haste thee! Gain our wild hold, and yon creatures of evil In vain shall have chased thee! Look! our guardian thorns shall meet Their coming, our woodvines entangle their feet.

Here secure doth the wallaroo (7.) feed, And the jimbuc (8.) though wanting both cunning and speed; And here, with the jungle-bird, fearless and free 122 As the eagles that fly In the clouds of the sky, Alone is there shelter for thee. Haste then! haste with a resolute will— Haste! that the Forest may know thee still.”

VII.

Right will the Flyer hath onward stretched— A moment’s grace, and the Copse were reached! Even now its spreading skirts (out tossed In strips and clumps that stand alone, As squadrons round a camping host In lines and clouds are thrown) Part hide him: now is he more near: And now,—as about to disappear, The Hunters shout at once their fear— “Lightning, take heed! our hopes are vain If once the Flyer that jungle gain— Thy strength were nothing there!” Thus they—and in the dells about One to another as it were,“Nothing 123 “Nothing there!” the echoes shout— “Nothing there!”

VIII.

But shouted they in vain? I trow not!—well their timely hint That conscious hound hath ta’en. For lo, by the energetic dint Of one long leap, Quick as thought His quarry is caught, And, backward pulled, adown the steep Wheels with him fastened, round and round, With sideward tug and upward bound— Till Towler, with a headlong aim, Throws in his aid and weighs the game With lunging downfal to the ground. Arriving in succession fast The rest pour in—and to the last All keen to grip, with bootless haste Their yet remaining vigor waste.

And wonder ye not, if his speech be uncouth, Nor look ye much for his rhyme to be smooth, Or that the flight should be lofty and free Of one with so little of learning as he, And all of whose aptest years were past In primal solitudes wild and vast.

Thus nurtured,—self-urged, first he knew Australia’s virgin Muse to woo, And of Song’s bright mysteries ‘gan to guess With a lone self-cherished studiousness: While toiling along the plough’s first tracks, Or ‘twixt the strokes of the felling axe, Or while upon the prairie wide Where the savage whooped and the wild dog cried, As round the squandered herd he hied.

Then be it enough that his rhymes run o’er A Sport that never was sung before: And let not the cavils of critic display, In thankless return for the sylvan Lay 126 Which he hath framed in the joy of his heart And poured on the winds of the world to day, Give his spirit in lonely abashment to smart, And as homeward he wends with glad Whip at his side, Humble the step of his bardic pride.

Notes to Part VI.

(1.)—Up in the vast of heaven an[e]w The sun hangs clear of each craggy height.

The reader will observe here, through the medium of the images introduced, the nolation of time. At starting the Kangaroo the sun was just seen as it were on the mountains: it now appears hanging clear of them in the sky. This will give—say, twenty minutes or so; the pursuit being thus unusually prolonged, as before observed, by two circumstances—namely, an extraordinary power of endurance in the particular animal pursued, and a baulk which the hounds had met with a little before the time noted. In general, the kangaroo either gets clear of the hounds altogether, or is overtaken and killed by half the time here given, or in less time. Still, I 128 believe, the license thus taken is perfectly allowable—the object of it being, to comprise as many characteristic circumstances as possible in the description of a single Hunt.

These remarks are not so much offered with any view of instructing the judgment of the intelligent reader (in which regard they may be needless) as for the purpose of forestalling in popular opinion the obstrusive cavils of that petulent tribe of self-installed Aristarchi—the ”small deer” of Literature—who have been sedulous to acquaint themselves with nothing of the critical art beyond its cant and captiousness; or who, on the other hand, are disposed to look for true mastery no where but in the rush-light performances emanating from members of their own petty cliques,—the sole things they are wont to praise. But such critics—or rather criticlings—when they do praise, always very sufficiently indicate, in their manner, their the sort of fraternity to which they belong: for they praise with a vengeance! that 129 is, with a vehemence of partiality that would only be laugh- -able, if it were only foolish, and not amazingly reckless at the same time.

(2.) Maroo is an aboriginal name of a sort of brush iron-wood. The word is a perfect iambus—the accent being hard upon the last syllable.

(3.)—Shot with creepers.

Shot, as it is here used, is a weaver’s term. When the general texture of a piece of clorth is pierced ar intervals with a stronger or more costly thread, it is said to be shot with it.

(4.)—Jehu’s urging note.

For the character of this bird’s note, see a passage in Part I, and note to same.

(5.)—The dog-bird’s sudden bark.

So much do the notes of this curious bird re- 130 resemble the barking of a diminutive dog, that even one well acquainted with bush matters, is at times so thoroughly mislead by them, as to look around him for the where-abouts of the little four- -footed companion of some fellow peragrator— until, perhaps, he detects the sounds as coming from the tree tops, and is so reminded of the bird written of.

A wallaroo, when disturbed, will descend the sides of the steepest mountain with facility, by leaping from crag to crag, although these may stand many yards apart, on the edges of the clefts, or below each other in a step-like series. But on a level this kind of kangaroo is far less fleet than the forester. It is also less elegant and tapered in form, having shorter and clumsier legs, and longer arms: and doubtless it has been thus adaptatively fashioned by Nature, in order to secure a perfect agreement between its powers of locomotion and the ruggedness of the mountains which form its habitat. It also differs from the ordinary forester in color—the male being much darker—almost black, and the female, comparatively, as much lighter— that is, almost white, or, more exactly, a sort of cream color.
