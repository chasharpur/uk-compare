AUSTRALIAN LYRICS.—No. 7.

THE MESSAGE.

You tell me, yet to talk she’s prone Of me, nor can forget The features of those days whereon We parted and we met; That still she haunts the forest scene Where once with me she strayed, Still cherishes the leafy screen I wove within its shade: You tell me this, and much beside— But is she not another’s bride?

You say, when she in words addressed Aught like my name may trace, A sigh will heave her pensive breast, A shade come o’er her face; That straight, with startled air, she’s led Each purpose to resign, And warbles o’er, with drooping head, One little song of mine: You say all this, and much beside— But is she not another’s bride?

Go, tell her he to whom she long Was fortune’s brightest beam, Doth charge her, by his burning wrong, To cherish not the dream That love, to mend her broken vow, Should still our hearts possess— Such hope in me were meanness now, In her is guiltiness! From me this tell her, naught beside, For is she not another’s bride?

Or, if aught more thou wouldst impart, Mark that I deigned to say, Had love’s first roses in her heart Not flourished to decay, Whatever rarely burns above In Cynthia’s blue domain, Whatever gentle spirits love In nature’s summer reign, Should through all ages have supplied Rich fancies of one poet’s bride.

What! they deceived her to the last? Oh, say not so again! Lest all the madness of the past Come rushing o’er my brain. Away! where’er my glances turn— On waters, hills, or skies— There, there, in dreamy bondage, mourn Love’s thousand memories! Friend, leave me, leave me to my pride! I will not love another’s bride.

Charles Harpur.
