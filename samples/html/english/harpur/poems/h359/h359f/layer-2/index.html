On the Proposed Recurrence to Transportation.

231

[In the earlier Colonial times, when the remote Squattages were mostly man[unclear]ed by assigned convicts, the Blacks of those regions were usually treated by them with great licentiousness and oppression. And if they happened to be somewhat resentful under this sort of civilised treatment —but especially if, from whatever cause, they became at all “troublesome” to the cattle, by ea[t]ing a beast occasionally, in lieu of a kangaroo, or by in anywise routing or unsettling a herd when gathered upon some chosen camping-ground, though by their inopportune presence only—then, these convict servants, on the merest hint from their Sydney-dwell- -ing masters, as to the desirableness of their doing so, were wont to slaughter them with as little scruple as if they were accounting, in the same bloody fashion, for so many dingoes. I myself once saw as many as nine convict stockmen hanged in one bunch, for the wholesale massacre of a tribe of Blacks, having been incited thereto, as was generally believed at the time, by their master or masters; though the misguided stockmen, by dying game, carried the damning secret away with them. But whither? Such a question can be even glanced at, only with a shudder!]

The shame of bondage is upon the Land Even yet, and a wild cry of blood complains To Heaven, like Abel’s, whereso bushward band The myrmidons of their polluted gains Who erst drove Mammon’s chariot-scourge in hand! And would they again, to puff their windy shows Of Lordship, spot us with the plague of those Who would perpetuate the shameful brand— The accusing cry—and thence through labor’s fee Cheapen our Manhood! Better that this fair New world once more a wilderness should be! Or that the cause of such unrighteous care, 232 Our cattle, like the swine of Gadara, were Devil-entered all and drownèd in the sea.
