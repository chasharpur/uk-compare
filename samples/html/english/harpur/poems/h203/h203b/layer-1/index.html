John Heki. (1847.)

Should Switzerland’s rude rocks be named the throne Of Liberty, God-chartered there to quell All tyrants,—there invincibly alone! Because the echoes in their caves that dwell Held converse once with the bold voice of Tell? And thine, New Zealand, yet be kept unknown To Glory, now that thy dark Chief hath thrown Around them a like patriotic spell? Should Wallace be a name Time ne’er may lose? And Hofer’s—his, who for his mountains died Rather than see the Spoiler there abuse Freedom’s high altar,—be a sound of pride? Yet, Heki, thine remain unglorified In the proud annals of the Heroic Muse? x

x It seems that some Americane skippers are to be branded with miscreancy “during duration” (as wooden-legged Marky Flood used to say) for having supplied the New Zealanders with certain barrels of gunpowder lately. But what if Jonathan should send them a whole cargo, and plead in justification, as he very likely would, that he furnished the said “villanous saltpetre” under the sanction of that national flag which the consistent British Government accorded to these same NewZealanders a few years since? and by virtue also of the admitted independence of their several Chieftanships. Could all the special pleading which might be pumped up on the other side, from the constitutional leakage of Downing Street, put even a tolerably clean face on any attempt to outface such a national, and at the same time —rational plea? And would any unclean attempt of the kind go down satisfactorily with the other Governments of the world, kindless and morally inert as they may be and are? No: not if if there is but one grain of political honesty extant in the nine- -teenth century.—As to John Heki, if there be any such thing as patriotism—he is a patriot. If Tell and Hofer were heroes— he is also a hero. If the resistance which Leonidas offered to to a foreign Spoiler was noble—so was his late outbreak, and so also will be any future one he may yet instigate and conduct. Noble I say, was that outbreak, whatever may be its ultimate issue; and consoling also to every true patriot, however he might deplore its concomitant bloodshed: for it [unclear] was an assurance to him, that the spirits of Sparta’s “three hundred”, with those of Wallace, and Tell, and Hofer, and Emmet had not all abandoned the unworthy earth.

C.H.
