Simplicity versus Craft

Simplicity enjoys while Craft contrives, Till life with both hath lost its pristine zest, And the mere memory of the past survives— Of pleasure or of care;—say, which is best?

Note—I would rather be simple than crafty: for with the simple life is a play which they enjoy as it passes; while with the crafty it is a game, and mostly a losing one in the end, because of the slight proportion which their gam[e]s bear to the pains they have taken to compass them. Suppose the play and the game nearly over, let us strike the balance. Simplicity has eaten the most of his cake; while Craft has his yet whole or in great part uneaten, but with no appetite for a final meal. The pleasure of Simplicity, though past, was positive. The pleasure of Craft, though yet present, is but the negative one of mere possession; a pleasure certainly, but not such as he thought to have—and herein lies his irremediable loss. But then, says Madam Economy, the cake remains,—to be eaten by somebody else; even by Wiseacre his son, or by need. his heir at law. Well, even this is but the spreading of a certain amount of enjoyment over a greater surface: and just so far as such a result entered into the motive of Craft’s game of life—just so far and so far only does it acquit him of folly—the dry folly of a cunning self-deception. And even they who may be gainers by it, owe him no thanks, but must know in their hearts, of the, pretend to remember him with gratitude.
