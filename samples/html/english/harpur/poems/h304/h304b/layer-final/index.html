Mr. Mort’s Testimonial.

The true man values Virtue for itself, And not its accidents, however golden; The sham one for the influence or the pelf Through which by him it is alone beholden.

Note.—I believe Mr. Mort to be a very worthy man upon the whole. I believe that when Mr. Manning said ”he had never heard any expressions from him on public or private matters, that did not do him honour,” he spoke sincerely, and was not altogether overpraising him. Nay, I even think that his permanent withdrawal from amongst us would be a social loss. And such, too, is my opinion of his good sense, that I think he would be glad to slip from under the impending honor, if the powers of Sham were to be balked in any other way than by treating them with contempt. But with this much premised, I would propound a query. Suppose Mr. Mort to be in every respect just as worthy as he is: but suppose, at the same time, that instead of having amassed immense wealth by his enterprising career, he had only been able, through the short-comings of others or through some malevolence of fortune, just to have kept himself afloat; and with this fact patent to the community, he were about separating from our shores— would the intending Testimonialists, whose names are blazoned at length in the public prints, be altogether so ready to come forward with their valedictory incense to his worth and character? I do not for a moment believe they would: and I think nobody else believes they would: or that the Herald and Empire would keep several columns open to catch the drippings of the occasion. Nay, many an honest fellow who shall read this, will be ready to assert, on the strength of his own Colonial experience, that some of them, in the reversed case I have supposed, would scarcely turn upon their heels to vouchsafe him a parting smile. But I care not myself to go so far as that. I would rather imagine that they have still amongst them a brazen half moon on hand, which they would fain confer on somebody: And yet, to conclude that they would not then do, all that they are now bent upon doing, is to conclude that the forthcoming Testimonial, whatever shape it may happen to take, will at best be only comparable to

“A goodly apple, rotten at the core:”

that is, could we but see it in its essence; inasmuch as it will be more of an offering to Mr. Mort’s wealth and material influence than to his intrinsic worth.

But should any one ask what moral harm there can be in such like displays, I answer, a great deal. The mere suspicion of their genuineness, to which we are unavoidably led by the prevailing tone of our great men, and by their ingrained Mammonism, tends to demoralise our faith in the public expression of all social opinion. And in this, too, that they tend to perpetuate in the getters-up of them, an habitual hollowness and a com- placency in pretences, of whose real nature and end they must very frequently be most meanly conscious.

“But you require men to be too sternly sincere, too loftily motived,” says some sleek apologist: “what you complain of is inherent in human nature.” But this is only putting a worse face upon the matter: for it is tantamount to declaring that human nature has a Midas-like curse upon it, and can only test virtue by turning it into gold: so muuch gold, so much virtue:—a doctrine I abhor immeasurably, and refer at once to His Majesty the Devil. Human nature, in its sturdy integrity, is all that I would have it. It is hearty, but impartial: it loves to confer honor, but only upon what is essentially honorable: it would do justice to merit, but it scorns to worship fortune.
