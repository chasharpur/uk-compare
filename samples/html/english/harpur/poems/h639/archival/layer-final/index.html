TO W. A. DUNCAN, ESQ.

“Principal Officer of Customs at Moreton Bay, and a Magistrate of the Territory,” on reading his “Plea for the New South Wales Constitution.”

I thought you once a sage, but soon found out That I had judged you with my judgment blinking, Studious you are, and accurate, no doubt, But very weak withal, from over drinking Of mental sillabubs, and much sickly swinking On musty Learning’s craziest round-about: Versed in the logic of mere words, without The piercing logic of original thinking. But open wide thy study window,—do, man! And let the breath of heaven upon thy mind Blow till it spirits it with a braver hue, man! Or if fast down thou still wilt keep the blind, Reserve thy chat for the gown-wearing kind, Thou bat-light loving, very learned Old Woman.

Charles Harpur.

Note.—Mr. Duncan’s mind is one of that botanically literal order, to which mere facts and their immediate significance, are absolutely everything. In Church Controversy he may be somewhat, for that is pre-eminently the domain of literal authorities. But out of it he is only a plodder—a mere grinder of facts. And be they subborn, or be they transitory, it is no matter: they are all equally grist for his wearisome mill. But he takes no account of the progressive intelligence that is for ever at work through them, and above them, ignoring some as rubbish, and gathering others together as foundation stones for yet loftier superstructures of the mind. Thus, because the facts of history tend to show, that all Govern- ments hitherto have been more or less imperfect and corrupt, his literal mode of half-reasoning, the only kind of reasoning of which he is capable, would conclude, that so all govern- ments ever will be, must be, from the nature of man and the co-existing nature of things. But this is erecting mere facts into principles; and is, in effect, reasoning in a circle,—from the past, round again to the past. It falls short of the plastic nature of things, and the progressive capability of man to mould and adapt them to new and to higher ends. Historica[l] facts, unlike those of science, only indicate, they do not establish the existence of causes, and carry no absolute proof with them of the permanency of what they indicate. They may be only so far referrable to causal conditions, as an alderman’s foregone love of calipash is, after he has become disgusted with it in consequence of a subject. They are, at farthest, only facts, not principles[.] Hence, though the facts of ten thousand years of history should show a constant imper- fection and corruptness in all tried forms of government, they would in no wise prove that other untried forms must inevitably partake of their evils. For our human perception of their imperfection, their corruptness, would be no less a fact, and one of a higher order of facts tending to their improvement and purification, or better still, to the devisement of still superior forms, reaching towards perfection. And even though this work of regeneration should be indefinitely procrastinated, or crippled again and again by inauspicious chances, still the very perception of its necessity,—forever gaining upon its cause,—must eventually lead to its com- pletion, and all the sooner the more bravely it is acted upon.

But Mr. Duncan will understand nothing of this: or he will only incline to accept the reasoning in so far as it may seem to tell for the patching up of extant imperfection. With per- fection for its good he will in no wise understand it.

The main arguments of Mr. Duncan’s Plea for the New South Wales Constitution is fairly characterized above. It may be imperfect, he argues; but all human things are so, and that therefore we should be satisfied with it. But I, for one, do not believe that all human things are inevitably imperfect. At all events, I am sure that they would be more perfect than they are, if men were habitually less satisfied with 3a their imperfection; and that the pernicious counsels of such philosophers as Mr. Duncan, have tended, and tend in no small degree, to the perpetuation of this imperfection. Hence the wisdom of our for ever endeavouring to found only the best—nay, perfect forms of government. Hence, too, the wisdom of our everlasting dissatisfaction with all authority that is hybrid and partial.

How characteristic of the peculiar order of Mr. Duncan’s mind, is the fond allusion in this Plea to Gatton and Old Sarum? What a sneaking kindness it indicates for the “old familiar faces” of constitutional rottenness? And what a monkish approval of the merest and most formal reproductions of olden abortions and governmental shams? Verily he is a philospher—but of the Dark Ages.

In the inscriptions of the sonnet above it is stated to have been written, on reading the Plea, &c. I should have said, on reading it in part; for who out of Twaddledom could read the whole of it?

March, 1856.

C. H.

C. H.
