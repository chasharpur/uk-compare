Terrible Truth.

Lo, there are truths so terrible, they seem To breathe a moral danger. Hence we fear To write them, but no less themselves they write On all our human doings. This is one: Nature’s a libertine, and ever makes All stolen fruit the sweetest. Is not sin, Even sin, a sauce to pleasure, giving it Prime relish, and the keenest edge of joy? And though the conscience, or whate’er we call By that fine name, may sometimes shudder, yet The greater the invasion, all the more The apetite is freshened, and enforced. 762b X Even dogs and donkeys like the bit they rob Better than what comes honestly—far better; And chatting magpies glory in a theft. Whom do men call a hero? Whom but he Who slays and plunders far and wide, and makes His eminence a pyramid of bones, A mountain-heap of murder! Whom is most The darling of the Fair? The dashing rake; The bold bad beautiful seducer, who Would break a heart as lightly as an oath. And these are Nature’s favorits—yea, on these She lavishs her graces and her gifts And heaps her bounties on them with a hand Unstinting, and a strumpet-like delight. 761b What then? Is Nature devillish? or would she But testify her lusty scorn of all Mere moral starvelings, with their timid codes, And prim proprieties that would in vain Strait jacket the strong will, and pale the cheek, And pine the belly, and freeze all the heart’s Wild flowing riot to a bloodless calm?
