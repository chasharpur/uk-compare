DIAL.

By Charles Har[pur].

I

A Huntress born of fancy Through a dream of olden lore, Lithely on bounding, lightens right past me, Full as the morning of hurry and vigor When I roam the mountains o’er:

See her bow!—I see it plain! And shafted quiver balanced nice! See her robe, blown back amain, Though looped in front with a sure device! Sweeping behind her like the train Of a Bird of Paradise!

II

Or when the forest shadows Are dew-silvered by the moon, Lonely forth roaming, than do I listen, till Suddenly sounding up, merry and mellow, Hark! I hear her mystic horn:

Then her buskined limbs how white They glance, as past she boundeth there! See her bow [gap] a curve of light Across her arm upraised and bare! And see how b[a]ckward streameth bright The torrent of her hair!

III

Or when the hills are sleeping In a silver silence deep. Musefully wand’ring, not long am I lonely, for Mystical harmony, billow on billow, Wells from every echoey steep:

Then an arc of rainbow light Sudden spans some tiny glade, And there, I see her, boom and bright Disclouding from the deeper shade, All girt with dancing Nymphs in white And festal robes arrayed!
