Charles Harpur’s Wild Bee of Australia, A Series of Poems, With Prose Notes.

Love in Excess.

The Passion that utterly conquers our reason, Is rare to the taste as fruits out of season; But as some disorder of body from these, If indulged in, is certain,—some moral disease Follows that, like its shade:—’tis the truth, were it treason! Yet a truth that the beauty-witched lover may seize on For aid, but as drowning men catch at a straw;— One as vain in effect, when applied to for ease, As was the design of the madnan I saw, Who endeavoured to hang up his hat on a breeze.

Say, how should we love then, if not in Excess, Since ’tis better to love so than not love at all, And since goodness in badness were worse if ‘twere less,— And since, too, the mean we call golden, I guess, Is for Love but a fine-woven funeral pall?

Note. To love too much is bad. But then, to love 475 too little is worse. And then again, neither to love too much nor too little, is, I am affraid, not to love at all:—it being not, in fact, a condition too perfect to be natural to any existence out of heaven; and therefore, by parity of fact, altogether too perfect to be other than a tasteless (neither bitter nor sweet) make -believe upon earth. My own prose conclusion, with regard to the whole matter, (patched with poetryas usual) is simply and straightforwardly this: that whenever some “lovely Thetis sits beside us”, the very best thing we can do, is just to take without question, and in toto, whatever “goods the gods provide us”.
