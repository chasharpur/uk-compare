[Pope.]

His Satirical Mastery.

In this supreme! With what a Jove-like ease, He crushed the Titans of the times’ disease! “Bared the mean heart that lurked beneath a star,” And ev’n with brazen Dulness waged victorious war.

Oh, could I wield so well in current song Scorn’s searching lance or Wit’s satiric thong, How the bold-vizaged Vices of the day Should crouch, or slink, like beaten curs, away! But chiefly Greed, that hell-enkindled rage Which burns into the spirit of the age! That Mammon-worship which makes outward show The prime morality where’er we go! Befrills Religion like a jade in vogue! Besaints a usurer—if a pious rogue! Enslaves even Justice, till she dare not see In lofty places palpable Villany! Brands honest Need with shame, till moneyed Hate Sees but a brutedom in a poor estate! Deems Learning beggarly, and Wisdom vain, Devotion weak, Philanthropy insane! 309 Impairs in souls once elevate and brave All noble scorn of every prosperous knave! Taints even the heart of childhood, and instills A social poison that all friendship kills!— Oh, I would lash it to its native Hell, Thenceforth with devils, not with men, to dwell!

Note.

I do not now relish Pope so heartily as I did when this rhymed Criticism was written. Though his poetry is the very perfection of its school, (the French-English school, which acknowledged nothing beyond the precisely beautiful, and a rhetorical imitation of the sublime,—not the sublime itself) his best pieces, as poetry, are inferior to those of the specifically English Muse, beginning with Chaucer, and last seen in its thoroughness (though defectively as to form) in Crashaw—until its reapp- -earance in the present century. Such, at least, is my opinion now.

What is very obvious as a defect in the poetry of Pope, is the confined Tea-garden littleness and poverty of his scenical pictures. Even his boldest peeps at Nature seem to have been taken as through an opera-glass: every- 310 everything is cut up into small circles, or reduced and restricted into parterre-like patches. The caves of his verse, like the grotto in his garden, have nothing rude or primative about them. His lakes are ponds. And if he sometimes ventures to talk about a mountain, it is with an evident consciousness of its being a very far off and outlandish sort of a monster;—a something that only exists to his imagination through a poetic tradition. In short, he fancies that he is quite rural and picturesque enough, when he sings of “bowery mazes and surrounding greens”. Now bowery mazes are no doubt sufficcently puzzling, but by surrounding greens he will certainly be thought by many to mean only incipient cabbages, or something of the sort.

It is in satire that his poetic supremacy lies; in refined and polished satire, every line like a lance, point- -ed and beaming. Hence his greatest poem, and most brilliant performance, in almost every respect, is—the Dunciad. His other satiracle pieces too, are amongst the best of his writings. His character of Atticus (Addison) is a marvel in its kind, with an untarnishable brightness upon every phrase of it, and the subtlest possible edge in every turn of expression. But then, even the best satirical poetry, like the best didactic, is essentially more the offspring of keen talent than of absolute genius.

As to the mere style of his verse, Sir Egerton Bridges (no mean 311 critic) has characterised it as being “mellefluously monotonous.” But it is not continuously flowing enough to be even so; all the lines being but as so many separate or intermittant jets from an artifical fountain—all of the same force and compass, all rising with the same squirt, and falling with the same patter. Still it must be conceded that he did much for English versification in the matter of linear precesion and finish. But he had no conception of paragraphical harmony: of that harmony which results from a continuous variety of movement occurring in a whole series of lines taken together.

Very often too, his poetry is at once vague and verbose in expression. The following couplet will exemplify my meaning.

Asleep and naked as an Indian lay, An honest factor stole a gem away.

This is all that is said about the event, and no doubt we infer from it, that while the Indian lay asleep, the factor spoiled him of a gem: but the expressions do not convey the fact. Then the phrase “stole a gem” is made the mere handle of a rhyme by the addition of “away”. This sort of verbiage was, however, all but necessitated by the given style of his couplet. And partly for the same reason, there is scarcely a passage in some of his poems, the grammatical construction of which, will abide a rigorous trial.

Nor is the morality of Pope’s writings so excellent as some seem to think. It may have breadth enough, but it lacks height. There is in it a manifest poverty of sympathy with the healthy and noble simplicities of nature. This is everywhere evinced by the conventional cast of his subjects; and hence smart, short-sighted and convenient views of things in general are substituted for that prime ethical wisdom which makes pure truth the centre of all good, and right and wrong the antipodes of each other.

His political philosophy is still worse. His often quoted lines

For forms of Government let fools contest, Whate’er is best administered is best,

are worse than nonsensical, for they gild a falacy. Whatever is best administered is best only in its kind: but its kind may be utterly abominable notwithstanding.

But beyond all this, Pope has great and peculiar merits. His sense is mostly clear and direct, his fancy elegant, and his wit keen and shining as the scimitar of a Saladin. But he wanted simplicity of faith in nature and ideal abandonment in art, and should never therefore be the Gamaliel of a young Poet. Our first teacher should be genius rather than talent. The influence of the 313 former penetrates like light into the deepest recesses of our mental being, to the strengthening of our faculties for conception on their own account; while that of the latter is but a wine- -like stimulant, and only provocative of an imitative emulation. In fine, Genius is a creator, while Talent is only a modifier; the one is a prophet, the other is but a doctor. Our young Poets then, should begin with Shakespeare rather than with Pope.
