Mary of Eulengo.

Of Womankind the dearest, My soul, in its sincerest Devotion, loves her so, That all-admired or lonely, I might be happy only With Mary of Eulengo.

At her heart’s mercy lying, Were some beauteous Empress sighing For mine, yet courted so, I’d turn from her with gladness, To share even pain and sadness With Mary of Eulengo.

Though in a palace dwelling, With triumph round me swelling, Yet were we parted so, O mine were the sleepless fears then, Deep sighs, and bitter tears then For Mary of Eulengo.

‘Mid scenes by Love made holy, With but a dwelling lowly, Of all the sun below, I ask no rarer pleasure Than Life to me shall measure With Mary of Euleng[o].

Note. It is somewhat singular that, not only the lost love-pleads, but the wives as well, of the greatest modern British Poets, have been nearly all Marys. Coleridge’s wife was a Mary. So was Shelley’s. So also is Wordsworth’s, if she be yet living. Then there was Byron’s Mary, his first love, in memory of whom he wrote, many years afterwards, when his “heart was as grey as his head”, the following beautifully pathetic verses:

“I have a passion for the Name of Mary, For once it was a majic sound to me; And yet it half recals the world of Faery, Wherein I dreamt of what might never be.”

Lastly, /not to mention the fact of its being the “one loved name” of the majority of our Sonneteers and song-writers/ there was Burns’ never-to-be-forgotten Highland Mary: she whose insphered spirit, in its earthward effusion, kept yearning through the inspirations of her yet mortal lover, even as the reflexion of the morning star when dilating within the wild waters of some mountain Lake,—large and lamping, though so deep-down and distant;—and whouse untimely death and burial suggested the following, the most sacredly sorrow- =ful stanza in the world! “O pale, pale now those ruby lips I aft hae kissed sae fondly; And closed for aye the sparkling glance That dwelt on me sae kindly; And mouldering now in silent dust The heart that loved me dearly;— But still within my bosom’s core Shall live my Highland Mary.
