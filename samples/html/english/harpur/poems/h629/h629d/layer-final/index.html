To the Lyre of Australia.

Lyre of my Country, remains it for me From the charm-muttering Savage’s rude-beating hand, To snatch thee; that so thy wild numbers may be No longer but writ on the winds of the Land? And though he who thy chords now would master, may never Evince in his verse all their rapture of tone, Yet—yet should Australia smile on the endeavour, The glory so won by the Bard were her own.

Wild sounding Lyre of the South, could I vie With a Burns in the strength of his patriot fire, O then I would raise thee in glory on high— The guardian of Beauty, of Valor the sire! And could I but hope that, as music assuages When heard in the pause of the gale sweeping o’er, My Country should list in the rush of her ages, To this Song, sounding onward,—I’d covet no more.

With her green forest round me, above her blue sky, I lap in thy measures some national dream; And I find that our songs, though unstudied, are high, When the glory of Future Australia’s the theme: And even thy Lay, to her sons and her daughters, Should breathe of the Land where the evergreen grows— To men’s souls like the strong wind that swelleth the waters, To the heart like a spring breeze that opens the rose.

This address to the Lyre of Australia was written in my teens—say, twelve years ago. But, alas! neither then nor since did my Country deign to award one smile of encouragement to the endeavours of her Poet. Her best and only gifts to him have been hunger and rags. Nay, 8 my literary enemies and disparagers all along, have been mostly countrymen. In short, my name and fame up to this moment, except as bye words in the mean mouths of such defamers and detractors, are utterly blanks in their day and generation;—and I conclude therefore, that either I am unworthy of my country and country= -men, or my countrymen are unworthy [unclear] of it and me. Time will ratify this conclusion one way or the other— and to Time I refer it accordingly C.H.

1847.
