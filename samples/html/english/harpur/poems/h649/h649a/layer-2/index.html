There are some Truths which that so wondrous ape— That provident, over-civilised monkey, Man, Esteems himself most wise in being blind; to And this is one of these—the one indeed: His earthy obstinacy least would see A sentence of the Sage, or of the Bard A simple song, that gifts Humanity With one new faith or hope, that helps to raise It heavenward, as on strengthened wings of light; Or that refines in it one lovely trait— Or that confirms in it one noble care Domestic, social, sexual or friendly; x Or that ws in it, that pure faith In M...ing which endured the Golden Age, Is of more moment to the race than ten Mechanical inventions, in themselves meant to meet The beggar’s needs alone—yea, or than ten However apt & good Great from their worth in men abstracted, Though towered with all the palace pomp that takes The heed of those who, knowing not the strength And wisdom of a simple life, would yet Build Babels to the emptiness of Pride. O What did the pillared pomp of Rome itself, But leave the Latin race, like Samson, shorn Of all its aboriginal strength divine? For Art is evil, when we lose in Art’s Imposing lures, and gross appeals to sense, All insight of the value of the soul That lies beyond it, even as the sky Embosoms the rude structure of the world. And Art is evil, too, when we merge in Art All pristine love of Nature and her Works, Which shrink it to a dream, and are indeed The Mother-Beautiful of whom ’tis born.
