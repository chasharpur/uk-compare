V

A Truth Men won’t see.

There are some Truths to which that wondrous ape— That provident, philosophic monkey, Man, Esteems himself most wise in being blind; And this is one—the one he least would see: A sentence of the Sage, or of the Bard A simple song, that gifts Humanity With one new spiritual hope to help to raise It heavenward, as on strengthened wings of fire; Or that refines in it one lovely trait— Or that confirms in it one noble care Domestic, social, sexual or friendly; Is of more value to the race than ten Mechanical inventions, though amongst The costliest that have been—yea, or than ten Great from their worth in men abstracted, Though towered with all the palace pomp that takes The heed of those who, knowing not the worth And wisdom of a simple life, would yet Build Babels to the nothingness of Pride. For Art is evil, when we lose in Art’s Imposing lures, and gross appeals to sense, All insight of the beauty of the soul That lies beyond it, even as the sky Embosoms the rude structure of the world.
