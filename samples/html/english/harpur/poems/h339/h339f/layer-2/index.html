XXI.

Old Billowy Hawksb’ry.

Old billowy Hawksb’ry, so bravely expanding, The same as of yore, as abounding and free! But alas! for that lone One beside thee now standing; How failure hath shrunk him since last he saw thee!Then 84 Then was glass’d in thy blue depth, untouch d by To-morrow, A vision-lit face with no hint of a furrow, But the fire-coultered plough of a wanderer’s sorrow Hath passed o’er his brow since he saw thee before!

And what did the Maiden he strayed by thy side with? She wedded another when darkened his day! And what of the Schoolmates he bathed in thy tide with? —None reck now of him, who then wandered away! Yet as shells breathe the tone of their once native ocean, How the life of the Past seems to stir in thy motion, Recalling a sense of his youthful devotion To Beauty and Friendship, when near thee before!

ut back comes the cold pang that made his heart shiver, When the home of his youth came in view, as of yore, Such hardship to him did it seem, thou bright river, To be as a stranger while passing its door! He thought of fond words that within it had sounded From welcoming Parents, as tow’rds them he bounded, Of Brothers that now in yon Churchyard lie mounded, Laid low with them both,—since he saw thee before!

nd alas! for the days that are gone, he cried lowly, With all they then yielded, and promised as well; Since feelings and hopes that once made yon roof holy, Have now such a mournful home-story to tell! Then from a tree he had planted, a blossom, He gathered and laid, like a child, in his bosom, His eyes ached—and lo, he hath come to repose ‘em On something unchanged since he saw thee before.
