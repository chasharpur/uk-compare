OLD BILLOWY HAWKESB’RY

Old billowy Hawkesb’ry, I see thee expanding, The same as in days past, as joyful and free! Not so with the lone one beside thee now standing, Him cold times have shaken since last he saw thee! Ah, then could I mark in thy blue flowing billow A vision-flushed face with no hint of a furrow, But the fire-coultered plough of a wanderer’s sorrow Hath passed o’er my brow since I saw thee before.

And what did the maiden I strayed by thy side with She wedded another when darkened my day! And what of the schoolmates I bathed in thy tide with? Ah, these have forgot me, those wandered away! Yet, as shells seem to sound of the sea-roar far made hence, The soul of the past seems to breathe in thy cadence, Imparting a gush of the fiery allegiance I dreamt unto Glory when near thee before.

But vainly, alas! to forget I endeavour How I passed the old cottage—my father’s no more! For bitter, indeed, was the task, my bright river, To feel like a stranger in passing its door! I plucked from a tree I had planted this blossom, And drew its green growth, like a child, to my bosom— Mine eyes burn’d as fire, and I fled, to repose ‘em On something unchanged since I saw thee before.
