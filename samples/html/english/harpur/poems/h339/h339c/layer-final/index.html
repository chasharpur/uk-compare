II.

Old Billowy Hawksbury.

Old billowy Hawksb’ry, I see the expanding As in boyhood I saw thee,—as joyful and free! ‘Tis not so with the lone one beside thee now standing, Him cold times have shaken since last he saw thee. Ah, then could I mark in thy blue-flowing billow, A vision-lit face with no hint of a furrow, But the fire-coultered plough of a wanderer’s sorrow Hath passed o’er my brow since I saw thee before.

And what did the Maiden I strayed by thy side with? She wedded another when darkened my day! And what of the Schoolmates I bathed in thy tide with? Ah, these have forgot me, those wandered away! Yet as shells seem to sound of the sea-roar far made hence, The soul of the past seems to breath in thy cadence, Recalling a flash of the fiery allegiance I dreamt unto glory when near thee before.

But vainly, alas! to forget I endeavour How I passed the old Cottage—my father’s no more! For bitter indeed was the task, my bright River, To feel like a stranger in passing its door! I plucked from a tree I had planted this blossom, And drew its green growth, like a child, to my bosom, Mine eyes burned as fire—and I fled, to repose ‘em On something unchanged since I saw the before.

Note. As none of my National Songs /though praised, and that highly, by almost every person that has read them/ have yet been esteemed worthy of a tune by Mr. Nathan or any of his fraternity, I will append by way of Note to the foregoing two, a few free remarks upon the folly of associating good music with trashy poetry. They originally formed part of a critique I wrote for the “Weekly Register”: but the Editor, Mr. Duncan, while he admitted their general justice, was yet afraid to insert them.

“There is no enquiry more vexatiously constant amongst persons of real refinement, than the following: How does it happen that the best modern airs are invariably allied to the very worst songs that issue from the Press— the very Calabaus, as it were, of the poetical creation? Now is this the result of wrong motives in the composers of these airs, or of sheer stupidity? Many believe of wrong motives —the brood of an ungenerous fear, that in the event of their being associated with equal poetry, the admiration of the public would be thereby too much divided: that is to say, the mere music would not then, in its appeal thereto, 109 stand so soveriegnly “alone in its glory”—it would not imply of itself so prettily “How perfect I were but for these verbal bonds”. But if the composers are actuated by such unworthily egotistical motives, in the production of their half of the offence in question, how blindly do they injure both themselves and their beautiful art! In yielding to them, they commit a species of artistical suicide; for let the most beef-witted and obtuce amongst them but look the matter searchingly in the face, and they must perceive, if not wholly recreant to common sense, that by thus endeavouring to force their bantlings above par, they stumble upon the precise means and method whereby to render them eventually uncurrent—to prepare for them, in fact, an almost certain oblivion with the trash to which they are so injudiciously wedded: seeing that a musical air has only a true vitality as the expressed soul of some genuine Lyric— as the meet spouse of immortal verse. For though it be true, that the Lyrical Paradise were Eveless without Melody, it is just as true, that Melody itself can only become the mother of definite and permanent delight, when in fruition with the surer sentiment of language, as this is moulded into the shapes of life and beauty by the magical hand of Genius. As it is, however, our composers, without the lucky mistake of an exception, continue to launch their productions upon the unrefluent waters of time, each with a verbal millstone hung to its unhappy neck, and expect notwithstanding, that the world will find them “after many days”—and of course are disappointed. But do these remarks dig into the veritable root of the offence in question?
