The Righteousness of Nature.

In vain let Logic prove That right, which still we know To be oppressive, mean, or selfish, Feeling it is so. Here honesty of heart’s The wisdom of the case, And to the Righteousness of Nature, Logic should give place.

Natural (The logic of Nature)

Note. The honest and healthy necessities of unsophisticated human nature, are instinct with a logic which sur- -passes that of the schools as far as the veracity of the simple sun-dial transcends that of the more complex watch.

At a meeting of laborers the other day in England, held under the wide cold heaven of a winter’s night,—for their employers had denied them even the shelter of a barn,—and at which the hunger-bitten speakers read their resolutions by the light of a morsel of candle held in the “cauld hand” of each, like the corpses in Tam o’Shanter galvanised for the occasion;—at this ghastly meeting, a poor woman gave her sense of Free Trade in these words:

“If Free Trade would only give us a chance of more bread, they /the Rulers of the land/ should give us Free Trade—for we be starving.”

This was the logic of nature; and no protectionist in Europe could attempt to refute the just political principle involved in those few simple, ignorant words, without diving at a plunge into the sophistry of selfishness, or flying off at a gallop into the declamation of dishonesty.

But when “reason is our bent” do any of us pay sufficient attention to the voice of sentiment and feeling pleading in every soul? I think not: and hence that perpetual warfare between the wisdom of the head and the wisdom of the heart.
