Social Charity.

‘Tis Life’s prime household wisdom, not to scorn An erring Friend too bitterly. If tried Severely in some weakness, all will turn To folly sometimes—till Love’s cheek be dyed With blushes such as for the fallen burn: So much even in the best is there to mourn And knowing this, we should be sadly kind, Not cruel, in reproof; for our own hearts Are wayward also. Though in One we find Those gems of worth, warm feelings and high parts, Set in the pure gold of a constant mind? Yet even that One subject is to starts Of evil: in the clearest well there lies A sediment—disturb it, and ‘twill rise.

By way of comment on the above, let the reader ask his experience, whether even his most virtuous & reasonable self has not been often confirmed in his follies rather than 98 convinced and divested of them, by the censures of such his best disposed friends. And the reason of this is just this, their censures were intemperate. That is, they painted his follies, as they would the devils, blacker than they were—and the natural effect of this was, to provoke him to defend them, and thence, by an equally natural, to like them rather. They were but his rake-helly acquaintances at first, but having had to take up the cudgles for them, of course they became his moral protegés. Besides, to make the censure of evil its abettor, it is only necessary jus to make it severe enough to make it arouse the defiance of the perpetrator—[unclear] and this is the pet method of its application on all hands, from mother Propriety to father Descretion. Purists and wiseacres may turn up the nose at all this, but I can tell them, and the wisdom of the future will repeat the assertion and enforce the assertion, that there is a social physic in it that, if taken heartily, would do even them a whole world of good.

C.H.
