LOST IN THE BUSH.

AN AUSTRALIAN SKETCH.

Lost in the bush—the night encroaching fast, And around loudening a cold-breathing blast! A sad affair ’tis! Fancy squatter Will Severely studying yon peculiar hill; From which he learns a lesson of dismay— It bluntly tells him, “Thou hast gone astray!” At this he backward hastes, intent to find Some prospect more familiar to his mind. How puzzling ’tis! still stranger seems to grow The backward scene o’er which he hurries so; Whereat Will mutters, scanning o’er the ground, “Dang it! the world is surely twisted round!” And then, with sudden jerk, the anxious swain Turns, just to contemplate that hill again. Gad! which is which?—a dozen summits lie As like as eggs against the twilight sky. With tart alarm, and comical surprise, Will opes his mouth at this, and eke his eyes; Squats on his hams, and strains his neck, thus bent, And this way bobs his head, and that—intent To put the pleasant fact to final test— Which done, he draws his breath and mutters “Well, I’m blest!” Scratches his head, and much that member loads With execrations: “What! forsake the roads For shorter cuts, so near the day’s decline, ‘Mongst hills and brushes?—Drat this head of mine! The night, I know, will fall as thick as mire, The bush too’s roaring like a world on fire! I am not one much given to foolish frights, And far too knowing to believe in sprites; Yet such a night, in such a curséd place, Might almost put one in a raving case: And somewhere here, for certain, it is said The blacks have burial—oh, this silly head! Besides, Peg may forget to bed the sow, Big Bess, that is so near her pigging now; Nor can she by herself, with all her care, Secure the stack from that downdacious mare; Or pen those gallows calves up from the kine— I must regain the road!—Oh, this thick head o’ mine!”

Thus bringing into catalogue his woes, On, dogg’d and desp’rate, he at random goes. Ah Will, dame Fortune surely owes thee spite! Thy blazing hearth shall know thee not to night! Vainly thy Peggy watches for her Will To loom in sight upon the ‘customed hill, Thus saying to herself, with gesture grim, “He’s boozing somewhere, but I’ll give it him!” And just as vainly dost thou onward toil, Dismally cooeying all the weary while, And wish—how kind! that thou hadst there with thee Thy neighbour Wilson—just for company.

Now thickest glooms blank every forest path, And, like a large Cyclopian eye in wrath, Two huddled masses of dark clouds between, One only star glares wildly on the scene. Those dun clouds streaking as he sails along, The owl pours forward his funereal song; Sounds of strange waters in the hollow blast, With distant howlings, frequent come and fast; Startlingly near, and spectre-like to see, Sharp-shrieking squirrels stream from tree to tree; And the wild cat, with breath suspending rush, Whisks like a devil from the shaken bush! Ah Will, such hour may well thy spirit grieve, Most woe-begone, unlucky son of Eve!

Worn out at length, he throws him down—and sleep There finds him, coiled beside a bleaching heap Of drifted leaves, which patteringly alight From the hoarse blast—while, loaden with its might, Huge trees sway solemn o’er th’ unconscious wight.

But now, when night, a limping hag, her shades Had westward called, and gray had grown the glades, Stung into partial consciousness, ‘twould seem, By the increasing cold, he dreams a dream:— Himthinks, a banished man, alone he fares ‘Neath polar skies, and battles hard with bears, Gigantic fellows! shagg’d with ice, and still Dev’lishly bent on dishing squatter Will: And, just as in the strife a leg is lost, (That luckless leg most bitten by the frost) Sweating and wild, he wakes. His face forlorn Then rubbed, he looks and hails the wished-for morn; Scans o’er the unknown scene with rueful eyes, And folds his arms, and shakes his head, and sighs: When hark! distinctly, although far away, He hears some house-dog’s thrice repeated bay— Starts from his huddled posture at a bound, And, joyful, pushes for the grateful sound. Not long his search; for now the morning star Wheels from the eastern steep her shining car, And in her pallid but effectual glow A hut and clearing stand revealed below. Thither he hastens, and is welcomed there, Consoled and fed with hospitable care; And when the sun shall crown the wintry day His kindly host shall set him on his homeward way.

Charles Harpur.Singleton, October, 1842.
