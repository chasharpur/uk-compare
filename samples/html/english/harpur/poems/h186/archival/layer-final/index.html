Original Poetry.

IGNORANCE.

[By Charles Harpur.]

What stoned the Prophets of the olden time, Viewing their ministry with hateful glance, Till the live truth became their deady crime? ‘Twas Ignorance.

And what in Homer’s ever classic clime Left him to wander the blind sport of chance; And murdered Socrates, its sage sublime? ‘Twas Ignorance.

What nailed the immaculate Jesus to the cross; And called the rabble demon-like to dance About the balefires of his Followers’ loss? ‘Twas Ignorance.

What dungeoned mild Galileo, wise and good; And what, bewitched in fancy, watched askance Some querulous Grandam’s mere decrepitude? ‘Twas Ignorance.

What burned her afterwards, and cried—No quarter To all such! lo, the hosts of Hell advance! And here is God’s own sanction for the slaughter? ‘Twas Ignorance.

What transformed Liberty into the birth Of murderous License, when the wrongs of France Called on mankind to fraternize the earth? ‘Twas Ignorance.

What stultifies America to arm Her peaceful genius with a conqueror’s lance And plume her sons with a barbaric charm? Mere Ignorance.

And what makes England’s “stupid starers” yet Fall into wonderment as in a trance, To see some fool’s head fill a coronet? Sheer Ignorance.

And what too in Australia makes the creed Of Mammon, the sole faith ‘neath heaven’s expanse That all men are devout in?—what indeed, But Ignorance.*

* ”Where Ignorance is bliss, ’tis folly to be wise,” said Gray, and many affect the possession of wisdom by the grace of quoting him with a profound shake of the head. But the profundity of such heads usually inclines them to the wrong side; and they mistake, even in this case, the scope of their author’s meaning, as limited by the first word of the context—”Where Ignorance” &. Even with this limitation, however, the philosophy of the say- ing is bad, standing in a detached shape as above. Bliss is never, under any circumstances, an essen- tial issue of ignorance. True, very ignorant per- sons may yet be very blessed;—blessed in their worldly position, their affections, their dispositions: but not by virtue of their ignorance—happiness and it, in such cases, being accidentally co-incident, and not one the result of the other. It may happenal so, that our misapprehension of a particular oc- currence shall conduce to the maintenance of our present peace; or that a false belief may be pro- ductive of pleasant emotion; but neither that peace, nor this emotion, are inherent qualities of our state of error, but of our state of feeling, which would be precisely the same were both circumstances true, as perceived under an equal degree of assurance thereof, with that permanency in addition, which is combined with the demonstrability of whatever is so. But admitting that there is a bliss proper to ignorance, of what kind must it necessarily be? Just pig-bliss —the indulgence of bodily appetite to repletion, and of animal desire to satiety. Within the corporeal supremacy of such a blessed condition, the spiritual graces of our being are nothing; while all beyond it, even to the material bulk of the earth, is a mere blank, or a vague wonder. And what man of re- finement, then, be his circumstances otherwise as they may, can soberly ask himself the question, and not feel that he would be incalculably a loser by an exchange of being with another, who, however for- tunate in all worldly respects, should be only capaci- tated for a felicity like this? With any one, indeed, to whom, beyond the circle of his physical occupa- tions and enjoyments, and a few vulgar hopes and as vulgar recollections, the universally impassioned face of Nature—impassioned with the spirit of Love and the grace of Beauty—is either an uninterpreted puzzle, or a meaningless extension of form. But there is no such man. For however we may revert in fancy to the bliss of non-intelligence, when im- patient perhaps of the apparent blindness of fortune, or the positive injustice of the world; yet it is the very triumph of Knowledge to disable us of any real or lasting faith in the happier results of such a condition; and the more universal our knowledge, the more universally triumphant in this respect, will both its moral and physical evidence be.

C. H.
