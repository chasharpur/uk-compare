THE EMIGRANT’S VISION.

BY CHARLES HARPUR.

As our Bark dashed away o’er the night shrouded deep, And out tow’rds the South I was gazing, O’er my soul first there gathered a dimness like sleep, Then the light o! a Vision amazing: As rises the Moon, from the white waves afar Came a Goddess, it seemed, of love wisdom and war, And on her bright helmet, encircling a star, Behold there was graven—Australia.

Her robes were as green as the mantle of Spring, Newly spread by the streams that so mildly Elapse through yon flock-legioned plains, or that sing ‘Mid those blue-ranging mountains so wildly; Her looks were as bright as the lustre that lies At morn on the seas of the South, and her eyes Were as deep in their joy as the clear sunny skies— The clear sunny skies of Australia.

O Stranger, she asked, hast thou fled from the home Which thy forefathers bled for so vainly? Does shame for its past thus disgust thee to roam, Or despair of its future constrain thee? In the far sunny South there’s a refuge from wrong, When the Shiloh of Freedom, expected so long, To Genius already is shouting his song— ‘Tis the evergreen Land of Australia.

There truth her abode on the forest-clad hills Shall establish a dweller for ever; And Plenty rejoice by the thence-flowing rills, Aye wedded to honest Endeavour; Till the future a numberless People shall see All equally noble as equally free! And the God they adore the sole monarch shall be! Then come;—build thy Home in Australia.

This said, tow’rds the South she passed brightly away, And at once as from slumber I stared;— But the cadences sweet of its welcoming lay Yet breathed of the Vision departed: And when o’er the deep these had fadingly spread, The swell of my heart, as it throbbed to my head, Seemed to break into words on my tongue—and I said Be the Home of my hope then—Australia.
