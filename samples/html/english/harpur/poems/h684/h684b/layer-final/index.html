THE DYING SORROW OF THE WIDOW’S BOY.

“I do not fear to die, mother, I do not fear to die; For is there not a better world Beyond the starry sky? But when past happy days and nights Come floating through my mind, With all thy tender looks, I weep At leaving you behind.

“‘Tis leaving you behind, mother, With little sister Bell; Whom we do love so tenderly, Who loveth us so well! I’m thinking of a thousand things That we have done in play; And that she will be brotherless When I am gone away.

“And who will bring you sticks, mother, And water from the well? And who—for ’tis a lonely way— Will go to school with Bell? I know ’tis sinful to repine, And good to be resigned— But, oh! I cannot cease to think Of leaving you behind.

“Give me your hand to feel, mother, My sight is failing fast; And kiss me once again—again, For all the happy past.” Then in the calm of perfect love, As silently he lay, Her gentle-hearted child looked up— Looked—smiled—and past away.

She mourns, and will not be consoled; And yet the widow’d one A fortunate mother I must hold Who mourns for such a son. For God, whose beams beneficent Doth to our hearts reveal it, If Love were mortal, ne’er had given Mortality to feel it.

That spiritual clinging to the past Which living love engenders; That kindred feeling which the heart In dying not surrenders: Both in their weakness and their strength To a right Faith declare, Even by the pang of parting Here, A happy meeting There.

CHARLES HARPUR.

Pyrmont, January, 1845.
