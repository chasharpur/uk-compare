The Widow’s Boy.

I do not fear to die, Mother,— Why should I fear to die, Since God hath many a better world Away in the starry sky? But thinking how the Past hath left Its sunsets in my heart, With all thy loving motherhood, I weep that we must part.It

151

It is leaving you alone, Mother, With little sister Bell, Whom we both love so tenderly, And who loveth us so well: I’m thinking of a thousand things That we have done in play— And how lonely will poor Bell be When I am reft away.

And who will bring you chips, Mother, And water from the well? And who—for ’tis a lonely way— Will go to school with Bell? I know ’tis sinful to repine— ‘Tis good to be resigned, Yet sorrow is in the thought of how I’m leaving you behind.

Nor can I well forego, Mother, The fondly cherished plan Of all I had to do for you When once I were a man: Ah! this, that erst in prospect touched The world with summer bloom, Has withered down to a cold grey waste Stretched out beyond the tomb

But when the flowers are growing, Mother, About the little mound That near my Father’s larger grave Shall ridge the Churchyard ground, Go you not there for sorrow then, But down on the grassy sod,To 152 To kneel and pray (as I’ve heard you oft) A Mother’s prayer to God.

Hark! hear you that sweet music, Mother, That breathes as from afar? And see you yonder heavenly light Outshining like a star? Its bright haze wraps me, and there falls An Image o’er my brain— I wander—for it seemed my Sire Was here with us again.

Give me your hand to feel, Mother, My sight is failing fast; And kiss me again—and yet again For all the faithful Past.— Then in the calm of perfect love, As silently he lay, Her gentle-hearted Boy looked up— Looked—smiled—and pass’d away.

She mourns, and will not be consoled, And yet the widowed one A fortunate Mother I must hold Who mourns for such a Son: For God, whose mercy never bruised A spirit but to heal it, If Love were mortal, ne’er had used Mortality to feel it.

That soul-felt life within the Past Which living love engenders; That kindred pulse which is the last The dying heart surrenders; 153 Do give our hearts assurance sweet The more their ends we trace, That here we only part, to meet In some supernal place.

For were the truth not herein said, Even Love were oft a curse, Since had we cherished Hate instead, It had hardly bruised us worse! But God is Love—and hence its power The life of Man doth leaven, To gift its frailty with a flower Whose final fruit is Heaven.
