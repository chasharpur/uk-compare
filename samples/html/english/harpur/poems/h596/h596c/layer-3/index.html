XXXII. ☞

To a Friend, On the death of one of his Children.

never yet, my Friend, have I lost a child, Yet can I feel what such a loss must be; Will apprehend how the heart waileth wild At the death-parting with a creature we Have loved so largely—to our misery! And how all objects that before had smiled In union with that love, must thence be soiled With the damp earth-taint of mortality! Still, though thus hurt, let us no less adore God’s ways. Our loss is only a remove Of the Belovèd to some heavenly shore; And whither her spirit, like a mystic dove, Hath trailed memorial clues. The strength of Love Might teach us this much were it all our lore.
