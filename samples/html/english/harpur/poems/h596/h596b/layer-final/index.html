XLIV. X

To a Friend, on the Death of one of his Children.

I know not what it is to lose a child, But I can feel what such a loss must be; Can apprehend how the heart waileth wild At the death-parting with a creature we Have loved so largely—to our misery! And how all objects that before have smiled In union with that love, must thence be soiled With a damp sense of our mortality.Still, 272 Still, though thus hurt, let us no less adore God’s ways. Our loss is only a remove Of the Belovëd to some heavenly shore, And Whither her spirit, like a mystic dove, Trails a way-proving clue. The strength of Love Might teach us this much were it all our lore.
