VII.

Grey.

The loud apt epithet, applying sure; The dim-drawn image, artfully obscure; 66 The perfect stanza, framed of words as choice And round as pearls, yet liquid to the voice; A pith of phrase, and musical array Of numbers;—these are the prime charms of Grey.

The naked majesty and open wonder Of true sublimity, heaped in lines of thunder; That artless grace wherewith the Olden Time Dandled the happy infancy of rhyme; That negligent melody which so shames the trick Of wire-drawn Verse, and verse-drawn Rhetoric;— These in our rich old bards abound, but these To him were Literary Heresies. x

x Previous to making my final copy of these Lines, I reread Grey with the view of finally testing their justice—and saw no reason to alter a word of them. Beyond an exquisite finish and a sculpture-like art of personification, I cannot see the vaunted wonderfulness of his productions. Nay, with all the figurative life of poetry, he seems to me to have been deficient, like Pope of its diviner breath:—that something which we cannot describe, nor always readily apprehend; but which, if faithful to our ideal instincts, we can always miss, where it is not.—Still Grey’s productions will ever be held in high scholarly esteem—and de- -servedly so. Their classical completeness, regarded simply as com- -positions, must forever secure to them a standard rank in English Literature.

I doubt not, the boldness of these criticisms will be objected to in

me; that they will be denounced by many as rebellious manifes- -toes recklessly dashed in the sapient faces of the whole host of the Aristar- -chi, past and present. Be it so. In all such matters, I can acknowledge but one great authority—the Truth, or what I believe to be the Truth. Hence (so to speak) I have no popular solicitude. Disposed in the first place to regard only the Truth, I can naturally dispose myself in the second, to wish even my own verdicts to become current only in the event of their being elucidatory or sug- -gestive of it—and of it alone. Within its pale then, I have no fear; and without it, I ask no favor. And let no man sneer at this profession of literary honesty. Could I write subserviently to the wills, wishes, or expectations of others,—what need were there for my dwelling alone, as at this present, in a mere shed, with Mount Kobanbonia in the prospect? C.H. 1847.
