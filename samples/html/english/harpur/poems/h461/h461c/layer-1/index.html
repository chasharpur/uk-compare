The style of these pieces may not be pleasing to all readers. Those especially who still think that 253 Pope and his associates were our model versemen, will be apt, I doubt not, to pronounce it loose. Yet I have chosen it advisedly. It is intended to be something betwixt the heroic rhyme of Dryden and that of Cowper, with also a running dash in it of the open manner of the Elders Masters of English verse. Hence, though I have partially avoided the super-precise couplet balance of Pope, as being in itself, if continuously adhered to in a poem of any length, a most restrictive and wearisome, though mellifluous monotony; I have yet considered that somewhat of such a balance should be maintained recurrently, or upon the whole, in our regular heroic rhyme. In other words, this balance, or somewhat approximating to it, should only be frequently varied—by unexpected breaks in the metres, for the sake of a stronger emphasis, or by runnings over from the rhyme, into a more impetuous cadence. But in thus giving freedom and room for invention to the formal manner of these pieces, I have been careful, on the other hand, not to slide to deeply into that blank- verse-rhymed style (if I may coin a term for it) which was so much used by both Keats and Shelley; because it was just such a failure as might be expected to follow from the meeting of extremes, however strikingly it may have indicated (as in fact it did) the prior ex- 254 existence of an opposite abuse.

Note.

We often hear Chaucer referred to, as being a well of undefiled English (the phrase coming, 256 I think, from Spencer,) and I would note first on this head, that our words of Saxon origin, though always somewhat harsh, are yet for the most part singularly expressive, and certainly not the less so for that very reason. The word think, for example, appears to me to possess in its mere sound, an originally spontaneous consonance with the cerebral sensation—the intense brain-throb that mostly accompanies the act of deep and sustained reflection. And no less expressive of their objects are the words smooth, rough, slow, swift, thrill, thunder, wrench, crash,—but this last may be from the French ecraser. Then there are slip, glide, flicker, rattle, rumble, shout, flight, spill, sudden, and a multitude of others that are equally echoes as it were of the ideas attached to them. Or rather, they are the native utterances of the things they go for, each speaking with its own voice and with no other, whether rough or smooth, strenuous or feeble. And this excellence in the language is no doubt due to the earnest and straightforward character of the several Northern races by whom it was first founded.

Even Pope, though in the main so unfaithful (from classicist prejudice) to his native perceptions, 257 was quite alive upon certain suggested occasions to the verbal proprieties here adverted to. See, for example, in his version of the Iliad, the whole description of the felling and drawing of timber in Mount Ida—part of which I will transcribe.

First march the heavy mules, securely slow, O’er hills, o’er dells, o’er crags, o’er rocks they go, Jumping, high o’er the shrubs of the rough ground Rattle the clattering cars, and the shocked axles bound. But when arrived at Ida’s spreading woods, x x x x x x x Loud sounds the axe, redoubling strokes on strokes, On all sides round the forest hurls her oaks Headlong. Deep echoing groan the thickets brown, Then rustling, crackling, crashing, thunder down.

In this passage, so remarkable for the agreement between sound and sense, at least four fifths of the words of the Saxon and its kindred tongues. And in the following lines from his Essay on Criticism, which are yet more remarkable perhaps for their verbal and sentimental concordance, there are only some seven or eight altogether—even including the two proper names— 258 that are not of the same growth.

When Ajax strìves some ròck’s vàst wèight to throw, The lìne toò labors and the wòrds mòve slòw. Not so when swift Camilla scours the plain, Flies o’er the unbending corn and skims along the plain.

But some suppose the verbal proprieties here insisted upon, are mere associative attributions—that is to say, are wholly derived from a relation which has long subsisted in our minds between the articulate structure of certain vocables and their objects or ideas; that the sound of the word slow, for example, if it had been thus mentally united to rapidity of motion, would be just as expressive of it, as that of the word swift. But this supposition is obviously erroneous. The sound and sense do not agree in this case, and in others like it, because of our having constantly associated a certain sound with a certain act or object, but the association itself has manifestly obtained because of a certain primordial congruency subsisting between them in their conjoint relation to a perceiving and inventive organism. The nature of the thing to be signified threw some mystical image of itself into the nature of the applying sound, and they were joined together 259 accordingly by the mind of man, as the intelligent witness of this impictured agreement, or intershadowing tendency of like to like.

To deny the correctness of this view altogether, were tantamount to asserting that sweetness, or harshness, or jollity, or sorrowfulness, even in music (the science and impassionment of sounds) is solely a consequence of the mood in which we may happen to hear it, and not a superinducement, as it actually is, of a peculiar and corresponding condition of soul.

Nay, this primative kinship between actual things and their verbal symbols obtains in a very evident degree even in regard to qualities of strength and magnitude. The word huge, for example, seems to me to be one of the most natural explosions that could break from the broad chest of an ardent and original man, upon his first beholding something vast beyond expectation— something enormous.

In fine, but for some such pre-existing agreement between the sound of words and the sense of them, as is above asserted, the poetical canon in that regard would be utterly useless. Their brightness or darkness, or beauty, or ugliness to the eye in relation to their objects, is more, though not 260 entirely, a matter of foregone mental association.— But to return to Chaucer.

To estimate the worth of his genius aright, we must take into account the crudity in all respects of the age in which he flourished. We must make large allowance also for the mutations which our language has since undergone; and in respect of his versification especially, we must be satisfied with just so much of grace and beauty as are here and there indicated in those passages which can be restored in the greatest measure to their metrical integrity. In this spirit I, for one, have handled his writings; and I do not hesitate to accept them, as offering abundant evidence throughout of being the mental raiment of a very great Poet.

In these writings themselves, however, considered apart from their author and the age in which they were written, I do not think so highly as some profess to do. I value them more as exhibiting unmistakeable indications of a great poetical spirit, in its workings within itself, and in its walkings abroad in a comparatively rude world, than as absolutely containing in themselves any consummate fruit of poetry. And of his value 261 thus far, the most cursory study of their ivy-mantled lineaments—of the Gothic arches, crumbled though they be, and pillared aisles (to speak allegorically) of this first great builder of English Poetry—will convince any one having ideality enough in himself to appraise them heartily and with a right critical instinct. For no such reader can fail to percieve, the fine strokes of passages in these olden rhymes like the following, or hesitate to assign them to the highest order of poetical perception.

A Perfect Knight.

And evermore he had a sovereign praise, And though that he was worthy he was wise, And of his port as meek as is a maid.

How finely robust is the expression here “a sovereign praise”?—a perfectly original one in Chaucer. And how otherwise could the poet so well have impressed us with an idea of the unaffected modesty of a perfect knight, or gentleman, as by the allusion in the last line?

How happily is the religious simplicity of the Prioress here indicated by the quality of her greatest oath! It is in subtle real-life seizures of this kind that one of the chief excellencies of Chaucer lies. Then he is not content to tell us that the gentle and good Nun has a very very scrupulous conscience and most tender heart, but with the full moral faith of a great Poet in his great moments, he will have her “all conscience and tender heart.”

Of a piece with this nice seizure of certain real-life characteristics which conduce so strikingly to the imagina- -tive realization of his persons, is this faither habit in Chaucer, of gathering about them, as with a simple dash of the pencil, the remotest circumstances of their foregone being. If, for example, he has to do with the frosty beard of an old sailor, he is sure to bethink him of how often it must have streamed, like a meteor, upon the mighty breath of the storm:

“With many a tempest had his beard been shaken.”

A Good Priest.

He waited after neither pomp nor reverence, Nor makëd him no spicëd consciënce, But Christ his love, and his Apostles twelve, He taught, but first he followed it himselve

That “spiced conscience” is exquisite. Under the fair front of a pleasant sin he was for detecting its leprous ultimate, and wrong, however perfumed, was no less wrong to him. And the other stroke in italics is also a thoroughly home one.

A Poor Scholar.

Of study took he mostè care and heed, Not a word spake he morè than was need, And that was said in form and reverēnce, And short and quick, and full of high sentēnce. 265 Sounding in moral virtue was his speech, And gladly would he learn and gladly teach.

What a noble draft is that! And with what a fine couplet does it conclude. It is the true English heroic couplet, not composed, like Pope’s, of two lines having each a cesural balance, but flowing freely forth, and yet at the same time open to every variety of modulation. And notwithstanding all that has been dinned abroad to the contrary, I cannot help thinking the true English heroic verse the best of all kinds of verse—entire, compact, continuous, and yet admitting of all the harmonic pauses being located anywhere in it, from the first syllable to the last; while the boasted hexameter itself, is not so much one prolonged [unclear] determinate verse, however varied, as it is two demi-verses, hinged together in the middle.
