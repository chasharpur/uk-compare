I

Chaucer.

Outstanding from the twilight of his days In bold relief, he takes the student’s gaze; As some grey-crumbling Abbey glooms sublime A failing wonder to a later time. And one so spiritual for his age, And studiously happy in the sage Sweet mystery of Rhyme, most surely claims High mention on the roll of Everlasting Names.

A well of English undefiled is he, Say scholiasts,—ever brimming bounteously! But what most takes me while I spell him o’er, Is an all-hearty love of mental lore, Together with a crowing, frank delight In primal Nature’s beautiful and bright Yet daily objects;—for it is the creed I ever carry with me when I read, That whoso had for these beloving eyes Was more than commonly both pure and wise;— Was one in whom an earnest joy in good More than redeemed the errors of his blood— Was stored in spirit with a portion rich Of that transcendent seraphism, which Of all things most, designed to intimate Man’s elevation from the brute’s estate, Declares the music of his life to be 52 Something that cannot perish utterly: But virtual in the world’s prime features still, (Sun, moon, and stars; clear stream, and vernal hill) An effluence that the Present with the Past Connects transfusédly—and which, at last, May constitute Humanity a whole, On-flowing through all time—one mighty Stream of Soul. x

x I will append by way of Note to this criticism an ex- -quisite bit of prose from the pen of an American Poet, (Lowell.) “There is in him the exuberant freshness and greenness of Spring. Everything he touches leaps into full blossom. His gladness and humour and pathos are irrepressible as a fountain. Dam them with a prosaic subject, and they will overleap it in a sparkling cascade that turns even an hindrance to a beauty. Choke them with a tedious theological disquisition, and they bubble up forthwith all around it with a delightful gurgle. xxx Reading him is like bursting brushing through the dewy glades at sunrise; every thing is new and sparkling and fragrant.”—There is a bright bubbling bead of beauty upon every word of this passage. For freshness of thought and felicity of expression it could hardly be surpassed. C.H.
