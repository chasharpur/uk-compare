No III.

Milton.

1.

His Epic Style.

In this what a grave beauty claims our praise Through the long march of his stupendous lays! The epithets how full, fit, loud! How terse The idioms, building up the lofty verse! Then in his management of tune and time How richly can we spare the oil of rhyme! What melody of cadence, flowing still Into one lengthened harmony—until (Even as a heaped up wave, with rising roar Onrolling, breaks upon the quaking shore) Some grand redundancy, up-billowing clear, With doubled thunder fills the astonished ear! Which thence more soothable, is soothed once more By concords yet more perfect than before. Such graces these of Epic style, that such Being wanting, Poesy would lack too much.

But why looked Johnson on his verse askew, So that for long quite critical it grew To fault it,—nay, to wish it cast anew? Simply through narrowness of system, bred From a restrictive tunelessness of head, That just a see-saw couplet’s limbs could scan, Filed up and jingled on the Grub-street plan; 310 Or a spruce quatrain’s alternations nice— But not the numerous peals of the Lost Paradise! To these no oracle within him gave Meet response,—even as some sea-shore cave Resounds in measure to the varying wave. In fine, for these he had nor ear nor soul, He could not mete their interflowing roll, Each being (try them, all who yet may doubt) A piece of “linkéd sweetness long drawn out.”
