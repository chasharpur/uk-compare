II.

My dear, dear Charley! can it be that thou Art gone from us for ever? While I sit Amid these forest shadows that now fall In sombre masses mixed with sunny gleams Upon thy early grave, and think of all The household love that was our mutual lot So late, and during all thy little life— Thy thirteen years of sonhood,—it is hard, (So dream-like wild it seems) to realise The shuddering certainty that thou art now In the Eternal World—as reft away 95 In one dread moment from thy Father’s heart! Thy young intelligence from his lonely side So reft for ever—and evanished thence As utterly as hath thy Shadow, once So surely there,—now gliding, and now still,— Thy slim Boy-Shadow! leaving him, alas! Thus sitting here forlorn—here by thy Grave New made and bare, as upon Life’s bleak brink, To stare out deathward through his blinding tears!

And lo, thy Brothers and thy Sisters, Charley how They miss their vanished Playmate, so beloved, And so endeared by years of happy help, And many a pleasant old-faced memory! I see them often, when thy name is breathed By me, or by their Mother, how they look Away enquiringly, out into space, As if they thought thy Spirit might be there, And still yearned tow’rds them with a saddened love Like that in their own hearts. And him, alas! Who at thy side, when death came swift upon thee, Sent out through the wild Forest such a shriek As never until then might break the peace That nestles in its lairs—ah! when to him Shall the so haggard memory of that day Be other than a Horror—such as clothed In terrible mystery, for ever keeps Stalking beside us in some ghastly dream.

But most I pity Her who bore thee, Charley; Whose mother bosom was at once the nest And fountain of thy infant life, and who Through all thy after years, was ever wont To shield thee with her love, and doat the while 96 (Though with some fear) upon those spirited ways, And nascent self-reliances, that seemed So bravely promissory of the Man, Loving thee more perhaps than even I— If that be possible;—and to whom, ’tis plain, All things are changed now through her loss of Thee! All home consuetudes, and hosehold wonts, And motherly providences, which before Did fill the passing hour so pleasantly, Changed now and irksome, as if life itself With all its motives, suddenly had grown In her distracted feeling of its state, Delusive as a dream. Then will she come, And gaze out hitherward, and up to heaven, With eyes so asking that they seem to say, “Where is my Darling, and why was he torn Away so rudely from a love like mine?” —In vain! in vain! Art thou so vacant then, O thou wide Heaven! that no pitying star May seem to breathe down through the forest trees, With mystical assurance that the Past Is living, and not dead! that no refrain Of lingering spirit-sympathy may for once Intone the melancholy wind, as thus Its waves surge overhead, with what might seem Some intimation from beyond the Grave That Love can never cease.

And is it wholly well, that it is thus So deaf, so dumb, so blindly separative, That even Love (though in the morning glow 97 Of Human Faith once visioned to have moved The inexorable profound of Hell itself) May stare tear-blinded from its hither shore And shriek to it in vain? that from beyond No quieting whisper may across it breathe Of peace from the Immortals? not a glimpse Of that Elysian beauty which enrobes As with the garment of the Diety, all Its heavenward coast, e’er reach us?—while we here Sit groaning,—full of wild misgivings,—full Of mournful memories and embittered wonts; And so engloomed, so overcast by dark Disquieting doubts, that we are often fain To leap from them at once, though out of life, Madly desirous to have done with time.

Broken as I am, Let me strive rather to believe that God Has ordered nothing otherwise than well: And thereby strengthened, let me teach my heart To hold with staunch conformity of faith, That he who now in this bleak world to us Is lost for ever—the bright Boy we loved— The Charley of our memory—whose death Came down amongst us in a guise so fierce, Was taken yet in mercy, and is now At home with Him! Nor is there aught methinks, Illogical in the piety that prompts To a believe like this—since God is Love, Since God is mercy;—Love and Mercy, both Not only infinite, but Fatherly; And since the soul our human weakness mourns Was but but a Child in time—a Child, and thence 98 Comparatively sinless.

Yes, were the heart Not tempered thus to sorrow for the Dead, It could not bless the Living as it does In shine and shower, and when the storms of time Are beating over with a fatal weight: It being the same tenderness that mourns For those so wildly, that loves these so well. —A weakness born of strength, since Jesus once Wept o’er a humble follower’s new-closed tomb— Wept in his human pity, even while He knew that his almighty power could call Its lowly tenant forth, to life renewed.
