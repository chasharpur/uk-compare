THE KANGAROO HUNT;

OR, A MORNING IN THE MOUNTAINS.

BY CHARLES HARPUR.

I.

Here, in the Forest, afar and free From the City’s enervate luxury— Luxury that ne’er attains To comfort, swallowing all its gains— That seldom knows, and ne’er enjoys Or health, or peace, for dust and noise; And reached not by those senseless rules That Folly learns in Fashion’s schools,— When first beyond the skiey trees That fringe some dawning height Aurora spreads her traceries Of grey and gauzy light— Whilst yet the western stars may be Glimmering far and fitfully, As, tarrying, they were fain to pay Apostate homage to the day— Glad as the spirit of the song Poured by the birds from every spray, (a) And fresh and lithe as the breezes strong That bear in their salubrious play That morning melody along— From a peace-curtained sleep we rise To look abroad with grateful eyes, Sallying forth to till the soil, Or resume in the forest our stalwart toil.

Or while Euroka (b) first displays His burning rim on the ancient hill, 297ba And the yet dusky world o’erlays With fanwise spreading, arrowy rays, And, rising gradual, greatens still— Greatens, till in full revealed, Like a vast and flaming shield Poised on that glorious eastern height, He floods abroad his golden light In one unbroken mass immense Of life-essential influence: Unveiling the glades of the forest then, And the winding depths of each shadowy glen, And sheathing every tripping stream With a quick and upward bickering gleam— While thus Euroka riseth red, Up, even to the kingly head Of some proud mountain, lo, we climb, Whereon, amid the crags sublime, Australian’s yet unchristened Muse,— A wandering Spirit of beauty rare, Loves oft to gem her streaming hair With heaven’s selectest durs, And scarf her bosom bright and bare With a robe of morning’s richest hues; Giving the while to all objects there, All sounds,— the water-drip, just heard— The hum of insect—voice of bird,— To every echo and every air A poetry unfelt elsewhere.

Lo, bodying forth to Fancy’s eye The glenward hanging clouds among, Her dream-drawn Train goes gliding by First, shadowed in a floating sphere Of rainbow colored splendor, strong Star-crowned Truth, and Beauty, appear, Twin Eons of all deathless song. Then all in love, though calm of mien, On these still gazing as along She paceth, Contemplation’s seen With dark-eyed Memory hand in hand. Then followed by a bright-haired band Of Cherub Joys,—lo, Temperance And Chastity, with air serene, In sage simplicity advance. Next pass, with sturdy Industry And concord for their sure defence, Those philosophic sisters three— Patience, Content, and Constancy. Here baby-featured Innocence To Wisdom meditation-pale, And lofty-browed Benevolence, Laughs out her happy tale. There stalks with rapture-lengthened stride Prophetic Feeling, side by side With Liberty,—both eagle-eyed. Then Duty like a shepherd clad, And Piety sedately glad, Precede Integrity long tried, And inward-smiling Peace, and she, A pleading angel,—Charity. Last of all the visioned throng Two winged Spirits seem to speed, As blameful of the past were they For hindering them so long;— Even Love and Hope, who seem alway About to do their happiest deed And breathe their sweetest song.

Many an eminent hill sublime Hast thou, Australia, like this we climb: Beyond the loud Napean they rise, And by old Hawskbury’s billowy tide, And by the rapid Hunter’s side, And where the Wallombi darkling lies, Or with a broken and lonely moan, Or with a sudden and strenuous chide, Adown its rocky channel hies;— Girt with wood to the craggy cone Up they go into the skies, O’er hills and vales and prairies wide Staring, as with stony eyes.

Now from some Crag brushed by the green Fresh sprays, wind-floated to and fro,— The extremest sprays of stately treen That find their roots in the ravine A dizzy depth below, Send the glad eye o’er a glorious View! Here ever-verdent vales are seen All veined with brooks that glister and glow— Green bays amid the mountains blue; Till brown by distance, and then grey, In winding indistinctiveness, they Behind those folding mountains go Upon their hidden way: Or else located plains appear As garnished by a genial year With Husbandry’s fair tribes—the soil Confessing well the Settler’s toil. Where into nooks the hollows run, There well dressed gardens timely smile; While orchards on the higher mould Their globy treasures manifold Hang forth in clusters to the sun Whose course in heaven is now begun: And where but dark lagoons erewhile Slept, and where yet the blind creek (c) strays, Their fields of tall and tassalled maize Stretch rank on rank away, Even as embattled legions stand Ere Slaughter’s thunder-bearing hand Hath shattered their array. And where, ’mid all, a line of Homes As from a verdent ocean looms, Hearth-smokes in wreathen pillars tall Grow in the stirless air— Ascending peacefully heavenward all Like metaphors of prayer: While from the steeming yards about The spotly kine are dotting out, And from the fold on yon bare slope That bounds the cultivated scope, There floodeth forth a fleecy flock, Even as from some mountain lock A sudden inundation flows And spreads in volume as it goes. 318ba And as the eye outruns it, through In broken depths of forest view, Fresh objects rise,—upcrowding small And smaller still,—like spectral shows Dimly intervolving all.

But hark! on yon high-waving bough That strokes the thin rack as it passeth now, The purple-head (d) with a flutter gay Gurgles forth his roundelay; And oh! in its melodious moods What thrilling meaning findeth aye Some listening Beauty of the Woods! Again his passion-tingling wings Quiver and spread;—again he flings Abroad the tale of his innocent thrall; When answering, from the lower dells A many-warbled chorus swells: The pipe of the brown thrush, to call His wandered mistress lifted high— The wren’s soft trill—the parrot’s cry— With the sounding troll of the valorous (e) pie, And the bell-bird’s pensive tinkle, (f)—all Mingling in melody, rise and fall; Or Ichu (g) in a fitful smack Ends his long note; the piercing crack Rages along with a gathering shock As Echo starts out from her cave in the rock, To wanton around in the hollow breeze And load the ear with her impish glees, Till from below the thronging strain Of the happy birds peals up again.

But oft these near delights foregoing, Soon as the morning star is glowing, The earliest Hunter’s call Is passed along from house to house Some tardy inmate to arouse— Until the younger neighbours all Are forth, each followed by his hounds; Then quickly gathered in a knot Upon the old accustomed spot, They wake the morn with merry sounds, As off they hie the Forest through To hunt the bounding (h) Kangaroo.

Far from the City’s dust and noise, Such are the inland dweller’s joys: Joys leaving him little besides to need In yielding him that which is riches indeed! It flows for him from a hundred springs, It rides on every breeze’s wings,— That priceless part of Nature’s wealth, That inward morning sunshine—Health! 319aa The sacred sourse of usefulness, Chief smoothener of life’s rugged path, And lightener of its worst distress! Which absent, even young Love hath Nor will to serve, nor power to bless! Which absent, the imperial crown But weighs its purple wearer down! For to Disease even Fortune’s rays Are warmthless, like the fen-fire’s blaze; And Glory’s animative power Serves but to throne Regret, as showing How flies unseized some star crowned hour— Is only a sunbeam glowing To the heart of a cankered flower.

(a) It is a very prevalent, but an erroneous notion, that the Australian birds are almost destitute of song. Often, indeed, our birds are wanting in sweetness of voice, and their song is yet more often defective, perhaps, in volume and con- tinuity; but this is very far from being the case universally. The magpie, for a familiar instance, has not only a merry, but a most musical chaunt. One set, in particular, of gurgling undertones which this bird warbles as it were to itself, is melodious to a degree almost incredible. There is also a kind of dwarf magpie, sometimes called ’the musician,’ which is notable for the extreme beauty of its voice. When several birds of this kind are assembled together in the same tree, or clump of trees, a concert of sweet sounds is the wonted consequence,—sounds mellow and sooth- ing as the purer tones of a flute, with occasional clarionet-like out gushes that are enchantingly spirited and musical. Of the notes of the butcher- bird, I have heard it admitted, even by persons somewhat prejudiced against all things Australian, that in richness and volume they compete with those of the nightingale, though they fail to sustain the comparison, in respect of a certain various and impetuous continuity; and all this I can very well believe. Several of our smaller birds too—birds yet nameless and fast becoming extinct—have a surprising variety of notes, which, when warbled together, are strikingly musical. Even the cries of the many parrot kinds, though somewhat harsh and discordant, are yet exceedingly gladsome. In newly settled localities, of a spring or autumn morning, I have heard the whole forest vocally alive as it were with the songs of birds, than which, in many instances, nothing could be more sweet and joyous—and certainly nothing more primatively exhilarating.

(b) Euroka is an aboriginal name of the sun. It is at once euphonious and robust, and therefore 297bb as soundingly indicative as a mere volcable can be, of the golden progression and godlike part of that paramount luminary: for which reason, I have here ventured, for once at least, to adopt it.

(c.) The channels or water-courses of summer- shrunk lagoons are called blind creeks. The term, I believe, is of Colonial growth, and very appro- priate; these channels being often so matted over with sedge and other water grasses, as not to be distinguishable from the general bed of the dry part of the lagoon—until the novice in ‘such branches of learning,’ has marched perhaps, like Richmond and his host (though in a different sense) ‘into the bowels of the land.’ I have heard the term also applied to mountain gulches that run only in rainy seasons. Blind, that is with- out the light of water, as seen generally.

(d.) The purplehead is a little elegantly formed bird, having a remarkably various and pleasing song. Not knowing its name (if it has any other than an aboriginal one), I have here called it after the most striking part of its plumage.

(e.) The Australian magpie called the ‘black and white,’ to distinguish it from another species less spotted, certainly deserves the epithet ‘valorous’ by which it is characterised in the text: especially in breeding time, when it is fierce even to fearlessness. Not only will the cock magpie readily give battle to any bird of prey that may approach nearer to his young than he is inclined to consider consistent with honesty of intention, but, assisted by his mate, he will pursue to a dis- tance, and sometimes kill the common bird-hawk. Nay, he will make good his ‘valorous’ repute by attacking even the lords of the creation. The author has had his hat dashed off by a bird of this kind, while passing unwittingly under the tree in which it had built its nest, to his no small alarm at the moment, he being wholly unexpectant of any such pugnacious salute, and unadvised also, as to the nature of the assailant. On an occasion like this, the parentally aroused magpie will come whizzing down at you with a really startling impetuosity, and you will hear his horny mandibles snap close against your cheek perhaps, with the severity of an iron rat-trap. Any one familiar with the country must have remarked the exceeding bravery of our native magpie.

(f.) This remarkable little bird—the bell-bird —is chiefly to be found in such mountain ravines as reserve a constant supply of water—its note being an infallible assurance of the vicinity of that element; that it is lying in the rock-tanks somewhere at hand, however hiddenly, or noise- lessly sliding through some concealed cleft. Hence, though the tone of its note is ‘pensive’ in seeming, 319ab it is exceedingly grateful to the thirsty way-farer. Each bird emits but a single sound, like the one tinkle of a very small bell; but when there are many of them in the vicinity, as there mostly are, disposed at equal distances in the surrounding trees, an irregular chime, ‘most musical, most melancholy,’ is kept ringing about one with an effect that is very wild and singular, and though always by association lonesome, yet pleasing in the extreme.

(g.) The bird whose note is here described is an inhabitant of densely wooded dells having water in them, and of thick cedar brushes near mountain 319bb creeks; for, like that of the bell-bird, its note infallibly indicates the vicinity of water. The bird itself is commonly called the ‘Coachman;’ a name evidently suggested by the resemblance of its note to that peculiar whistle with which carriage horses are sometimes incited forward, and by the farther circumstance of its ending in a sharp snap nearly as loud as the crack of a whip, and very much like it. I have written Ichu instead, thinking it equally characteristic, and certainly (though I am anything but squeamish in such matters) a trifle more poetic.

(h.) We say the bounding deer—the bounding horse, and so forth; but how peculiarly emphatic is the epithet when applied, as here, to the motions of the Kangaroo?
