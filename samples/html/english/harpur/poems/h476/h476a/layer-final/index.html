Rhymed Criticisms &c

XVI.

Polemical Tracts.

In worthy fellowship, see yonder strewn Such Books as one might fancy from the Moon, So strangely brain-affecting are they all And full of “lunes”—for they’re polemical.

Here Check and Counter-Check, together bound, Are in such loathing combination found, That surely nothing but the binder’s art Keeps them from huffing suddenly apart. Two curs, rank snarlers, that in schoolboy fun Are coupled, thus go tugging as they run— Tugging to tear assunder, though in vain, They run, they tug, and tug and run again, Whilst glaring eyballs and bared teeth display The horrible disgust of either Tray! x

Are these dark sophists in the simplest creed— Are these the true lights of the Church indeed? As drilled on their dogmatical parade, Behold how by their various “lunes” she’s made 314 To show her postures—nay, her linen too, Like a state strumpet at a common stew! And ever to appease some bigot, she Must drop some trapping called idolitry. These vow it savors of the Whore to wear Robes that are beautiful, or rich, or rare; Whilst those, all scandalised, as fiercely hold Such robes alone prevent her taking cold! Some say her hood becomes her not, and some Against her small-clothes push their quarrel home; Until amongst them she’s denuded so, That infidels flock round as to a Show!

Hark, one declares no Churchman now is right Save Doctor Black; another, Doctor White; A third pits against both Professor Second-sight! A fourth thinks gravely that the scriptures none Should handle but through Bishop Such-a-one! A fifth maintains that certain reverend eyes See plain in Daniel—what a sixth denies As one of the Devil’s deadliest home-brewed lies!

These irk my faith;—these tire and tantalise My patience,—till, all languid as she lies, 315 She looks this sentence through her half shut eyes: “Let all such Books, and all such Doctors, be Thoughtfully doubted—till they all agree.”

But such are not the ways by Wisdom trod: She searches nature till she finds her God! Better the open bounty of the Morn Shall show the scorner how thrice-poor is scorn! Better the mild divinity of Eve Shall teach even Vice and Folly to believe! Better the lilies of the field impart A sanctifying odour to the heart! Or be it mine, since Books thus jar in creed, The thoughtful stars and loving moon to read: These, in eternal visitation,—these Best gift us with eternal sympathies; Best show us how beyond the storms of time Infinte peace lies smiling and sublime; Best witness, while their perfect course they run, That God is Truth:—and Truth and Love are one!

Note.

X I regret that I do not still possess the book—or more exactly; the two books within the same binding— 316 which is referred to in the second paragraph of the text. It was a perfect curiosity in its way. A split, as it appeared, had occurred amongst a congregation of obscure religionists somewhere in Yorkshire, and a Tract was forthwith fulminated by one division against the heresy of the other. Whereupon, as it appeared farther, the parties so denounced, possess- -ing themselves of as many copies of this as they could, had them securely bound up with a Pamphlet of their own, in refutation, and then reissued in great apparent triumph, under the motto from Addison’s Cato

“My bane and antidote are both before me.”

A word as to the drift of the third paragraph. For myself, I think Religion can scarcely be too plain and simple in her ceremonial array, as being a creature altogether spiritual. What I would stigmatise in the text, is that eternal squabbling amongst sectaries, as to whether she should or should not be so, which has hitherto filled the vineyard of the Lord with angry unloving faces, and made even the Angel of it appear a termagant.

With reference to the matters and things satirised in the poem throughout, I would note, that it was not so much the singular differences existing amongst theologians 317 that provoked my ridicule, as their singular want of charity as well, and of that philosophic spirit which extends to those who differ from us in opinion, however widely, the benefit of our own doubts as to our own infallibility, or of that insurety of mind as to what is absolutely true or false, which must be a part of the consciousness of every sane disputant upon matters so abstruce, and even inconceivable, as are many of the doctrines and mysteries of Religion.

As to the truth or falshood—or rather the desirableness or undesirableness—of any eccleastical systems, the tests by which I guide my own judgment are the two following: Can this or that given system, said to be divinely instituted, be applied without an obvious wresting to purposes of political or social or moral evil? If it can, it is not of divine appointment, but rather a creature of the devil. Then is this or that given clerical system or polity entirely compatible with liberty of thought and discussion? If it is not, it cannot be of God, the maker of the material world, but the father of mind; the entire freedom of which must be essential, as a logical consequence, to the progressive development, and final possession, of all the benefits of this divine relationship.

In matters of pure ideality I can be as believing as most men. In the regions of all things of wonderful 318 beauty, or of beautiful wonder, I can allow my- -self to be as flighty as an oriental prophet or a boreal scald. But it is nevertheless the conviction of my reason, that in trying all human pretensions to religious power and scantity,—all human stewardship of divine mysteries,—we cannot be too rigidly guided and guarded by the axioms of common sense. We must, I would say, be so led, and so fortified, to prevent the great name and holy attributes of God from the possibility of being associated with the selfish divices of human passion, or of being fraudulently traded with by the human intellect, when it has become debauched, as it does inevitably, by the insidious influences of an undue authority, whether this authority itself happens to be old, and thence time-hallowed, or but the rank growth of yesterday.
