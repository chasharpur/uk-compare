Polemical Tracts

In worthy fellowship, see yonder strewn Such Books as one might fancy from the Moon, So strangely brain-affecting are they all And full of “lunes”—for they’re polemical.

Here Check and Counter Check, together bound, Are in such loathing combination found, That surely nothing but the binder’s art Keeps them from huffing suddenly apart. Two curs, rank snarlers, that in schoolboy fun Are coupled, thus go tripping as they run— Tugging to tear asunder, though in vain, They run, they tug, and tug and run again, Whilst glaring eyeballs and bared teeth display The horrible disgust of either Tray!

Are these dark sophists in the simplest creed— Are these these the true lights of Church indeed? As drilled on their dogmatical parade, Behold how by their various “lunes” she’s made To show her postures—nay her linen too, Like a state strumpet, at a common stew! And ever to appease some bigot, she Must drop some trapping called idolitry. These vow it savors of the Whore to wear Robes that are beautiful, or rich or rare; Such robes alone prevent her taking cold! Some say her hood becomes her not, and some Against her small clothes push their quarrel home; Until amongst them she’s denuded so, That infidels flock round as to a Show!

Hark, one declares no Churchman now is right Save Doctor Black; another Doctor White; A third pits against both Professor Second-sight! A fourth thinks gravely that the scriptures none Should handle but through Bishop Such-a-one! A fifth maintains that certain reverend eyes See in plain Daniel—what a sixth denies As one of the Devil’s deadliest home-brewed lies!

These irk my faith;—these tire and tantalise My patience,—till, all languid as she lies, She looks this sentence through her half shut eyes: “Let all such Books, and all such Doctors, be Thoughtfully doubted—till they all agree”,

But such are not the ways by Wisdom trod: She searches nature till she finds her God! Better the open bounty of the Morn Shall show the scorner how thrice-poor is scorn! Better the wild divinity of Eve Shall teach even Vice and Folly to believe! Better the lillies of the field impart A sanctifying odour to the heart! Or be it mine, since Books thus jar in creed, The thoughtless stars and loving moon to read: These, in eternal visitation,—these Best gift us with eternal sympathies; Best show us how beyond the storms of time Infinite peace lies smiling and sublime; Best witness, while their perfect course they run, That God is Truth:—and Truth and Love are one!
