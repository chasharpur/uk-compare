To the Rev. B. Quaife, on reading his Lectures and Letters on (against) the, “Moral Lawfulness of Capital Punishments.”*

The lack of hope hath left me oft supine— Gloomed round with ignorance and selfishness, That, as twin destinies, would fain possess My native land;—when with a sudden shine Rousing me into sympathy divine, The light of some clear spirit hath gloried out And made a mental sunrise round about— Of some clear spirit, instinct with thought, like thine! Thus one beholds, when ‘nighted on his way, Suddenly, over the drear woods, afar, Some friendly beacon’s salutary ray Yearn through the dark, like a new-kindled star.— Long from the paths where Truth and Mercy are The genius of my country cannot stray.

Charles Harpur.

* Starting from the admission of God’s moral government of the world, the right of society to punish criminals is merely a secondary or relative one, arising out of its temporal necessities and obligations, the whole scope and tendency—the “moral law- fulness” of which is strictly limited and determined by the cor- rective applicability of its punishments thereto. With the temporal or adventitious end of correction otherwise answered, society would have no right to coerce at all:—for “vengeance is mine, saith the Lord; I will repay.”

Thus stands, or should stand, the principle of social right, with

regard to the entire scale of legal inflictions, up to capital punish- ment: and here (admitting a future state of rewards and penalties) the question appears to be simply this. Has society, upon any plea whatever, a judicial right to amerce the individual member of aught that is clearly out of its own gift, or that has not directly resulted from its paternal conservation? Whatever else we may be said to owe to society, in no rational sense surely can its credi- torship be held to extend to life itself, which is primordially the gift of God. And therefore, by deliberately slaying even the mur- derer, a repetition of whose offence, in his particular case, it can otherwise prevent, it becomes likewise a fratricide:—it participates with him in the blood-guilty example of Cain! It does more;—it deliberately perpetuates it. A brother’s blood is upon his hands; but the shedder of his blood must be also a brother. He has justly forfeited his social liberty; but as God only could bestow the life he has destroyed, let God only dispose of his. In thus referring the matter to Him who cannot err, how can we err? “But it is politically expedient to exact life for life,” say t

old moralists; and “whatever is expedient is right.” And does so- ciety still play the part of echo to this heartless logic? Does it still prefer the cold cunning of the past, to the earnest wisdom of the present—to the moral glory of the future? That this doctrine of mere expediency, however dispassionately restricted, has yet an inveterate determination towards the tyrant-plea of necessity, is evinced by the painful fact, that it led even Paley into moral and political justifications of the Draco-code at that time obtaining in England; when the life of a man in the scale with that of a sheep— kicked the beam! Nay, it absolutely led him into the sophism, that he who was hanged by a mistaken sentence, might be heroi- cally considered as “falling for his country!“ And he could write this too, without (it would seem) even one dawning thought of how tremendously fearful a thing it was, thus to immolate an innocent man upon the altar of political selfishness! It behoves us then, the men of the present, to look forth from higher ground. Let it be admitted that the requirements of society may judi- c

lly commission it to take away, under circumstances of extrem- ity, and according to the perceptible degrees of their offences, any and everything short of life;—but not of life itself: for it is the sole hinge upon which depend the God-arrangements, not only of the existing, but of the to-be. It is the one frail but faithful re- flector between time and eternity of all our primary and final accountabilities—that is, to the unerring eye of God, which can alone perceive all their relations and extent. True, the murderer has impiously violated this order or condition of Providence; but can it therefore be morally lawful for society, by deliberately slay- ing him, to climax the example? It cannot. The power of civil government to punish capitally, even in his case, to be morally lawful, should premise in its behalf, a judicial paternity over its individual subjects, amounting in comprehension to a full know- ledge, both as to kind and degree, of all their liabilities in the life to come, as the ultimatum of the present one; or else ground its warrant for so doing upon the open denial of there being any liabilities at all beyond the condition of the death which it dares to inflict. Having the capacity to view the question thus in its true light, can society longer arrogate to itself, in fact, whatever it may admit in theory, a governmental equality with God—and yet be held morally guiltless?
