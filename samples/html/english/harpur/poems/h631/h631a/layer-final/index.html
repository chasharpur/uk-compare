Sonnet.

To the Rev B. Quaife, on reading his Lectures and Letters on (against) The “Moral Lawfulness of Capital Punishments.”※

The lack of hope hath left me oft supine,— Gloomed round with ignorance and selfishness, That, as twin Destinies, would fain possess My native Land;—when with a sudden shine Rousing me into sympathy divine, The light of some clear spirit hath gloried out And made a mental sunrise round about— Of some clear spirit, instinct with thoughts, like thine.

Thus one beholds, when ‘nighted on his way, Suddenly, over the drear words, afar, Some friendly beacon’s salutary ray Yearn through the dark, like a new-kindled star.— Long from the paths where Truth & Mercy are The Genius of my Country cannot stray.

x Starting from the admission of God’s moral government of the world, the right of Society to punish criminal’s is merely a secondary or relative one, arising out of its temporal necessities and obligations, the whole scope and tendency —the “moral lawfulness” of which is strictly limited and determined by the corrective applicability of its punishments thereto. 60 With the temporal or adventitious end of correction otherwise answered Society would have no moral right to coerce at all:—for “vengeance is mine, saith the Lord; I will repay.”

Thus stands, or should stand, the principle of social right, with regard to the entire scale of Legal inflictions, up to capital punishment:—and here ( admitting a future state of rewards and penalties) the question appears to be simply this. Has society, upon any plea whatever, a judicial right to amerce the individual member of aught that is clearly out of its own gift, or that has not directly resulted from its paternal conservation? Now, whatever else we may be said to owe to Society, in no rational sense surely can its creditorship be held to extend to life itself, which is primordially the gift of God; and the strength of the possessive right therefore, which is combined with it, as such, could only be finally determined by a power competent to take into moral account the whole measure and complication of its tendencies;—that is, by a power equal to the Giver’s. But is Society such a power? Are the wisest human Laws?
