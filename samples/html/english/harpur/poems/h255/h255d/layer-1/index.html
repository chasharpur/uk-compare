Personal and other Sonnets.

Luther and Knox.

[It is exceedingly unphilosophical to cavil at the characters of such men as Luther and Knox (demollishers by destiny, more than builders), for any want, however great, of mildness and placability. It is even puerile to require that such men should have been other than they were; headstrong even to rashness, and as inexorably self-willed as they were publicly valliant. The tool or instrument must be always fit for the kind of work to be done. It were manifestly absurd to attempt to batter down stone walls with balls of cotton, or to break through the chained and bolted gates of a Bastile with wollen mops. Solid globes of tremendous gravity are necessary for the one sort of work, and stout crowbars, or ponderous-headed sledge-hammers, must supply the needs of the other. Or it were inconsequential to a degree, to look for the delicate arm and hand of a courtier in one whose business it is to beat iron into anchors—or even into horse-nails. And just as absurd is it, and just as inconse- -quential, to require that the work of a Reformer, in rugged times, should have been appointed to any man so implicitly venerative (for example) as a Francis de Sales; or to any such man as Fenelon, who could build up for himself nothing robuster than Quietism, and abandon even that to a Papal condemnation.]

There are two kinds of moral heroes. One Consists in suffering passively all wrong For Truth’s great sake,—yea, all that can be done To blame it; or in carrying what’s begun By hardier spirits persuasively along; But should the tide turn, all its fa[r]ther skill Is to die well: The Other, in a strong 129 Self-sanctioning sublimity of will; Prompt to affront the error it would kill— The bondage it would break—with battering shocks, As ocean’s mighty waves assault the shore; Nor less firm to withstand the refluent roar Of popular remorse, than reef-based rocks: And such was Luther’s, such too that of Knox.
