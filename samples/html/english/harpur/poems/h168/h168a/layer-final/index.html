II.

Happines and Faith.

No man, no woman, can be wholly happy. Some flying cloud of infinite despair— Some haggard sense of destiny—will come Over our spirits, when with those we love, Even in the morning of our fairest joy: For so it must be, in a world like this, With mortals, conscious of mortality;— Conscious that love, so mighty in itself, Is weaker—weaker than a brook-side reed, When it would save from casualty and loss, And change, and sorrow, and decline, and death, Its object! Something of all this must wring, Even the thoughtless in their gladdest hour,— Must shudder at their hearts, and give their eyes To see life’s brightest things grow dimm’d awhile, Under the shadow of a swift despair! And hence the wisdom of a heart prepared For living in the world, in the belief, That God is with us, and in his good hands Are all the things beyond it. Thus to live, Is, through our dread misgivings, but to grow, As by recoils, to higher ground, the more Assured of justice, if not reconciled To all that hath befall’n or may befall.
