The Unrepresented Ideal.

Thou who, whilst wandering in the pathless woods, Or gazing from the solitary mountains, Hast felt the spirit of the Fathomless Come down on thine, entrancing,—tell me, what Those thrilling intimations, from the dim Unsounded depths brought into being then, Even as the utmost stars of Heaven appear Within the astronomic tube, a host Of bright particulars, in one white blaze Commingling beyond fixture—what do they, Those incommunicable Things of Soul, Owe to the visible Outward? Nothing. They In its wide circle have no voice, except The heart’s own tingle to imagined strains Heard through the trance of thought; nor semblance have they Nearer to earth than are those nameless lights The unassisted eye yet never saw. x

x Is Locke’s theory of their being no innate ideas altogether correct? It cannot be denied that there are innate feelings: and these being granted, their intimations, as a species of primordial ideas, must, be also granted. True, they are perceptible, in the sense he attaches to that term, only in connexion with their material affinities. But neither is the mind itself, and yet does not it exist innately? The outward types then which manifest this class of ideas, only so far constitute the ideas themselves, as the visible world does the whole mind which perceives by virtue of a relative existence. It strikes me (but I may be wrong) that the view of which this note is the briefest possible outline, if fully carried on, whould hit nearer to the transition-point between Spirit and Matter, than any other I am aware of. But I would remark here, once for all, that I do not profess to understand Metaphysics as a science. In fact, I am not even versed in the technicals current amongst professional metaphysicians: and when speculating therefore, as above, I am simply giving the cursory results of my own reflexions, in such English as I can command for the occasion. I would also remark, that I have but an old memory of Locke’s precise theory, and can make, at this present, no references, having by me no books of the kind, and only three of any kind.o
