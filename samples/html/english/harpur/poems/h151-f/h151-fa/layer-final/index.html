CHORUS OF SPIRITS OF THE EARTH.—

FROM THE “SORROWS OF CHATTERTON.”

(By Charles Harpur.)

Ah! that Childhoods happiest days The more enjoyed, should be the deadlier food Therefore for bitter-voiced Regret In Misery’s after mood! That all the more their flowery maze May bloom in memory yet, The harder still should seem the present’s barren ways!

And why, with soul so free, Should young Humanity thus straightened be? Although pursuing its appropriate bliss, It can but live to rue the road! Ah, in a World so excellent as this, Why should a feeling heart become its heaviest load?

To still these questionings of Pain, The sages of the Earth have striven in vain Since Love first wailed beneath the skies, And men to their true interest blind, Grew careless of their kind; Although a voice in every soul replies— A voice their logic never deigns to heed, How movingly soever it may plead, Declaring that full brotherhood of mind, And that equality it would devise, Is the one only social Creed (As by true Charity defined) Whence life-long pleasantness can flow indeed.*

* The honest and healthy necessities of unsophis- ticated human nature are instinct with a logic which surpasses that of the schools as far as the veracity of a dial transcends that of the more complicated watch.

At a meeting of laborers the other day in England, held under the wide cold heaven of a winter’s night, —for their employers had denied them even the shelter of a barn—and at which the hunger-bitten speakers read their resolutions by the light of a morsel of candle held in the “cauld hand” of each, like the corpses in Tam o’ Shanter galvanised for the occa- sion;—at this ghastly meeting, a poor woman gave her sense of Free Trade in these words: “If Free Trade would only give us a chance of more bread, they (the Rulers of the land) should give us Free Trade—for we be starving.” This was the logic of nature; and no protectionist in Europe could at- tempt to refute the just political principle involved in those few, simple, ignorant words, without diving at a plunge into the sophistry of selfishness, or flying off at a gallop into the declamation of dis- honesty. But when “reason is our bent,” do any of us pay sufficient attention to the voice of sentiment and feeling pleading in every soul? I think not: and hence that perpetual warfare between the wisdom of the head and the wisdom of the heart.

C. H.
