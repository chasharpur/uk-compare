Oft he thought she could not love him! For with looks so calmly meek, Half in absence, would she speak Of the chance that might remove him. And then he’d turn away, inclining All to self, and thus repining: Surely—surely she will fret, Though not yet, not just yet.

But when came the day of parting, Plainly then, as in a book, Might he read in every look How her secret heart was smarting: And in the agony-betraying Writhe of her pale lip, when saying: Was it but for this we met? Go not yet, not just yet.

Then he left her, all relying On the love he’d so mista’en— Only to return in vain When that gentle Girl was dying! And then how bitterly Repentance Made his own that by-gone sentence: Was it but for this we met? Go not yet—ah, not yet! x

x The simplicities of song which dallied with the innocence of love and beauty, or centred their melodious f spells amit the [unclear] quiet sanctities of Home, are fast giving 174 in our popular literature to what?—to versified ball-room rivalries, and metrical Whiskerandoses. Still the “fit audiance though few” ask for the “old familiar faces” of Poesy, or others equally truthful—and occasionally, equally homely. Of this I am sure, that there is not anything more strikingly indicative of the characters of individuals than the quality of their favorite songs—of the characters of ladies especially. Those for instance, who affect the moonshine of “Meet me in the willow glen” nothingnesses, have usually all the outside show, and as little sterling pith, as the no-things they execute. Again, the would be “Rover’s Brides”, are rather too remarkable for a sort of melodramatic contempt of the “mine and thine” proprie- -ties of ordinary mortals. Those who would “march, march away as dashing white sergeants”, will march away whenever oppor- -tunity offers, that’s flat—and in the indicated direction. Those on the other hand, who would be “butterflies born in bowers”, and “fairies living in flowers” are mostly such sexual inanities, physi- -cally as well as morally, as the epistolary Amelia Scraggs: the best very best specimens of which species [unclear] only at home in making indifferent wax-work flowers, bad verses, and worse puddings. But show me those amongst our women, who love with a quiet yet earnest constancy the simple touching pathos of our old home ditties, and I will show you the, reader, the jewells richer than all their sex— and yet how often thrown away, by the “base Indians” Desdimona-like, by the “base Indians” of Fortune. To speaks plainly, I will show you the women whose warm hearts are in the right place; whose modest sympathies are sweet and tender and natural as the blooming beauties of spring, yet strong as death, and far more precious, rightly estimated, than all the gold in the universe.

C.H.
