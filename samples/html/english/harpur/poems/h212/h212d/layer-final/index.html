Lyrics. By Charles Harpur.

A Lament

Flowers in their freshness are flushing the earth, And the voice-peopled forest is loud in its mirth, And streams in their fulness are laughing at dearth, Yet my bosom is aching.

There’s shadow on all things—the shadow of wo; It falls from my spirit wherever I go, 89 As from a dark cloud drifting heavy and low; For my spirit is weary.

If I ask of my heart, why is it not cheery? If I ask of my spirit, why is it so weary? Hark, from the depths of a dead love how drearly Wails the answer of Sorrow!

Wails from those dead depths, so darkling and lonely, To where in the lap of Despair I have thrown me, Though by my listening spirit heard only— That wild answer of Sorrow:

“What can be flowers in their freshness to thee, Or glad voices that people the green forest tree, Or the full joy of streams—since thy soul sighs, Ah me! O’er the grave of thy Mary?”

Under the blithe face of Nature, her face Hath carried down with it all beauty and grace! Pale lies it there, in that dark silent place— Mary! O Mary!Pale

90

Pale comes the wraith of it home to my heart, Comes through the feeling that will not depart, Of how once it could smile me to peace—and I start, Stung through with keen anguish!

Stung as if living to anguish alone; Wild for a moment;—then cold as the stone At the head of thy grave, do I stand there, and groan Mary! O Mary!

Who that had been in the days of his youth Won by thy beauty and blest by thy truth, Would live in a world where nor anguish nor ruth May avail the bereaved one?

Fierce glares that thought, like a flash in the night When a tempest is rushing from Mindelow’s height, And never may any less terrible light Illumine my sorrow!

Children are by me—her children! O God! To see where their feet have unwittingly trod Tiny tracks in the loam of the new-broken sod Betwixt them and their Mother!

91

‘Twixt them and the true One who loved us in truth! Who bore them—to die ’mid the hopes of her youth! Who would live in a world where nor anguish nor ruth May avail the bereaved Ones?

Yet must I live, lest her spirit should say, Meeting mine in its flight from this wild world away, “Where are our little ones? Where do they stay? And why did you leave them?

“Dying I thought of the loss they would rue In their Mother, and dire was the pang that it drew, And who was then left to brood o’er them but you? Ah! why did you leave them?”

If for them only then, so must it be! See I remain with them, Mary!—but see How lonely we stand in a world without thee! Mary! O Mary!

I live—but Death’s shadow is over me cast: And even when weary Woe sleepeth at last, Some dream of the dead, sighing out of the past, Is alive in the darkness!

92

How can my heart then be ever more cheery? How can my spirit be other than weary? Hark, from some dead depth of being how drearly Sorrow waileth her answer!

Wails from that dead depth, so darkling and lonely, To where in the lap of Despair I have thrown me, Though by my listening spirit heard only— That wild answer of Sorrow!

Heard by my spirit alone: while it makes Its listening silence so felt that it aches, Till with a mystical shudder it breaks At the thought of our children.

Could I but weep, it were comfort, though brief; But the fountain of tears by the fire of my grief Hath been dried to its dregs, and can shed no relief On the thirst of my eyelids!

As music that wasteth away on the blast, As the last ray by the sunken sun cast, All my heart’s gladness hath died in the past! Mary! O Mary!
