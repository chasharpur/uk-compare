A Requim.—A Keen

Flowers in their gladness are flushing the earth, And the voice-peopled forest is loud in its mirth, And streams in their fulness are laughing at dearth[,] Yet my bosom is aching!

There’s shadow on all things—the shadow of Woe, It falls from my spirit wherever I go, As from a dark cloud drifting heavy and slow, For my spirit is weary!

If I ask of my heart, why is it not cheery? 685a If I ask of my spirit, why is it so weary? Hark, from the depths of a dead love how dreary Wails the answer of Sorrow!

For what can be flowers in their gladness to me, Or the voices that people the green forest tree, Or the full joy of streams,—since my soul sighed Ah me! O’er the grave of my Mary?

Under the glad face of Nature, her face Hath carried down with it all beauty and grace: Pale is it there in that dark silent place—. Mary! O Mary!

x Pale comes the dream of it home to my heart! Comes through the feeling that will not depart, Of How it once smiled me to peace—and I start Stung through with keen anguish!

Stung as if living to anguish alone— Wild for a moment:—then cold as the stone At the head of her grave, do I stand there, and groan Mary! O Mary!

Who that had been in the days of his youth Won by her beauty and blest by her truth Could live in a world where nor anguish nor ruth May avail the bereaved one

Fierce glares that thought like a flash in the night When a tempest is rushing from Mindelow’s height And never may any less terrible light Illumine my sorrow!

684a

Children are by me—her Children! O God, To see where their feet have unconsciously trod Tiny tracks in the loam of the new broken sod Betwixt them and their Mother!

‘Twixt them and the true one who loved us in truth! Who bore them—to die ’mid the hopes of her youth! Who could live in a world where nor anguish nor ruth May avail the bereaved ones?

Yet must I live, lest her spirit should say Meeting mine in its flight from this temple of clay “Where are our little ones? Where do they stay? And why did you leave them!

Dying I thought of the loss they would rue In their Mother, and dire was the pang that it drew— And who was there then left to love them like you? Why did you leave them?

If for them only then so must it be! See, I remain with them, Mary,—but see How lonely we stand in a world without thee! Mary! O Mary!

I live—but Death’s shadow is over me cast: And even when weary Woe sleepeth at last, Some dream of the dead sighing out of the past Is alive in the darkness.

How can my heart then be ever more cheery? How can my spirit be other than weary? Hark, from some dead depths of being how dreary Grief waileth her answer!

684b

O Could I but weep, it were comfort, though brief, But the fountain of tears by the fire of my grief Hath been dried to its dregs, and can shed no relief On the thirst of my eyelids.

As music that wasteth away on the blast, As the last ray from the sunken sun cast, All my heart’s gladness hath died in the past— Mary! O Mary!
