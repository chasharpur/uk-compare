Critical Rhymes on Critical Reviews.

Heaped in yon Corner, dusty as thy cell, Oblivion! and which therefore suits them well; And where in brown repose my pipe now lies, On which they light the frequent sacrifice; Behold some Critical Reviews! they’re all Of note—and, therefore, must be critical. Stale mouthy gatherisms. built of musty rules; A kind of literary dancing schools, Wherein an Author’s placed, and bad to shew Certain set steps—but aim at nothing new; Unless he would ensure for life at least The saintly hate of some reviewing Priest; Or iritate the ulcerated backs Of scape-goat Editors and party Hacks: Those foredoomed Sages, at whose birth, some say, The sign of “Horsewhip” hath celestial sway.

From these I learn, that Authors in our hearts Now worn, were wanting both in grace and parts: That Byron even a Poet grew to be From credit at the Bank of Memory! That Shelley’s solemn bursts, and gushing gladness, Were but the flood-tides of—what think you?—Madness! 48 That Wordsworth was a dolt! and Coleridge worse! And Hunt’s most dainty hand, so skilled to nurse Young truths of Song—but fit to cut a purse! That Keats was a mere driveller! and poor White Strangely misled in thinking he could write! That though an exquisite so much in vogue, Bulwer’s strong passion for a dashing rogue, Were low his sphere, would passing soon afford Himself the glory of a “penny cord!”

But on the other hand, I’m told, that wit And worth were one with some forgotten cit, Like Cottle; or that still each Grace severe Kept tryste with Milman when “bemused in beer”: Or that the Northern Bard, whose antique dress But cloaked his Muse’s ideal meagreness, Wrote Epics that had made even Homer pale With envy, could he but have seen their—sale! Or that sleek Bowles, with several friendly Blues; And frouthy Southey,—hammering his iron Muse As though she were an anvil,—were divine! And therefore all-in-all with all the Nine.

Thus much I learn from these Reviews, and more; And so the Devil take them by the score!

But what of their Writers? Many were of note Amongst the several Cliques for which they wrote; And living thus by lying, truth to tell, Lived long, fed heartily, and digested well.

1845.

Note. As a comprehensive commentary upon these four and sixty lines would ask the space of a volume, I can only refer the reader to the British Monthlies and Quarterlies contemporaneous with the authors whose names are mentioned in them. And if the literary verdicts contained in any five or fifty numbers of each, taken promiscuously, when tested by the reversive justice of even an immediate posterity, do not choke up as with rotten timber, the channel of his faith in a sub umbra Magazine Aristarchi thenceforward forever,—why, he himself must be an incorrigible jargon-monger by nature, or have become such by the very process of the enquiry which is here suggested. (1848.)

When I spoke in the Text of Hunt’s “most dainty hand”, it strikes me now, that it must have been more with reference to his poetical criticism, than to his poetry:—though even this, is very often as dainty and sweet, and beautifully loveable as youthful womanhood. But in a certain genial, delicate, racy appreciation of the spells, and golden veins, and “spicëd airs” of the writings of other men of genius,—men after his own large heart and believing 50 imagination,—he has to my thinking, but few equals. And he can set all this forth in a prose style that is both in its structure and bloom, always graceful and sometimes perfect, as the lovely Miss L. is in her face and figure. [Still he has a critical failing— not in his style, but in his kind of discriminativeness. He is too nice: that is to say, he is too prone to point out in his favourite poems, as artistical proprieties, certain alliterative and other minute felicities, which so far from being designed were never, it is probable, even so much as conciously perceived by their authors themselves, critical petting of the like, may lead in the future to a good deal of disquisitional trifling and poetical foppery. In other words, this nicety approximates so nearly to an abuse in Hunt himself, that it is perilously likely to occasion an over fastidiousness in his critical imitators,—and he is sure to have many such; and also in the poetical imitators of the authors he has so super-subtitely criticised. +

The verdict in the Text, on the Muse of Sir Walter Scott, will no doubt be dissented from by many: still I believe it to be, in the main, a just one. With much of the fire and strength of poetic genius, he had scarcely anything of its ideal subtlety and grace. He could out- -line the poetical face of things correctly enough, 51 but the spiritual expression,—the “living soul” of it escaped him. And when his Poems are apparently brightest, they are so, like the waters of a shallow stream, from their very want of depth. Take away the proper names, and the merely local associations, and his ballad epics,—even the best of them,—were but so many picked bones.+

The sneer at Southey is hardly just. Certainly, his poetical metal was such as to require much “hammering” to work it into shape - but it was not of mere iron. The truth is, Southey has written several great Poems;- great in learning; great in imagination; great in structure, like the Pyramids, but cold and passionless as they, and with as little universality of human reference. They want that breath of life, and vitality of purpose, without which the Ideal even in Poetry, is only of a piece with mere statuary or mere painting. Such, upon the whole, is the character of his Poetry. [”But his prose is perfect,” said Byron: and it is nearly so. It is elaborately finished, and ponderously musical; and yet, unlike Johnson’s, it is “native to the soil.” In fact, it is the olden English prose style, somewhat modernised, but not at all Frenchified; nor rendered too colloquial: that style in which a too great elision of the auxiliary parts of speech is even studiously avoided; and doubtless a too rigid cashiering of those little but helpful unities of language is had in prose. It proceeds from and indicates, I think, in the persons over attached to it, a parsimonious organ of Language, and also of Tune. [The prose style of the whole 52 ”mob of gentleman who wrote with ease” in English some century ago, has a remarkably shorn appearance in consequence of a too finnicking addiction to this species of phrase-paring. They indeed, followed it out, to a man, even to the extent of grammatical obscurity. In poetry much liberty of this kind of elision is necessarily and gracefully allowable. For instance, in the often quoted line, “From grave to gay, from lively to severe,” the definite article, though not used at all, is understood to be in no less than four places. In prose the sentence would stand thus: “From the grave to the gay, from the lively to the severe”; and as prose, it would be all the better—all the more musical for the repetition of the article in each of these several places. Such at least is my opinion, and such also, judging from his practice, would have been Southey’s.
