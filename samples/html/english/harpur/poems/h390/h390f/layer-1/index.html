A Poet’s Wife.

O wert thou mine, belovéd Girl! How rich I were with such a mate, Albeit around me still should whirl The most injurious waves of Fate: No more disgust, no more despair, Should snake-like coil about my life! So could’st thou bless me, didst thou dare To be a Poet’s Wife.

Before us see how frets the sea Of Being—a dread changeful state! And think ere thou putt’st forth with me Where heart alone can cope with Fate; How many new and clinging cares, World-born to an unworldly life, Must be her crown of thorns, who dares To be a Poet’s Wife.

A spirit gentle and resigned Must speak her boundless faith in love, And in the temper of her mind The lioness obey the dove: A heart above mere Fashion’s shows, All the petty prides of life; These must be her high dower, who goes To be a Poet’s Wife!

And these are thine, beloved Girl! And I, secure of such a mate, Believe that Love’s full risen sun Can fill with light the dark of Fate; And that all needful toil and care Shall breathe for me this psalm of life: Dare all for her who all could dare— A destined Poet’s Wife.x

Final copy. 110c

x The Heroine of this Poem—or rather she who might have been the Heroine of it, was a very excellent young person. But so shocked was her prudence—so suddenly galvanised, as it were— by its rigid statement of terms, that she at backed out of whatever engagement there happened to be between us. Leaving me ”all alone for to die”, she afterwards wedded a well-doing butcher;—had her gig; had her servants; had her table supplied with the best of “boiled and roast”; bore several butcherlings; did a deal of gossip; and finally puked up her super-prudent existence at the age of twenty eight: her mortal ailment, according to the doctors, having been caused by over- feeding—a malady that could hardly have been so privileged against her, as a Poet’s wife, instead of a butcher’s [unclear] while the poem which 111 that she was no fool, as she used to say of herself, in reference to a certain “silly old affair”, and as all her friends used to say of her; immediately follows in the text is the per contra account, and gives the feeling in which I looked at that particular defection of hers. 1867.
