XVII.

THE CAROUSE OF DESPAIR.

(A LAY OF PASSION.)

Come Alan, I will flood the bowl, And thou shalt sing that song of mine, Which dates the storm that wrecked my soul— Which fits me for the fiery wine! Yes; often may I lift it up— Keen misery makes my will so strong! Ere I can lose in this deep cup The thought of Her I loved so long.

As comes the Moon when rising bright, To one who treads a foreign shore, She rose upon me like the light Of something I had loved before! The record of a former tie Seemed written in my heart and brain— A destined thrall foregone and by, Seemed cycling into act again.

I loved the genial smiles of Day, With every voice of these that told; I loved the Night, in her array Thus spangled with celestial gold; I loved all mediums too, through which The soul of Genius breathed—but She Of these—of all became one rich, One final Ideality!

And was I loved?—Pour high the wine! Behold her! to our tryste she trips! Again I feel the fire on mine That passioned from her burning lips! But there was treachery in her vow— And future blight in every kiss! With all the infamy that now Is in the consciousness of this.

And yet I grieve, remembering still How that first passion sphered my soul; How sovereign ’twas for good and ill Or to impel me or control! The dream was scattered—but it left A hungry fire within my brain! And strange! of such a dream bereft, I wished its madness mine again!

And since, the songs of birds and bees— Of every wind that wanders by; And that rare music, mixed with these, That filters from the stilly sky; Seem, as Regret in every place Stands listening with me when alone,— Seem to have lost all native grace, And taken up a foreign tone.

And daily too, the morning flowers That flush the fields with joy’s excess; And all that make the evening hours Instinct with thoughtful happiness; The gleaming waters of the Earth— The glowing wonders of the Skies— Have but encreased one mighty dearth That burns and blinds my loveless eyes!

But let us drink!—I know too well Thy soul is dark—even dark as mine! And hath a tale of wrongs to tell That might excuse excess in wine! But met we not, my Friend, to quaff In Agony’s defiance? Yea, Until our bitter hearts should laugh All memory of the Past away!

There’s one far tone, by Passion heard, Alone on Life’s wild-sounding shore, Which nothing to the heart it stirred But after suffering may restore! And such, to night, is dirging by, A mystery of regret to thine; And such it is, even now, that I Would stifle in this fiery wine!

But Alan! why that piteous smile? The wounded Eagle highest flies! And be it ours to reach awhile That madness wherein Misery dies! Yet, thinking of the time when we Were gladsome as the morning’s light, I weep to know that hearts so free Thus desolate were to be to night!

Fill high again the potent bowl! That Thought is stinging like a snake! Despair is clouding from my soul Like Mist from some benighted Lake! A shapeless Gloom—but fiery tongued With fierce regrets that now should be To one by Love so direly wronged, The buried things of Memory.

But Alan, you forget.—Come sing That welcome to an early Grave: It soothes despair, as Zephyr’s wing Might soothe some storm-begotten wave: And while its measures gather up Love’s dying tears, to crown the song, I’ll pledge a last—a brimming cup To that false One I loved so long.
