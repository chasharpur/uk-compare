The Scenic Part of Poetry. X ᴓ

What’s Poetic? I ask, if a green wood is not,— A green wood by balmy winds stirred; And a runnel that slides with a flash from its grot, And a trill like the song of a bird: And we prize them, when pictured by Poesy, more Than we did when old outlying things, Because they are brought as it were to our door Through the spell that around us she flings.

What’s Poetic? I ask, if a sunset is not, When it turns all the depths of the west Into jasper seas rolled round some diamond-cragged spot, Or some purple cloud-isle of the blest: And we prize it, when pictured by Poesy, more Than we did when to Nature confined, Because ever thence it is fixed, as a store, In the scenic world-dome of the mind.

What’s Poetic? I ask, if a tempest is not, With its dragon-winds billowing by, 69 And its thunder-flames jagging out hissingly hot, As through fractures and flaws in the sky: And we prize it, when pictured by Poesy, more Than we did while its terror was known, Because its dread face then looks in at our door In the strength of its grandeur alone.

What’s Poetic? I ask, if some Hecla is not, Standing grim in a wild winter-world, With its lava-floods seething from chasm and grot, And its fire-rocks at heaven up hurled: And we prize it, when pictured by Poesy, more Than we should in the outward alone, Because it shall ever thence riot and roar As a wonder-raised birth of her own.

What’s Poetic? I ask, if the ocean is not, Or in storm, or when plain-like it sleeps, Lying wide as the heaven whose face is begot Again in the womb of its deeps: And we prize it, when pictured by Poesy, more Than we did while around us it wrought, Because it is then, not a thing, as before, But a mighty possession of thought.
