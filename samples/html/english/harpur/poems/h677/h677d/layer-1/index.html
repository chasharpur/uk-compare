XXIII.

What’s Poetic?

What’s Poetic? I ask, if the day-god is not, As rolling through measureless space, He dilates with that life-glow, on all hands out shot, Which the planet-orbs drink from his face: And we prize him, when pictured by Poesy, more Than we do ‘neath his actual power, Because we are then by her art shaded o’er As it were with a Paradise bower.

What’s Poetic? I ask, if the moon’s orb is not, And the clustering glories of night; 87 Hanging all, she and they, in their heavenly lot, Like isles of immortal delight: And we prize them, when pictured by Poesy, more Than we do while familiarly seen, Because on her dream-wings our spirits then soar As it were in their deathless demesne.

What’s Poetic? I ask, if a green wood is not— A green wool[unclear] by balmy winds stirred, And a runnel that slides with a flash from its grot, And a trill like the song of a bird: And we prize them, when pictured by Poesy, more Than we did when old outlying things, Because they are brought as it were to our door Through the spells that around us she flings.

What’s Poetic? I ask, if a sunset is not, When it turns all the depths of the west Into jasper seas, rolled round some golden-banked spot, Or purple cloud-isle of the blest: And we prize it, when pictured by Poesy, more Than we did when to Nature confined, Because it is thence ever fixed, as a store In the scenic world-dome of the mind.

What’s Poetic? I ask, if a tempest is not, With its dragon-winds bellowing by, And its thunder-flames jagging out hissingly hot As from fractures and flaws in the sky: And we prize it, when pictured by Poesy, more Than we did when its terror was known, Because its dread face then looks in at our door In the strength of its grandeur alone.

What’s Poetic? I ask, if some Hecla is not, Standing grim in a wild winter-world, With its lava-floods seething from cavern and grot, And its fire-rocks at heaven uphurled: And we prize it, when pictured by Poesy, more Than we should in the outward alone, Because ever thence it shall riot and roar As a wonder-nursed birth of her own.

What’s Poetic? I ask, if the ocean is not, Or in storm, or when plain-like it sleeps, Lying wide as the heaven whose face is begot Again in the womb of its deeps: And we prize it, when pictured by Poesy, more Than we did when around us it wrought, Because it then, not a sight,—as before,— But a mighty possession of thought.

What’s Poetic? I ask, if all glad life is not, From that of the bird to the bee; With all happy motion—kids skipping—a knot Of revellers dancing in glee: And we prize them, when pictured by Poesy, more Than we do in Time’s jading displays, Because they live not in her light,—as before, At the mercy of wearisome days.

What’s Poetic? (in fine) if all passion is not That is lovely, terrific, or grand; Whether wakened by Beauty, Hate, Glory, or what Ambition hath wrought in the Land: And we prize it, when pictured by Poesy, more Than if ‘twere our burthen indeed, Because 89 Because it is then, through the balm of her lore, From the ache of its earthliness freed.

Thus the realm of the Muse is as wide as the world, And as high as the heaven of night, While the wings of her power day by day are unfurled In the orient banners of light: Till tending through all that is meet in its kind, In the guise of a mystical dove, She nestles at will in some beautiful mind, And the brood she discloseth is—Love.
