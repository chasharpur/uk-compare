XXIV.

On the New Land Orders.

[On the first publication of this Sonnet in a Colonial Newspaper, it was followed by a Note, which seems to me, even at this distance of time, worthy of preservation, and which I will therefore prefix to it in this place.

“Should the Squatting System be restricted by no better Regulations than those deplored in this Sonnet, all our lands beyond the Boundaries will be likely to fall within the grasp of one class alone—the great Stockholders, “to them and to their heirs for ever”; and in that event, the future of Australia will be but a bad copy of the national perditions of Europe. How long will our substantial townsfolk and yoemen fail to see this momentous question in its true light? Can they think that it matters little how, or by whom, those vast ever opening tracts of country are held, so long as plenty of wool and beef is grown upon them; as if a true social progress and the vulgar “Go-a-head” impulse were in anywise identical? Or As if a mere overgrown chattel prosperity, gathered about a few, can ever harmonise with the solid social welfare of the many? If our Townsfolk & yeoman do think thus, they err most greviously—and to the detriment, it may be, of unborn millions. Besides, a splendid is never a happy land. While it flames, like a reversed sky, with starred nobles and jewelled ladies and gentlemen, it swarms yet more 39 abundantly, (as does a putrid carcase with the lower forms of being), with sneaking menials and the most venal minions, with pimps, with bawds, with criminals, and with outcasts! And this great heaven-mocking disparity of social condition grows at first out of an unjust division of its acres, however remotely it may afterwards, as an effect, refer back to this as its primal cause. Immense inequalities in the ownership of its soil are the arch curses of Eurobe, giving birth to two very marked national monsters—a heartless magnificence in the rich, and a social hoplessness in the poor, which become in turn the parents of an audacious denial in the one class of the common claims and universal brotherhood of man, and a sullen distrust even of God’s providence in the other, ”which cannot love (it is thought) the wretch it starves”—yea, starves in the midst of abundance! Such, comprehensively stated, is the condition of Eurobe. And wherever, in any society, there are hereditary Pariahs, something radically fraudulent in the structure of that society is the cause of the evil. Were it or could it be otherwise, the blame would be with Providence. But God is just.

No! a splendid is never a happy land. Wherever there are palaces there are hovels. And this should determine us, in Australia, to have as few of either as possible: for the former are but the glaring evidences of political imposition or sump- -tuary injustice, the social cost of which is indicated, and reproached, by the inevitable neighborhood of the latter. Nay, wherever even mansions are very frequent, and gentlemen, in the useless sense of the term, exceedingly numerous, there also poor houses abound, the energy of the laborer serves but to keep him longer from his only possible rest in the grave, every town has its Pariah quarter, and unconfessing paupers starve together in heaps! Such, universally, is the condition of the city and village populations of Europe. And our present and future legislators should perpetually propound to themselves the question, whether the same national enormities—a bloated patrimonial pomp on the one hand, 40 and hereditary destitution on the other—are to be extended to a virgin Australia. By keeping this question constantly at heart, they will assuredly grow up to something better. And it is the more urgent upon them to do so, from such an extension of the old world’s national structure being very manifestly the hope, if not the design, of but too many amongst us, whom mere wealth, however come by, and however ill-deserved, has made snob lordly and influential.”] in the land

I long did hope the soil of this bright Clime, Being unaccursed by oligarchal sway, Was a meet cradle for the birth sublime Or just Equality at no distant day; And which, when once consùmmated, should ray Brightly abroad and hither draw the prime Regard of Man, thence wiser for all time, And studious more to choose the Godward way Of Perfect Freedom, then first rightly run! But now this hope is shrunk into a fear! Old-World feudality its worst hath done To sow the seed of splendid evil here! In Sheepshanks we behold a destined Peer, And Oxtail’s stockmen shall “my lord” his son!

1843.
