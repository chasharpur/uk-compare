Impromptu

The Empire boasts that England’s ruled By what is now called ‘Journalism’: Yet may we never here be school’d In our state optics (to be fool’d) By looking through so mere a prism; But measure all things (to be wise) With naked individual eyes.

Note. I very much question whether a numerous (and especially a daily) Newspaper Press, conduces so much to the progress of the National mind as it is generally supposed to do. Talk is good; but the strongest individual intuitions and purposes, may be maimed and wasted by too much of it. In like manner, the mind of a nation may run too much into babble, and be over talked to. A Newspaper—a daily one especially—cannot have, and has not time to grapple effectively with its subjects, and give them the needful literary roundness and organic vitality. It has time only to skim them, and it does no more. Sometimes, no doubt it whips them of their cream, but as often, and oftener, of nothing but their froth. Its own mental habit, therefore, is hasty and inconclusive, however dogmatical; and the mental characteristics of its readers are very observably injured by the influence upon them of this prevailing habit. X It has long struck me, that the newspap[e]rs of both England and America, from their being too ma[n]y of them, tend more to dissipate and sperad out thin, the wisdom and integrity of public opinion, than they do to concentrate and give to it any lofty 667 and permanent effect. By making them its oracles; it explodes in talk, or dies of a daily dose of it. Or it lives under their influence, as in a social hot house, where everything is forced, and nothing comes to a wholesome maturity. A sedate and thoughtful family should never take in more than one news paper, and that one a weekly issue or most a bi- weekly. In a country like this especially, where there is no current literature beside a daily newspaper must be more or less distractive of all continuous mental discipline. CH.

Read, for example, a leader in even the best of our Colonial Journals,—the Herald or the Empire,—and, if a person of intelligence, just that knowledge of the subject which lies hoarded in your own mind in the shape as it were of nuggets, beaten and labored into into the thinnest possible goldleaf. The writer had, by bargain to fill his column and a half, and he does it—with words!
