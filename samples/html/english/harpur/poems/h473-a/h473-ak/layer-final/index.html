Coleridge’s Christabel.

See yon Runnel, how tis flowing, Like a sylvan Spirit dreaming Of the Spring blooms near it blowing, And the sunlight o’er it beaming— Bright from bank to bank, or growing Darkly interfreaked when streaming Where some willowy shade hangs bending O’er it in green mingled masses Lights, and shades, and blossoms glowing, All for greater beauty blending In its vision, as it passes.

Where that shelving rock is spied, There, with a smooth warbling slide 139 It lapses down into a cool And brimming, not o’erflowing pool: Then between its narrowed banks Playing mellow gurgling pranks, It gushes—till a channell’d stone Gives it a more strenuous tone.

Or away it curveth thence With an even profluence, (Beaming to the sun afar Like a mighty scimitar Dropt by some love-vanquished God, And sunk into yielding sod!) Or betwixt thick reeded beaches It whispers low mysterious speeches; Or with an underswirling spread Over a wide pebbled bed, It bubbles with a gentle pleasure Ere some new mood change the measure.

Such a Runnel typeth well The sweet wild verse of Christabel. And if, all suddenly, at length In the fullness of its strength, It sunk, a broken end to make In some subterranean Lake, A further type we might behold Of the Story—half untold.

But what—with its sweet verse-flow (snapt Short as ‘twere and half perdu)— What might figure to our view In a picture-light as apt, The Wonder-World it warbles through?
