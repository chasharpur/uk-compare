THE HARP OF THE EMPIRE.

THE VERSE OF COLERIDGE’S “CHRISTABEL.”

By Charles Harpur.

Mark yon runnel how ’tis flowing, Like a sylvan spirit dreaming Of the spring-blooms near it blowing, And the sunlight in it gleaming.

Where a shelving rock is spied, There, with a smooth warbling slide, It lapses down into a cool And brimming, not o’erflowing pool; Then between its narrowed banks, Playing mellow gurgling pranks, It gushes—till some channell’d stone Gives it a more strenuous tone.

Or away it windeth thence With an even profluence; Or betwixt thick-reeded beaches It whispers wild mysterious speeches; Or with an underswirling spread Over a wide pebbled bed, It bubbles with a gentle pleasure, Ere some new mood change the measure.

Such a runnel typeth well The sweet wild Verse of Christabel. And if, all suddenly, at length In the fullness of its strength, Sinking, it an end should make In some subterrean lake, A further type we might behold Of the story half untold.

But what might fitly figure too The wonder-world it warbles through?

March 1852.
