THE CLOUD.

BY CHARLES HARPUR.

One Summer morn, out of the sea-waves wild A speck-like Cloud, the season’s fatal child, Came softly floating up the boundless sky, And o’er the sun-parched hills all brown and dry.

Onward she glided, through the azure air Bourne by its motion without toil or care: When looking down in her ethereal joy, She marked Earth’s moilers at their hard employ: And—Oh! (she said) that by some act of grace ‘Twere mine to succor yon o’erlabored race; To give the hungry meat, the thirsty drink— The thought of good is very sweet to think.

The day advanced—and the Cloud greater grew, And greater: also her desire to do Some charity to men, had more and more As the long sultry Summer day on wore, Greatened and warmed within her fleecy breast, Like a dove fledging in its downy nest.

The heat waxed fiercer, until all the land Glared in the sun, as ‘twere a monstrous brand, And the shrunk rivers, few and far between, Like molten metal lightened in the scene. Ill could Earth’s sons endure their toilsome state, Though still they labored, for their need was great. And many a long beseeching look they sped Tow’rds that fair Cloud, with many a sigh, that said We famish for thy bounty! For our sake, O break thou! in a showery blessing, break!

I feel, and fain would help you! said the Cloud, And tow’rds the Earth her bounteous being bowed. But then rememb’ring a tradition she Had while a child learned from her native Sea, That when a Cloud adventures from the skies Too near the altar of the hills—it dies! A while she wavered and was blown about Hither and thither, by the winds of doubt. But in the midst of heaven at length all still She stood:—then, on a sudden, with a thrill Of light she said within herself—I will! Yea, in the glad strength of devotion, I Will help you, though, in helping you, I die!

Filled with this thought’s divinity, the Cloud Grew world-like vast, as earthward more she bowed! Oh, never erewhile had she dreamt her state So great might be—beneficently great! O’er the parched fields in her angelic love She spread her wings out like a brooding dove: Till, as her purpose deepened, drawing near, So God-like terrible did her front appear, That men and beasts all trembled at the view, And the woods bowed,—though well all crea- tures knew That near in her, to every kind the same, A great predestined Benefactress came.

Yea, I will help you! said the Cloud again: Receive me—lo, I die for you—in rain! Wide flashed then—yea, through all her full grown form, The glory of her will! the agony and the storm Of Life’s dire dread of Death, whose mortal threat From Christ himself drew memorable sweat! Flashed seething out of rents amid her heaps. Of lowering gloom, and thence with arrowy leaps Hissed jagging downward, till a sheety glare Illumined all the illimitable air! The thunder followed; a tremendous sound Loud doubling and reverberating round, Till one wide rumbling intonation rolled Through all the shuddering mountains mani- fold! Strong was her will, but stronger yet the power Of Love, that now dissolved her in a shower, Dropping in blessings, to impregn the Earth With health and plenty at one blooming birth. She bled in rain,—bled unto death; but died Only to higher being—God beatified!

Far as the rain extended o’er the Land, A splendid bow the freshened prospect spann’d As the last greeting of the Spirit pure Of Love self-sacrificed, and thence made sure, Because in record on the Eternal writ In Faith and hope, and therefore one with it: One in duration, and in glory one, With all that Godward out of Time hath gone. —The rainbow vanished, but the blessing craved Long rested on the land the Cloud had saved.
