Part VI.

“Within a rustic chamber, dark and low, And with wild-looking men and women thronged, I seemed to wake—but knew not what I was; Nor how I had been thither brought; nor why. I was not large; my consciousness at once Had measured that fact. Inwardly I felt No briskness of existence, but a sense Of languor rather, or revival slow. And evermore the men and women came And looked at me with wonder, and with shouts Significant of confirmed astonishment! Then would they whirle about the room in dance, Abandoned to their barbarous delight.

“And thus I surely strengthened into life. Then looked about the chamber, half in hope And half in fear, of seeing it might be 217 The mighty Angel that now ruled my lot. My gestures were mistaken for a wish To breathe the open air, and I was borne Tow’rds a wide casement. Like a picture lay The world without, and instantly confirmed My hitherto vague impressions that a new Life-change had passed upon me. On all hands Nothing but mountains feathered to their tops With a dense fell of pines, and valleys filled With a cold darkness that was lit alone By the broad flashes of the furious streams That leaped in thunder out of marble gaps, Was seen in prospect. Over all a low Dull vapour, like a canopy of smoke, Did so obscure the sun, that I had thought The scene that now I saw was not of earth, But for a golden flush that now and then Would touch the higher ranges. What I had Indeed become I knew not, but I still Retained impressions of my former wonts, And often made vain efforts to expand The wings I had no longer, and sail off, And through those sullen vapours—up, and up Into the mighty silence of the blue.

“The day was fading, and a blare of horns With many voices and much trampling noise Heard from without aroused me. And ere long The women that had been erewhile around me, Again rushed in—each bearing some rich robe, Or some gay bauble, wherewithal they soon Arrayed me to their taste; and holding then A mirror up before me, I percieved My soul had this time passed into the form 218 Of a young female. The whole truth was this: A matter that long afterwards I learned) The being whom I now reanimated Was the sole child of a Circassian Chief; A Maiden of most memorable beauty, Who had been long regarded by her house As its chief treasure, and as such reserved For him alone, no matter whence he came, Whose hand could dip into the longest purse. But Envy lurks in the Circassian hills, As elsewhere, and a dose of opium well Administered by one who had been long The rival beauty (of a neighboring tribe), Had served to quash a bargain quite complete Save in the final payment of the gold, Which had been even offered and told down, And only not accepted, through some old Delaying ceremony of the tribe: And in which luckless circumstance, ’twas plain That both my admirable parents saw The unkindest turn of all.

“On all hands forth Had scouts been sent to summon the whole tribe To attend my obsequies, and then forthwith To exterminate our ancient enemies Through all their tents;—such was the fierce resolve! But while these things were tending, lo, the light Had broken, like a new morn, from the eyes Of the dead beauty; on her cheeks had dawned A roseate color; from her moistening lips Low murmurs too had broken: whereupon My parents, in the exultant hope of still Consùmmating their bargain, had transformed 219 The funeral to a general tribal feast, And loaded me with all the family gauds And ornaments they held. The Persian too Had been invited to renew his purchase, And carry me at once beyond the reach Of future opium doses.

“With the rest Back had he galloped, and was now without, Eager once more to bear me to the arms Of his long-bearded Lord. Within he came, In wonder, but full ready to make good His former bargain and pay down the price. My worthy parents tenderly embraced me With many tears, protesting that I was The life-light of their eyes—their rose of joy— Then stretchered their palms out for the proffered gold, And hurried off to tell it o’er in secret— The dear recovered treasure they so late Had mourned, as lost for ever. On that night I was packed carefully on a camel’s back Beside a case of porcelain pipes and jimcracks, And carried Persia-ward, by stages safe, From the Circassian mountains.

“At the Court I soon became the favorite of the king; Lived sumptuously, but in perpetual fear. My food was served to me on plates of gold Whose rims were rough with graven sprays, and set With jewels simulating various fruit; I slept on beds the canopies of which Were royal purple looped in folds, that hung Massively downward, as all sprigg’d with braids 220 And fringed with countless pearls: and yet no less I envied the poor slaves who swept the floors. My Persian lord for one whole month had been Sluggishly loving—for a month and more: And then I learned my place was to be filled By a new favorite. And strange to say, Albiet I loathed him as we loathe some cold And reptile creature, when compelled to touch Its so abominable being—yet I could not bear that he should thus supply My presence with another’s. I would make Some effort to regain his faded favor: For I was beautiful, and therefore vain, As beauty needs will be. And to this end, I now arrayed myself in robes that seemed Gossamer vestures of ethereal texture, While scarfs of purple such as only Queen’s May purchase from the Indus caravans, Barred them with rainbow tints; a burnished band Or purest gold, whose midst throughout was raised By a full row of pearls—all perfect ovals— Held in the wavelets of my sunny hair: A gem that seemed indeed a frozen mass Of white fire, flashed upon my silk-soft brow. Then, in my loveliness, even thus arrayed, I swam into his presence (though indeed My hid feet rowed me) graceful as a Swan.

“The Harem girls applauded, while there seemed Even in his own dull eyes almost a flash As of extorted joy: but which became At the next moment a malignant scowl, Which had its dark cause in such thoughts as these: (As I learned after from a Nubian girl 221 Who always waited on his baser needs) ‘What! did so soft and ignorant a minx Think to enchant again a man like him, Who was the sharpest fellow, past a doubt, In the whole Orient? Did a minx so raw Think to befool a mine of such intelligence And worldly wisdom, on all hands allowed, As he was—he! the paragon of kings! By floating in before him, like a swan, A little better feathered than at first?’

“Even while this mischief thus was brewing—lo, The Harem ladies had been waved away The Newbian girl remaining only. Then A conclave of those strange demoniac Dwarfs Who from their secret dens and crypts would come On state occasions, in the briefest space, On given signals forth, were summoned in: Wizard-like beings, with enormous heads, Splay-feet, and monstrous spider-fingered hands. Nor was the council long: I on that night Was to be poisoned—and with a pomegranite. One of a number to be pluckt and brought At supper, from the Summer Garden forth, For every lady of the Harem one— And one too for himself, though he disliked them.

“The Nubian had slipped away, and told me All she had heard: yet her news moved me not, So sure I felt that was not my doom: Or moved me but to this—the packing up Of such gay properties as ladies doat on. But time stays not, and we were summoned all To supper. On the centre-board there stood 222 A silver crate piled high with crimson gourds, Which soon amongst us did the king dispense, To every lady one: till two at last Alone remained, one of them being marked. This came to me, nor could I but accept it: But mindful of the part I had to play, Even as I took it I fell fainting down, And flurried so the ladies, they all thronged About me—flurried even the king himself! With infinite dexterity I then Revived as suddenly, and contrived the while Amid the bustle to exchange the gourds— Took his, and left him mine. His soon I ate, And praised as being the very soul of fruit: He also, with a rollic in his eye, Ate that which had been poisoned, and avowed He never knew before what fruit it was; Nay, that it held within its crimson rind The juice of Paradise.

“While this went on, I slipped away wholly unseen to where My goods were packed, and to prepare for flight; Nor had I long withdrawn, ere to my chamber The Nubian girl came running,—this her news: That soon the King had with a monstrous snort Fall’n prostrate, while the ladies all away Had scattered like a flock of gossamer clouds Before a storm-blast in a summer sky! And that the chamber now was thronged instead To suffocation, with a fusty crowd Of bare-poled hakims and grey-chinn’d physicians, Who all concurred at least in this: the dark Affair in hand was quite unlooked for—quite! 223 And had strong symptoms of a “poison case”. And as she told her news the Nubian laughed With infinite delight—so loved are Kings! And I laughted also, feeling that I had A natural and a perfect right to do so. —But no time might be wasted—and we fled Together, loaded both with costly spoils. Still as we passed beneath the latticed Court Wherein the Monarch suffered, all in vain I strove to dash my womanly desire To have a last look at him. So aloft I mounted, on the slave’s bent back,—and looked. The King was sprawling on the pillowy floor With quite a host of anxious quacks about him: Jews and Armenians, infidels and slaves, All ranks, all races; and as still he took Their nostrums, with grimaces horrible, He still kept threatening them, that if they failed To cure him, ere that dreadful night were past, The dawn should see them headless—every man! Then would he wonder why he ever chose Pomegranites for his supper. Then in rage He cursed his blunder, and the palpable trick By which a silly woman had o’erreached him! Then would he sprawl and bellow like a beast! And so I left him sprawling, while the sound Of his brute ragings followed us beyond The Garden bounds as through the dark we fled. —We heard long after, that he died ere he Had time to cut off all the doctors’ heads.

“‘

Twas plain and patent to my inmost self, That in this last change, I had always been Regenerating, more and more: for though 224 A spirit of mischief frolicked in my head, At heart I was not bad: for they who knew Me closely, and the closelier they knew me, Well seeing this,—and not the less perhaps, If of the woman sort, because ’twas plain My conduct most was from its balance thrown By whimsies, and by humors almost mad— These always, and the closelier they knew me, Loved me,—nay, served me as the Nubian did. —And now as no one else might sell me—lo, I sold myself, and made what I believed A capital bargain, till I found myself Installed as queen of a rude Afric King, Black as a log of ebony, and who As still at stated times, in motly state, On bow bent shanks and big pot-bellied bulk, He strode amongst us, bore I must confess Much brother-likeness to a huge baboon. But luckily, this monster soon preferred The Nubian to myself—a consummation That filled me with much thankfulness of heart. ‘‘

I was the captive of a Bedoeen Shiek, And fed his camels while I served his lust; Was sold in the slave-mart of Astrachan And carried thence India, to be crowned A Rajahpoot’s sultana; from which state Flying at length, I fell into a worse, Being pounced on by a Turcoman horse-stealer, Who made me, with much lofty self-applause, At once his mistress and his water-carrier. At Alexandria I became the slave Of a harsh Roman matron, who was wont 225 To flog and famish me to make me good, And when I owned myself converted, then She flogged and famished me the more, to make My goodness lasting. But this mush of change In the contexture of my fatal life But troubles and distracts me. The extremes Is what I best remember: I was now A Queen, and now a drudge: and finally Fell stabbed in Cairo—slaughtered by a slave.
