Part VI

Within a rustic chamber, dark and low, And with wild-looking men & women thronged, I seemed to wake—but knew not what I was. Nor how I had been thither brought: nor why? I was not large, my consciousness ere long Had measured that fact. Inwardly I felt No briskness of existence, but a sense Of languor rather, or revival slow. And evermore the men & women came And looked at me with wonder, and with shouts Significant of confirmed astonishment Then would they whirl about the room in dance Abandoned to their barbarous delights!

As thus I surely strengthened into life I looked about the chamber, half in hope And half in fear, of seeing it might be The mighty Angel who now ruled my life My gestures were mistaken for a wish To breathe the open air; and I was borne Towards a wide-casement. Like a picture lay The world without, and instantly confirmed My hitherto vague impression that a new Life-change had passed upon me. On all hands Nothing but mountains feathered to their tips With a dense fell of pines, and valleys filled With a cold darkness that was lit alone By the broad flashes of the furious torrents That leaped in thunder down from marble gaps Was seen in prospect. over all a low Dull vapour, like a canopy of smoke, And so obscured the sun, that I had thought The scene that now I saw was not of Earth But for a golden flush that now and then 151 Would touch the highest ranges. What I had Indeed become I knew not, but I still Retained impressions of my former wonts And often made vain efforts to expand The wings I had no longer, and sail off And though those sullen vapours—up, and up Into the mighty silence of the blue.

The day was fading, and a blare of horns With many voice and much trampling noise Heard from without around me. And ere long The women that been before around me Again rushed in—each bearing some such robe Or some gay bauble, wherewithal they soon Arrayed me to their task, and holding then Armour up before, I perceived My soul had this time passed into the form A young female. The whole truth was this The being whom I now reanimated Was the sole child of a Circassian Chief A maiden of most memoral beauty Who had been long regarded by her house As its chief treasured, and as such reserved For him alone no matter whence, he came Whose lust could dip into the longest purse But Envy lurks in the Circassian hills As elsewhere, and a dose of opium, Administered by one who had been long The rival beauty of a neighbouring tribe Had served to quash a bargain quite complete Save in the final payment of the gold Which had been even offered and told down And only not excepted by some old Delaying ceremony of the tribe; And in which luckless circumstance, ’twas plain That both my admirable parents saw The unkindest cut of all.

On all hands forth Had scouts been sent, to summon all the tribe To attend my obsequies, and then forthwith To exterminate our ancient enemies! But while these things were pending, lo, the light Had broken like a morning from the eyes Of the dead beauty; on her cheeks had dawned A roseate color; from her moistening lips Low murmers too had broken: whereupon My parents, in the exultant hope of still Consummating their bargain, had transformed The funeral to a general tribal feast And loaded me with all the family gauds And ornaments they held. The Persian too Had been inverted to renew his purchase And carry me at once beyond the chance Of future opium doses

With the rest Back had he galloped, and was now without, Eager once more to bear me to the arms Of his long-bearded Lord. Within he came In wonder, but full ready to make good His former bargain and pay down the price My worthy parents tenderly embraced me, 153 With many tears, protesting that I was The life light of their eyes—their rose of Joy! Then stretched their palms out for the Persian gold, And hurried off to tell it oer in secret: The dear recovered treasure they so late Had mourned, as lost for ever. On that night I was packed carefully on a camel’s back Beside a case of porcelain pipes and gim-cracks And carried Persia-ward, by stages long From the Circassian mountains.

At the court I soon became the favorite of the King; Lived sumptuously, but in perpetual fear. My food was served to me on plates of gold I slept on beds the furniture of which Were fringed with countless pearls; and yet no less I envied the poor slave who swept the floors My Persian Lord for one whole month had been Devoted to me—for a month and more: And then I heard my place was to be filled By a new favorite. And strange to say, Albeit I loathed him as we loathe a snake, I could not bear that he should thus supply My presence with another. I would make 154 An effort to regain his faded favor: For I was beautiful—and therefore vain As beauty needs must be. And to that end I now arrayed myself in robes that seemed Aeriel ultimates of perfect whiteness, While scarfs of purple such as only queen’s May purchase from the Indus Caravans, Barred them with rainbow tints; a burnished band Of purest gold, whose midst throughout was raised With a full row of pearls—all perfect ovals— Held in the wavelets of my sunny hair: A gem that seemed indeed a frozen mass Of white fire, flashed upon my silk-soft brow. Then in my loveliness, even thus arrayed, I swam into his presence (though indeed My hid feet rowed me) graceful as a swan!

The Harem girls applauded, while thus seemed Even in his own dull eyes almost a flash As of extorted joy: but which became At the next moment a malignant scowl, Which had its dark source in such thoughts as these: (As I learned after from a Nubian girl Who always waited in his baser needs) 155 “What! did a soft and ignorant minx like me Think to entrap again a man like him, Who was the sharpest fellow, past all doubt, In all the orient? Did a minx so raw Ween to outwit a piece of such intelligence, By floating in before him, like a swan, A little better feathered than at first?”

Even while this mischief had been mustering up, The Harem ladies had been all been dismissed, The Nubian girl alone remaining; then A conclave of those singular gnome-like dwarfs Who came no one knew whence, were summoned in: Nor was the council long: I on that night Was to be poisoned—and with a pomegranate, One of a number to be pluckt and brought For every lady of his Harem, forth At supper, from his favorite Summer Garden, And one too for himself, though he disliked them.

The Nubian had slipped away and told me All she had learned: yet her news moved me not, So sure I felt that such was not my doom; Or moved me but to this—the packing up 156 Of such gay properties as ladies doat on But time slid on, and we were summoned forth To supper. On the centre board there stood Of silver crate piled high with crimson goards And these the king despersed amongst us soon, To every lady one; till two at last Alone remained, one of them being marked. This came to me, nor could I but accept it, But mindful of the part I had to play, Even as I took it I fell fainting down And flurried all the ladies, till they thronged About me—flurried even the king himself. With infinite dexterity I then Revived as suddenly, and contrived the while Amid the bustle to exchange the goards— Took his, and left him mine. His soon I ate And praised as being the very soul of fruit. He also with a rollic in his eye Ate that which had been poisoned, and avowed He never knew before what fruit it was, Nay, that it held within its crimson rind The juice of Paradise.

While this went on I had slipped away wholly unseen to where My goods were packed, and to prepare for flight. 157 Nor had I long withdrawn, ere to my chamber The Nubian girl came running; telling me That soon the king had with a monstrous snort Fall’n prostrate; while the ladies all away Had scattered like a flock of gossamer clouds Before a storm wind in a summer sky And that the chamber now was thronged instead, To suffocation, with fusty mass Of bare-poled hakims and grey-haired Physicians, Who all concurred at least in this—the dark Affair in hand was quite unlooked for—quite, And had strong symptoms of a “poison-case” And as she told her news the Nubian laughed With infinite delight—so loved are Kings! And I laughed also, feeling that I had A natural and a perfect night to do so. But no time might be wasted—and we fled Together, loaded both with costly spoils. Still as we passed beneath the latticed Court Wherein the Monarch suffered, all in vain I strove to dash my womanly desire To have a last look at him. So aloft I mounted, on the slave’s bent back—and looked. The king was sprawling on the pillowy floor, With quite a herd of anxious quacks about him; 158 Jews & Armenians, infidels and slaves, All ranks, all races; and as still he took Their nostrums, with grimaces horrible, He still kept threatening them, that if they failed To cure him ere that dreadful night were past, The dawn should see them headless—every man! Then would he wonder, why he ever chose Pomegranates for his supper. Then in rage He cursed his blunder, and the palpable trick By which a silly woman had o’er reached him! Then would he sprawl, and bellow like a beast! And so I left him sprawling, while the sound Of his brute ragings followed us beyond The Garden bounds as through the dark we fled! —We heard long after, that he died ere he Had time to cut off all the doctor’s heads.

ith a strong spirit of mischied in my head, I was not bad at heart; for they who knew Me closely, and the closlier they knew me Though they saw thus,—and not the less perhaps, If of the women sort, because ’twas plain My conduct most was from its balance thrown By whimsies, and by human almost mad— These always, and the closlier they knew me, 159 Loved me—nay, served as the Nubian did. And now as no one else might sell me—lo, I sold myself, and thought that I had made A capital bargain, till I found myself Throned as the wife of a rude Afric Chief, Black as a log of ebony, and who As still at stated times, in motley state, On bow-bent shanks and big pot-bellied bulk, He strode before us, bore I must confess Much brother likeness to a huge baboon But luckily the monster soon preferred The Nubian to myself—a consummation That filled me with much thankfulness of heart

Twere long to tell whay haps befell me next: I was the capture of a Bedoine shiek And fed his camels, while I served his lusts; Was sold in the slave mart of Astrachan And carried thence beyond the Ganges, where A Rajahpoot received me as his queen. A Turcoman horse stealer owned me next, At once his water-carrier and his wife. At Adrianople I became the slave Of a harsh Roman matron, who was wont 160 To flog and famish me to make me good; And when I owned myself converted, then She flogged and famished one the more to make My goodness lasting. But this much of change In the contexture of my fatal past But troubles and distracts me. The extreme Is what I best remember: I was now A queen; and now a drudge: and finally Fell stabbed in Cairo—slaughtered by a slave.
