Memo.

In the following poem, it now seems to me, that I purged away most, if not all, of the perilous stuff that weighed upon my heart and life up to and during the period of its composition.

The Sorrows of Chatterton.

The marvellous Boy, The sleepless Soul that perished in his pride.

Wordsworth.

Preface.

The following Poem was written in the Author’s early youth, and was founded, as the title indicates, on the fate and character of the “marvellous Boy”, Chatterton. 169 It should be observed, however, that the author did not derive either his inspiration or materials from any ex[unclear] regular “Life of Chatterton”; he never having read a work of the kind, prior to its composition; but from incidental notices of his genius or his misfortunes, and from the a popular tradition of these, floating vague and unauthentic, like the drift wood of a wreck, along the more remote and solitary shores of Literature

4b

※ In its design, the monologues are a psychological, step by step, developement of the conceptions and peculiar sufferings that are too surely leading his wild and unfriended genius to a disastrous and self inflicted death; while the Choruses annexed to them, are spiritual cognitions of these peculiar moods and sufferings—but raised and tempered and illumined throughout with prophecies of a better era for the children of genius, and with suggestions conducive to its speedier advent.

5b

[This being the case, opinions and feelings are broached in the composition, many of which, of course, are not to be imputed to the present Writer, who holds himself strictly chargeable, as an individual, with those only that are embodied in the several Choruses. Nor would he have it inferred from a certain passage in the Poem, that he is at all disposed to justify Chatterton’s attempts at literary imposition. Yet he would 6 be understood to excuse them. Chatterton’s circumstances were pain- -fully peculiar, especially in their literary aspects. He had “set his life upon a cast”, and one, perhaps the chief, object of his palming cer- -tain poems on an old and forgotten Author, was doubtless to seduce the Literati of the day into a ready notice of productions which, if honestly fathered, he knew by expe- -rience! were likely to be received, if noticed at all, with the coldest indifference. And as he had much reason for believing that these poems were well worth (as such things go) the price to be paid for them, in the event of their publication, his poverty might have argued, that beyond this, the interest of the buyer of the 7 articles in relation to that of their manufacturer, should not be much considered or nicely respected. [This reasoning is not just: but would it be going too far to assert, that under equally desperate circumstances, such reasoning would be quite sufficient to confirm in a similar design, many who at this moment (being comfortably provisioned both immediate- -ly and prospectively) imagin themselves fortified against every possible assault of the Tempter, by an uncleavable panoply of (self) righteousness? [With regard [unclear] simply to such persons as would have been allured to the purchase of Chatterton’s books solely by the supposition of their being “curiosities”, it may be remarked, that as they were of that class of which Shakespeare 8 has said,—”When they will not give a doit to relieve a lame beggar, they will lay out ten to see a dead Indian”,— the device which aimed at converting to the benefit of a living author, the expense they were only disposed to throw away upon a dead one, if not praise- -worthy, was at least pardonable. In fine, Chatterton was stung to the quick by neglect, and rendered de- -fiant by the apparent blindness of fortune. His eccentricites too, had tarnished what little reputation had been awarded to his talents, while his poverty exposed him to contamination and contempt. Besides, he was very young—ah, so young! and when we consider all this, surely, the un- -happy memory of one so gifted may now be wholly forgiven the stratagem 9a by which he hoped to triumph over the pretentions of those who, as he rightly suspected, would be sedulous to trumpet to the skies as the remains of a Rowley, what they would scarcely condescend to glance at as the effusions of a Chatterton! The fact is, this “marvellous” boy despised his literary seniors too much from the first; and what respect could he gather from his subsequent knowledge of such lettered Fashionaires as Horace Walpole and his set, for the then reigning mag- -nates of British Literature? What indeed!

It is for the reader himself to determine how far, and in what way, he may be affected by this Poem; but to me, its author, I can most truly affirm, it has been an exceeding benefit. It proved the occasion—undesigned indeed—of the purging away of much perilous stuff that weighed upon my heart and life up to, and during the period of its composition. The working out of details enforced me to gather into a certain measurable, and thence more healthy consistence, all the vaguer and wilder feelings and fancies, and darker gropings, of a young and sensitive, and, it may be, over- -speculative nature; affording them, at the same time, a most opportune and artistic outlet. And moreover, its composition became the means (especially the Chorusses) of balancing the account of suffering,—whether real, as affecting myself, or imaginary, as attributed to Chatterton,—with instinctive prophecies of that sure, though distant, reversion, which is forever taking wider hold upon the universal destiny of the race, through the peculiar fortunes of its more gifted indi- -viduals.

Introductory Stanzas.

See the young Poet in life first essay, Ere Hope’s bright legions with disaster meet; 10 While his boy cheek is flushed with beauty’s ray, And daring are his hands and wingëd are his feet.

Loudly the echo of his soul repeats Those deathless strains that witched the world of old; And his high spirit dwelleth o’er the feats Of names enshrined therein even like heroic gold.

To Love he fans the ode of vocal fire,— To Valor’s deeds, and Freedom’s sacred dreams; Devotion’s incense gathers from his lyre Round Glory, where above the martyr’s grave it gleams.

Or as he wanders waking dreams devise New Edens, pictured on the future’s scroll; While on the wings of rapture he outflies The faltering mood that warns in his prophetic soul.

All doubt away! he cries with trustful pride, From Destiny’s unknown but good can rise, 11 Since this full heart attests how much may ‘bide Of love & joy beneath these kindly conscious skies!

Thus holiest Shapes inhabit his desire, And Love’s glad turtles sing along his way; Thus Hope, all eagle like, keeps mounting higher As Promise lovelier streaks the morning of his day.

Ah Poet! thy young heart is strung too high To bear the jars of this harsh world’s estate! And I do mark in thy too beaming eye The inward rolling blaze that bodes a wayward fate.

Soon on the morning’s wings shall Fancy flee, The sun of Song in tempest disappear; And thy wild heart, so billowy and so free, Be to the vultures given of Anguish, Doubt & Fear.

Ah yes!—Awhile, and of each stay amerced By Fortune, and amongst the sordid cast, 12 All that fair Promise warbled to the erst Shall grieve into a dirge mourned from the hollow Past.

I mark him in the City’s huddling crowd, Haggard & pale yet graceful not the less; To quick to scorn the mean,—defy the proud,— Distant to coldness now—then free to recklessness.

Yet oft what touching woe his pride assails, When generous Faith slain on the past appears To Memory, where she hoveringly bewails Even like a plundered bird—then blood is in his tears.

Henceforth must his companion be Despair, His lyre remain unstrung, his Muse forlorn: As never more some blighted tree may bear, Shall fruit of his young genius never more be born?
