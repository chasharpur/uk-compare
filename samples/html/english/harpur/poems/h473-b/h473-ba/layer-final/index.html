II

His Ancient Mariner.

Under a dim low sky of leaden hue A still sea glimmers in wide shoreless view, And midward—there, that sea and sky between, A sole Ship, idle as a cloud, is seen, Dark lying, with her Shadow, as twould seem, Hanging dead down with the watery gleam. On her lone deck no living Wight doth stir, Save One—even he, the doom-struck Mariner! But round the shadow of that ship supine With slimie life writhes all the rolling brine. While in the haze that to her topmast clings, Behold those vanishing visionary wings— Those vague-seen signs of a strange Yet-to-be, Through ghostly Powers at work on that mysterious Sea!

Such was Coleridge, upon the whole, as a poet. But as a poetic critic he was, at all times of his life he was only safe in his generalities. These are often finely comprehensive, with a depth of suggestiveness that is at once instructive and stimulating. But when he came to particulars, no good critic was ever more unsure as a guide, or ever made mistakes more gross and memorable—that is to say, memorable, from being the mistakes of such a man.

Charles Harpur
