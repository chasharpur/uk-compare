The Muse Firstlings.

Simple Effusions of my earlier days, Could I so long forget you? Could it be That ye have lain so long neglected by, Unsought for, unremembered, unperused, Yet once so gloried in? Once,—and even now, Sweet songs,—even now, perusing you, I feel Regenerate within me that bright faith 257 In the all-capable virtue of the Soul— That glowing, glorying expectancy Of Good which blest me then; and gave the hours Of wakeful meditation golden wings, Meet to onbear them in their shining range Over all possible and exalted forms Of Beauty, with its influence,—even Love; And rendered those that shed the balm of sleep Dream-gates to Heav’n, through which the Angels came To meet my spirit, and to lead it thence Into the peace of God. Ye must be lovely, For from a heart leaping with love you sprang Whene’er ’twas given to transport! Yes, and thence This sweet wild influence that even now Rolls through that heart a renovating tide, Though not for long, or only, while old times As conjured up by mystic incantations, Goes sweeping, wraith-like, through the light of thought. Ye must be pure as lovely; for while thus I con you o’er, a holy Presence seems To live around me in the studious hush— To live and listen. . . . . May it be, that some Returning Eon, willing yet again To rule my sympathies, would have me own In you the relics of a worthier self, As vestages whereby to mould anew A one sure claim to Heaven?

Even thus In some distracted State degenerate much To what she was, when in her simpler days Of unaffected greatness Justice framed Her laws, and Nature in her councils ruled, And Freedom swept her lyre,—some patriot muse Reviving, with a pious eloquence based 258 On moving memories of a noble Past, Conjures her straight to turn and ponder well Her former, better self; that so prepared She might her broken measures reconstruct Upon their pristine plan: sure means by which Once more to prosper—being innocent.

Though since I have ridden upon bolder Themes, And with the music of my lonely lyre, Though struck but in the intervals of toils Most lowly, and in hunger undergone, Even for my daily bread,—I have not failed To win scholastic auditors;—though since I’ve clomb the difficult promontory whence The Epic Muse surveys the boundless sea Of Time and Change,—not by those royal roads That Learning’s hinds for Fortune’s minions smo[o]th, And finger-post, and fence where perilous deemed; Those want had shut against the friendless Bard— But by adventurous leaps from crag to crag! And with the notion of a glorious fraud There smitten, as by inspiration, taught My lyre the accents of a buried Age, And called harmonious thunders from the grave! Till I grew unfamiliar with myself, And felt disturbed, and wondered at the range Of mine own spirit!—Yet, ye simple songs More than all these I love you; for you formed The flora of a mental Eden, where I wandered innocent, and over which The rosy rising of a darkened morn Shed all its orient wealth.

Then knew I all The inward glorying transport that exalts 259 Him who is skilled to modulate the verse Sounding or soft—a torrent or a brook! And coin the golden phrase, wherein to set The smiles of Fancy and the tears of Love; Without his studious cares who hopes to leave His voice to future times. For Fame held then No council in my thoughts, as since, whene’er I swept the lyre;—no, it was Love alone That prompted,—sweet first love of all we see And hear of beautiful on earth, or dream Celestial regions hold. He, as the ray That earliest warms some warbler into voice, When dipping in the flushing cloud his wing Or fluttering in the dew-illustrious bush, Came o’er me, hovering in balm-laden winds, And mantled with the streaky scarf of morn, And girdled with the choicest hues of even: For a wild bird of the woods, I knew His presence chiefly in those loveliest hours That usher in the day, and those that lead Between the earth and its descending scale Fair Twilight, queen of new-appearing stars. He came; and of his visionary cup gave me drink largely, bad me next, Thus sanctioned and inflamed of heart, indulge The willing Muse. Him I obeyed, and forth Flowered these my earliest Songs.

What though they be Too simple for the pedant, who would have Poor Nature hunted up with furious terms And baited till she roar? What though they be Condemned by ledger-learned critics, who Nothing admire but stilted odes in praise Of new raised civic halls, and stock exchanges, 260 And widened streets, and late-invented pipes For cheating stagnant water of its right, Time out of mind, to aldermanic cellars? Though by the generous and the just they be But classed as promissory blossoms, yet, Methinks that he who on the fire-plumed wings Of wild-eyed Ecstacy hath known to soar; Or he who in his warm and loving youth, And nobly ignorant of the world, hath roved Freely in Fable-land, can never quite Despise them—no; but he may prize them well, And wed them to the memories of Love In many a cherished air: such airs as breathe The flavor of a sweet dead Past, and seem To insphere the souls of buried Pleasures, known Only to Life’s glad morn. . . .

Is not this sweet, Albiet a fragment, and thence only like The one mature leaf of an unblown rose Too hastily gathered?

‘Twere strangely wrong, if they who yearn With all a Poet’s earnestness, Might only foster Love, to earn The pangs of unsuccess.

It were as natureless as though The fairest flowers by Summer nurst, Might only loveliest bloom, to know The canker’s venom worst.

But not so doomed was he whose teen From Fancy won this golden song; For he by her he loves hath been 261 Beloved in secret long.

And ’twas a Woman wile—the spurn That lately gave him to despair, Whereby to probe his heart, and learn If truly love lay there.

And knowing now the truth, she would Do penance through a bitter ire Against herself—for that she should Have tried him as with fire!

And seeing he afar doth rove, And can not view her blushing face, Her love, in words as fond as Love, Her hand hath dareth to trace:

In words that do as surely speak The feeling they are meant to bring, As roseleaves from a casket breathe The sentiment of Spring.

And so it dies. . . Another, all too well Doth indicate the downward track that led To where I stand, even now.

Once, did I only stand in thought beside The grave of one who had for Freedom died, Or on some spot made holy by the vow Of tuneful love, though of an ancient day, My very life would thrill—and am I now Journeying away

From that fraternal interest which cast Around me then the feeling of the Past? 262 I know not—but my heart no more will leap Even to the tromp of some Homeric lay! Bad progress is it, if from that I keep Journeying away!

Journeying away—and whither?—Scorn such strains Who will, I care not. But for me, as oft As the cold-blooded falshoods of the world, Through a redounding indignation, shocks Into fresh act the tendencies to truth And justice, with each brave protesting impulse, That in the secret places of my soul Yet linger,—even as the seers of old, Proscribed and banished, from the mountain caves Of their unworthy Land yet lifted loud Their warning voices,—I will utter them Over and over to my listening heart, So to revive its purer fires. But ah! Not without tears sometimes,—tears shed to think Of the lost freshness that bedewed the days Which they commemorate! Ah, that I could Be once again the wild and trustful Boy That sang them in the Past!—the wish is vain. And I must now content my hungry heart As best I may, with songs conceived in pain, And sickness, and a wild desire to give Some else intolerable burthen meet Expression: songs entitled

“Misery.”

As the moaning wild waves ever Fret around some lonely isle, There are griefs that no endeavour Stilleth even for a while, Beating at my heart for ever— 263 Beating at it now; Beating at my heart—and aching Upward to my brow.

Like the wild clouds flying over, High above all human reach, Their are joys that I their lover Cannot even scale in speech! Flying o’er my head for ever— Flying o’er it now; Flying o’er my head—and shading With despair my brow.
