Original Poetry.

LOYALTY.

NATIONAL PÆAN, BY CHARLES HARPUR.

Shall we sing of “Loyalty,” To the far south’s fiery youth? Yea; but let the pæan be Of loyalty to God and truth: To man, to progress, and to all, The free things—nobly free, Of which their loved Australia shall The golden cradle be.

Hark! her star-eyed destinies Pour their voices o’er the seas: Hither, to the land of gold, All who would be free! Here a diadem behold For immortal Liberty? Not for old world queens and kings, Villain slavery’s outworn things!”

Shall we sing of “Loyalty,” In this new and genial land? Yea; but let the pæan be, Of loyalty to love’s command: To thought, to beauty, and to all The glorious arts that yet In golden Australasia, shall Like spirit-gems be set!

Hark! her star-eyed destinies, &c.

Note.—Mr. Nathan, by marrying one of his seemliest musical daughters to the above thorough-bred Australian Lyric, may transmit to posterity (in this country) so much at least of the blood-royal of his quavering “loyalty,” as the natural law of con- sanguinity admits of; wherefore, I counsel him not to let the occasion slip through his harmonious fingers. Yet he will let it slip through them; having no lot, as a composer, in the Australian Future.

“I like this ‘Pæan,’ as you counterminingly term it, and coin- cide with its spirit entirely,” said a friend of mine counselling me for the best; “but do not publish it—at least not yet.” Why? “It may make you enemies in high quarters.” That is likely enough; and besides, it would appear that there is a considerable sprinkling of these loyal bunkumites nested amongst us in all quarters—and that some of them are as rabid, too, as mad dogs; as was instanced by that truculent display the other day in the Empire. But notwithstanding all this, here is goes, clean in their teeth. The man is unworthy of having friends, who is afraid of making enemies, by pursuing whatsoever he believes to be the right.

C. H.
