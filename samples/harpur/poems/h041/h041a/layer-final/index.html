To My Neece, Mary, The Daughter of J. J Harpur.

Dear Mary, I have often thought To frame a verse of two for thee; That henceforth thou mightst feel, as taught By something learned of me.

Thou knowèst (seeing ’tis thy lot To commune with a Sire refined) That Loveliness which fadeth not, Is only of the mind.

But there’s another phase of Truth, Which yet, perhaps, thou dost not know; And which, if given to thy youth, May teach thee how to grow.

There are even in the body springs That mix with what we still approve; And thereby mould it tow’rds the things We most admire and love.

With Goodness, then, keep friendship still, With Truth, and Chastity, and Grace; And lo! the sweetness of thy will Shall settle in thy face.

With Poesie communion make, And with all Arts of gentle kind; And lo! thy maiden form shall take The temper of thy mind.

Note. I would confirm the impression of the above verses by noting here in plain prose, that I do verily be- lieve our mental tasts (in early life at least) are greatly influential in shaping our bodies and characterising our bodies: not merely or solely by outward appliances open to all, in conjunction with habit,—such as staying 469 the bust, to taper and keep it erect, drilled attitudes, studied featural expressions, and so forth; but also by superinducing certain physical idiosyncracies which operate from within, and are limited to individuals, or classes of individuals. And I gather this belief from much observation, as well as from the idealisms of the [unclear] Muse.
