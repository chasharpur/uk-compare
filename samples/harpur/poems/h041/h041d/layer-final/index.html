(THIRD PAPER.) VII.

TO A GIRL.

Dear Maiden, I have often thought To frame a verse or two for thee, That henceforth thou might’st feel as taught By something learned of me.

Thou knowest, seeing ’tis thy lot To commune with a sire refined, That loveliness which fadeth not Is only of the mind.

But there’s another phase of Truth Which yet, perhaps, thou dost not know, And which, if given to thy youth, May teach thee how to grow.

There are even in the body springs That mix with what we still approve, And thereby mould it to the things We most admire and love.

With goodness then keep friendship still, With meekness, chastity, and grace, And lo! the sweetness of thy will Shall settle in thy face.

With poesy communion make, And with all arts of gentle kind, And lo! thy maiden form shall take The temper of thy mind.

I would confirm the impression of these verses by noting, that I do verily believe our mental tastes (in early life at least) are greatly influential in shaping and characterising our bodies; not merely or solely by out- ward appliances, open to all, in conjunction with habit, ———such as staying the bust, to taper and keep it erect, drilled attitudes, studied featural expressions, and so forth; but also by superinducing certain physical idio- syncracies which operate from within, and are limited to individuals, or classes of individuals. And I gather this belief from much observation, as well as from the idealisations of the Muse.
