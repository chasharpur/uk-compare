SONGS, EPIGRAMS, NOTES, AND OPINIONS, &c., BY CHARLES HARPUR.

No.16.

I.

Form of a Song.

A song to be perfect in its form, should consist of three, and of only three, stanzas, or metrical divisions. Of course, the number of verses in a ballad is to be determined by the compass of the story. So also a lyrical poem, being shorter or longer according to its subject, may be divided accordingly. But with a song it is different. By a song—specifically a song—I mean a simple outgush of emotion: an effusion, artlessly spon- taneous, or apparently so, like the carol of a bird. And the form of such a poem appears to me to demand naturally three metrical divisions:—the Prelude, the Theme, and the Close; or

I. The heaving-up of the subject or sentiment; II. Its full wave-like development; III. Its subsidence.

It is true that there are two or three very happy examples in Burns of songs which consist of but two stanzas. One of these begins with the line, “Had I a care on some wild distant shore,” and another with the words, “Had we never loved so blindly.” But even these, to my thinking, would be all the completer with a middle stanza, affording, as it were, an inmost shrine for the spirit of the subject. Or when a song has thus but two metrical divisions, it should be, or appear to be, a fragment only, suddenly terminating in the unspeakable, as a billow in its full strength and profluence is all at once burst upon a reef. Or when, from the milder nature of the subject, a two-versed effusion cannot be thus abruptly ended without detriment to its lyrical pur- pose, still it should have (and will, perhaps, of necessity, for want of a middle member or culminating cone) a more incidental effect than would be desirable or proper in a full proportioned song. The following beautiful little thing by Shelley is an example in point:—

“Music, when soft voices die, Vibrates in the memory; Odours, when sweet violets sicken, Live within the sense they quicken.

Rose leaves, when the rose is dead, Are heaped for the beloved’s bed; And so thy thoughts, when thou art gone, Love, itself, shall slumber on.”

And as an example of the propriety of the triple division herein mooted, I will adduce a little piece of my own. I adduce it. because, in the first place, it is nearest at hand; and because, in the next, it is very brief, and will therefore the more pertinently exemplify the requirement which I suppose essential to the dueform of a song:—

LOVE IN THE PAST]

One summer evening, passed beside yon river, Hath left its sunset in my soul forever: Passed by yon river side With One who should have been my beauteous Bride.

The mouth I pressed then, like a bee-broached blossom, As with new honey, overflowed my bosom: Those magical sweet lips, They thrilled me even to the finger tips!

But onward, like the stream that by was flowing, Hurried that sweet time, with its sunset glowing; And She is far away, Who blest my being on that happy day.
