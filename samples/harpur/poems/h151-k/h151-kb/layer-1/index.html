X

Chorus of the Hours.

O why is not this beauteous Earth The Eden men imagine? the fair seat Of fruitful peace, pure love, and sunny mirth? And why are its prime children souls, though so complete In apprehensions of a Godlike state, The subjects ever of fraternal hate— Oppressing or oppressed! That so the portion is of all, deceit And fear, and anger, sorrow and unrest?

There’s not one bright enduring thing In this great round of nature that appears,— No shining star, no river murmuring, No morn-crowned hill, no evening-goldened scene, That hath not glimmered and distorted been Through the dim mist of tears— Hot from some human brain, Throbbing and aching with unpitied pain!

There is not one green mound, existent long, In any region, nor old wayside stone, On which some weary child of social wrong Hath sat not,—there alone 274 To bite his pallid lip and heave the unheeded groan!

And such hath been the state of Man Since first the race’s recreancy began; And thus his piety is scared away From Earth, its proper home, To seek vague Heavens above the source of day; Or out beyond the gorgeous gloom Wherewith dusk Evening curtains up the west;— There flying, like the Psalmists’s dove, to rest In sinless Gardens of perpetual bloom, And Islands of the Blest.

Not but there is a place of purest bliss Beyond this Earthly, where the self refined And virtuous soul whom evil failed to bind; But that state should not o’ermuch lure from this Faith’s spirit (though to highest good inclined,) Since one might be as perfect in its kind Even as the other is. And yet awhile—a weary, weary while, And some immortal Intellect shall plan A social scheme for Man, Whereby the face of life shall smile For all throughout their pleasure-lengthened days— Days running like glad brooks through green and flowery ways, And rendering them at last content to die, Full to enjoyment’s best capacity.
