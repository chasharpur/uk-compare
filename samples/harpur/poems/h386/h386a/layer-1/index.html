Poetry & Bread.

Although we cannot eat, in very deed, A poem, as we can a baker’s roll; This only serves the body’s present need, That stays the ancient hunger of the soul.

Note. Poetry is not bread. I know that as well as the prosiest mortal alive. I know moreover, that whatever capacity I may possess, over and above a capacity for worldly labor, would not avail in this present Australia, to keep me from starvation—unless I could become the menial of other people’s mental views, and at the same time perhaps, a recreant to my own. But I know at the same time that a true man—a man proper— does not live by bread alone, but also by the virtue of many spiritual verities which may be compassed only by the highest reaches of the mind: and hence it is (lowly and toil-worn as I am) thatI continue to cultivate my poetic faculty, and feast as I best can upon empyreal fruits.

Thus Poetry refuses to be terminated by the by the business question, Will it buy bread? High out of the shot of a worldly utilitarianism does it live forever, because it is truly a piece of our mental nature, and needful in some sort to the entire self expression of the [unclear] mortal [unclear]: for all men, upon occasion, are more or less poetic, and have their part in Poetry.
