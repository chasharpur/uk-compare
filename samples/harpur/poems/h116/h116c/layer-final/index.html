Poems in early Life by Charles Harpur

The English Emigrant Girl’s Departure

She is going in her beauty,— In the morning of her day, From the kindred hearts that love her And would fain that she should stay: For weal or wo she’s going— To obey or to command Beneath another heaven In a far off wild-faced Land!

There is grief upon the waters! For the anchor now is weighed, And the ship is moving outward, And the last farewell is said By the fond old friends who knew her In her better days of yore, And whose thoughts are mourning in them— “We shall never see her more!”

Yet there’s hope upon the waters— And waved is many a scarf From the boat of those returning Without her, to the warf, And she is calm, though pensive, For the burst of grief is o’er, And her soul is roaming outward Tow’ards a dream-depicted shore.

Could we look into the future— Could we lift its cloudy veil, 53 There beneath a warmer heaven, In some maize-clad mountain dale, Or by some wild wood river, Or on some prairie wide, We might see a lonely mourner, Or behold a happy bride.

But vainly would we vision What the future must conceal: And let it comfort nature To wish largely for her weal: And is she not a Woman? And how can aught so dear, Amonghst creatures that are human Have anything to fear?
