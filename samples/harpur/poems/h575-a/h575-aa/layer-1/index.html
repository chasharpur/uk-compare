A Scene from Homer.

Then arose in the might of his soul, Achilles, the favored of heaven And crystal eyed Pallas then cast The broad shield of Jove o’er his shoulders And round his bold head she crowned A golden mist, lightning with splendor As a cloud of the East flaming abroad In the mountain-top glory of sunrise: And as, when a smoke goeth up From a town in an ocean-girt island, Whose foes, from a mighty hold Pouring out, have the day through assaulted; So soon as the set sun hath drawn The grey strip of twilight down with him How thickly the nimble tongued fires Are seen to rush up through the darkness And flicker the arch of the sky, And light-path the face of the waters, Advising both neighbouring Chiefs To party in the face follow with succor: May haply be coming with succor 228 Lo from the proud head of Achilles Went up that miraculous glowing splendor

On the mound of the trench he stood forth Beyond the camp wall, that it girded Aloof from the host of the Greeks, Being held by the hest of his mother; And there, standing up like a tower, In his terrible anger he shouted! And his shout, as it wrathfully rang O’er the slaughter-gored fronts of the battle, Minerva enlarged by a cry That fell, with a shr heaven! And then, as that clear ringing voice Went forth, like the blast of a trumpet When it threatens the gates of a town By merciless foemen beleaguered, In the host of the Trojans there rose A wild multitudinous tumult! 229 And when too that fierce cry fell Fell o’er them, fell with it confusion, And a terror that broke this array, And a horror that felled them with darkness Their legions deforming, as clouds Are torne by the winds of a tempest! For the noble maned horses, dismayed Ran huddlingly round with the chariots With but eyes foreseeing full well, Calamity, clothed in the omen! And the hearts of the charioteer Were smitten, as still they kept staring At the fire on the beautiful head Of the god like great-minded one burning, As Pallas ordained,—raying forth With an active and ominous glory, Thus thrice from the trench did he shout And the Trojans, as oft, with the allies, Were whirled in a dust-clouded heaps And rolled backed in utter disorder: So that twelve of the captains renowned In the rout and astonishment perished, 230 Crushed to death by the jambing of wheels And under the wrecks of the chariots.

Making a glow in the sky, And pathing with light the dim waters When, haply, some insular chief Is urging his galleys, with succor

The measure of the above paraphrase is, I think, as near an approach to the Hexameter as can be well made in English. Our one-syllabled and lop-sided words will not fus reaily enough into quantities, to gallop freely under its ceasural saddle. To drop the figure, they cannot be read trippingly, and at the same time adjusted to its metrical balance. Whether South has shown to the contrary, I do not yet know; but I defy any one to read Southey’s attempts in the Vision of Judgment, without being his organ of tune, for the time being, shuddered as it were to pieces, like a burst soda bottle. Even the heroics of Donne 231 (in their kind) are scarcely more jumbled and dotty. Nay, even the jaunty slip-sloppish, shimble-shamble of the Month Poets (a re-spawn of the Della Cruscaen) is to by thyking better; for that, by a very dexterous reader, may be shuffled along with, to something like a tune.
