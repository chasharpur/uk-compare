A Battle Piece from Homer.

Up he rose in the might of his soul, Achilles, the favored of heaven And azure eyed Pallas then cast The broad shield of Jove o’er his shoulders And she put round about his bold head A golden mist, lightning with splendor Like a cloud of the mornings, inflamed With the mountain-top glory of sunrise: And as, when a smoke goeth up From a town in some far away island, Whose foes, from their own stronger hold Pouring out, have the day through assaulted; So soon as the set sun hath drawn Down with him The grey strip of twilight How thickly the nimble tongued fires Are seen to rush up through the darkness And flicker the arch of the sky, And light-path the face of the waters, By confederates, who over the sea To party in the face over the sea with his succor May haply be coming with succor 228 Lo up from the head of Achilles Went glowing aloft an effulgence.

On the mound of the trench he stood forth Beyond the camp wall, though still keeping Aloof from the host of the Greeks, Being held by the hest of his mother; And there, standing up like a tower, In his terrible ardour he shouted! And his shout, as it wrathfully rang O’er the slaughter-gored fronts of the battle, Minerva enforced by a cry That fell, as it shrieked out of heaven! And lo, as that clear ringing voice Tore out, like the voice of a trumpet When it threatens the gates of a town Which implackable foemen beleaguered, In the host of the Trojans there rose A wild multitudinous tumult! 229 And when too that brazen cry fell Among them, fell with it confusion, And terror that broke this array, And horror that shuddered within them Their legions deforming, as clouds Are torne by the winds of a tempest! For even the haughty necked steeds Swerved, huddled, and stood on their haunches foreseeing full well, as it seemed, Calamity, clothed in the omen! And the hearts of the charioteer Were smitten, as still they kept staring At the fire on the terrible head Of the god like great-minded one burning, As Pallas ordained,—raying forth With an active and ominous splendid glory, Thus thrice o’er the trench did he shout And the Trojans, as oft, with the allies, Were whirled into dust-clouded heaps And rolled back in utter disorder: So that twelve of the captains renowned In the rout and astonishment perished, 230 Crushed dead by the jambing of wheels And under the wrecks of the chariots.

Making a glow in the sky, And pathing with light the dim waters When, haply, some help bringing chief Comes ploughing the deep with his galleys

The measure of the above paraphrase is, I think, as near an approach to the Hexameter as can be well made in English. Our one-syllabled and lop-sided words cannot be fused readily enough into the requisite quantities, to gallop freely under its ceasural saddle. To drop the figure, they cannot be read trippingly, and at the same time adjusted to its metrical balance. Whether Longfellow has shown to the contrary, I do not yet know; but I defy any one to read Southey’s attempts in the Vision of Judgment, without being his organ of tune, for the time being, shuddered as it were to pieces, like a burst soda bottle. Even the heroics of Donne 231 (in their kind) are scarcely more jumbled and doggy. Nay, even the jaunty slip-sloppish, shimble-shamble verse of the Month Poets (a re-spawn of the Della Crusca memoirs) is to by thinking better; for that, by a very dexterous reader, may be sometimes shuffled along with, to something like a tune.
