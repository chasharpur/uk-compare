The Tower of the Dream.

A Lay of the Land of Sleep.

Part I.

As in the death-like mystery of Sleep My body lay impalled, my soul arose And journeyed outward in a dream of wonder. Methought that, in that voiceless midnight hour, I roamed the margin of a waveless Lake, Which, like a huge round dim Cyclopian eye Welled in the knotted forehead of the Land, Stared moodily heavenward; and that I had Also a vague and mystic consciousness, That over against me, on its further shore, Which yet I might not see, there stood a Tower St Such as we read of in some old romance.

The darkness darkened, until overhead Solidly black the starless heavens domed, And earth was one more blank—when suddenly A Light, hung midway up the Tower, which I Not seeing before, was conscious of, at once Brought out its rounding structure from the gloom Wherein, till now, it seemed substantially shut: And as the Light then steadied, hanging there, 32 Suspended as by magic, I might see Its broad reflexion in the L stirless Lake, Between us, like a fiery column fall Down through the waters.

Long in speechless wonder It kept me gazing—till I thence could feel A strange and thrillingly attrative power In gradual operation; and ere long My bodily weight seemed witched away, and up I mounted, through the passive air,—still up And forward glidingly, sheer o’er the Lake; Which far below, as tow’rd the wondrous Light The attraction drew me, I beheld ingleamed Even to its sullen depths, which with shifting beams, That tangled Tower-ward into one broad blaze Of multifarious splendor.

On I slid: Nor knew I why, but my amaze wore off As thus I voyaged o’er the Lake, and still Approached the Tower and that so wondrous Light! And soon, instead, an interfusing warmth, Even like the spirit of new love, began To tingle in my Being, and with this 33 So sweet conception,—lo, a beautiful change!

Within a semi-circling balcony, That midway up the Tower, emporched a huge And brazen door, a Lady all of Light, And lovelier than lustrous, stood alone; And as she witched me tow’rds her, beckoned still With tiny hand more splendid than a star: Beckoned and smiled—as Morning’s streaky lips Smile, reddening lengthwise, under and above The pearly clouds; and with a voice intense As the quick glance of dying bliss, and oh! More silvery than the radiance of the Moon, When from some promontory looking out Over the Ocean,—she kept asking aye, “Where hast thou stayed so long?”

I heard—but felt Pass from me then a cry of instant fear, As swooning with intensity of joy, Methought I drifted—wither? All was now One wide cold blank, the drearier for that A gleam of dizzy and clinging consciousness Kept flickering from mine inmost pulse of life, Like a far meteor in some dismal marsh:— How long I dreamt not—but the thrilling warmth, 34 Which, like the new birth of a passionate love, Erewhile had pierced me to the heart, again Tingled within it—more and more, until Mine eyes had opened under two that made All else like darkness; and upon my cheek A breath that seemed the final spirit of all Life holds of sweetness, harbingered once more The fond enquiry of that silvery voice, Which to have heard was never to forget— “Where hast thou stayed so long?”

I answered not, But followed her in mute love her starry eyes Within mine, as on that balcony against The brazen door, methought I now reclined, Close at her lustrous side, upon a couch Of purple, looking forth. Nor did I marvel As hers thus led my vision, to remark That the broad Lake, with its green shelving shores Swarming with flowers, or draped with vines, and thence The prospect all,—hills, skies, and winding vales, And bloomy woods of an unspeakable beauty, Were sleeping in the radiance of a day More blessedly serene, than ever yet 35 Might so enrobe the common world of man, Since Paradise was lost.

I saw all this Surpassing beauty—but I saw it still Through her superior life, her moon-like sphere Of being, that seemed transpicuously the scource— The inexhaustible source of breathing thoughts And effluent idealities; and more Of rosy passion, and affectionate joy, And earnest tenderness, than many hearts Of the most fervent daughters of mankind United might possess;—all interflowing From the fine issues of a love at once Wilful and nice, but, therefore, not the less Sanctioned by its own purity. Nor might The brimming Lake below, then give the print More quickly of the wind’s invisible steps, Than instant tokens of communion sweet With the rare soul of outward beauty, passed Into her eyes, to make the eloquent whole Of her bright countenance alive with bliss.

But as upon the wonder of her beauty My soul now feasted, even till it seemed Instinct with kindred lustre,—to, her looks 36 Suddenly saddened, and abstractedly Outfixing them as on some far wild thought That darkened then over our peace, she flung Her silver voice into a mystic song Of many measures, gathering as they went Into a sliding flood of glorious verse, Sweetly abundant! And with this, as still She poured it out, invisible Singers mixed A choral burthen that in concord so Prolonged it, till the echoes of the hills Came forth in turn, and refluently afar Blent all into one mystery of sound, One manifold cadence,—dying down. Part II.

She ceased; and a deep tingling silence fell Instantly round: silence complete, and yet Instinct as with a breathing sweetness, left By the rare spirit of her voice, now hushed: Even as the fragrance of an exquisite flower Is felt in the mute air through which erewhile It hath been carried by the loving hand Of some sweeted Maiden. Turning then Her bright face tow’rds me, as I stood entranced, Yet wonder-stung, she said:

“I love thee, yess; But oh, even Love—this purple-wingëd Love, Is but a honey-bee of Paradise, That seldom strays into the common world Of Man and Woman; and even then, alas! ‘Tis only wilfully wandering from the home Of its pure birth, whither it must return Ere the night cometh.” And with these, her eyes Ran o’er with lustres as they opened up Under mine own now melancholy gaze.

39

And so we stood, turned one unto the other, Till love again grew glad, even from the rich And wine-like luxury of its own tears. And we looked forth once more,—happily silent. Alas! not long: for with a short low gasp Of sudden fear, she started; nor might I Stand unalarmed. For hark! within the Tower A sound of strenuous steps, approaching fast, Rung upward as it seemed from the hard pavement Of a steep winding stair; and soon the huge And brazen door that shut behind us there, Burst open! with a clang of loosened bolts— A clang like thunder, that went rattling forth Against the echoes of the distant hills.

With deafened ears, and ghasted eyes, I had turned Tow’rds the harsh noise, there to behold, between The brazen jambs in the Tower-wall, from which The door swung inward, a tremendous Form! A horrid gloomy Form, that shapeless seemed, And yet in its deformity of bulk A hideous likeness bare to Man! Still more Deformed it grew, as forth it swelled; and then Its outlines, shadowing outwards, so were lost In a dim indistinguishable haze 40 That hung around them, even as dull grey clouds Beskirt a coming Tempest, dark itself, And murkiest in midst.

The Lady of Light Had rushed to my extended arms, and hid Her beamy face, fright-harrowed, in my bosom! And thus we stood, made one in fear; and still That grisly Form glared out upon us—glared With horny eyes, the horrider for that No touch of passion vitalized their fixed Cold stare, as tow’rds some destined task they seemed To guide its pitiless strength.

With one dark stride Then glooming forward, and extending straight One shadowy arm, from my reluctant grasp It tore the Radiant Girl, muttering the while “This is forbidden!” in a voice, the tones Of which were like low thunder, as ’tis heard Outroaming from the rugged-winding glens Of neighbouring mountains: and as suddenly, Seized by the other, I was backward thrown Within the Tower, and heard, ere I could rise From the cold pavement, the huge brazen leaves 41 Drawn to upon me, and its bolts all shoot Into their sockets with a thundrous jar!

I may not paint the horrible despair That froze me now. I listened; all was still! Without,—within,—all silent, stirless, cold! What was my doom? And where was she, my late So luminous delight? Gone—gone! And I For some all-unimaginable cause, A dungeoned wretch! Time, every drop of which Was as an age, kept tingling on, but there Brought no release—no hope—not even a breath That spake of life beyond myself. I stood Utterly blank. And when at length I cast A desperate glance,—so desperate, that it the power Of vision stung me like a deadly curse,— Up and around; in pity of myself I wept aloud: till resignation, that Meek flower of grief which hath its root in tears, Grew out of mine, and with it, strength of will To inspect my Prison.

‘Twas a lofty Cell, Half round, and had a second door, embossed With iron studs, which might have opened in Athwart the stairway (as I guessed) that led Down through the Tower; and by the side of this 42 A massive stead upon four dragon claws Stood naked, with a tripod placed before it. Bare too, and dull, were the rude walls, but pierced High out of reach, by two small ports, that looked One east, one westward.—As I noted these, Full on my sight a transcient sunbeam fell Slantlingly through, and in my heart I guessed That tow’rds the mountain tops the sun was then Rapidly sloping.

By degrees the night Came on, and the Cell darkened: yea, I saw The stead and tripod—all its furniture, Fade gradually, melting more and more Into the darkness; even as a fish, Through the dense medium of its element Retiring down, is in its outlines seen More shadowy—till ’tis lost. Then all was black. And to and fro I paced, hour after hour, And heard my step, the only sound to me In the wide world, reverb with a dull blow Down through the hollow Tower, that seemed to yawn Immeasurably beneath! Hour after hour; And still no other sound was living there! But I grew sensible at length, methought 43 Of seasonable change;—that now the Cell Kept clarifying, till the darkness seemed Marbled with grey: and then the stead again, And tripod, figured indistinctly through The thinning gloom, and gradually thence Came forth in full definement, by the same Degrees reversed as they faded in it.

It was the Dawn: and thus it clearing kept, Till through the eastern port a golden rod Of light came transciently, and so bespake The sunrise! Oh! it was a desolate pass, To feel,—immured in that so deadly place, That the great Sun was then upon the hills Rejoicing in his glory! Then to know That he was wheeling up the heavens, and o’er My dungeon-roof:—hour after hour, to think That he was filling with a step of fire His midway circle, and thence steeping down Into the west! But this was my dire fate, While seven times came and [unclear] went the day; and yet I slept not; nor did hunger pinch me there, Nor thirst inflame. Mere wonder at my doom Relieved despair.

But on the seventh night, hark! 44 What might I hear?—a step?—a light small step Ascending by the stair! Swiftly it came Straight to the inner door—then paused. When hark! Another? yea, the same big-beating tramp That once I had heard before, came following fast! Approached—and then, a low sad cry without Went to my heart, and I might hear ensue A struggle as of one forced down the stair By that so pitiless Guard!—down, till the Tower Once more was still. But not for long! again The Monster’s hateful tread came booming up— Up to the door! It opened; and he stood Full in its darkened space: but ere the Fiend Might enter further, rage and hate at once Possessed me, and I charged him! For a space His terrible glooms involved me; but, methought, I burst with irresistible strength away! Rushed through the door, and down the stair,—down, down An endless depth, till a portcullis, hinged In the Tower’s basement, opened to my flight. No sooner had I passed it, than it fell In thunder too! and thence my passage lay Along the difficult ledges of a rock Bordering the Lake.

45

And when at length I had paused In that so giddy flight, methought I saw The Lady of Light up midway in the air Ascending, with a downward look on me Of parting recognition,—full of love, But painless, passionless. Upward she passed, Above the Tower, and o’er the clouds,—and when Her radiance melted through the marble cope Of heaven, and left it vacant in its vastness, A sense of loss fell on me, all so wide And bitterly desolate, that I awoke! Awoke, to find it but a dream of wonder. Yet ever since to feel as though some pure And guardian Thing, out of the day and night, Had passed forever from the reach of Love.

Note. The foregoing Poem is, in its substance, as its title implies, the record of an actual dream,— recollectively elaborated into verse, and restricted to something more of method, perhaps, than was observed by itself in the order of its passing. A very remarkable circumstance respecting it, is, that while dreaming it, I was at first partly conscious that I was dreaming. Altogether, it may be justly regarded by the reader as a physicological curiosity. For myself, I regard 46 it as something more; as something in which there may be seminally treasured, as within a partially quickened seed, a great deal more than is dreamt of in our present philosophy. The science of Mesmerism, perhaps perhaps, will eventually declare such to be the fact, by showing that sometimes our mere dreams, like our more spiritual self-abstractions and idealities, are absolute, though dim and imperfect, glimpses of that vastitude of mystical being, upon the border of which the spirit of man, even in this his material existence, is occasionally an inspired wanderer: such a wanderer (to use an analogical figure) as was the great Newton, in the conscious parvitude of even his science, beside the infinite ocean—the Great Deep of undiscovered yet discoverable Truth.

Since the above was written, the following passage in Sir Thomas Browne, has been pointed out to me.

“We are somewhat more than ourselves in our sleeps, and the slumber of the body seems to be but the waking of the soul. It is the ligation of sense, but the liberty of reason; and our waking conceptions do not match the fancies of our sleeps x x x In one dream I can compose a whole comedy, behold the action, apprehend the jests, and laugh myself awake at the conceits thereof. Were my memory as faithful as my reason is then fruitful, I would never study but in my dreams; and this time also would I choose for my devotions; but our grosser memories have then so little hold of our abstracted understandings, that they forget the story, and can only relate to our awakened souls a confused and broken tale of that which has passed.”
