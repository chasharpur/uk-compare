Preface.

The following Poem was written in the Author’s youth, and was suggested, as the title indicates, by the fate and character of the “marvellous Boy”, Chatterton. 169 It should be observed, however, that the author did not derive either his inspiration or materials from any regular “Life of Chatterton”; he never having been able to meet with a work of the kind, prior to its composition; but from incidental notices of his genius or his misfortunes, and from a popular tradition of these, floating vague and unauthentic, like the drift wood of a wreck, along the more remote and solitary sea shores of Literature

It is for the reader himself to determine how far, and in what way, he may be affected by this Poem; but to me, its author, I can most truly affirm, it has been an exceeding benefit. It proved the occasion—undesigned indeed—of the purging away of much perilous stuff that weighed upon my heart and life up to, and during the period of its composition. The working out of its details enforced me to gather into a certain measurable, and thence more healthy consistence, all the vaguer and wilder feelings and fancies, and darker gropings, of a young and sensitive, and, it may be, over- -speculative nature; affording them, at the same time, a most opportune and artistic outlet. And moreover, its composition became to me the means (especially the Choruses) of balancing the account of suffering,—whether real, as affecting myself, or imaginary, as attributed to Chatterton,—with instinctive prophecies of that sure, though distant, reversion, which is forever taking a wider hold upon the destiny of the human race, through the peculiar efforts & experiences of its most gifted indi- -viduals.

Vestibulary Stanzas.

See the young Poet in life first essay, Ere Hope’s bright legions with disaster meet; 10 While his boy cheek is flushed with beauty’s ray, And daring are his hands and wingëd are his feet.

Loudly the echo of his soul repeats Those deathless strains that witched the world of old; And his high spirit dwelleth o’er the feats Of names within them treasured like heroic gold.

To Love he fans the ode of vocal fire,— To Valor’s deeds, and Freedom’s sacred throes; Devotion’s incense gathers from his lyre Round Glory, where above the martyr’s grave it glows.

Or as he wanders waking dreams devise New Edens, pictured on the future’s scroll; While on the wings of rapture he outflies The faltering mood that warns in his prophetic soul.

All doubt away! he cries with trustful pride, From Destiny’s unknown but good can rise, 11 Since this full heart attests how much may ‘bide Of love & joy beneath yon purple-clouded skies!

Thus holiest Shapes inhabit his desire, And Love’s glad turtles sing along his way; Thus Hope, all eagle like, keeps mounting higher As Promise lovelier streaks the morning of his day.

But ah! too high his harp-like heart is strung To bear the jars of this harsh world’s estate! And all may guess from his too fervent tongue How burns the fire within that bodes a wayward fate.

Soon on the morning’s wings shall Fancy flee, The sun of Song in tempest disappear; And his wild heart, so billowy and so free, Be to the vultures given of Anguish, Doubt & Fear.

Ah yes!—Awhile, and of each stay amerced By Fortune, and amongst the sordid cast, 12 All that fair Promise warbled to him erst Shall grieve into a dirge mourned from the hollow Past.

Now mark him in the City’s huddling crowd, Haggard & pale yet graceful not the less; To quick to scorn the mean,—defy the proud,— Distant to coldness now—then free to recklessness.

Yet oft what agony his pride assails, When his Boy-Faith to Memory appears World-slaughtered, over her loss, she wails Even like a plundered bird—then blood is in his tears.

Henceforth must his companion be Despair, His lyre remain unstrung, his Muse forlorn: As never more some blighted tree may bear, Shall fruit of his young genius never more be born?
