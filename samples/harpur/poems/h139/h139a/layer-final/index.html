The Forgotten.

He shone in the senate, the camp, and the grove, The minion of Glory, the darling of Love! He fought for his Country—the star of the brave! He died for high Freedom—the hope of the slave! And Wisdom and Valor long over him wept; And Beauty, for ages, strewed flowers where he slept; And the bards of his People inwrought with their lays The light of his story, and truth of his praise. But in Time’s dark backward have faded their strains, And now of his being what record remains?— Within yon lone valley a Tomb crumbles fast, The name of whose Sleeper is lost in the Past.x

x While transcribing these lines from memory, I had some dim recollection of their having been suggested by a 35 a stabilsing effusion of feeling and imagination over the Outward; the other, a concentration of all external things, past and present, upon the Inward: and that this address to the Echo is peculiarly of the latter species, as the poem immediately preceding it is of the former.— If I might illustrate this difference by references to the poetry of others, I would say, that Shelley’s classes mostly with the first kind, and that Byron’s comes chiefly under the head of the last; while that of Milton and Keats is mainly a blending a both. The ideal complexion of the one species is green and golden, of the other (or latter) an intense but cloudy azure; while their blended effect has its semblance, or outward type, in that of the green hills and blue waters of the earth, when canopied with the morning or evening radiance of the heavens.
