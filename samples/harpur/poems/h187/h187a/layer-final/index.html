IMPROMTU.

“I have been elected the successor of the greatest man who ever trod this country.” “You have made me by your votes the most distinguished (i.e. greatest) commoner in the land.”

Vide Mr. Parkes’ closing address from the Hustings.

I like, you friends Parkes, but don’t like the morality Of your notion of greatness, so bloated and crude; Setting down what we can to the score of guarrulity, Leaves enough in’t to set you with thinkers at feud.

Such a crotchet. I fear, has made many a Nero; Of all that’s despotic, I know, ’tis the seed: And would make the great Czar, not a Spoiler, but Hero,— And the devil a very great fellow indeed.

Charles Harpur.

Note—The great defect of Mr. Parkes’ intellect is its tendency to regard human power and greatness as being identical. This, however, is utterly erroneous: the one may be, and very often is, wholly adventitious; the other must be purely inherent. But with him, Mr. Wentworth, for instance, (if he succeeds) is powerful enough to brand his country with a Constitution cal- culated to damn it for half a century, or at least up to the advent of a Revolution, and is, consequently, a great man. Or, (from whatever causes) no other individual in the country is powerful enough to do this, and therefore he must be the greatest individual in it. Considered in connection with many other of his publicly expressed opinions of Mr. Wentworth, this is absolutely the spirit of what he says above. Why, then, not go a step farther, and assert that material prosperity is a necessary element in the condi- tion of greatness? Nay, he almost does so, when he tells the electors that they have made him the most distinguished, or greatest, commoner in the land. Now this is fulsome—perfectly fulsome; false in feeling, and false in philosophy. His election by them has not made him a whit greater than he either was, or had it in him to be, before that event: greater, I say, for it is silly, and vulgar as it is silly, to boast of any distinction apart from what is essentially worthy of it. At all events, all the greatness thence accruing to him, is but an Attwood of Birmingham sort of greatness—a fizz, a bit of a blaze, and then nothing. To dis- tinguish a fool, or a rogue, or a coward, is only to brand him like a bullock. To elevate an ordinary man is only to write mediocrity more plainly on his forehead, and give perhaps, to history the trouble of recording one more worthless name, with a perfect consciousness of its worthlessness. In fine, Mr. Parkes’ idea of greatness (up to this time) is evidently too much limited to con- temporary influence and action, apart from the nature and per- manency of results.
