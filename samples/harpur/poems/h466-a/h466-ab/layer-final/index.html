Pope.

His Poetic Genius.

Refinement’s Bard! my study when a boy— The limped source of many a flowing joy.

What though some moderns, mystically sage In metaphisics, and whose critic rage Is drawn, I fear me, from the Nightmare’s dugs, Or from the crude paps of such wonder-bugs As troubled Coleridge in his flightiest days, Or Wordsworth, when he wrote his lyric lays— What though, thus crudely literate, these refuse His soul the deeper insights of the Muse, And granting learning and a wit refined, Deny him native amplitude of mind? To such I answer: That as hills appear Less looming in a lightsome atmosphere, Than in a hazy one, even so a thought, However reaching, that is clearly brought Within our vision will not seem high wrought: 302 And (to be plainer yet) as to the eyes Perfect proportion ever softens size, The Bards conceptions will not some immense Where all is ease, propriety, and sense, Even though they be;—and that the present case Is strictly thus, all see who have the grace. But they, with the wild German mania bit, Take big disorder for the prime of wit, And barbarised, with Gothic tongues abuse The classic structures of a chastened Muse.

What though his manner may be deftly spann’d By any verseman with a cunning hand— What though mere Pendantry by moiling hard In verbal joinery, may seem a Bard, Under its test?—this only, to my mind, Approves its formal finish in its kind.

But let his critics cavil as they may Pope will remain a princely Poet aye! Even though his lays, as models, are not such As Keats-like spirits should be bound by much; Nor yet his modes of thought the proper guage Wherewith to sound the depths of Wordsworth’s page; 303 Or test the fancy voluble and free Of Hunt, or Coleridge’s weird ecstasy.
