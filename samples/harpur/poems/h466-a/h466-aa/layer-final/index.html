VI.

Pope.

[His poetical Genius.]

Refinement’s Bard! my study when a boy— The healthful source of many a polished joy! What though some moderns mystically sage In Metaphysics, who, their critic rage Imbibe, I fear me, from the Nighmare’s dugs,— And thence are sticklers for such wonder-bugs As troubled Coleridge in his flightiest days, And Wordsworth when he wrote his “lyric” lays;— Though such, thus crudely literate, refuse His soul the deeper insights of the Muse; And granting learning and a taste refined, Deny him native amplitude of mind: To such I answer—That, as to the eyes 63a Perfect proportion ever softens size, The Bard’s conceptions will not seem immense Where all is ease, propriety, and sense, Though such in fact;—and that the present case Is strictly thus, all see who have the grace. But they, with the wild German mania bit, Take big disorder for the prime of wit, And barbarised, with Gothic tongues abuse The classic structures of a judging Muse.

But let critics cavil till their tired— 63e Pope was a Bard with genius fervour fired 63c Even though this poems be not models such As Keats-like spirits should be bound by much; Nor yet his style of thought the proper guage Wherewith to sound a Wordsworth’s nervous rage, Or test the fancy, voluble and free, Of Hunt,—or Coleridge’s wierd ecstasy.
