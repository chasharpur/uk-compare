II.

Shakespeare

Standing alone, a study in itself, How Shakespeare’s Volume glorifies my shelf! For thence his spirit forth on mine hath shined Like a great morning on the hills of mind.

Sphered in the light of his creative powers, A world of wonder shadowing this of ours Gathers around us, like the peopled haze That orbs some wanderer in a dream’s weird ways. Lean fatal Hags ride in the troubled air, And wing’d Immortals meet us everywhere: These of a silken loveliness that shows Like the dim beauty of a moon-lit rose, As firmly lined as sculptured iron, those. Lo, now Futurity uplifts her veil And pours her Phantom Kings before the tyrant pale! Now in the moon’s quick glimpses gleaming cold A steel-clad Monarch’s spectral form behold, Whilst o’er, as sounding from oblivion’s coast, Comes the deep speech of the unquiet ghost! 274 Turn we a page—Oh, for some power to save The meek mad Maiden from her timeless grave! “Sweets to the sweet!” with the sad Queen, we groan, As o’er her shroud the gathered flowers are thrown, Or think how wildly ev’n the best may die, And dash the sacred tear drop from our eye!

But seek we surer knowledge, paven hard With ethics such as time-schooled minds regard, Or such as, breathing the soul’s fervour, primes Our piety, and our moral faith sublimes? How many a noble page is shared between Wild wit, love, prudence in its sagest mien, And that high wisdom which informs us still Heaven “shapes our ends, rough-hew them how we will”; And that, though vain and erring, Human Nature Is yet a Soul of most angelic feature; Material—ethereal—each, yet both; A body built of clay;—a mind of Godlike growth!

Thus constantly some vital moral shines, Like sunlight, in the current of his lines. Ambition’s worshipper in Cæsar’s death May see how mortal is mere glory’s breath, 275 And learn from Richard’s spectre-haunted hour To dread the ghastliness of Godless power. What sensualist, while thunders the loud page With falling Antony’s majestic rage, But must, with profit to himself, deplore The fatal witcheries of the regal Whore? What princely spendthrift, seeing Timon’s end Shall yet not doubt the too—too flattering friend; Yet curse not, as indignantly he starts, The heartlessness of fashionable hearts; Thence to be prudent, and (though rich) the sure And generous helper of the virtuous poor? What magisterial letcher but shall fear, Through Angelo, to be less righteous than severe? What husband viewing dark Othello’s deed, But shall with caution, at the worst, proceed? Even Shylock’s spite must show, how, soon or late, All scorn imperils in begetting hate— Hate too self-sanctioned or to fear or pause, And raised,—yea, half enobled by its cause! The sire may learn to shun the rival pride To point whose moral gentle Juliett died; The child be chastened by the filial tear Shed for the wrongs that maddened royal Lear: When in scenic agony we find Distempered matter and distempered mind, + Brought as of face to face:—a Tempest, and Grief Blacker than midnight, and past all relief! While, still to swell the tragic terror,—hark! How rage and rumble through the hollow dark Nature’s wild roar and the yet wilder speech Of mightiest human woe—each storming into each!

Now with desire of laughter are we bit? Let Falstaff be our boon companion fit, And wrap us in a hurricane of wit! If fierce our mood—another leaf will yield Meet rage—the tumult of a battle field; With all its wrack of passion let at large, The gathering huddle—the close-thundering charge— The death-shriek drowning in the exultant shout Of victory, flooding like a deluge out!

But hating scenes of violence and blood, Would we to Innocence devote our mood? Behold, how spotless from this world of guile Is she who waves to yon Magic Isle, Miranda,—lovely even to Caliban, That hag-born, lump-faced mockery of Man! x (So crudely, self-betrayingly, astute— The half-hatched human egg-bound in the brute) With injured Virtue would we mingle tears? Lo, Katharine, or Hermiöné, appears. Would we condole with lonely love? Oh, then, Behold a mortal angel—Imogen! With joyous Goodness thirst we to rejoice? Belmont is vocal with its Portia’s voice. Would we be spiced with Lady-wit? One Kiss Of bright, frank, bee-like stinging Beätrice, 277 (But sweet as sharp)—have we the grace to snatch it, Shall make us Benedicts: and lo, we catch it! Comes o’er our hearts, as over the harp’s strings Walks music, full of dim memorial things, The thought of once loved groves, whence rise amain Memories of pleasures shadowing into Pain! In woody Arden then let’s wander wild With buoyant Rosalind and Celia mild; Or with the melancholy Jaques complain How blind is Fortune, yet how worthless Gain— Gain or of gold or glory—both a jest! Or power,—a solemn mockery at best! Then roam we on in thought, to join afar Those princely revellers in green Navarre, Taking for joy’s completeness in our way The shepherd-feast that glads Bohemià, To talk with Perdita of flowers, from whose Rich warbled words, as from a shower of dews, They borrow fresher scents and still diviner hues! Would we be so laced with a song? Sweet lays That breathe the innocence of ancient days, Like drops of liquid gold, all through and through The glorious volume sparkle into view.

How oft, thus musing, while the parting day Went through the West’s engoldened gates away, By some wild stream in Austral woods apart, I’ve fondly said, as to my listening heart, “While sweetest Shakespeare, Fancy’s (darling) child, Warbles (for me) his native wood-notes wild” Let others strive for wealth and power, and dwell In costly mansions—I can spare them will.

Note.

The most racy characteristic of Shakespeare’s writings (regarded as Poetry), is their wealth in beautiful metaphor and short and striking similitudes, derived, for the most part, and always so when happiest, from the spontanious productions of positive Nature. His genius, in these respects, has all the freshness and originality of a North American Indian’s oration. Not more prodigally does the hand of Aurora “sow the earth with orient pearl”, than he has showered imperishable lustres of happy comparison over every page of his glorious Book. They sparkle up on all hands like the beads upon champagne.

For example:—Is the popular munificence 279 of Antony’s rivels the subject? Then are we told that

His delights Were dolphin-like, and showed themselves above The element they lived in.

Is it necessary for the purpose of Iachimo that the fair Imogen should have some indelible though secret mark upon her chast and lovely person? Then what a delicate expedient is at once hit upon, and what a redolency of the Spring-time is there in the information which conveys it:

On her left breast A mole cinque-spotted, like the crimson drops In the bottom of a cowslip.

Does Viola conceal her love till it becomes her burthen, and preys, through the tender freshness of her beauty, upon her very life itself?

She lets concealment, like a worm in the bud Feed on her damask cheek.

Had Shakespeare ransacked all nature for the need 280 of the occasion, could she have yielded him anything more apposite than that? I have never elsewhere met with any similitude so happy in its brevity, or so perfect in its happiness.

In another vein of this most characteristic quality of Shakespeare’s poetical genius, to bepraise or illustrate the highest loveliness, or the highest worth, or whatever for the occasion is to be held unparagoned, is said to be needless as

To paint the lily, To throw a perfume o’er the violet.

And in the same spirit, the effect of impassioned music upon the pensive soul of love, is declared to haveits similitude in

The breath of the warm South That bloweth o’er a bed of violets, Stealing and giving odour.

Stealing and giving odour! Was ever the fervent spirit of music, with the agreeing sympathy of all beautiful passion, clothed in a material type more fresh and exquisite, and of a simplicity at once so natural and so lovely! 281 The habitude of sorrow unspeakable, and therefore apparently patient, has often been a subject with poets. The wide world of imagination has been searched for the most striking illustrations, and yet it has never been adequately figured by any but Shakespeare. He, by simply following his original instinct, has hit at once into the very heart of it, and in his most characteristic manner—that is, by means of a happy natural similitude. He is speaking of the grief of Hamlet:—

Anon, as patient as the female dove, Ere that her golden couplet is disclosed, His silence will sit drooping.

How exquisite, and in what elegant taste, is that characterisation of a dove’s newly hatched pair of yellow younglings—her golden couplet! And yet Shakespeare by the French (and Frenchified) critics, has been called the barbarian!

A hundred other examples, equally beautiful and apt, of this excellent bent in the genius of our Poet, are glowing up in my memory like a disclouding cluster of stars. But I must confine my remarks to two or three of the most peculiar of these. The following is a pure, though a prolonged, or rather doubled, 282 metaphor, and peculiarly Shakesperian in its build and quality. It is spoken by Macbeth on the eve of Duncan’s murder, and is expressive of criminally disposed, yet irresolute ambition:—

I have no spur Wherewith to prick the sides of my intent, but only Vaulting ambition, which o’erleaps itself And falls on the other (side.)

That which follows is a sort of mixed metaphor— that is, it partakes of the nature both of a metaphor and a figure after a manner primarily Shakespeare’s. The endangered chastity of the sleeping Lucretia is made to represent her whole being for the occasion, thereby to enhance its importance, and at the same time intensify our sense of the outrage which is impending over it. The effect is very striking.

Tarquin thus Did softly press the rushes, ere he wakened The Chastity he wounded.

There is yet another kind of mixed metaphor 283 which is very prevalent in Shakespeare’s poetry, and which is employed with so supreme a mastery by no other Poet. I mean a species of similitude which, commencing in a metaphor, is afterwards elaborated into a kind of short allegory. There is a fine example in one of the speeches of Wolsey. Observe what a perfect progression—or rather, what a nicely-linked sequency there is in the order and drift of the circumstances; and then the terse plenitude of every expression:

This is the state of Man:—to day hĕ pūts fōrth The tender leaves of hope; to morrow, blossoms, And bears his blushing honors thick upon him: The third day comes a frost,—a killing frost! And when he thinks, good easy man, full surely His greatness is a ripening,—nips his root, And then he falls, as I do.

I have made special mention in the text of Shakespeare’s songs, which are often models of lyrical effusion: not so much in their mere form and execution, as in their spontaneity of spirit, artless bird-like expression, and consequent entireness of effect. They are, in fact, in their origin and design, just what songs should be—not so much the music of 284 thought, as of the heart, by itself, “warbling wood- -notes wild”: and warbled by the heart thus self-secluded, less for the improvement of time, than simply to beguile it.
