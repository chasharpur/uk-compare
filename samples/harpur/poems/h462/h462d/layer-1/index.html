No II.

Shakspere.

Standing alone, a study in itself, How Shakspere’s Volume glorifies my shelf! For thence his spirit forth on mine has shined Like a great morning on the hills of mind.

Sphered in the light of his creative powers, A world of wonder, shadowing this of ours, Gathers around us like the peopled haze That orbs some wanderer in a dream’s weird ways. Lean fatal Hags ride in the troubled air, And wing’d Immortals meet us everywhere: These of a silken loveliness that shows Like the dim beauty of moon-lit rose, As firmly lined as scultpured iron, those. Lo, now Futurity uplifts her veil And pours her Phanton Kings before the tyrant pale! Now in the moon’s quick glimpses gleaming cold A steel-clad Monarch’s spectral form behold; Whilst o’er, as sounding from Oblivion’s coast, Comes the deep speech of the unquiet Ghost! Turn we a page—O for some power to save That meek mad Maiden from her timeless grave! “Sweets to the sweet”, with the sad Queen we groan, As o’er shroud the gathered flowers are thrown; And seeing how wild a death the best may die, We dash the sacred tear drops from our eye!

But seek we surer knowledge, paven hard With ethics such as time-wise minds regard, Or such as, breathing the soul’s fervour, primes Our piety, and our moral faith sublimes? How many a noble page is shared between Wild wit, love, prudence in its sagest mien, And that high wisdom which informs us still Heaven “shapes our ends rough-hew them how we will;” And that, though vain and erring, Human Nature Is yet a Spirit of most angelic feature: Material, yet ethereal—each in both,— A Body built of clay; a Mind of Godlike reach!

Thus constantly some vital moral shines, Like sunlight, in the current of his lines. Ambition’s worshipper in Cæsar’s death May see how mortal is mere glory’s breath, And learn from Richard’s spectre-haunted hour To dread the ghastliness of Godless power. What sensualist, while thunder’s the loud page With falling Antony’s majestic rage, But must, with profit to himself, deplore The fatal witcheries of the regal whore? What princely spendthrift, seeing Timon’s fall, Shall yet not doubt the too—too flattering friend; Yet curse not, as indignantly he starts, The heartlessness of fashionable hearts; Thence to be prudent, and though rich, the sure And generous helper of the virtuous poor? What magisterial letcher but shall fear, Through Angelo, to be “less righteous than severe”? What husband viewing dark Othello’s deed, But shall with caution, at the worst, proceed? 254 292 Even Shylock’s spite must show, how, soon or late, All scorn imperils in begetting hate— Hate too self-scantioned or to fear or pause, And raised—yea, half enobled by its cause! The sire may learn to shun the rival pride To point whose moral fervent Juliett died The child be chastened by the filial tear Shed for the wrongs that maddened royal Lear: Where, in the scenic agony, we find Distempered matter and distempered mind Brought as if face to face;—Tempest, and Grief Blacker than midnight, and passed all relief! While still to swell the tragic terror—hark! How rage and rumble through the hollow dark Nature’s wild roar, and the yet wilder speech Of mightiest human woe—each storming into each!

Now with desire of laughter are we bit? Let Falstaff be our boon companion fit, And wrap us in a hurricane of wit! If fierce our mood—another leaf will yield Meet rage,—the tumult of a battle field, With all its wrack of passion let at large, The gathering huddle—the close-thundering charge— The death-shrieks drowning in the exultant shout Of victory, flooding, like a deluge, out!

But hating scenes of violence and blood, Would we to Innocence devote our mood? Behold, how spotless from this world of guile Is she who waves us to yon Magic Isle, Miranda,—lovely even to Caliban, That hag-born, lump-faced mockery of Man! 293 (So crudely, self-betrayingly astute— The human as yet egg-bound in the brute.) With injured Virtue would we mingle tears? Lo, Katherine, or Hermiöné, appears. Would we condole with lonely Love? O then, Behold a mortal angel—Imogen! With joyous Goodness thirst we to rejoice? Belmont is vocal with its Portia’s voice. Would we be spiced with lady-wit? One kiss, In fancy, from the bee-like Beätrice, (Stinging, yet sweet) have we the grace to snatch it, Shall make us Benedicts—and lo, we catch it! Comes o’er our hearts, as over the harp’s strings Walks music, full of dim memorial things, The thought of once-loved groves, whence rise amain Memories of pleasures shadowing into Pain? In woody Arden then let’s wander wild With buoyant Rosalind and Celia mild; Or with the melancholy Jaques complain How blind is Fortune, yet how worthless Gain— Gain or of gold or glory—both a jest! Or power—a solemn mockery at best! Then roam we on in thought, to join afar Those princely revellers in green Navarre, Taking for joy’s completeness in our way The shepherd-feast that glads Bohemiā, To talk with Perdita of flowers, from whose Rich-warbled words, as from a shower of dews, They borrow fresher scents and still diviner hues! Would we be solaced with a song? Sweet lays That breathe the innocence of ancient days, Like drops of liquid gold, all through and through The glorious volume sparkle into view.

How oft, thus musing, while the parting day Went through the West’s engoldened gates away, By some wild stream in Austral woods apart, I’ve fondly said, as to my listening heart, “While sweetest Shakspere, Fancy’s (darling) child, Warbles (for me) his native wood-notes wild,” Let others strive for wealth and power, and dwell In costly mansions—I can spare them well.

Note.

The most racy characteristic of Shakspere’s writings (regarded as Poetry), is their wealth in beautiful metaphor and short and striking similitudes, derived for the most part, and always so when happiest, from the spontaneous productions of positive Nature. His genius, in these respects, has all the freshness and originality of a North American Indian’s oration. Not more prodigally does the hand of Aurora “sow the earth with orient pearl”, than he has showered imperishable lustres of happy comparison over every page of his glorious Book. Or they might be said to sparkle up in it, on all hands, like the beads upon champagne.
