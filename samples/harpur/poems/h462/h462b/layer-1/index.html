II.

Shakspeare.

Standing alone, a study in itself, How Shakspeare’s Volume glorifies my shelf! Sphered in the light of his creative mind, A world of wonder, shadowing this, we find. Lean fatal Hags ride posting on the blast, And wing’d Immortals throng around us fast; 53 These of a silken loveliness that glows Like the dim beauty of a moonlit rose, As firmly lined as sculptured iron those. Lo, now futurity uplifts her veil, And pours her Phantom Kings before the tyrant pale! Now in the moon’s quick glimpses gleaming cold, A shadowy warrior’s stalking Form behold! Whilst o’er, like echoes from oblivion’s coast, Comes the deep speech of the unquiet Ghost. Turn we a page—O for a power to save The meek mad Beauty from her timeless grave! “Sweets to the sweet”, with the sad Queen we cry, And dash the sacred tear-drop from our eye.

But seek we surer knowledge, paven hard With such stern precepts as grey minds regard; Or such as breathing the soul’s fervour, primes Our piety, and our moral faith sublimes; How many a thoughtful page is shared between Fancy, and prudence in her sagest mien, And that high wisdom which informs us still, Heaven “shapes our ends rough-hew them how we will;” And that, though vain and erring, Human Nature Is yet a mystery of angelic feature, And godlike in its spiritual stature.

Thus constantly some vital Moral shines Like sunlight, in the current of his lines. Ambition’s worshipper, in Cæsar’s death, May see how mortal is mere glory’s breath; And learn from Richard’s spectre-haunted hour, To hate the hideousness of godless power. What sensualists, while thunders loud the page With falling Antony’s majestic rage, 54 But must, with profit to himself, deplore The fa[t]al witcheries of the regal whore! What wealthy prodigal from Timon’s fall Shall aim not to escape the flatterer’s thrall? Nor scorn, as then indignantly he starts, The heartlessness of fashionable hearts? What magisterial tyrant but shall fear, Through Angelo, to be less righteous than severe? What husband viewing dark Othello’s deed, But shall with caution, at the worst, proceed? The sire may learn to shun the rivalous hate That consummated gentle Juliett’s fate; The child be chastened by the filial tear Shed for the wrongs that maddened royal Lear.

Now with desire of laughter are we bit? Let’s roar with Falstaff in a storm of wit. If fierce our mood,—another leaf will yield Meet rage, the thunders of a battle field! To innocence would we devote awhile? Miranda beckons from her magic isle. With injured virtue would we mingle tears? A Katharine, or Hermioné appears. Would we condole with lonely love? O then, Behold,—a mortal angel,—Imogen. With joyous goodness thirst we to rejoice? Belmont is vocal with its Portia’s voice. Would we be spiced with lady-wit? I wis One bee-like stinging, yet most honeyed kiss Of bright frank Beatrice, if we’ve grace to snatch it, Shall make us Benedicts—and lo, we catch it! Comes o’er us, like a long remembered strain, The love of groves we ne’er may know again? In shady Arden then let’s wander wild With buoyant Rosalind and Celia mild; 55 Or with the melancholy Jaques complain, How blind is Fortune, yet how worthless Gain;— Gain or of gold or glory—both a jest! Or power—a solemn mockery at best! Then wander on in thought, to join afar Those princely revellers in green Navarre; Taking, for joy’s completeness, in our way The shepherd-feast that glads Bohemiá, To talk with Perdita of flowers,—from whose Rich warbled words, as from a shower of dews, They borrow fresher scents and still diviner hues. Would we be solaced with a song? Sweet lays That breathe the innocence of ancient days, Like drops of gold and gems of rarest hue, Are interspersed the glorious Volume through.

How oft, when wandering in the woods away, As goldenly around me closed the day, And musing thus upon each Scene I’d read— How often to my listening heart I’ve said: While “sweetest Shakspeare, Fancy’s (darling) child, Warbles (for me) his native woodnotes wild,” Let others strive for wealth and power, and dwell In costly mansions—I can spare them well. x

x For more than a year of my youth, Shakspeare was nearly my whole reading—was, in fact (and luckily) almost the only book my means could at that time command. At first I perused him rapidly right through, in order to take in at one mighty draught his entire spirit. Then I repe- -rused him with minute attention,—that is, critically; un- -derlining every beauty I could perceive, even to a felicity of phrase. I next reconsidered the whole, transcribing as I proceeded, almost all that I had previously underlined. 56 After this, I again read him attentively through, with an eye to the punctuation alone; and yet again the sole purpose of testing the progressive steps in his versification. From this last reading, if I recollect aright, I came to the conclusions, that all his British historical Plays, with the exceptions of Henry VIII., Cymbeline, and Macbeth, were amongst his earliest productions; and that his Roman Plays, Hamlet and Othello, with all his Comedies, except the Comedy of Errors, the Two Gentlemen of Verona, Romeo and Juliet, and another or two, ranged subsequently, with the Tempest last; in which his blank is quite as interflowing as Milton’s, though, of course, somewhat looser and less measured.

Of the style of these Critical Rhymes, I will remark here, once for all, that though I have avoided in them the pre- -cise couplet balance of Pope, as a mere melleflous monotony, I have yet considered that something of such a balance should be maintained, upon the whole, in our regular heroic rhyme; believing moreover, that a certain blank-verse-rhymed style, (if I may use coin the expression) now much in vogue, is just such a failure as might be expected to follow from the meeting of extremes, however strikingly it may indicate (as it does) the prior existence of an opposite abuse.
