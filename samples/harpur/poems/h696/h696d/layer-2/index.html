But the Divine Intelligence conceives and interpenetrates these starry systems of the Universe, at must at the same time be external to and above them: as a human intelligence not only gives birth and fashion to its own peculiar thoughts, but is also independently existent. They are not it, though they are of it. In other words, though they proceed from it, as spectators, they do not constitute it.

And as the mind of man has power to comprehend these presented forms and systems of the Universe, which cannot comprehend themselves, it must be something which in its nature is different from them—must, in fact, be an offspring having absolute kindred with the Divine Mind itself, however much lower in degree the Infinite in relation to the finite may declare it to be. Though dependent, then, upon the Deity, as its almighty Parent, it has yet a Self,—a spiritually dictinct iden- tity, and is therefore individually immortal.

On the other hand, the material or bodily things of the Universe are purely attributive to the mind of God, as the images of its thoughts; and thus proceeding, however progressively mutable within themselves, they must yet be eternal. ※ In other words, whatever an eternal mind has once conceived, must in virtue of its origin, be likewise eternal, however it may be subsequently transformed within itself or tempered in substance or elaborated in aspect. 🖙 But of course I do not mean that they are eternal in the same sense that God himself, their author, is eternal. He in being must always have been: they by being, must always be, even though concealable at his pleasure from the peoples of his creation. Our thoughts, in their finite degree, are likewise enduring, though likewise subject to progressive mutation. o To God they are everduring. It is the media, the arbitrary symbols through which the human mind expresses its conceptions that alone are perishable /so to speak./ Were these equally real itself, all our manifested thoughts would continue as glowingly palpable as the stars at night: since wherever it—that is, wherever mind was there would they be Nay, this may be said to be almost the fact as it is: I mean in those cases in which, from their sympathetic mastery as the fruits of Genius, they are committed over and over again to some perpetuating channel of communication such as the Press. In reading Shakespear, for example, can we for a moment confound his glorious thoughts with the mere print and paper of his book? True, these are instinct with them,—are the media of their 584 revelation, but they themselves exist by virtue of a vested spirituality in union with Truth, as the eminations of an immortal intelligence, itself the personal offspring of an eternal one—the intelligence of God.
