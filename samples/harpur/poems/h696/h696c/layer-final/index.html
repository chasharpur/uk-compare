POEMS BY CHARLES HARPUR.

I.

COSMOPLASTICUS.

From the crude records which mysterious Time Hath graven on the mountains’ rigid ribs, And sea shores wild, and watery chasms dim, And in the deep-down bowels of the Earth, We learn that she, at dateless intervals, Hath been the plastic and predestined mould Of awfullest changes. Knotted reptile forms, Stolid, but terrible through multitude, Have crowded all her watery disc, like worms Clothing a corpse; or beings unto which The monstrous serpent of the Indian waste Were but a minim, in her miry lap Have wallowed, snorting in the ponderous joy Of life enormous: but to perish, when Her altering surface grew the meet abode For things of greater beauty:—Whether wrought Into such meetness by fixed laws of change, For ever actively distributing Their forces through all time; or by the sweep Of sudden cataclysms, wild and vast; Or by the rage of subterraneous fires, Instant and universal. Lastly, Man, And all the world of creatures over which He holds dominion absolute, with forms Of comelier mould, and inwardly endued With spirits and faculties to nicer ends Nicely adapted, gave her tempered Orb That heightened loveliness we now behold.

Then on the hills the hunter’s voice went forth, And in the valleys the glad chaunt of birds Mixed sweetly with the gurgle of pleasant streams And sway of bloomy woods; and echoes wild, Housed in the hollow rock or twilight dell, Made merry with the happy noise that came, Mellowed by distance, out of lowing glens And bleating pastures, belting here and there Some low-roofed dwelling of an early world, And field of rustling corn: till Nature taught Art Music, and bright Poesy arose Out of the dayspring like a morning star Upon the awakened spirit of delight, And Form idealised in hues, or wrought From the rough rock into a life that spake The language of immutable loveliness, Adorned the abodes of Learning;—sister Arts, Three stster Arts in fellowship divine— A triune glory of exalted soul.

But dare we say the awful Law of Change, From good to better still, hath here an end? That Earth is Man’s for ever?—his and theirs, The creatures over which he reigns predominant, As king, his only kingship? No: the Hours That shall behold her the prepared abode For new successions in the scheme of life, May even now (like a long flight of storks Far looming into view) be winging up, Amid the clouds of the eternal future, Their dim but destined way.

But whence, ’tis asked, Might spring these novel Orders? From the womb Prolific of our mother Earth, wherein, Even from the first, their causes shall have slept In pregnant preparation, waiting there The evoking word of God,—expressed through laws Which are, in their designed results, the sure Expressions of His will: or rather, they Were but progressive changes in the sum And garniture of that divine Idea, Whereof the Earth’s so solid seeming bulk Is, with its bodily populations all, The outward shape in manifold reflection, Ever fulfilling, more and more. its part In the pre-ordered harmony of the world: Even as a human thought (so far as what Is infinite, and imperfect therefore, may With Infinite compare)—as knowledge grows Before it, and combines all relative things— A necessary progress undergoes In its accruing unity with Truth.

But come what changes may, yet in the Soul, As individualised in all mankind, Beyond the swoop of accident, and throned High o’er the whirl of matter. She, complete In her sole being, evermore aspires, A missioned Liberty with power to break All bondage. She, a Virtue purer far Than that invisible etherial fire,— The vital spirit of the world,—that breathes Through Nature’s boundless lungs, can neither know Decadence nor corruption. Thus, a spark From God’s internal brightness, goes she forth All deathless, and to clothe for evermore Her mighty life and wondrous faculties In the so fertile beauty of the Earth, And other such communicated modes Of his star-shining Thoughts;—each thought a link Of heavenly gold in one eternal chain Of progress—to Perfection. Here we rest.
