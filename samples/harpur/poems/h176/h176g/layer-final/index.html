The Honey Bird.

[The native bird thus called, or more commonly, the honey-sucker, is quit a curiosity in natural history. Its bill is a mere tube, about as large in girth as a darning needle, and nearly as long. It is, in fact, nearly equal in length to all the rest of the bird’s body, the tail (as in the case of all fowls with very long bills) being short and stumpy, like that of the kingfisher. In this tube there is a sort of natural pump-sucker, and the bird is thus enabled to come at the honey which iscontained in the deep cups of the mountain bugle and bell flowers. Hence the habitat of the honey-bird is always to be found in those localities (invariablymountainous) which give birth in profusion to flowers of the kinds above mentioned, and of others kinds akin to them. In such places only grow these deep-cupped flowers, and in such places only is there a tube-billed bee-bird to batten upon their sweetness; so cruriously adaptative in her principles is our wonderful mother, Nature.

And here, no doubt, the question will occur to some one: But having equipt this bird in a manner so peculiar, how does Nature manage to preserve its being during the winter season, when there are no flowers? In answer to this I have only a guess to offer—namely, that perhaps it becomes and lies dormant in cold frosty weather, for it certainly has not sufficient strength of wing for migration to any such distance as would reach to a climatic difference of temperature.

It should be added, that though the bill of the honey-sucker is a mere tube, the bird is not therefore wholly dumb, but contrives to blow through it, as it hops about amongst the flowers, a somewhat slender yet agreeable note.]

Wherever in some mountain bower There blooms a honey-yielding flower, There too dwells a Bird, to sup Out of its delicious cup, And sing at times, lest it should be O’er fed into satiety.

And so, wherever Loveliness Dwells secluded—dwells to bless, Not dazzle; there some destined Spirit Feeding on its lucious merit, Can at peace with passion be Only through glad Poetry.
