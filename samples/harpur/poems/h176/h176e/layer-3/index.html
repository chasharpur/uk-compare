XXX.

The Hony Bird.

[The native bird thus called, or, more commonly, the honysucker, is quite a curiosity in natural history. Its bill is a mere tube, about as large in girth as a darning needle, and nearly as long. It is, in fact, nearly equal in length to all the rest of the bird’s body, the tail (as in the case of all fowls with very long bills) being short and stumpy, like that of the kingfisher. In this tube there is a sort of natural pump-sucker, and the bird is thus enabled to come at the hony which is contained in the deep cups of the mountain bugle and bell flowers. Hence the habitat of the hony- sucker is always to be sought for in those localities (invariablymountainous) which give birth in profusion to flowers of the kinds above mentioned, and of otherkinds akin to them: so curiously adaptative in her provisions is mother Nature.

And here, no doubt, the question will occur to some one: But having equipt this bird in a manner so peculiar, how does Nature manage to preserve its being during the winter season, when there are no flowers? In answer to this, I have only a guess to offer—namely, that it becomes 99 and lies dormant in cold frosty weather, for it certainly has not sufficient strength of wing for migration to any such distance as would reach to a climatic difference.

It should be added, that though the bill of the hony-sucker is merely a tube, the bird is not therefore wholly dumb, but contrives to blow through it, as it hops about amongst the flowers, a somewhat slender yet agreeable note.]

Wherever in some wildwood bower There blooms a hony-yielding Flower, There too dwells a Bird, to sup Out of its delicious cup, And sing by times, lest it should be O’erfed into satiety:

And so, wherever Loveliness Dwells secluded—dwells to bless, Not dazzle; there some destined Spirit, Feeding on its luscious merit, Can at peace with Passion be Only through glad Poetry.
