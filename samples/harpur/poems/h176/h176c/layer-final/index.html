THE HONEY BIRD.*

BY CHARLES HARPUR.

Wherever in some wildwood bower There blooms a honey-yielding flower, There too dwells a Bird, to sup Out of its delicious cup, And sing by times, lest it should be O’erfed into satiety: So, wherever Loveliness Dwells retired,—dwells to bless, Not dazzle; there some destined Spirit, Feeding on its lucious merit, Can at peace with Passion be Only through sweet Poetry.

* The native bird thus called, or, more com- monly, the honeysucker, is quite a curiosity in natural history. Its bill is a mere tube, about as large in girth as a darning needle, and nearly as long. It is, in fact, nearly equal in length to all the rest of the bird’s body, the tail (as in the case of all fowls with very long bills) being short and stumpy, like that of the Kingfisher. In this tube there is a sort of natural pump-sucker, and the bird is thus enabled to come at the honey which is contained in the deep cups of the mountain bugle and bell flowers. Hence, the habitat of the honey- sucker is always to be sought for in those localities (invariably mountainous) which give birth in profusion to flowers of the kinds above mentioned, and of others akin to them: so curiously adapta- tive in her provisions is mother Nature.

It should be added, that though the bill of the honeysucker is merely a tube, the bird is not therefore wholly dumb, but contrives to blow through it, as it hops about amongst the flowers, a somewhat slender yet agreeable note.

C. H.
