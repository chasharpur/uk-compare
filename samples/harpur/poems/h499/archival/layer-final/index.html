II.

SHORT ETHICS PRODUCED.*

A new Bard sings ‘Rather be good than clever, Do noble things, not dream them, all day long, And so make life, death, and that vast for ever, One grand sweet song.’

Ay, let us all be good—But if not clever How shall we know we are good indeed? That witless virtue is the saintliest ever, Is Hundrum’s creed.

And we must dream of ‘noble things,’ and be Accustomed through our own souls so to view them Ranked, as the Good and Beautiful, or we Shall seldom do them!

Though by such things well mused, and thence well done, That lofty life-tone we may best attain, Which maketh Time and the Eternal—one Like flowing strain.

* I have not written the above from any mere wish to quarrel with Kingsley’s ethics, but simply because I have, myself, no abiding faith in any course of moral action which has not, in its kind at least, been mentally deliberated, or to vary the terms, dreamt of in soul. Neither do I believe in the constancy of any human virtue that comes not, habitually, out of some foregone train of thought and idealisation. The kindness, even, of mere instinct, or of the natural affections unraised by meditation, is not always goodness, for it often spoils and sometimes kills its darling; while that heroism of the blood, or of impulse backed by the will, which Emerson makes so much of, is quite as likely of itself to work for evil as for good. Be this, however, as it may, not the less is our own dream-evoked Ideal our proper Angel,—our Zeas, our life-long Inspirer; and if noble things are not the stars of its heaven, we shall be likely to do such things only as Lady Byron sometimes wrote good verses—that is, by chance. Moreover, we need leisure to be good. Hurry is the devil’s broom, and sweeps us into mischief. We need leisure to be good, and must therefore secure to it some quiet high-place in our lives—some Illimini of vision, private and unprofaned, and reaching far into the holy silence of the Infinite. And though we do little through our much dreaming, what matter, so long as we dream nobly? What we shall in that case do will more than compensate for all we leave undone. It will have more of the odour of soul in its quality than it would have had in the gross, and less of animal fume. It will also be rightlier aimed, and at higher objects. In- deed it might only be otherwise if we lived by bread alone. or had bodies merely, or could serve God and Mammon equally well at the same time; or if the first and last question to be asked of all effort, all doing, all success were Will it gild our walls and carpet our floors, perfect our cookery and sublime our drinks?—that is, in short, Will it coin sovereigns? C.H.
