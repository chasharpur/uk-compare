Floridness of Diction

A common brown Bush will better beguile Both the eye and the mind, Than a Rose Tree, designed In tinsal—that is, when we’ve seen it awhile

There is nothing deters us from a second perusal of any work, whether in prose or rhyme, like an extreme floridness of diction. We care not to refresh the unmittigated glare that it has left in our memory, or venture again into the super-sunny dazzle that has heretofore oppressed us. Like one who has jadded himself with wandering through the garish aisles of a christal palace, we would rather repose our “wearied virtue” amid the quiet of a common green field or under the familiar shelter of a shadowy hill. Hence who cares to read Gillfillan twice? or having done so, who is he that would not gladly take refuge from the effects of such a feat, in the comparative quietute of Goldsmith or Cowper, or amid the ungaudy mountain nooks and homely green pastures that impicture the pages of Wordsworth? Or who can read Atherstone’s Fall of Nineveh even once, without having almost a horror of the mere mention of “blazing diamonds” “flaming rubies” ‘burnished gold’ ‘purple robes’ and ’flashing chariots” for at least a week afterwards? Sotherby’s Saul is similarly objectionable; and the Lalla Rook of Moore almost intolerably so.
