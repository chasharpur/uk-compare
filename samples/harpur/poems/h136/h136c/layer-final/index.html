FLORIDNESS OF DICTION.

A real brown bush will better beguile Both the eye and the mind, Than a rose tree designed In tinsel——that is, when we’ve seen it awhile And fathomed the trick of its structure and style.

There is nothing deters us from a second perusal of any work, whether in prose or rhyme, like an extreme floridness of diction. We care not to refresh the un- mitigated glare that it has left in our memory, or ven- ture again into the supersunny dazzle that has hereto- fore oppressed us. Like one who has jaded himself by bustling through the garish aisles of a chrystal palace, we would rather repose our “wearied virtue” amid the quiet of a common green field, or under the familiar shelter of a shadowy hill. Hence who can read Gilfilian twice? or, having done so, who is he that would not gladly take refuge from the effects of such a feat, in the comparative quietude of Goldsmith or Cowper, or amid the ungaudy mountain nooks and homely green pastures that im- picture the pages of Wordsworth? Or who can read Atherstone’s Fall of Nineveh even once, without having almost an horror of the bare mention of “blazing diamonds,” “flaming rubies,” “burnished gold,” “purple robes,” and “flashing chariots” for at least a month afterwards?

C. H.
