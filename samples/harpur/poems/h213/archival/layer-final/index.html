THE LAMENT OF THE BARD.

(Suggested by the Story of Chatterton.)

BY C. HARPUR.

I.

I am weary,—I am weary Of all I loved of yore;— I nothing now can do but weep, And wish my day was o’er: For tho’ by nature high of heart, ‘Tis doom’d it shall but warm a slave; Ah! I would hide this aching head, For aye,—tho’ in a nameless grave.

II.

I am weary—I am weary Of the day, and of the night; They have swept to darkness, and to death, Each vision of delight: The day is but a charnel now, Where loving fancy shrouded lies; Thro’ the long night my spirit hears, Young hope in flight,—forsaking wise.

III.

I am weary—I am weary Of the songster, and the grove, Of the rising and the setting Sun— Of woman and of love. Sweet woman’s sigh—the warbler’s song— The murmuring breeze—the moonlit sea, But swell one haunting voice of doom, “This wide world owns no place for thee!”

IV.

I am weary—I am weary E’en of the gust of woe,— Of every decency of life— Of every friend and foe: For, ah! more lonely I were not Among the white-worms of the tomb, Than mentally I’ve ever felt, Within my very father’s home.

V.

Then I am weary—Oh, I’m weary Of all beneath the Sun! Doubly weary, weary, weary! Oh!—I would my day was done: For tho’ by nature high of heart, ‘Tis doom’d it shall but warm a slave! Then must I hide this aching head, For aye,—tho’ in a dastard’s grave.
