Ideality.

(Written in 1843.)

By Charles Harpur.

Spirit of Dreams! When many a wandered height Shut Paradise from exiled Adam’s sight, Two wedded Powers were given thenceforth to stray On either hand, companions of his way: This Hope was named in heaven whence he came, And that of Melancholy bore the name. Thy parents these—who clothed thee with a ray Snatched from Perfection as she passed away, And inwardly endued thee with the grace That once suffused unfallen Nature’s face.

Queen-Mother of Elysiums, and all fair Hesperian Gardens, hear thy suppliant’s prayer! Lend me the hues which from all splendors found In God’s great world, thou dost so well compound: From nameless flowers that wild in forests run, Up to the glories of the setting sun— From twinklings in the dew-bespangled sword, Up to the banded glows of rainbows broad— From those white flickers that round fountains leap, To the phosphoric lightnings of the deep— From wave-wet sands that glisten on the shore, To blazing diamonds and rich-beaming ore; And thence from their idéals, as combined And interblended with the light of Mind:— Those hues now lend thy suppliant, for he The brilliant steps would trace of thy whole ministry.

474

O for a golden lyre, whilst first I sing How thou dost lovelier make the loveliest thing! Woman is beautiful!—no more: until She stands as sanctioned by thy regal will! Than sweeter sweets her being at once invest Than breathe in gales of Araby the blest! A thousand bright particulars are given, And she outshines the very Moon of heaven! This light how glorious!—a God-woven robe Wrapping in living warmth the fruitful globe: Even so—and yet, thy thaumaturgic ray Additional brilliance poureth o’er the day! Lovely this prospect! Yet thy presence here Doubles each glory of the golden year! Breathes but thy influence o’er a pasture plain, And lo, ’tis flushed with Eden-glows again.

O he whose wild heart leaps to thy wild call, Hath yet some joy whatever may befall! Hath yet some wealth where Destitution reigns— Nay, even some inviolable gains Where Rapine sits enthroned, and Slavery clanks her chains! Still his possessions compass whatsoe’er Of good and beauteous Nature treasures there! Still the lone hill, wild vale, and pathless grove To him belongeth—each a mine of love! For him what gold is in the morning’s hues! What unbought jewels are the lucent dews! What regal mansions are the brook-side bowers! What gay assemblies the balm breathing flowers! Then every bird that pipes a matin lay Doth unto him authentic tribute pay: Yea, every grace of earth and air and sky Are vassals to his soveriegn ear and eye.

And when the busy coils of Mammon’s brood Fail like the voices of an ebbing flood, And from the scenes that glared with her display Pride-drunken Fashion rolls fatigued away; When from mild Evening’s shadowy robe the breeze Shakes cool delight and odorous messages; And when no longer the sun’s beams fire-crest The dusky hills, but round him, in the west, Are gathered, even like a summoned host In gorgeous tents on the horizon’s coast, Ere to the regions of a separate day He rolls at length in purple pomp away;— Whilst yet—some moments yet, withdrawing gleams Travel the air and die along the streams, That symphonise in many-sounding moods To vespers shrilling through the gloaming woods: A sumptuous festival that hour shall prove To bathe his chosen soul with harmony and love— Yea, fill it, like a fountain, with immense Content in the great God’s so great munificence!

When up the unclouded heaven, in starry sheen, Walks Night, like a gem-spangled Ethiope Queen, And from her solemn curtain wide unfurled Shakes dense repose upon the drowsy world, No sordid appetite—no passion mean— Chains down thy Votary to this mere Terrene; But, following thee, he freely soars afar From moon to planet, and from star to star! Yea, outward still, if venturous, he may trace Thy sounding flight through dimmest depths of space, Till from the boundless gaining boundless might, He feel his being alike infinite!

And now, though mournful be the current theme— A tale of hapless love—or faded dream Of human worth, whose spirits, once so bright, Like halcyons gemm’d the Rivers of Delight, But fled Misfortune’s wintry floods before, Never to show their shining plumage more! Still thou, thy hope-tired Follower ever near, Sublim’st each sigh, and hallow’st every tear, Till ev’n despair, in his high toned and free Expression, half ethereal grows to be, And deepest anguish mounts to highest luxury! Nor rests with him thy influence alone, Men love him for the sorrows he hath known And sung so sweetly—for the measured moan Of his complaint, unlike the worlding’s care, Is pleasing—yea, is like a plaintive air Heard mourning across waters wide and lone, When in them all heaven’s golden isles are sown— A time of starry peace, when even Grief Becomes itself the path to its own best relief.

Through all things thus—through pleasure and through pain— Thou stead’st thy Follower with some special gain. Doth Danger threaten doth Terror scream? O’er their wild fronts a reconciling gleam Thy wings reflect! The storm that rends the sky And ploughs the ocean-deeps, if thou art nigh, Gives to the heart like strength and lifts the soul on high! In Madness, ev’n, an eloquence thou canst find That fortifies while it afflicts the mind! Canst win from lone Remorse such pleas as well May make even Vengeance sternly placable! Throw a fierce loveliness o’er Ruin’s face, And over Death’s calm brow an enviable grace!Nay 477 Nay, be thy glance but turned where devils dwell, And a sad glory lightens out of Hell!

Spirit of Dreams! it yet remains to say How thou hast brightened my peculiar way.— When most beset by troubles stern and new— When foes most triumphed—friends were most untrue, How much through thee of sweetness and of worth Was with me still, where’er I wandered forth? Then as I breasted full the sounding gale On some lone hill—or, melancholy-pale, Hid me adown some seldom trodden vale, Brown with primeval trees that solemn grew By winding waters struggling into view, My soul’s distractions thou hast all subdued Through dreams of Love and Liberty and Good.

Now travelled far—companionless, and bowed Wearily down on the rock-roughened road— Thee in that desert meeting, I have found Baronial halls in the hoar caverns round, And wrapt in high chivalric vision, there Have bearded recreant knights and succored ladies fair!

Anon, slow wending over fertile plains, Where lay some worldling’s ill-deserved domains, At Fortune’s blindness I have much repined, In heart defiant and faith-hurt in mind: Till ‘neath thy brooding wings out o’er me spread, A new soul-born content hath mildly smiling said: “What though the soil another’s right may be, Not less its vernal beauty lives for me! The bright streams mirrors me with heaven above— The soft airs greet me with a kiss of love— 478 The flowers laugh round me in their sweet delight— Birds shower down gladness in their passing flight;— And while these shine abroad, and freshly grow, And purely breathe, and set their songs a-flow, Even thus for me—O say, what here of fine, Fresh, bright, and glad hath he, that is not also mine?”

Now, like a wizard, standing wild and lone On some great mountain’s cloud-frequented cone, Meet altar of a universal Fane Where no fiend-rites had left their murder-stain! Thou gav’st me Faith’s prophetic powers, to sieze High consolations out of mysteries— Those mystic vapors that low mutt[er]ing roll Through Time’s dim vast—the storm-clouds of the soul

O Spirit! thus attend me to the last; For though thou lay’st not thou yet warm’st the blast Of bitter-cold contumely, when ’tis blown, As always, ‘gainst all worth, if lowly and alone! And by thy parent Hope from hour to hour Revested with a promissory power, Distill’st for Genius that neglected strays, A draught of triumph from its future days, Or stretchest, as a rainbow strides the sea, Over all time—all ages yet to be— The assurance of its immortality! Or in thy ministry more widely traced Thou’rt restoration even to the disgraced; And; to the cast-away unsought of all, Dost bring the heart-taste of a friendly call;+ Art to captivity an eagle’s wing, Airs from the hills by Freedom roamed to bring; x ’Mid Winter’s grief a sense of Summer’s mirth Dost give a living to Death And cool in its anguish with thy spirit-breath! On the waste Sea art the green joy of Earth; ‘Mid Winter’s grief a sense of Summer’s mirth: Lap me in Visions! which may no[u]ght dispel Till the hard Real deserves the heart’s applause as well!

Euroma, 1867.
