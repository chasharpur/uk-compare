LITERATURE

The Anchor.

By the late Charles Harpur.

Strong trust of the bold sailor on the shores of sudden storm, How massive is its structure, and how able is its form! Old Ocean’s bed rock gripping, with the hurricane’s tug ‘twill cope, And therefore ’tis the emblem of all brave and noble hope.

Where never plummet sounded by the bleak and stormy steep, Like a thunderbolt down rushing, it is shot into the deep; Down where some mute sea monster its so hideous bulk uprears, And mightiest silenced drowned hath lain for many a thousand years.

In some famed bay of battle when ’tis plunged with sullen roar— In the Nile, in Naverino, or by Danish Elsinore, How meetly there it slumbers, deep beneath the storied waves, Amid the bones of gallant Tars, in Glory’s watery graves?

In the bright and land-locked harbour, the weary voyage o’er, When only pleasant breezes breathe sweet welcome from the shore, Its noble form down-dropping to its quiet bed below, Gives signal to the first glad boat that beachward pants to go.

In the calm, thou firm-fixed surety—thou strong helper in the storm— Even a child, when first beholding all the fitness of thy form, Might well divine the dangers wherewithal ’tis thine to cope, And take thee as an emblem of all brave and noble hope.
