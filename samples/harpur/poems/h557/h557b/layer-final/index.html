THE SPIRIT OF THE BOWL.

A strong Man and a Boy, With a Damsel young and fair, Went forth in eager quest of joy, Defying sober care: So a draught to each was brought By one who stoutly swore, On earth was nothing to be bought Might glad their spirits more.

The strong Man drunk it up, And I beheld at length, A poison lurking in the cup Had burned away his strength! Hark! fearfully he groans Because of horrid pain— Disease is gnawing at his bones, And madness at his brain! Whilst all—even they who bear his name, Abandon him for very shame.

The Boy then tasted too, And in a little space, I saw that health’s celestial hue Had withered from his face! Hark! reeling to and fro, Thus doth the urchin rave— I would my father’s head were low, My mother in her grave, That I with freer hand might fling Their wealth abroad, and drink and sing.

The Damsel coyly eyed The bowl, to sip at last, And soon the robe of decent pride From her fair neck was cast! Hark! ruined in an hour, She boasts with gestures wild, That Sin shall be her paramour, And Infamy her child! For all that once had chill’d her blood, Seems genial now, and fair and good.

O if unto the soul And to the body such Be found the Spirit of the Bowl, Should any taste or touch? No:—dash it to the earth! Its sparkles to destroy! There’s riot ever in its mirth, There’s ruin in its joy!

CHARLES HARPUR.
