NATIVE LITERATURE.

In the course of Mr. Deniehy’s lecture on Modern English Poetry, at the School of Arts, on Tuesday evening last, allusions were made to our native poet, Mr. Charles Harpur, and a high eulogy passed on his genius. Though it is, we believe, the intention of the lecturer to enter more fully into the merits of his gifted countryman at the close of his series of discourses, we, ourselves, cannot resist the opportunity afforded us by Mr. Deniehy’s observations, of saying a word or two in connection with the matter. Mr. Harpur is actually, and in very truth, a poet of high order. Many of his productions, scattered through local publications, are not only replete with beauty, but are also thoroughly Australian—thoroughly native in spirit, in feeling, and their peculiar imaginative cast. An Australian born and edu- cated, Mr. Harpur, we think, deserves more at the hands of his countrymen, than he has hitherto received. We are informed that an important work in the shape of a series of poems, to which he has devoted the labour of years, and on which he is content to stake his reputation,—his “Wild Bee of Australia”—is now ready for publication.
