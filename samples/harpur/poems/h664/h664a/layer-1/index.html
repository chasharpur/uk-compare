Angels with colored wings.

I have been told by one whose judgement I greatly value, that I have been guilty of a chromatic sin in having, visioned, in the following Po 24 as being otherwise than white: that to color them is to violate the traditional ’lore of the sister art of Painting. But Poetry and Painting, though sisters, are not twins: though daughters of the one mother they are of distinct births—and are born each of them to a peculiar mission. Hence the vision of the Poet is not that of the Painter. His office 25 is to discribe to the imagination, not paint to the eye. In the very Vision below, there is scarcely anything that could be pictured, except through words: as the reader himself cannot fail to perceive.

A VISION OF AN ANGEL.

I had a vision of an Angel, who Within the dim verge of my slumber stood, Shadowing the snowy beauty of her form With ample-folding wings, whose plumes all glowed With interfusing colors, such as mix In the bright borders of an evening cloud. And could I now revoice to human ears The wondrous things she spake,—spake with a tongue 27 That seemed itself all spirit, and from which Her words came raying, each one like a star, And sounding with a golden cadence, such As Beings attone who breathe not air, but light;— Could I revoice those oracles—Oh! then Should all who heard, in utterance jubilant, Conjoin a symphony in words like these: “Nations no more shall trade in war, no more Shall banish Charity from the homes of Earth; 28 But all mankind, together knit in love, Shall henceforth move in concord with all truth And goodness; while, as from one stream, they drink Eternal beauty from one heavenly theme.

Besides for thus coloring of angelic wings there is the very highest poetical authority—the only authority, besides that of his own ideal instinct, which should be held paramount by a poet. Here is an example from Spencer, who, be it observed, 29 has even been called, and not without propriety, the Painter-Poet.

Beside his head there sat a fair young man, Of wondrous beauty and of freshest years, Whose tender bud to blossom new began, And floroush fair above his equal peers; His snowy front curled with golden hairs, Like Phœbus’ face adorned with sun[n]y rays, Divinely shone; and to sharp wingèd shears, Deckèd with divine plumes, like painted jays, Were deckèd at his back to cut his airy ways.

30

No doubt an angel with wings such as are here described would look somewhat gaudy in a Painting,—that is, as beheld by the bodily eye; but when seen through that of the imagination only, it is so far removed into the ideal—so far spiritualised by the process, as to become divested of all offence of that kind. So at least it appears to me. And this difference of medium through which we look at the pictures of the poet and of the painter, must 31 naturally suggest to the former, a peculiar liberty as to the means employed in their creation.

But there is the authority of Milton for the occasional coloring of the wings of Angels; part of wh with whose description of Raphael, I will close this paper. It is uniquely superb.

A Seraph winged: six wings he wore, to shade His lineaments divine; the pair that clad Each shoulder broad, came mantling o’er his breast 32 With regal ornament; the middle pair Girt like a starry zone his waste, and round Skirted his loins and thighs with downy gold And colors dipt in heaven.
