A Vision of an Angel X

I had a vision of an Angel, who Within the circle of my consciousness Stood, where its dim verge met the dark of Sleep, Even as the Moon, on the horizon, stands Within the dream-gaze of the slumbering Earth: Stood shadowing her snowy beauty so As but the more confirmed its glorious grace, With ample-folding wings, whose Paradise plumes Glowed with such hues as, still intenselier, mix In the bright borders of an evening cloud. So stood she, a resplendent Apparition! And could I now revoice to human ears The wondrous things she spake,—spake with a tongue That seemed itself all spirit, and from which Her words came raying, each one like a star, Though fusing all into one luminous wave, And flowing with a golden cadence, such As Spirits attune who breathe not air, but light;— Could I revoice those oracles—O then Should all who heard, in utterance jubilant, Conjoin a symphony in words like these: ‘Nations no more shall trade in war, no more Shall banish Charity from the homes of earth; But all mankind, together knit in love, Shall henceforth move in concord with all truth, 612 All goddness; while, as from a well, they drink Eternal beauty from one heavenly joy. x

x I have been told by one whose critical judgment I greatly value, that I have been guilty of a chromatic sin here, in having visioned the wings of an Angel as being otherwise than white: that to color them is to violate the traditions of the sister of the art of Painting. But Poetry and Painting, though sisters, are not twins: though daughters of the one mother, namely Ideality, they are of a distinct birth—and born each of them to a peculiar mission. Hence the vision of the Poet is not that of the Painter H[is] office is to describe to the imagination, not paint to the eye; nor are his pictures to be measured by literal standards: nay, their very vagueness is often their merit.

But between Poetry and Painting, sisters though they be, there are not only these lines of difference, but many others that cannot be interpassed. In the very Vision above, as the read will see, there is scarcely anything that could be pictured, except to the imagination through the medium of words. Moreover, for the coloring of angelic wings, there is the highest poetical authority—the only kind of the authority, besides that of his own ideal instinct, which should be held paramount by a Poet. Here is an example from Spencer, who, be it observed, has even been called, and not without propriety, the Painter[’s] Poet.

Beside his head there sat a fair young man, Of wondrous beauty and of freshest years, Whose tender bud to blossom new began, And flourish fair above his equal peers; His snowy front curlëd with golden hairs, Like Phœbus’ face adorned with sunny rays, Divinely shone; and two sharp wingëd shears, Deckëd with diverse plumes, like painted jays, Were deckëd at his back to cut airy ways.

No doubt an Angel with wings such as are here described, would look somewhat gaudy in a Painting,—that is, as beheld by the bodily eye; but when looked at through that of the imagination only, it is so far removed into the ideal—so far spiritualised by the process, as to become divested of all offence of that kind. And out of this difference of medium through which we look at the pictures of the Poet and at those of the Painter, there naturally accrues to the former a peculiar liberty of elaboration.

But for the occasional coloring of angelic wings, there is also the authority of Milton; part of whose description of the Seraph Raphael is superbly in point.

A Seraph winged: six wings he wore, to shade His lineaments divine; the pair that clad His shoulder broad, came mantling o’er his breast With regal ornament; the middle pair Girt like a starry zone his waist, and round Skirted his loins and thighs with downy gold And colors dipt in heaven.
