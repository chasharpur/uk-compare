The Festival.

And this has been a day of festival To myriads? I alone, it seemed, of all The multitudes who thronged the public ways, Was sad, and utterly companionless. I only, too, of each mirth-noisy group, Did mark not by my air, or gait, or garb, A glad occasion. This methought, at times, Some noticed, with distasteful looks, as though Such apparition struck a hateful chill Into their merriment. But what to me Were they? and why should still the thought of this Embue me with such bitterness extreme? Yet, when with wandering glances I beheld Some public walk all flowered with the fair forms Of rich-robed women, whose expressive eyes Were bright with joy or big with tenderness, Although not envious of others’ good, I saddened! for not one enchanting face Was lighted up for me! not one warm glance 244 Approved me! And a deadly sense of this Wormed cold and venomous through my feelings, when In spite of misery they had flourished forth Into the general joy—wormed like a snake That wantons amid flowers; or as amid The warm hours of the Spring a wintry blast Breathes merciless blight: so deadly cold it came And passed within me—me, who even when A frock-clad child led by a mother’s hand, Had fondly brooded with a burning cheek And asking heart,—with lips apart, and breath Hushed to such silence as the matron dove Preserves when while warming into life her young, Over the secretly disclosing hope Of finding in the fulness of my youth Some sweet congenial One to love—to call My Own! my Beautiful! In the warm light Of mutual passion cultivating then Our sympathies so strictly, as to make The moods, and tendencies, and aims, and all The heart-felt though unutterable truth Of both our lives—one soul-world.

This were love Indeed! and loving thus,—with an untold And spiritual consent inscrutable, Oour beings at once, though separate far, might glow With the kindred hope, or chill with the mutual fear; Enlarge with the same plenitude of joy, Or writhe with a like wrench grief, and not A dream pass unpartaken. Hence, when sphered At length in wedlock’s sacred sanction, each Should to the other be throughout this life’s Whole pilgrimage, a cool Horebian rill Of blessëd water in the world’s wilderness 245 A palm-tree on the sultry glebe of toil; A hospice on the wild and wintry alp Of outcast effort; a perpetual star, Shining peculiar and apart, to guide In every enterprise; and on the coast Of Ruin , a beacon, sending far Over the dread commotion of the deep Its tranquilising splendor: Finally, If shipwrecked there, even there unchanged, would each Yet be to each, a sun-flush in the cloud That wrapped the unborn day, or in the hopes That flickered on eternity’s dread front A promissory passport.

Yes, with one Equally capable for love, methinks I could assimilate with such entire Conformity of feeling, that our souls Under the plastic power of habits formed By the sweet intercourse of wedded years, Joining in their extremes, should gradual thence, With larger interchange of life, begin To take each other’s likeness nature; and, at last, Commixing utterly, even so inhabit Our separate bodies—one.

And for the weal Of one thus loved and loving, what were past My daring? I could mock the ghastliest dangers! Even on Destruction’s frightful brink could sing Songs of sure passion and triumphant love! Nay, be a villain while in strife with villains, To vindicate her welfare! Where her foot Left its light pressure on the common dust, And her fair locks played with the wandering wind; 246 Where’er her slender shade were wont to fall, Or her so rare though so familiar voice Awake a gentle echo,—there might be My happy home, however far removed O’er Indian mountains or Arabian wastes, Or ’mid the dun illimitable sweep Of Polar solitudes, O there might be My happy, happy home—home dear as though ‘Twere bosomed in the loveliest vale that belts With whispering shades and diamond-bubbling springs, (As with the spirits of ) some warrior hill Of this my Native Land!—

But ah! how vain Are these fond boasts! Who heeds them? Still my heart Aching with very fulness, is but like An unknown fountain in some desert way, Whose waters, though so precious, no one seeks To drink of—and to bless. ‘Tis even so.

But once it was not. There was one whose soul Once felt the kindred influence of mine, Al Albeit between us the sweet name of ‘Love’ Passed never, to bring blooming to the cheek Those rosy shames that burn it on the heart, Indelibly;—the one fixed symbol there For ever after, of all Thought can dream Of Heavën, and the synonym of God. Yet not the less, a sympathy that heard Through many a token Love’s sweet spirit itself Low breathing in the silence of our souls, Knit us together with a still consent.

And she was beautiful in outward shape As lovely in her mind. None saw unmoved 247 The bright abundance of her tresses—bright And diffluent, like the gushes of a rill Split into wayward beauty by the stones That reef the channel, where it slideth broad O’er some s sloping rock,—a downward mass In flashing ringlets and long lucid coils Breaking and fanwise flirting as they fall Regathering into rolls, and sudden shapes Like crystal bugles sprigg’d with jets of spray. While from the starry heavens of her deep Emotional eyes, there rayed a glory forth So genial, that it kindled joy in hearts Even old and world-worn. O such eyes were they As burn in the far depths of passionate thought, And hint the immortality of Love, While yet the visionary heart of youth Is lonely in its hope! Who saw, extolled The peachy freshness of her cheek, the smooth Yet intellectual structure of her brow; And ne’er hung cherries on their native stem More ruby-rich, more delicately full Than were her lips,—and when her young heart would, A smile ineffably bewitching played The unwitting conqueress there.

Her light round form Was fawn-like, with a graceful poise in all Its impulse manifesting motions fair, And was to the bright purity of her life, What some young tree whose shapely shadow dwells Within a stream’s smooth glass, is to that tree In unity of beauty,—when its boughs Move in the morning air, or when its green And pleasant top sways gently to and fro In evening’s balmy gale. Yea, her whole being Was as harmonious to the mind as are 248 Most perfect strains, of purest tones prolonged, To music-loving ears.

But full of dole Is what remains to tell. Ere twice eight Springs Had bloomed about her being, a most fell And secret malady did feed amid The roses of her cheek, and on the moist And rubious pasture of her lips, but still Felon-like shunnedd the lustre of her eyes, That more resplendent grew. And so before Those glowing orbs had turned their starry light Upon one human face with other troth Than a fon daughter and fond sister yields To those relations that precede our choice; Ere her white arms and heaving bosom held A nestling other than the near-feeble head Of sickness, or a stranger babe, the grass That whistled dry in the atumnal wind, Was billowing round her grave!

———And yet I live With dogs and cats and heartless human things, Within a world that knoweth her no more! Yet let me try if my now broken shell Can send into the dark of the dead Past, One last wild wail oer what hath been in vain.
