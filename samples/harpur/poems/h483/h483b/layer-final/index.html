THE ROSE OF ABANA:

AN ORIENTAL ECLOGUE.

How beautiful was she whom men once called The Rose of Abana! my early youth’s Enchantment, and my manhood’s promised bride, When my full strength was like a lion roused, My fleetness like a roe, in pride of speed, Seen bounding on the mountains! But my head Is waxen white as Winter,—and oh! where Is Myrza? where the enchantment of my youth? The promised glory of my manhood—where? Like Nature for her lost child sorrowing yet, An echo from the mountain, with a far And shuddering intonation, answers Where?

Since in the triumph of the harvest time, Amongst the virgins I beheld her last, Dancing, like Evening’s joyous star amid The sparkling throngs of heaven, how many years Have joined their shadowy portions to the past? And all that weary while have I been thus Alone—alone! For in her Father’s house Myrza died early. Into the cruel grave Even in her virginal gladness did she fall!

To-day I called her mine—the morrow came, And who might then dispute her with the worm! And never her light coming step again Might call out rapture from my listening heart, Even as an Echo from her silent cell With a wild throb in happy wonder leaps, Or make my soul, through its serenest depths, To shudder with a pleasure so extreme That it resembled pain. Yet though long years Of weary days and melancholy nights Have worn me with their cold and constant drip, Her memory lieth warm upon my brain, Her form is pictured on my eyelids yet In all its wondrous beauty. In the fresh And sweet-voiced Spring she died—and never more Came Spring to me! Its freshness with her bloom Had faded, and its once glad melody But marked the loss of her harmonious breath.

Like clusters of the vine, when in the light They gleam with ripeness, in bright masses hung The curlëd riches of her graceful head. And under smoothest brows her azure eyes Yearned deep as the unpillared dome of heaven Unfilmëd with a cloud: serenely blue, Yet glowing, like moist violets, when first The sun is stepping from the eastern steep, And while the globy dews of morning yet Flame in their fleeting beauty. Like two gardens Of roses and lilies mingled were her cheeks— Two gardens over against each other blooming Upon the slope sides of a sunny hill, When summer flushes with her earliest fruits, And mating wild birds warble. And most like A singing bird of price, within a cage Of ivory, bordered with a crimson brede, Her well-taught tongue melodiously discoursed In the sweet prison of her gracious mouth. And sweeter was her breath itself than air The odorous pantings of a timid breeze, When fresh escaped out of some bowery maze It cometh, after soft and sunny showers Have wept, with a light grief, the flowery death And burial of the Spring. Her slender neck Was as a vase of polished alabaster Having a spirit of warm life within;— As smooth-wrought marble with a quick rich blood Coursing and mantling upward in its blue And branching veins. Her gentle breasts, beneath Their silken robe, were like a downy pair Of nestling turtle doves,—there, in their soft And innocent concealment breathing both So warmly. And her ever graceful motion Was as the pacing of a glad gazelle— One of a year old that hath never looked From out the secret places of the hills Upon the form of man.

Thus richly meet And beautifully blooming to the eye Was Myrza! And no guile, no evil lurked Like a bower’d serpent hid in flowers, beneath Those visible graces: for her young desires Were innocent as a troop of snowy lambs, Seen sporting by the summer brooks that spend Their cool and murmurous lives amid the shades Of fruitful Abana.—But what availed Her innocence, her loveliness, and all Her wondrous beauty? In her Father’s house Early she died! Into the cruel grave Even in her virginal gladness did she fall! And I—her chosen one, with time and grief Thus bleached and broken,—am not I the slave Of merciless strangers in a distant land!

In the deep silent solitude of night A solemn Vision, like a sudden cloud, Shadowed my soul. Loved voices and far tones Flowed, as if breathing out of the dead Past, About me, till, with a dim awful joy, I felt a sweet mysterious Presence come And stand in the near gloom, over my bed, Stilly and tall:—and when I thither looked, ‘Twas Myrza! the Belovëd of my youth! Even as in her lifetime she had stood Beside me amid Abana’s green shades: The same—but that her sweet face now was pale, And that her dove-like eyes, with moveless lids, Shone fixed and gleamy, as still waters shine Uner their palmy fringe. Forth then I stretched My arms,—but empty on my yearning breast Together fell they folded! and again The weary weight of many lonesome years Rolled whelming, like a monstrous wave of pain, Back on my heart! The white and solemn light Of the cold midnight moon came slanting through The lattice from a dim cloud’s lurid verge; The mixing murmurs of the wind grew loud Amongst the sycamores beyond the brook; A sudden bleating from the hill-side fold Poured troublous down; and at the gate my dog Howled mournfully, as though even over him The spirit of the bygone, like a wail Heard sorrowing through the dark, had also swept To leave a weary sense of loss behind!

CHARLES HARPUR.
