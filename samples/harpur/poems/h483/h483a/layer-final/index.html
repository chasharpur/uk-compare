[THE ROSE OF ABANA]

And that her dove-like eyes, with moveless lids, Shone fixed and gleamy, as still waters shine Under their palmy fringe. Forth then I stretched My eager arms; but emptily they fell Folded together on my breast—and lo, I was alone! The white and solemn light Of the cold midnight moon came slantingly Through the barred lattice from a cloud’s dim verge; The mixing murmurs of the wind were loud Amongst the sycamores beyond the brook; A sudden bleating from the hill-side fold Came troublous down; and mournfully and long My dog howled at the gate.

O, let me gaze— Let me gaze westward, where she sleeps afar In Death’s dark silentness;—where her white brow Lies in the damp dust tow’rds the setting sun.

Note. The quality of the imagery of this Poem, classes it, I think, very markedly with the Rhymes called “the Poet” (page ), as does also the spirit of its design—an attempt to compensate the heart’s inward destitution or loss, by surrounding its complaints with the bloomy riches and happier pulsations of the outward and visible. Hence the jar which this its general character receives from the concluding circumstances: which suggest, in turn, the question, Should we not dispose of our dead rather by the pyre than by burial?

I think we should. The memory of their beloved countenances would then live with the living, unallied to the cold and wormy loathsomeness of the grave. They would perpetually recur to our hearts and minds, either in the beaminess of life, or in the holy and eloquent repose of recent death;

“Before Decay’s effacing fingers Hath swept the lines where beauty lingers:”

and not as now, in horrible union with the festering secrets of the shroud, and those writhing heaps of “life in death” which must colonise, in process of time, 484 the damp dust of their burial: while, in a sanitary respect, the pyre were indisputably prefer- -able to any mode of internment.

It may be objected, however, that it were an heathenish practice. True, the Jews always buried their dead; but they had many other customs, both social and pious, which christians have been wise enough not to adhere to: and I think it can be but a matter of small religious account with any en- -lightened man of the nineteenth century, by what mode our mere bodies, after life is extinct, shall be resolved into their primative elements. And this restores the question to its simple sanitary bearing.

Be it by fire then, the grand elemental ethereal- izer, the supreme material purifier of this our earth. Nor would the pyre deprive us of the venerable ashes of our ancestors. It would only preserve these to us in a more wholesome condition: so much so, that the urns enclosing them might be made the innoxious ornaments of our private homes or public gardens.

Still men’s prejudices will work strongly in favor of internment, as in the case of long standing usages of every kind. But however obstinate these may be, and are in fact, the contrary practice of the pyre would utterly irradicate them, it is likely, during the passing away of a single generation: so circumstan -tial a creature is man. In writing thus, however, I may have been viewing the question too exclusively through the atmosphere of poetry; and on the edge of this doubt I will even leave it.
