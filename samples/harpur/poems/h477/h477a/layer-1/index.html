IV.

To a Lady with a Copy of Love=poems.

Many a weary day and night Have ta’en their unregretted flight, Dawned many a hopeless morrow, Since this wild wreath in scenes afar, 16 I wove in joy and sorrow. And long it was ere I could deem That the so gratefully glowing star Of Morning Love did only beam To gild a morning dream.

But so it was: although she’d die, The Maiden vowed, if Fate our lot Cast separate; nor in vows was I Behind a single jot! But Fate, in the vile shape of pelf, At length between us thrust herself, And yet (however strange to tell) We both lived on—and—to get well.

Alas! a tear is in mine eye, To think our very truth was—what? At best—a golden lie! And hence, although I will it not, The Real is battling from my head All—all the dear Romance that spread O’er other days and nights Such heart-bewitching lights; And Caution dinning me the while, With—Have a care of Beauty’s smile; For though a thing so rosy-rich And tender, ’tis the stuff of which Is woven Passion’s painful thrall.

Yes, of the Love that left me lonely, Such, Lady, is the sequel only, And such the profit all! And for the days that bore it by These lines are—one long written Sigh; Wherewith (so singular is grief) In earnest half and half in jest, Mine heart I banter for relief 17 Sometimes when ill at rest.x

x Alas! that the Woman of our imagination and the Woman of a experience should be such very different creatures, the one, Desdemon like, all love and devotion, the other all selfishness, frivolity, and worldliness, far beyond the world- liness of man; and with an utter disregard, if not insensi- bility, to real worth in the opposite sex; and even to comeliness. Indeed, to form what is called a thorough “woman’s man,” Na- ture has but to enclose the dispositions of a monkey in a man-milliner sort of prettiness, and Fashion to furnish this insignificance with a pop-gun battery of small talk. A tom-tit of this kind is sufficiently accomplished to carry any one of ninety nine out of any hundred not already preoccupied in favor of such another. Of course are women, few and far between, who are only to be won by manly sense and worth; and but for those rare exceptions, I am afraid I should be disposed to estimate the whole sex, as I should so many laying hens and growing pullets.—The Notes I have thought fit to append to my poems in thus collecting them, will, I doubt not, occasionally offend against [unclear] some, if not many tastes: Still I must go on as I have begun, being conscious that they will possess at least one merit— fearlessness: that they will be of some value, as the after- thoughts of one who has determined to set forth the sum of his experience, on all occasions, undisguisedly. Still I would restrict in others, as I do in myself, any depression consequent upon this cutting into the marrow of things as they exist at present. I would prevent it from settling into morbidity, by remarking, that Experience is not always our safest counsellor; that there is a large faith in human nature, arising from a lofty consciousness of its capacities for indefinite improvement, which its darker influences should never be allowed wholly to overshadow. Would it be rational to predicate contemptuously of individual maturity from the crudities of childhood? Assuredly not: and neither is it wise 18 to augur evilly of our entire humanity, which is essen- tially progressive, from what it is and has been. And hence the best women we know, may be properly re- garded as individual manifestations of what all the sex might have been, and capable of being, and that speedily, were it but true to its own better feelings and perceptions, in defiance of the loveless teachings of a sordid and unbelieving preceptorhood of fashion-mongers. 1847. Nay, I believe the greatest and worst human falsehoods wear the shape of Woman, and plentyfully; I believe at the same time that the best and brightest human truths have the same outward types.
