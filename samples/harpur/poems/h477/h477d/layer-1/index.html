RHYMES TO A LADY, WITH A COPY OF LOVE POEMS.

Many a weary day and night Have taken their unregretted flight, Dawned many a hopeless morrow, Since this, this wild wreath, ’mid scenes afar, I move in joy and sorrow. And long it was ere I could deem, That the so gratefully glowing star Of morning love did only beam To gild a morning dream.

But so it was;—although she’d die, The Maiden vowed, if Fate our lot Cast separate; nor in vows was I Behind her a single jot! But Fate, in the vile shape of pelf, At length between us thrust herself; And yet (however strange to tell) We both lived on—and—to get well.

Alas! a tear is in mine eye, To think our very truth was—what? A golden lie! And hence; although I will it not, The Real is battling from my head All—all the dear Romance that shed O’er other days and nights Such heart-bewitching lights; And Caution dinning me the while With—Have a care of Beauty’s smile; For though a thing so rosy-rich And tender, ’tis the stuff of which Is woven Passion’s painful thrall.

Yes; of the Love that left me lonely, Such, lady, is the sequel only, And such the profit all! And for the days that bore it by, These lines are—one long-written Sigh: Wherewith (so singular is my grief), In earnest half, and half in jest, Mine heart I banter for relief Sometimes when ill at rest.

Note.—Alas, that the woman of our imagina- tion should be, in the main, so very different a creature from the woman of our experience: the one, Desdemona-like, all love and devotion; the other, generally, all selfishment, frivolity, and worldliness; and with an utter disregard very often, if not insensibility, to real worth in the opposite sex, and even to comeliness.

It is not wisdom, virtue, valor, wit, Strength, comeliness of parts, nor amplest merit, That woman’s love can win, or long inherit; But what it is, hard to say, Harder to hit, Which way soever men refer it.”

Chorus in Samson Agonistes.

How commonly true this is. Indeed, to form what is called a thorough “Lady’s man,” Nature has but to enclose the dispositions of a monkey in a man-milliner sort of prettiness, and Fashion to furnish this insignificance with a pop-gun bat- tery of small talk. He should also, to be perfect in his kind, have the nasal trick (if nothing more) of a little boarding-school French, and be able to execute, if not to understand, an Italian opera song. Thus plumed, he is a modern love’s bird of Paradise. He is a tom-tit nevertheless: but a tom-tit of this kind is sufficiently accom- plished to carry any one of ninety-nine women out of every hundred not already preoccupied in favor of such another.

Of course there are women, though few and far between, who are only to be won by manly sense, bearing, comeliness, and worth. Nay, though I believe that the worst and bitterest human fallacies inhabit the shapes of women, and that plentifully, I believe also, that the best and sweetest human truths have the same bodily abodes of beauty. In the warm, earnest faith, and delicate discrimination, and quick sensibili- ties of a virtuous and intelligent woman, there is indeed some of the profoundest philosophy— some of the most beautiful wisdom in nature: for such a woman may be said to think with the heart, which in many things, and in those espe- cially a right judgement of which is mainly to be arrived at through the medium of right feeling (and they are many) is even wiser, or at least less erring, than the most powerful masculine head. Still there are but few such women—few and far between, like the green spots of a desert, the lovelier for surrounding barrenness, but making it, at the same time, all the more disheartening.

Alas, then, for woman’s love! the young heart’s rosy idol! the early imagination’s bird of Para- dise! the new soul’s honey-bee of sympathy and affection!—alas! for in actual life it is mostly both “common and unclean,” and rarely of an enduring nature. What a death of heart-faith do we not experience when we find it in reality so much the creature of vulgar necessity, and worldly convenience, and mere juxta position!— a thing of shifts and turns!—a marketable com- modity!—nay, a merely fleshly appetite, nearly 713 as variable as any other to which “flesh is heir to.” Still, in poetry, to make poetry an earnest and affectionate suggestor, its character should be founded in devotion and endurance,—in chas- tity, piety, benevolence, faith, single-heartedness, and hope;—should be a blossom of peerless fra- grance in this world, and promissory of an im- mortal fruit in the next.

And, finally, speaking of Love as the joint pro

perty of both sexes:—If destiny vouchsafeth one draft of nectar to our passions, and we, through wordly wrong-headedness, will for ever keep faithlessly adulterating it with sordid mix- tures, so as to deprave it into porter—or, at best, into brandy, we clearly deserve a beverage no better. There is nothing under the sun but love that can purchase love; neither is there any thing in the great round of the world so justly precious as a genuine store of it; and yet, every nine out of ten men and women who make up the sum total of this sordid generation, would be right willing to sell their best chance of its possession “For so much trash as might be grasped thus.”

Yet I must proceed as I have begun, being conscious that they will possess the prime merit of an honest fearless- ness; and believing, moreover, that they will be of some value to the world as the after-thoughts of one who has determined to make them the media of his experience, set forth on all occa- sions undisguisedly. Still I would restrict in others, as I do in myself, any depression conse- quent upon cutting so straightforwardly into the marrow of things as they exist at present. I would prevent it from settling into morbidity, by remarking, that experience is not always our safest counsellor that there is a pious hope of human nature, arising out of a lofty conscious- ness of our capacity for next to infinite improve- ment, which its darker influences should never be allowed wholly to overshadow. Would it be rational to predicate conmptemptuously of any in- dividual’s maturity from the crudities in thought and conduct of his or her childhood and youth? Assuredly it would not: and neither is it wise to augur evilly of our entire humanity, which is es- sentially progressive, from what it is and has hitherto been. Hence the best women we at present know, may be properly regarded as indi- vidual manifestations of what all the sex might have been; and what all the sex is capable of being, and that speedily, were it but heroically true to its own better feeling and perceptions, in defiance of the loveless teachings of its hitherto sordid and unbelieving preceptorhood of fashion- mongers.
