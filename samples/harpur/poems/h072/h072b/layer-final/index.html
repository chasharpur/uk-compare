THE COMING ON OF FREEDOM.

The day must come—for Freedom must keep pace With Knowledge—when the accident of birth, Or emptier imposition of mere pelf, Shall neither arrogate to any man Distinction as of right, nor qualify Any for power, by a sole influence, Over his fellows. But all men shall stand Up proudly, under the wide roof of Heaven, As God-created equals: each the sire Of his own worth, and the joint sanctioner Of all political partainment—all Moral and social honour! Yea, for such Is Freedom’s Charter, written on the heart Of capable Humanity, when purged From the foul stains of Time, and his base rout Of arrogant hell-suggested superstitions, And customs born of Error.

And let none Despair of such an advent: for, as when Some solemn Wood’s familiar cadences, Deepening and deepening all around, portend The salutary storm; even so, the all- Pervading instinct of a sure revolt Against the ancient tyrannies of the earth, Breathes on before it in the living stress Of Knowledge,—omening the unborn change, By harshening still to the fine ear of Thought The daily jar of customary wrongs.

N

ote.—I have called property in this poem an emptier imposition than even the accident of high birth; and such, I repeat in prose, it assuredly is, when made the direct qualification for political or social power of any kind: because the latter, though in itself more questionable, is yet oftener the ante- cedent to at least a passable education. Otherwise, though I account great inequalities of property as amongst the greatest evils of society, I hold, at the same time, that the only means for the smoothing down of these, which can be safely entrusted to any governing power, are the preclusion of entails and laws of primogeni[t]ure. The individual’s right to possess and enjoy whatever wealth he may lawfully inherit or acquire, must ever remain at the founda- tion of a sound social system, for in no other way could it secure to its mem- bers than individuality of will, which is the very key-stone of social, if not of oll liberty. This prime necessity of society is fatal, then, to the Owenian or socialist philosophy. I was once somewhat smitten by the apparent benevo- lence of Owen’s views, but a further observation of human character, in its individual tendencies, has led me to prefer the frame work of almost any ex- isting form of community, to that monotonous series of mere physical har- monies which his system would be only calculated to e[l]icit, age after age, even from the aggregated union of all our social energies; because such a mere regimental sort of unity would emasculate them in the same degree, of all personal free will, and consequently of all true moral glory. In fine, I can see nothing to covet in a dead social level after the Chinese pattern, how- ever it might be smoothed into a painless perfection by the superior arts and appliances of European civilization.

As to the “jar of customary wrongs,” becoming daily harsher to the “ear of

thought,”—of this, I would say, the signs are manifold. At a meeting of intelligent working men not long since in that happy country, Eng[l]and, held to remonstrate against certain persecutions they had undergone at the hands of their fox-hunting “pastors and masters” (as the church catechism has it), one of the speakers put the matter in the following unsophisticated form: “Why, in a free country, and amongst free men, should any subject be tabooed? Why should any man be molested for freely using that reason which God has given him for his moral and intellectual guidance?” But the logic of cant would put down the honest intelligence of this noble son of toil, by quoting, it is likely, the saying of Solomon: “He that encreaseth know- ledge encreaseth sorrow,’, with this compassionate conclusion tacked to the end of it: “Therefore the many, to be able to bear with life at all, must leave it to the few who make them poor laws and sermons.” On the other side, the logic of the aristocratical colleges would make a similar dictum the polished apex of a more elaborate disquisition upon popular happiness; thereby proving, however, as our hard-fisted orator above quoted would cer- tainly aver, altogether too much—namely, that men collectively may become too wise, or that it is the final nature of wisdom to impugn and deprave itself. But the day of Bible cant and learned imposition is palpably drawing to its close in the past, and that of absolute freedom as evidently dawning from the future.
