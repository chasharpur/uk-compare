The Coming on of Freedom.

The day must come—for Freedom must keep pace With Knowledge—when the accident of birth, Or emptier imposition of mere pelf, Shall neither arrogate to any man Distinction as of right, nor qualify Any for power, by a sole influence, Over his fellows. But all men shall stand Up proudly, under the wide roof of heaven, As God-created equals: each the sire Of his own worth, and the joint sanctioner Of all political part—all Moral and social honor! Yea, for such Is Freedom s Charter, written on the heart Of capable Humanity, when purged From the foul stains of Time, and his base rout Of arrogant hell-suggested superstitions, And customs born of Error.

And let none Despair of such an advent: for as when Some solemn Wood’s familiar cadences, Deepening and deepening all around, portend 72 The salutary storm; even so, the all- Pervading instinct of a sure revolt Against the ancient Tyrannies of the Earth Breathes on before it in the living stress Of Knowledge,—omening the unborn change, By harshening still to the fine ear of Thoughts The daily jar of customary wrongs.

I have called property in this Poem an emptier than even the accident of high birth; and such, I repeat in prose, it assuredly is, when made the direct qualification to political or social power of any kind: because the latter, though in itself more questionable, is yet oftener the antecedent to at least a passable education. Otherwise, though I account great inequalities of property as amongst the greatest evils of society, I hold, at the same time, that the only means for the smoothing down of these, which can be safely entrusted to any governing power, are the preclusion of entails and laws of primogeniture. The individual’s right to possess and enjoy whatever wealth he may lawfully inherit or acquire, must ever remain at the foundation of a sound social system; for in no other way could it secure to its members that individuality of will, which is the very keystone of social,—nay, of all liberty. This prime necessity of society is fatal, then, to the Owenian or socialist philosophy.

I

was once somewhat smitten by the apparent benevolence of Owen’s views, but a further observation of human character, in its individual tendencies, has 74 led me to prefer the frame-work of almost any existing form of community, to that monotonous series of mere physical harmonies, which his system would be only calculated to elicit, age after age, even from the aggregated union of all our social energies; because such a regimental sort of unity would emasculate them in the same degree, of all personal freedom of will, and consequently of all true moral glory. In fine, I can see nothing to covet in a dead social level after the Chinese pattern, however it might be smoothed into a painless perfection by the superior arts and appliances of European civilization.
