Poems: By Charles Harpur.

Homer’s Iliad. Book I.

O Goddess, sing the much-destroying wrath Of Peleus’ son, Achilles; which erewhile Brought countless woes upon the warlike Greeks, And many valiant souls of heroes down To Hades hurling, left their bodies prone A prey to dogs, and to all birds obscene; (For Jove’s great will was being then accomplished); Even from the time when first the king of men, Atrides, and divine Achilles, both In civil broil contending, were disjoined.

Which was it, then, of all the Gods, who thus Provoked between the two such hateful strife? Jove and Latona’s son; for he, enraged Against the king, amid the army sent A sore distemper (so that day by day, The people troop on troop kept perishing); And this because the son of Atreus had Dishonored Chrysis, his great worshipper, Who to release his captive daughter came Unto the sharp-beaked galleys of the Greeks, Bringing along with him a countless ransom, And bearing in his hands the arrowy God’s Known ensyns, all in shining fillets wreathed Upon his golden wand: and then to all The Greeks he said, though supplicating most The sons of Atreus,—leaders of the people.

“Ye son of At[r]eus! (thus spake the priest) And all ye other well-greaved Greeks! To you May all the Gods who hold the heavenly dwellings, Grant Priam’s city for a spoil, and thence To voyäge home in safety. But for me, 543 O yield me up my daughter, and take these My offered ransoms; reverencing so (As is most meet) the quivered son of Jove, Apollo the far-darter.”

And on this The other Greeks all shouted in assent: As willing all to reverence the priest And takes his splendid ransoms: But the son Of Atreus, Agamemnon, in his mind Dissenting, drove him scornfully away, And added this harsh mandate as he went.

“Begone! old man; and let me find thee not Now loitering longer at the hollow barks Or afterwards returning, lest the staff And fillets of thy God avail thee nought! For her (thy daughter) I will not set free! Sooner old age shall come upon her there Within our house at Argos, from the land That bore her far away. There shall she ply The crafty shuttle and prepare my bed. But go—at once! nor try my temper more, Lest thy return be anything but sure!”

Thus spake he, and the old man was afraid, And straightway did obey him. And he went Along the shore of the much-sounding sea In silence, grieving. Going then apart, The lonely and bereaved old man appealed, In earnest prayer to King Apollo, whom Bright haired Latona bore.

“Hear thou my prayer, God of the silver bow! Thou who art wont To shelter Chrysa and the heavenly Killa And rulest mightily in Tenedos! O Sminthius! if ever heretofore Thy beautiful temple I have hung with gifts And if, moreover, I at any time, Have burned the fattened thighs of bulls & goats Upon thy sacred altar, be for me This wish forthwith accomplished: Let the Greeks Pay for my tears through thy death-dealing bolts!”

Thus spake he, lonely praying: and to him Phoebus Apollo harkened. From the tall Olympian heights, with raging heart at once Down came he, bearing on his shoulders broad His silver bow, and quiver lidded close. And as he moved the shafts within it made A rattle, as in answer to his wrath, So went he on, making himself the while A moving darkness, like advancing Night Till drawing tow’rds the galleys he sat down Apart a space, and right amongst them sent An arrow; whereat from the silver bow A deadly jarring rushed on all hands out, Breaking the still air with a terrible clang[.] At first his vengeance visited the mules And lazy dogs; but afterwards he aimed A piercing arrow at the Greeks themselves, And smote them sore, so that on all hands round The funeral piles of the unnumbered dead Rose blazing, day and night. And thus the God Made havock with his arrows through the port For nine dread days. But when the tenth arose Achilles called a meeting of the people, He thereto having been in spirit moved By white armed Juno, anxious for the Greeks, Because she saw them dying. And when they At this assembly, were all met together, Uprising in their midst, thus did the swift Achilles speak.

“O son of Atreus Now verily I think we would consent Home to return, defeated in our purpose, If haply Death should let us, seeing how Both war and pestilence eat up the Greeks. But come now, let us of some priest enquire, Or of some prophet, or of one who is Advised by dreams (for they too come from Jove) Who may reveal to us on what account Phoebus Apollo rages thus against us: Whether he blames us for some broken vow 582 Or hecatomb withheld; and if, at once, Partaking at our hands the savour sweet Of lambs and perfect goats, he haply might Be willing to avert this pestilence.

Thus having spoken, he sat down. And up Before them rose the wisest of the augurs, Calchas the son of Thestor, who alike The present, and the past, and future knew. And who the galleys of the Greeks had steered To Ilium by that prophetic art Which bright Apollo gave him. He, complying, Thus, answered.
