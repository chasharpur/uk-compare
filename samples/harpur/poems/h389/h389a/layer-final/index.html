THE POET’S WISH.

A FRAGMENT.

Here, in this lonely, rill engirdled spot, Each foe forgetting, by each friend forgot, Save one—the sharer of my solitude— How blest it were to draw as noiselessly from Time, As hidden springs from their cool grots exude, My yet remaining prime.

Here, early rising from a sinless bed, How sweet it were to view Aurora shed The first white glances o’er the dusky wood: Powdered with liquid gems then thro’ the grey mists gleam The trembling sprays, like hopes of future good, Or like some old-world dream.

And while the clouds imbibed a deeper hue, And purple streaks grained yon ethereal blue— Then, by the voice of every early bird, Even as some brimming fountain by each zephyr free Is into an unuttered gladness stirred, My spirit’s depth should be.

And as like burning bullion brightened still The cloud-hung east, above yon misty hill I d watch the sun, illustrious presence, come— Filling the forest glades with floods of christal fire, And the green spaces round my rural home, Where slept my heart’s desire.

When, if sweet memories of her waking smile Should my devotion thitherward beguile, Robbing the day-god of his homage due, Oh then my happy voice should not be wanting long, To wile her forth with loving transport true, Or wake her with a song.

“Awake, my fair one! for the glowing skies Desire thee, and a thousand flowery eyes Look for thy coming from each pathway side! With all things freshly green, and beautifully bright, The earth adorning like an eastern bride— Arise, my best delight!

“What can be deeper than the heavens o’erbending? What might be richer than the col ours blending Amid the green cones of the misty hill? What gladder than the birds, and the rill’s silvery fall? And yet my spirit asketh something still! ‘Tis thee—the crown of all.”

Charles Harpur.

Singleton, Patrick’s Plains, September, 1842.
