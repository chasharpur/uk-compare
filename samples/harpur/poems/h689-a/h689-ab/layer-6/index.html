The Witch of Hebron A Rabbinical Legend

205b From noon until the setting of the sun, The Rabbi Joseph on his knees had prayed, When from the Synagogue a messenger 205f Came to him with an Ethiopian page Who bowed before him, and besought that he 205d Would hasten whither he was sent to lead him, 205g Even to the mansion of a Lady, once Of princely rank, and who now lying sick Even unto Death, and grievously tormented, Implored the comfort of his holy prayers.

The Rabbi, ever to the call of sorrow, Open as day, arose; and girding straight His robe about him, with the page went forth; Who swiftly led him deep into the woods That hung, heap over heap, like broken clouds, Along the southern terraces of Hebron: When through a broad glade suddenly beheld, A wondrous structure, like a palace, showed Its gleaming front and many-pillared porch, Fretted with sculptured, vinage, flowers and fruit, Or rough with figures exquisitely wrought, As by some Phedian hand. 140b ☞ But interposed A certain space in front, and belting all The splendid structure with its beauty, smiled A low-walled Garden, cut with paven paths Into divisions of a hundred forms, Yet all related, as concurring all To form one perfect whole;—that glowed throughout With rarest aromatic shrubs, and flowers Effusing odors such as seemed to bring Tidings from Paradise, so rich their fume! 140e While intermixed judiciously with these At happiest intervals, were pleasant growths Of higher verdure, gemm’d with golden fruit Or bright with silver blooms, and which below Were glassed in fountains raying to the light, 140d Each like a shield of pearl.

But if thus rich Was all without,—within, when he had entered, 206 More wondrous yet was what the Rabbi saw. Halls worthy of Herod the magnificent! With pictured walls, and columns gleaming white As Carmel’s snow, or blue-veined as with life, And from whose capitals suspended being Sidonian tissues all with threaded gold Embroidered, or all colored through with dyes Of puce and purple,—rich as sunset clouds. And letting off from these were chambers, filled With pillowy couches of carved citron wood And tables in mosaic, upon which Were lucent vases of American art, In crystal and in alabaster, mixed With ewers whose luminous transparency Were stained with inward splendors,—caskets clamped With ivory edges, and all spregged with pearls, And myrrhine cups, and lamps with globes of gold, Or feathery branches bearing pine-like cones, [Clasps], viols, mirrors, of unspeakable cost— A pomp of orient wealth, till thus beheld, Merely Incredible!—While in every hall, And in each chamber, passing or engaged, Were trains of servitors of every clime, Greeks, Persians, Indians, Ethiopeans,—all In richest, though in various, raiment clad. 207 Short time for wonder had the Rabbi, ere The page returned and led him to a still And distant chamber, where the sick one lay. Which entering soon, such brilliance met his gaze As shrank the costliness of all that he Had erewhile seen, though wondrous. Small indeed This chamber was as with the rest compared, Yet obviously contrived to fit a nice And individual privacy. While all Within it showed a sumptuousness extreme, And told of lavish habits,—glowing all With purple & gold, or interblazing quick With royal jewels of a sumless worth And in the midst of all this orient pomp, On profuse pillows of the swan’s new down, Encased in the soft silk of Shiraz,—lo, A queen-like Woman lay:—so beautiful, That though upon her moon-bright visage, pain And languor, like eclipsing shadows, gloomed, The ancient Rabbi’s pious heart was thrilled With singular tremours as he looked upon her. Young was she—but alas! from time to time, Oer her fair face a hetic flush would break, To leave that utter pallor which portends The manner of the tomb.

208

From youth to age, Much had the Rabbi Joseph sought in herbs And minerals, the virtues they possess, Soothing or pungent; and, by mixing these, And so completing them in their effects, With absolute integrity of art, Had for all ailmemts fit medicaments. Of these he offered her what to him seemed Then needful. But the dying Woman said: Alas! I want not these, nor did I send To implore thy presence, thinking thou couldst cure A malady like mine,—so fell and fatal— Yea, fatal as the funeral steps of Time! But simply that I might discharge my mind Of a dread secret that has been to me An ever-haunting and most ghastly fear, Hanging and darkening like an ominous cloud O’er all the ways and prospects of my life, And which must end it ere the morning come.

The Rabbi answered: If indeed it stand Within my power to serve thee, pour at once Thy sorrow out before me. But, if not, And if thy woe hath any mortal taint, 209 Forbear, O Woman to acquaint my soul With aught that if advised, it would not seek, x Though covetous of wisdom: Or with aught that only thenceforth could for horror chase The memory of a man of Israel.

I am, said she, the daughter of thy friend Rabbi Ben Bachai—be his memory blessed! But now the widow of a Prince, though one Of Ishmael’s race. Behold you this abode! Did ever yet the tent-born Arab build Thus for his pride or pleasure? See you all These riches! Tell me, when were such amassed By the grey Desert’s wild & wandering son? Riches he could not even hoard in dreams, As not by him conceivable. Ah, no! Deadly and desperate was the game that won them! And with a burning bitterness at best Have I enjoyed them ever. And at last How vainly now would I forego them all And mend with a repentant heed my peace In abject poverty. Their very taste, Though to my sense but as a thing foregone, How vainly now do I abhor—too late!

210

Thus having spoken, calmly for awhile In her pale loveliness she lay, with looks Dreamily self-involved,—as if her mind Were vaguely roaming in the fatal Past: When suddenly her beauteous countenance grew Bedimmed and dreary, like the eclipsed moon; Then darkened more, and writhed with horrid pangs Frightful to see; while evermore in this Her burning agony she tosses about Madly convulsed, and from her foaming lips Words so weird a meaning smite the ear Of Rabbi Joseph, that he bowed his head And cast his eyes in holy terror down!

And thus a space, bowed rigidly, he stood As one transfixed—though pitying, and with prayer Strengthening his soul. And when again his looks He lifted to the Woman—lo, that face Which erewhile showed so ghastly-wild and weird, Was cloudless as a summer heaven! The late Dark brow was bright; the late pale cheek suffused As with inevitable roseate bloom; And wondrous more than all, her eyes that seemed Before so sunken and so weary, now Were big with splendor, and around her shot 211 Electric influence! while her lips that erst Were thin & faded as autumnal leaves, Now full and crimson, curled with stormy pride, Or quivered with a fierce voluptuousness.

Well might the Rabbi stand in disbelief Of all that now he saw. Could thus be she By whom, as dying, he so late had knelt? This bold and beauteous being that now looked So lovely-fierce—yea, terrible, in all The bright audacity of passionate power? But seeing his amazement, thus she spake.

“These are the common changes of the full And fatal ailment, that with torments strange And varied, as thou seest, is tearing up The dark foundations of my mortal state, And sinking all its structures, hour by hour, Into the dust of Death. But nothing now Is left me, but to meet my nearing doom As best I may, and suffer.”

Hearing which, The Rabbi in his wisdom knew that she 212 Was troubled thus by the indwelling power Of some strong Spirit of Evil, and on this Sent straightway for an unction, wherewith erst The famous ansestor whose name he bore,— Herod the Great’s chief hakim,—had expelled The Demon haunter of the dying king. ‘Twas brought him, and therewith did he at once Annoint her forehead, and her eyes, and touch The tips of all her fingers. Next he made Within a consecrated crucible, A fire of citron-wood and cinnamon; And splashed the flames with insense,—every rite Inspiriting with the influence prayer. Then as the smoke arose, he bowed her head Into its coils, that so she might imbibe The salutary odour, till the Fiend That dwelt within her should be thence expelled.

Again a change came over her. Her face Once more grew pale with pain; once more she writhed In burning torment, uttering many words Of most unhallowed meaning! Yet her eyes Were fixed the while, and motionless her lips! Whereby the Rabbi certainly perceived 213 ‘Twas not the Woman of herself that spake— But the dread Spirit that possessed her soul. And thus it cried aloud:

“Why am I here, in this my last resort, Purturbed with incense and annointings? Why Compelled to listen to the sound of prayers That smite me through as with the fire of God! O pain, pain, pain! What of this Chamber be So full of us—for Legion is our name— Yet look around! Is it not also full Of our immitigable punishers? Full of avenging Angels, holding each A scourge of thunder in his potent hand, Ready to lighten forth! and who, then armed, For ever chase and wound us as we fly! Nor end with this—but in each wound they make Pour venom sweltered from that tree Asgard, Whose deadly shadow in its blackness falls Over the lake of everlasting doom! —Five hundred years ago, I who thus speak, Was an Egyptian, living at the Court Of Ptolemy Philadelphus. To the top 214 Of mounatainous power, though roughened with unrest, And girt with dangers as with thunder-clouds, Had I, resolved by all resorts to climb, By truth and falshood, fair and foul alike: (right & wrong) And I succeeded. Thus high-built in power Second alone to my imperial lord’s I crowned with its impunity my lust Of Beauty,—sowing, broadcast as it were, Such sensual baits as might lure Through pleasure, or though interest entrap, The fairest daughters of the land—and lo, Their lustrous eyes surcharged with passionate light The chambers of my harem! But at length, Wearying of these, though sweet,—I set my heart On riches, heaped to such a fabulous sum. As never one man’s hoard, before or since, Might match; and to acquire them, steeped my life In every public, every private wrong, [Lies frauds, and secret murders.] Till at last A favored minion I had trusted most, And highest raised, unveiled before the king. The so abysmal badness of my life. But dearly did he rue it; nor till now Guessed I how deadly grateful was revenge! I stole into his chamber as he slept, And with a sword whose double edge for hours I had ground and ground, to fit it for the deed, There staked him through the midriff to his bed. Where he lay writhing till Morning came. And showed the murder new: but meanwhile I had fled. 215a But first I sent, as oft I had used before, A present to the mistress of the man Who had in secret prompted my betrayer, Had Of scented wine and those Damascus cakes. On these So prized by Luxury—Ha, ha! Both feasted, and both fell together down Rolling and writhing in their dying pangs, Like poisoned desert dogs!

But I had fled A swift ship bore me, which my forecast long Had kept still ready against some such need As even now involved me. O’er the waves Proudly she rode—But on the third day, lo, A mighty storm strode o’er us, trampling all Her masted bravery flat, and driving still The wave-swept ruin tow’rds a reefy shore! Meanwhile, amongst the terror-striken crew 215e + An ominous murmur passed from mouth to mouth And ran them in councils, which ere long, Grew loud and furious into surmises wild And maniac menaces,—all aimed at me! 215f I was a sorcerer, abroad flying from the King! My fugitive head it was at which so loud The thunder bellowed. The wild-shrieking winds And roaring waters held in vengeful chase Me only!—me, whose signal crimes alone, Had brought on all this anger of the Gods! 215d And thus reproaching me, with glaring eyes They would have seized & slain me: but I stabbed 216 With sudden blow, their leader to the heart, And with my poignard scaring off the rest, Leaped from the deck, and, swimming, reached the shore: From which, in savage triumph, I beheld The battered ship, with all her howling crew, Heel, and go down amid the whelming waves!

round me the Great Afric Desert now Stretched vast and objectless: and inland still Starving and sunscorched, and a-fire with thirst Intolerable, I wandered—till I came To where amid the dun and level waste, In frightful loneliness, a mouldered group Of ancient tombs stood ghostly.
