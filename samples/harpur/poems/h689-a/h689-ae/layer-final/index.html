The Witch of Hebron:/ A Rabbinical Legend:/ By/ Charles Harpur./ An Australian Composed 1868

Part I.

From morn until the setting of the sun The Rabbi Joseph on his knees had prayed, When, from the Synagogue, a Messenger Came, as directed, with an Indian page Who there had sought his presence, and who now Before him bowing low, besought that he Would hasten whither he was sent to lead— Even to the mansion of a Lady, once Of princely rank, and who, now lying sick Nigh unto death and grievously dismayed, Implored the comfort of his holy prayers.

The Rabbij, ever to the call of sorrow Open as day, arose; and girding straight His robe about him, with the Page went forth; Who swiftly led him deep into the woods That hung, heap over heap, like broken clouds, Along the southern terraces of Hebron: When, through a broad glade suddenly beheld, A wondrous structure, like a Palace, showed Its gleaming front and many-pillared porch, Fretted with sculptured vinage, flowers and fruit, Or rough with figures exquisitely wrought, As by some Phidian hand.

But interposed A certain space in front, and belting all The splendid Structure with its beauty, smiled A low-walled Garden cut with paven paths Into divisions of a hundred shapes, Yet all related, as concurring all To form one perfect whole;—that glowed throughout With rarest aromatic shrubs, and flowers, Effusing odors such as seemed to bring Tidings from Paradise, so rich their fume! While intermixed judi[ci]ously with these At happiest intervals, were pleasant growths Of higher verdure gemm’d with golden fruit, Or bright with silver blooms, and which below Were glassed in fountains gleaming lambent lights, Each like a shield of pearl.

But if thus rich Was all without—within, when he had entered, More wondrous yet was what the Rabbi saw. Halls worthy of Herod the magnificent! With pictured walls, and columns gleaming white As Carmel’s snow, or blue-veined as with life, And from whose capitals suspended, hung Sidonian tissues all with threaded gold Embroidered, or all colored through with dyes Of puce and purple—rich as sunset clouds. And letting off from these, were chambers, filled With pillowy couches of carved citron wood, And tables in mosaic, upon which Were lucent vases of Armenian art, In crystal, and in alabaster, mixed With ewers whose luminous transparency Were stained with inward splendors,—caskets, clamped 183 With ivory corners, and all sprigged with pearls, And myrrhine cups, and lamps with golden globes And feathery branches bearing pine-like cones, From which long radiancies, like spears of fire, Protending, touched as into dazzling life Clasps, viols, mirrors of unspeakable cost— A pomp of orient wealth, till thus beheld, Merely incredible. While in every hall, And in each chamber, passing or ingaged, Were trains of servitors of every clime, Greeks, Persians, Indians, Ethiopeans,—all In richest though in various raiment clad.

Short time for wonder had the Rabbi, ere The Page returned and led him to a still And distant chamber where the sick One lay: Which entering soon, such brilliance met his view As shrunk the costliness of all he had Seen erewhile, though so wondrous. Small indeed This chamber was as with the rest compared Being obviously contrived to fit a nice And personal privacy: but all within Its limits showed a sumptuousness extreme And spoke of lavish habits,—glowing all With purple and gold, or interblazing quick With royal jewels of a sumless worth, Wherewith all things of personal use and wont— The mirrors, instruments, coronets and clasps, Were studded as with stars effulging flames! And in the midst of all this orient pomp, On profuse pillows of the swan’s new down, Encased in the soft silk of Shiraz,—lo, A queen-like Woman lay;—so beautiful, That though upon her moon-bright visage, pain 184 And languor, like eclipsing shadows, gloomed, The ancient Rabbi’s pious heart was thrilled With singular tremours as he looked upon her. Young was she—but alas! from time to time, O’er her fair face a hectic flush would break, To leave that utter pallor which portends The nearness of the tomb.

From youth to age Much had the Rabbi Joseph sought in herbs, And minerals, the virtues they possess, Soothing or pungent; and, by mixing these With much-informed integrity of art, And so completing them as powers, that work In their effects to a salubrious end, Had for all ailments fit medicaments. Of these he offered her what to him seemed Then needful. But the dying Woman said: Alas! I want not these, nor did I send To entreat thy presence, thinking thou couldst cure A malady like mine—so fell and fatal— Yea, fatal as the funeral march of Time! But simply that I might discharge my mind Of a dread secret that has been to me And ever-haunting and most ghostly fear, Hanging and darkening, like an ominous cloud, O’er all the ways and prospects of my life, And which must end it ere the morning come.

The Rabbi answered: If indeed it stand Within my power to serve thee, pour at once Thy sorrows all before me. But if not, And if thy sin hath any mortal taint, Forbear, O Woman, to acquaint my soul 185 With aught that, if advised, it would not seek, Though covetous of knowledge; or with aught That only thenceforth could for horror chase The memory of a man of Israel.

I am, she said, the daughter of thy friend Rabbi Ben Bachai—be his memory blest! But thou has long forgotten one, who once Played at thy side, a laughing child,—and who Is now the widow of a Prince,—a man Whose state was mainly pedigree, being one Of Ishmael’s race. Behold you this abode! Did ever yet the tent-born Arab build Thus for his pride or pleasure? See you all These riches! Tell me; when were such amassed By the grey Desert’s wild and wandering son? Riches he could not even hoard in dreams, As not by him concievable. Ah, no! Deadly and desperate was the game that won them! And with a burning bitterness at best Have I enjoyed them! And how vainly now Would I at last forego them all, to mend My broken peace with a repentent heed In abject poverty! Their very taste, So doubly deadly, as a thing foregone, How vainly now do I abhor—too late!

Thus having spoken, calmly for awhile In her pale loveliness she lay, with looks Dreamily self-involved,—as if her mind Were vaguely roaming in the fatal Past: Then suddenly her beauteous countenounce grew Bedimm’d and dreary, like the eclipséd moon When o’er it passes the predicted wraith; 186 Then darkened worse, and writhed with horrid pangs, Till more and more in this her burning woe She tossed about, as one through all her frame, Madly convulsed! while from her foaming lips Words of so weird a meaning smote the ear Of Rabbi Joseph, that he bowed his head And cast his eyes in holy horror down.

And thus a space, bowed rigidly, he stood As one transfixed—though pitying, and with prayers Strengthening his soul. And when again his looks He lifted to the Woman—lo, that face Which erewhile showed so ghastly-wild and weird, Was cloudless as a summer heaven! The late Dark brow was bright; the late pale cheek suffused As with inevitable roseate bloom; And wondrous more than all, her eyes that seemed Before so sunken and so weary, now Were big with splendors that about her shot Electric influence! while her lips that erst Were thin and faded as autumnal leaves, Now full and crimson, curled with stormy pride, Or quivered with a fierce voluptuousness!

Well might the Rabbi stand in disbelief Of all that now he saw. Could this be she By whom, as dying, he so late had knelt? This bold and beauteous Being that now looked So lovely-fierce—yea, terrible, in all The bright audacity of passionate power? But seeing his amazement, thus she spake.

These are the common changes of the fell And fatal ailment, that with torments strange 187 And varied, as thou seest, is tearing up The dark foundations of my mortal state, And sinking all its structures, hour by hour, Into the dust of Death. But nothing now Is left me, but to meet my nearing doom As best I may, and suffer.

Hearing which The Rabbi in his wisdom knew that she Was troubled thus by the indwelling power Of some strong Spirit of Evil,—and on this Sent straightway for an unction, wherewith erst The famous ancestor whose name he bore— Herod the Great’s chief hakim—had expelled The demon Haunter of the dying King. ‘Twas brought him, and therewith did he at once Anoint her forehead, and her eyes, and touch The tips of all her fingers. Next he made Within a consecrated crucible, A fire of citron wood and cinnamon; Then splashed the flames with incense,—every rite Inspiriting with the influence of prayer. Then, as the smoke arose, he bowed her head Into its coils, that so she might inhale Its salutary odour—till the Fiend That dwelt within her should be thence expelled.

Again a change came over her. Her face Once more grew pale with pain; once more she writhed In burning torment, uttering many words Of most unhallowed meaning! Yet her eyes Were fixed the while, and motionless her lips! Whereby the Rabbi certainly percieved ‘Twas not the Woman of herself that spake, 188 But the dread Spirit that possessed her soul.
