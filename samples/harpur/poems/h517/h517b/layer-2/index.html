Social Charity.

Tis Life’s prime household wisdom not to scorn An erring Friend too sternly. If but tried Severely in some weakness, all will turn To folly sometimes,—and Love’s cheek be dyed With blushes such as for the fallen burn: So much even in the best is there to mourn. And knowing this, we should be sadly kind, Not cruel, in reproof, nor sourly churn Sin into madness. Though in One we find Those gems of worth, high motives, feelings strong, Set in the fine gold of a constant mind? Yet even that one’s prone to starts Of evil so: in the clearest well their lies A sediment—disturb it, and ‘twill rise.

Note. There is no social truth more meaningful, nor more neglected, than that which is figuratively conveyed in the old proverb: Give a dog a bad name—and hang him. No matter whether the ill name be deserved or not, be it but heavily laid upon him, and it will be enough in a vast number of cases to hound him to the gallows. Hence the exceeding great need there is for propounding some enlarged doctrine of Social Charity.

Men (as well as dogs) are seldom so bad as they are reputed to be; and between the best and the worst there is not a great deal to boast of. Of almost any two we compare together, one of them will be found better than the other in some respects, and worse in some; or there will be an interchange of quality in their most characteristic differences in both directions;—an unlikeness undefinably blending into likeness, as in the several colors of the rainbow. But if of any such two in comparison, one of them do turn out every way inferior to the other; that man also,—be assured of it,—will be the least charitable of the twain, when sitting in judgment upon his neighbour.

But the greatest cause of the faultiness of our social charity, is a certain judgmatical self- -reference. That is to say, we are too much disposed to see only those virtues in others which are kindred to our own, or those vices which we ourselves are 114 the most free from. But the proper medium through which to estimate others rightly, is the knowledge,—employed charitably by how few!—that both the virtues and vices of men do very frequently lie in opposite directions, and tend to different objects; that one man may be inclined, either by constitution or habit, to do some good or bad thing, towards which, in particular, another may, from like predisposing circumstances, be wholly disinclined—and vice versa.

Still even our social Charity may be carried too far; carried into that “dangerous levity” of which Doctor Channing somewhere speaks. But he adds, “there is, too, a severity far more common, and perhaps more ruinous.” Indeed is there. Let but the reader ask his experience, whether his most reasonable and virtuous self, has not often been confirmed in his follies rather than convinced and divested of them, by the sour censures of his very best disposed friends and advisers? And the reason thereof was just this: their censures were intemperate. They painted his follies, as, in a similar spirit, they would have depicted the fiends,—blacker than they were; and the natural effect of such a course, was to provoke him to defend them, and thence, by an equally natural process, to like them rather. They were at first but his rake-helly acquain- -tances, from which a judiciously indirect reproof might have detatched him altogether; but having had, under the sting of an over-severe one, to take up the cudgles for them, of course they thenceforth become, as 115 it were, his moral proteges. as well.

Thus to render the censure of an evil its absolute abettor, it is only necessary in most cases, to make it just over-severe enough to arouse the defiance of the delinquent. By affixing the extreme penalty to the theft of the lamb, what do we but lead the culprit to conclude, that he had as well purloin the ewe also? In that case the mere punishment for the lesser offence inevitably suggests the greater one. Our very proverbs ,—old as the hills, should teach us this wisdom. But they do not. Still the over severe one is the pet method of the application of reproof on all hands, from mother Propriety to father Discretion.

Purists and acrimonious wiseacres may shake the head at all this, as mere satire, but I can tell them,—and the wisdom of the future will repeat and enforce the assertion,—that there is a social physic in it, which, if taken heartily, would do even them a whole world of good.
