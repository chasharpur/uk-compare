EARLY SUMMER.

BY CHARLES HARPUR.

‘Tis the early summer season With its skies so clear and blue; And the wide warm fields are glad with corn As green as ever grew; And the hill-side growing wattles are All golden to the view; And the woods are whitened over by The loud Cockatoo.

O there is a conscious smiling In a heaven so clearly blue; And it must—it must be a felt joy That thus comes blooming through The great mother heart of Nature when The golden year is new; And the woods are snowed all over by The white Cockatoo.

Note.—When the forest trees come into bloom—sooner or later, according to the badness of the season—the white cockatoos suddenly appear in some locali- ties in vast numbers; for the blooms of these forest trees, particularly those of the so-called apple, contain a good deal of saccharine matter; and it is upon these, at this season, that the cockatoos feed. And while thus engaged, they will hang more or less thickly all about the wide-spreadboughs of an apple tree— for instance, like large patches, and de- 194b tached fragments of snow. As many as a thousand, perhaps, may be seen hang- ing in this manner about the umbrage of half-a-dozen such trees, standing clumped together; and as many as five thousand, or more, similarly disposed, or multitudinously drifting, as it were, hither and thither in flight, within the compass of a couple of English miles.

What is said above, however, will not apply to an advent of cockatoos in any of the settled districts of the colony; for in all of these they have, on account of their propensity to depredate in the maize fields, been so long and perseveringly warred against by the farmer, as to have become wonderfully thinned down, and in some localities wholly extinct. Their penchant for half-ripe maize, indeed, is absolutely a vice in them, and nearly in its effects as ruinous to their race, as the abject love of rum is to that of their aboriginal neighbour, the fast-disappear- ing blackfellow. Thus a flock of these birds, in a state of half-starvation, will hang about a maize field on the borders 195a of the forest during a whole season; whereby, not only do many of them meet with destruction at the hands of the ex- asperated owner, but all the rest are so harassed and ill-fed in consequence of his watchfulness, as to be prevented from propagating.

But to come to the matter, I wish more particularly to put ito print: When a flock of cockatoos are thus hanging about a maize field, and competing in cross purposes with its much-vexed owner, one of their characteristics is especially remarkable. They have plainly a language, or system of vocal articulation, however limited in compass. For example, when they are routed out of the maize by an approach of its gun- bearing proprietor, or some one on his behalf, the whole flock, with the excep- tion of a single scout, will retire in a body into the bordering wood—the scout remaining perched upon some tree in the foreground which commands a full view of the maize field, and is at the same time, well out of the range of any shot that might be discharged from it. And now, pending the farther issue of the matter, he and his mates in the wood keep up a constant and very significant conversation. If the farmer seems dis- posed to remain in the field, he thereupon informs them, by a series of utterances with peculiar terminations, that such appears to be the state of the case, and enjoins them (it would seem) to be as patient as possible, and keep safely where they are; and to this bit of sen- sible advice they, or some of them, respond with an ejaculation that is very evidently equivalent in its meaning to 195b ’all right,’ we are attending to what you say. Or, if the farmer endeavours to dodge them by enshrouding himself somewhere amid the maize, the scout, by another set of sounds, conveys to them an account of this new turn in the affairs, advising them to remain still where they are, until such time as he shall have made a flying survey of the entire field. This part of the business becomes very amusing. He will now leave his perch, and fly at a good height hither and thither over every part of the field, peering searchingly down, as he does so, with the knowing air of a cockatoo who has a great responsibility upon his wings, and who is not easily to be done; and sure enough, if but a leg or an arm of the ambushed enemy be left unmasked, he will inevitably perceive it, and send the result of his search to his listening fellows in the background, through the sudden medium of a curt and most discordant vocable. But if it appear to him, after due search in this way, that the enemy is really non est, and the coast clear, he will return to his watch-tree, and indulge in a jolly string of word-like articulations, obviously differing in their termination from any hitherto used; and no sooner will he have done so, than the whole flock will come suddenly swarming up out of the wood, to renew their depredations upon the maize-field.

The above account of some of the characteristics of the cockatoo will, I doubt not, appear strange to many; yet it is in nowise a fanciful one—but literally true.
