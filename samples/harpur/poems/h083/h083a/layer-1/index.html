Crowds

I’m not unsocial, yet dislike a Crowd: It may be that I’m over sensitive, And love the more in solitude to live Because I love to think aloud, And throw my spirit’s motions out As men do in the forest high, Or ’mid broad pastures under the wide sky, Without a furtive look about, To forestal, as it were, some critic ear or eye.

Note. I more than dislike—I hate a crowd. Its animal furore disgust me. Did I even otherwise affect a crowd, the blindly incautious, fly like manner in which it ventures upon and starts away from danger, would both irk and shame me. Then its heady, rushing, impulsive, heedless curiosity annoys me as much. Its very joy is [unclear] hideous; while its rage is one of the most brutal and merciless manifestations under heaven. Hence I avoid crowds, as all do who dislike to look upon beastly faces bloated with either a f stupid, a fierce, or a wild excitement; and such faces make up the middle mass—the thick—of all the faces we ever behold in a crowd. Still, of course, there are differences in crowds, according to the spirit that has gathered them together. For example, an assemblage met for some laudable purpose, however numerous, is not only made up of better faces, , but is less pervaded by that animal fume which is so perceptible in the crowd at a public ex[e]cution, or a pugilistic encounter,—that strong, strang, fleshly odour, the smell as it were of sin.
