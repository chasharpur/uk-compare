THE DEATH OF SHELLEY.

I.

Fit shroud for thee Was the eternal sea! Fit dirge the Tempest’s slave-alarming roll! For yokeless as the waves alway Thy thoughts went thronging forth, as they Were marching to the trumpet of the universal soul!

II.

Yet tell me, Spirit bright, Was Fate not sad for thee? That day, veiled not the sun his light When rolling over Italy? Paled not the conscious moon, that night, When gazing on the doomful sea? Feeling for once the eclipsive might Of Death’s disastrous spell? Resolve me—for from under them Was never reft away a richer, purer gem, Inrayed with a so deep peculiar light, Such as no words may tell! Nor never in their day and night Did Ruin work before such spite— Never triumphed over aught they loved so well!

III.

Thus from an Austral mountain’s cone, A Minstrel of that region rude Poured for the dead his questioning moan, Even as gushed by times From his full heart the unmeasured rhymes; And silent then, and listening, stood. But all unanswered was the strain— Only that in like melancholy mood Echo, afar, its questions asked again: And soon the summery mirth Of winds and leaves and Nature’s murmurous broods Was heard alone;— Upcoming from the streamy plain And from the dusky woods: While down upon the gladdened earth The sun in cloudless glory shone, And the young moon, beyond the main, Sat smiling on her silver throne.

IV.

Folding his fingers tremulously hard For passionate complaint, the mournful Bard Resumed his mournful lay: And—Ah, so soon to fleet away To Death for ever dumb! In the broad noon tide of his day, With much to build within—without to overcome! This was the burthen of the doleful song That wailed along: When from a sphered and nameless light That, as it floated by, Waned like the moon, and vanished from his sight, A stilly voice made sweet reply.

“Whom the Gods love die young! Flowers blighted fall where weeds still thrive apace! The battle is not always to the strong, Nor to the swift the race! But where the odour of the flowers remain Can they not give even to Regret A sweet consolatory grace? Nor vainly forth was the lost battle set, Nor vainly urged the race, Whence flow inspiriting examples yet.”

V.

Yes; Poesy and Passion’s darling Son, Though thou didst walk the world as one Proscribed by stars unkind, While mitred Persecution dread Raged—an incarnate Fiend—behind! With Ignorance, her slave abhorred! And who, in mercy as they said, With many a muttered mystic word And baleful glance upthrown, Petitioned Heaven to thunder down Its wrath on thy devoted head! Yes; though thus tried, most gentle one, This was the meed for thee by Destiny designed: That men, to nobler motions born, Should well atone their fathers’ scorn, By glorying in the loving might of thy surpassing mind.

VI.

All then was well, Though brief—too brief thy stay! Thy name remains, a strengthening spell To all who, banding against Error rude, Would do the unwilling world some good, Nor idly pass away— A vapour, nothing more,—a cloudlet grey— A dull and wasting stain in the blue dome of day! And though the heart and brain be food For hungry Death, where erst the sisterhood Of thy bright dreams—a seraph choir—did dwell, What light around on these, remaining, fling? For lovelier splendours never fell In star-drops from Urania’s wing! And Freedom in her promised age Shall constellate her spheres with glories from thy page.

VII.

But hark! yet from her haunted cell Built on the dubious brink of the Unknown Where Fraud and Fear and Frenzy dwell, Superstition’s sullen bell Tolls thee to her deepest hell! Blind Fury! she alone Can darkly dare to think A soul like thine, though in its earthly shell Bedimmed by many an error, Should at her bidding sink Lossward in penal terror! Enough!—Wherever Love may soar Beyond the grave of our mortality, There is it now—there will it be For evermore!

1840

CHARLES HARPUR.
