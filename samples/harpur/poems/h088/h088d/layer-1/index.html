The Death of Shelley.

1.

Fit winding sheet for thee Was the up-heaving eternal Sea— Fit dirge the Tempest’s slave-alarming roll! For yokeless as the waves alway Thy thoughts went sounding forth, as they Were marshalling to the trumpet— Were marshalling to the trumpet of the universal soul.

2.

Yet tell me, Spirit bright, Did Nature sorrow not for thee? That day, veiled not the sun his light When rolling over Italy? Paled not the conscious moon, that night, When gazing down upon the doomful sea? Feeling for once the drear eclipsive might Of Death’s disastrous spell? Resolve me—for from under them Was never reft away before a richer, purer Gem Than was thy being, wherein love did dwell, 196 With joy, and natural piety as well,— Inraying it with a so wildly bright, So sweetly deep peculiar life, Such as no words may tell! And never in their day and night Did Ruin, with the Beautiful at strife, Work before such horrible spite! Never trod down at once so much of musical delight!

3.

Thus from an Austral mountain’s topmost stone A Minstrel of that region rude, Poured for the dead his questioning moan, Even as from his full heart, by times, Gushed forth the impulse-modulated rhymes, And silent then, as he were listening, stood. But all unanswered was the strain, Only that in like melancholy mood Echo, afar, its questions moaned again: And soon the summery mirth Of winds and leaves and Nature’s murmurous broods, And waters bubling to the wild refrain Of wingëd creatures happy from their birth In man-less mountain solitudes, 197 Was heard alone;— Upcoming from the dusky woods Where in their shadowy depths quick watery glimpses shone: While down upon that primal scene remote The sun’s unclouded splendor gloried free, And the young moon, as in a silver boat, Sat dim, but smiling,—eastward o’er the sea.

5.

Folding his fingers tremulously hard For com passionate complaint, again the Bard Resumed his wildly mournful lay: And—Ah! to be hurried so soon away To Death ever clammy and cold as clay— To Death ever blind, and deaf, and dumb, In the high noon of a disclouded day, With much to build within,—without to overcome! This was the burthen of the doleful song That wailed along: When from a sphered and nameless light That, as it floated in its mystery by, Waned like the moon and vanished from his sight, A stilly Voice made sweet reply:“Whom 198 “Whom the Gods love die young! Flowers wither where weeds thrive apace! Nor is the battle always to the strong, Nor to the swift forever sure the race! Yet if the odors of the flowers remain, Are they not, even to Regret, A sweet consolatory gain? Nor vainly forth was the lost battle set, Nor was the lost race urged in vain, Whence flow inspiriting examples yet.”

6.

Yes, Poesy and Passion’s darling son, Though thou didst walk the world as one Proscribed by stars inimical to mankind, While mitred Persecution, dread And deadly, raged in mortal hate behind! With Ignorance, her dull slave abhorred; And who, in mercy as they said, With many a muttered madly-mystic word, And venegeful, hot, God-wounding glance upthrown, Implored the heavens to thunder down Their christian wrath on thy devoted head! Yes, though thus tried,—thyself—so gentle and kind! 199 This was for thee the meet memorial crown By the Great Spirit of all good designed; That men, to nobler motions born, And more to a world-wide charity inclined Should well reverse their fathers’ bigot scorn, By yearning o’er thy story, And learning thence how gnome-like ars spirits freedom-blind; And by glorying in the glory Of thy love-illumined mind.

7.

All then was well—yea, very well: Though brief—too brief, here on the earth, thy stay! Thy name is with us, for a strengthening spell To all who, banding against Wrong’s bad brood, Would do the unwilling world some good, Nor idly pass away— A vapour, nothing more,—a cloudlet grey By every wind transformed and drivin— A dull and wasting stain in the blue dome of heaven. And though the heart and brain be food For hungry Death, where erst the sisterhood Of thy bright dreams (a seraph choir) did dwell, What light around us these, remaining, fling! 200 For lovelier splendors never fell In star-showers from Urania’s wing, And flourishing Freedom, in her golden age, Shall constellate her spheres with glories from thy page!

8.

But hark! yet from her ghostly cell, Built on that dubious brink of the Unknown Where mystic Craft and Fear and Frensy dwell, Cowled Superstition’s sullen bell Tolls thee to her deepest hell! Blind Fury! she alone Can darkly dare to think A soul like thine, though in its earthly shell Bedimmed by many an error, Should at her bidding sink Lossward,—down, in penal terror! Enough! wherever Love may soar Beyond that mound which mortals blench to see— That last low mound on Time’s change-beaten shore, There is thy Spirit now, fire-wing’d and free, And there a shining dweller shall it be For evermore!
