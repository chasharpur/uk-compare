In advancing these, however, my object is not so much to reason men out of any belief whatever, as to reason them into a rational liberality of thought—and conduct in respect to all those who may perchance differ from them, however widely. To begin:—I myself believe in the existence of a God, the one great Creator and providential Disposer of all things. Nothing, I apprehend, could shake me from this belief; but am I therefore to denounce the atheist, who in all social repects may be a better man than myself,—seeing that many so believing, according to the general notion, have been singular for the moral beauty and purity of their lives. By such a course, what do I more than instigate him in turn to denounce his denouncer, till between us, Charity,—that which should be “all mankind’s concern”, is hunted from our homes. If it be a damnable sin to disarm any established or peculiar idea of the Deity, upon the discoverer’s own head be it at the tribunal of heaven, where Justice and Mercy and Truth are perfect—but not before any earthly one, where prejudice and cruelty and ignorance and error may be alone prevalent and alone powerful. Out of blending contrarity harmony arises, and our moral differences should but constitute the moral music of life: for however individual Truth may be in its essence, it is manifold in its form; and hence to be comprehensively commu- nicated with, it must be perceived through a variety of media answering to the different constitutions of men;- wherefore that Knowledge were alone profoundly and universally applicable which should be a complete com- bination of the results of this perceptive multiformity. But I would previously observe, because of the shocking import of the term Atheist, that perhaps no highly intellectual man is, or ever was, what is commonly understood thereby. Shelley, for instance, was not. He was eminently spiritual in his speculations: and, in fact, his Energy of Love, or Spirit of Good, has many if not all of the attributes with which our best conceptions of the Deity endeavour to characterise his beneficent Supremacy. On the other hand the vulgar irreligionist is never an Atheist. He neither believes nor disbelieves: his intellectual state being, with regard to spiritual things, simply that of a brute indifference. And he, I admit, is always a dangerous animal: which the philosophical atheist, supposing such a being to exist, can hardly be; because, to be such, he must be a reasoner, and therefore so far intelligent as to perceive the beauty of goodness, and that to be socially happy, he himself must be socially just. Reason would infallibly teach him thus much, even though it should unfortunately stop short of the Deity. The ignorant religionist is a being far more dangerous. e two Islands in the midst of a wide sea, several leagues apart, one of which was inhabited, and the other apparently not; the uninhabited one being, at the same time, from the abrupt nature of its coast, wholly inaccessible to the dwellers upon the other. Yet these might perceive in clear weather, from their own Island which they called Earth, that a mighty Tree grew upon the summit of the other which they had named Heaven. Now with regard to this Tree, of the several Earthites who had approached it as nearly as possible, in a large canoe built for the purpose, and named the Treeologist, some declared that its leaves were broad, its blossoms blue, and its fruit oblong; which others of the expedition denied, by affirm- -ing, that the leaves were long and narrow, the blossoms white as snow, and the fruit perfectly round. Upon this, great disputes arose amongst the inhabitants of Earth, and they parted into Sects. A beautiful and winged Virgin who had alighted upon their Island, from the direction of Heaven, and who went by the name of Philosophy, would have reconciled them to these differences, by referring them to contrarities of perception amongst the voyagers themselves, or to some occult property in the object in dispute, or in the atmosphere surrounding it, or to some spectatorial bias of position severally and 22 progressively obtaining:—but in vain! Still one Sect continued to defame another by the appellation of Non-Treeists; because, said the disputants on all sides, to controvert our description of the Tree of Heaven, is virtually to deny it either leaves, blossoms or fruit—the very concession of these, with an irreconcilable difference, being, in effect, to invalidate the existence of the Tree itself. While, with reference to the whole dispute, the more apathetic of the Islanders contented themselves with saying: Be it as it may; we care not. Again: I

may opine that the believer or disbeliever of a particular creed is wrong—miserably wrong! But the reasons which satisfy me of this, do not satify him: Nay, the very feelings which pre= dispose me for the reception or rejection of certain evidences, either may not exist in him at all, or exist so modified by circumstances, as to produce contrary convictions.:—And what then? Am I to endeavour for that cause to get him hissed into silence by the advantage of numbers, or starved conventionally into conformity? What can such a mode, at the best but make Truth a tyrant; and at the worst, but instigate him, and others, to use the like means upon like occasions? And thus we see, that a speculative difference, if pushed into coersive act, eventuates 23 in just this: that it is the right of Might to impose and enforce whatever it can, not in matter of action only, but of mere opinion also. But does

not such a principle, thus stripped of the sophisms of selfishness, of arrogance, of superstition, and beheld in its essential deformity, revolt every honest mind, and disgust every generous heart? No one, then, should be socially injured or legally amerced, either in fact or in effect, on account of his consciencious opinions of a speculative kind, however widely they may differ from those of others. If left, upon their own merits, to the universal freedom of discussion, they will assuredly stand or fall by the virtue or vice that is in them: and Truth, therefore, as it is progressively developed by the mental energies of ages, should be their only, as it is their proper test.
