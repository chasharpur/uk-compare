ORIGINAL POETRY.

THE ONE THING NEEDFUL.

(Inscribed to Mr. C. E. Walthall, Tobacco Manufacturer, Maitland.)

Good living is prime without doubt, And gold more desirable still; ‘Tis sport for a prince to kick out A dun after paying his bill:

That peace is divine, all allow, Friendship, too, is an excellent thing; Yet as pleasant sometimes is a row, And a fight, in or out of the ring:

‘Tis rousing to sail on the sea For a day, or it may be, a week— That is, with no rocks on the lee, And no danger at all from a leak:

‘Tis delightful to witness the day Arise, as the mountains we pass— When towards breakfast we’re posting away, And there is not much dew on the grass:

‘Tis stirring to witness a storm With its thunder-glooms over us float, Provided we’re cozy and warm, That is—clad in a double-great coat:

‘Tis joyous to sport for a day, If ’tis neither too cold nor too hot, And the kangaroos meet you half way, And the birds have a taste to be shot:

‘Tis highly refreshing to rest A whole month at the house of a friend— Provided his wit’s of the best, And his dinners all good to the end;

And that, if he’s blest with a wife, She knows what to his visitor’s due, And has daughters, all brimful of life, And all mightily taken with you:

A night with a dozen, or so, Of wits is a polished delight; But, to make it the clean thing, you know, One must shine as chief star of the night:

‘Tis fine, too, to make a long speech To a crowd—if top orator there; And finer (ask parsons) to preach, If the hearers are wealthy and fair:

(From which, by the bye, it appears, That the souls of parishioners poor Are naught to Sir Thousand-a-year’s And his lady’s—the gems of the cure:)

‘Tis bliss thrice distilled to deceive A doctor who thinks you will die; ‘Tis consoling to laugh in one’s sleeve, And to mouth at “The Cove” on the sly:

Cent. per centum is joy for a Jew, Say Christians;—I wish they would show More ruth when a bill becomes due, On which they get ninety or so:

There is joy too in hatred sometimes, And in scandal, alas, that there should! And eke, in the church-going chimes, To those who would cheaply be good:

‘Tis prideful a debt to discharge, Contracted a twelvemonth ago, When the total is not very large, And the pocket is not very low:

‘Tis exalting to dare to the strife One whose anger is not to be stirred; ‘Tis easing to threaten a wife, When too far away to be heard:

‘Tis tickling, when mischief’s agog, And one scarcely knows what to be at, To kettle the tail of a dog, Or to shorten the ears of a cat:

(But in a parenthesis here, Thus much just permit me to say, Such jokes, oft to boyhood too dear, I have passed not this many a day:)

‘Tis sweet to steal fruit, at a time When your mother don’t know you’re out; And sweeter, perhaps, ’tis to rhyme Of the girl one’s half crazy about:

And sweeter than all is a smile From the darling your rhymes are upon— If the writer suspects not the while That’s she’s taking a goose for a swan:

But, oh, to be thoroughly blest, To improve e’en the pleasures of love— To be rapt like a saint in his rest, One must smoke good tobacco, by Jove!

Charles Harpur.

Jerry’s Plains, 28th November, 1843.
