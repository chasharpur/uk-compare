The New Land Orders. (1847.)

I long did hope the soil of this bright Clime, Being unenfeoffed to Oligarchial sway, Was a meet cradle for the birth sublime Of just Equality at no distant day: The which, when once its truthful beauty’s ray Maturely shone, should southward draw the prime Regard o’ the world, thence wiser for all time, And studious more to join the harmonious lay Of Perfect Freedom perfectly begun: But now this Hope is shrunk into a Fear! England’s misgovernment its worst hath done To sow the seed of splendid evil here! In Sheepshanks we behold a destined peer, And Oxtail’s stockmen shall “my-lord” his son. x

x Should the Squatting System be restricted by no better 41 Regulations than those deplored in this Sonnet, all the lands beyond the boundaries will inevitably fall within the grasp of one class alone—the great Stockholders, “to them and to their heirs forever!” and in that event, the national future of Australia will be but a bad copy of the national perditions of Europe. How long will our shopkeepers and yeomen fail to view this momentous question in its true light? Can they think that it matters little how, or by whom, these vast tracts are held, so long as plenty of beef and wool is grown upon them; is if social progress and the vulgar “go-a- head” impulse were identical? If so, they err greviously—and to the detriment of unborn millions. Besides, a splendid is never a happy land. Immense inequalities in the proprietorships of its soil are the arch curses of England, giving birth but to two national monsters,—a heartless magnificence in the rich, and a social hope= lessness in the poor, which become in turn the parents of an au= -dacious denial in the one class of the common claims of humanity, and a sullen distrust even of God’s Providence in the other, which “cannot love (it is thought) the wretch it starves” in the very heart of abundance. Again I say, a splendid is never a happy land, because wherever there are palaces there are hovels: and this should determine us in Australia to have as few of either as possi- -ble. Nay more. Wherever even mansions are very numerous, and gentlemen in the useless sense of the term exceedingly plentiful, there also poorhouses abound, there also paupers starve in heaps. Such, universally, is the condition of England: and our present and future legislators should perpetually propound to themselves the question, Whether the same national mockeries—patrimonial pomp on the one hand and hereditary destitution on the other—are to be extended into Australia? By keeping this constantly at heart, they will assuredly grow up to something better, and that speedily. And it is the more urgent upon them to do so, from such an extension of its national structure being palpably the ulterior design of the Home Government. But shall it succeed?

C.H.
