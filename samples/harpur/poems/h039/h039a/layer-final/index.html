The little Poem which follows in this section is hardly moral in the vulgar acceptation of the term:—that is, supposing some of the concluding [a]llusions to be exclusively credited with a sexual significance. Hence I have been more than once on the verge of sacrificing it upon the altar of Puritan- -ism; but my good opinion of it as poetry came as often to the rescue. 697c x [And I said to myself, “Why should I defer to that morality] which is but a cowardly strait-jacketting of human nature, [unclear] effect is either to cramp it into some deformity, or fret it into some secretive yet gross excess? 698b x Why should the healthy blood-warmth of it be be sacrificed to the mere in danger of prostitution? Or if it be so misused, who is to blame? Be the evil with him whose imagination is tainted, and whose thoughts are impure.” 697d Besides, I have a notion that all poetic beauty,—all artistic excellence, is essentially moral and moralising. This position may be best illustrated by an extreme instance from the art of Painting. Let us take a nude picture of surpassing female loveliness—an Eve in the garden of Eden or a Diana fresh from the bath. Now even in this case, if I am not greatly mistaken, all sensual stimulation will generally become subordinated to a moral feeling and conclusion of artistic triumph A refining sense of the Beautiful will be the permanent impression of the picture. Let us also take an instance from Sculpture—even the world-famous Apollo. In this case, it is universally acknowledged, that all sense of sexual revelation or personal exposure, is instantly merged in a moral tribute to the Artist’s conception of divine beauty as manifested in his work. And the 698 final effect of even poetical cyprianandes of the best kind is very similar. Though in design they may have been the offspring of a moral defection in their authors, they do yet, by virtue of their artistic beauty, indirectly moralise, because they intellectualise, those very appetites which are the objects of their luxurious appeal: they so far defeat their animal grossness, as they do undeniably breathe into their essence, the ethereally conservative influence of the imagination. Moreover, “to the pure all things are pure.”
