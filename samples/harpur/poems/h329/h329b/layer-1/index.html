Winter and Summer or Neither will do.

Winter, I hate thee! In thy frigid lot there is Little that’s pleasant and less that is cherishing! Give me the Summer! no matter how hot it is: Heat may distress one; but cold!—it is perishing! 116 Summer, I hate thee! thy sun it so fiery like is Drought too’s a thing worse than language can tell it is! Give me the Winter! no matter how miry ’tis Cold may be perishing; but heat!—what a hell it is!

Note. Are these squib verses good? They ought to be so, at all events; seeing that to improve the joke and make it the more wilful, they are constructed upon a principle of strict metrical quantity:—a versification-feat quite practicable in our said-to-be-unpliant English, notwith= =standing the several failures of the learned Bob Southey.

And I would note here, that the metrical structure of many of our common Nursery Rhymes is singularly quan= =titative. Some of them indeed, in this respect, are quite a study. The following one, for instance,—the Song of Sixpence,—has a striking recurrence in the metres through= =out, and in accordance too with the subject, such as it is: the first three lines of each stanza, taken together, being strictly quantitative, and the fourth of each tallying exactly.

Sing a song of sixpence—a pocket full of rye: Four and twenty blackbirds—were baken in a pie: When the pie was opened—the birds began to sing, And wasn’t that a dainty dish to set before the King?

The King was in his office—counting out his money: The Queen was in the parlour—eating bread and honey: The Maid was in the garden—hanging out the clothes And there came a little blackbird and snapt away her nose.
