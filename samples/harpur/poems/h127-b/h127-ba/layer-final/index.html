[THE FAMOUS NIGHT SCENE IN THE VIII ILIAD]

II.

AS MORE CLOSELY RENDERED IN BLANK VERSE.

The sweating steeds are loosened from the yoke And bound with halters, all to their own chariots. Out from the city oxen and fat sheep Are meanwhile brought, with wine and home-baked bread. Then gathered faggots into fires are built, From which the savour of their supper soon Spreads o’er the plain upon the winds of heaven, While in the cheerful blaze, in ranks of war, Greatly elated feast the Trojan troops.

As when aloft the golden stars appear Keenly outshining round the lucid Moon, And not a breath under all heaven is blown, Distinct, the jagged rocks, and lofty tops Of mountains, with the darker woods, are seen Outlined against the ether’s wide expanse, Thus luminous, thus alive with crowding lights, Whose nimble points, all intershooting, blend Into one hoary shine: while in his watch Gratefully gladdened is the shepherd’s heart.

Over the wide extended plain, even thus, So many and thick, the Trojan-kindled fires Before the towery walls of Ilium shine, And ‘twixt the ships and the lit streams of Xanthus: A thousand fires up-blazing on the plain, While in the red glare of each fire there sit Full fifty armed men. But ranged apart, Beside the chariots o’er their fodder bide The steeds, unyoked;—as all awaiting so The beautiful-thron’d Aurora’s orient rise.
