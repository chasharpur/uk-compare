POEMS BY CHARLES HARPUR.

I.

LOVE’S DOINGS.

I know not why it is so— Indeed, I only this know, There’s something in the stars and moon That with Love never jars in tune! No: but they seem to smile and say, “Kiss and be happy while ye may!” As though thus placed above us, By proxy they would love us. And hence not one to glow is seen That hath not been the go-between To some young pair, when first a gazing Alone together at them blazing! Who, having duly cried—”Amazing!” Then drew their wonder from the skies To pour it in each other’s eyes, And tremble with the bliss then That clung about a kiss then.

See two just ripe for wooing, For billing and for cooing, Go forth to pay the dutiful Unto the Night so beautiful! And all its splendours so to see, Their very chat is poetry! She, wonder-rapt in Nature’s, Forgets her own sweet features; But he, from all things bright above, Soon turns him to the light of love That streams from her orbed eyes while gazing At those heavenly wonders blazing! And when, at length, she sighs—”Amazing!” And full on his turns those lit eyes, At once, to his own sweet surprise, The happy dog has kissed her, As though she were a Sister.

In wonder at his daring, Of pouts she is not sparing: “How could his thoughts deceive him so? I never will forgive him—no! Well, I’m sure! in breeding he Is impudent exceedingly! And were it clear unto me He knew what shame he’d do me, I never more would walk with him! Never! nor even talk with him.” But, thus resolved, is’t not “amazing” That she should still continue gazing With him at heaven’s wonders blazing; Although it should be very plain To any reasonable brain, That he’ll offend again so If longer they remain so.

‘Tis even as I said now! And all with anger red now, She says, “I’m used exceeding ill! I’ll tell my Ma—indeed I will!” But though she blamed and pouted so, She never told about it though! And for this after reason All through that summer season, She strolled yet with the knave himself, To—teach him to behave himself, When with a lady friend a-gazing At heaven’s noctural wonders blazing:— And found his aptness quite “amazing!“ Wherefore I’d swear, that in the time Spread over by this jolly Rhyme, She suffered kisses seven For each prime star in heaven!

1840.
