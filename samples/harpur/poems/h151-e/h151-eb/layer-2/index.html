[GENIUS LOST. THE SORROWS PART TWO: THE BOOK]

(make this the second)

Away, fond Book! thou talkst of mirth & joy, And peace and love;—what mockery to me! O it is irksome to the child of want, To hear some full-blown author, sitting soft On easy chair, with cate in hand, and wine Before him, mantling in its christal fount, And raying inward, like a wealth of riches Smelted and running into one red mass Of intervolving light,—to hear him, thus Complacently mellifluous, numbering o’er In his nice-paring and most formal rhymes, As on his fingers, the so many good, And lovely, and pleasant, and adorning things To which the heart is heir! What mockery To him who finds with Misery his account! What peace, what joy have I? What recompense For all the waste of feeling in a young 32 And social heart, ever intent to build Schemes of unutterable kindness, yet Doomed only to inhabit solitude, And languish thus alone?

The stormy petrel A thousand leagues from land amid the sprays Of wild seas that no voyager hath yet Scarr’d with his keel, is less alone than I In the man-crowded mart, or sabbath walk At even,—nay, in the familiar paths Of daily life! The bannd and vagabond fox,— Even he hath portions that in contrast scoff At my life’s poverty! Yon eagle, though He saileth now along the only speck On the wide verge of heaven, hath yet a mate Poised on some distant rock to watch his coming And welcome back the aeronaut! But who Regards the passages of my wild fate? And if I reap a feeling of delight From the rich influence of arising, or 33 Of setting suns; or from the melody Of morning birds, or from the vernal sweets Of evening flowers, and smile,—who smiles with me? And if I turn, and sit me down to weep Over my broken fortunes, and the dimmed And vanishing rainbows of my earlier hopes, Whose loveliness Time heeded not,—there’s none Will mix a tear with mine!—Away, fond Book! Thou prat’st of mirth and joy, and peace and love,— What mockery! Go!

Here rather be it mine With objects to converse that hold through ruin A kind of fellowship, reflecting back Through many a mystic feeling of the past My fate’s drear tendency. ‘Twould seem indeed That som such relevance, in secret, had Hither attached my wandering feet; for fast By yon old willow trees that slant their growth Over the stream’s abrupt and mossy bank, And,—their redundant tresses downward all, Massing impervious—solid shadows cast 34 Upon its waters where with darkling gleam They deepen into stillness, even as shades Of horror dusk the secret soul of Guilt,— And near to where is heard the mournful note Of some lone water-fowl, and low, at times, A solitary splash, as with its weight The seeded flags sways downward;—even there Yet stands The Rose tree! which, once, in a mood Industrious, and filled with boyish glee, I planted: and long cherished,—till, at last, It withered like my hopes. And O, ’tis strange, And yet most true, that I did grow to have A vague but haunting notion that as it Did flourish in the future, so should these.

How freshly yet in Memory’s glass I see Reflected, even through the stain of tears, Each joy-inspiring circumstance that streaked My novel Being on that summer eve,— One of those placid eves whose cool delight 35 Doth seem distilled from Heaven’s serenest depths, As though the peace of the eternal world Were passing into ours, and stilling down Its turmoil to unwonted adoration! I see myself, a child, as then I bent Busily mute, and with important hand Mounding the crumbled mould of richest sort About my leafy client. All the while, Weighed on their burnished pinions overhead, The passing birds, observant, trilled abroad Their choicest songs, and round about, as charmed By their smooth-warbled eloquence, the flowers, Sweet prodigals! thick fumed their balmiest smells. And when at length, my pleasant labor done, Clapping my hands for perfectness of joy I bounded up exultant,—in Day’s last Resplendent glances, the proportions huge Of all this mighty prospect swam at once, Enormous, on the wide dilating eyes 36 Which spake that moment’s rapture:—yon rude hills Uppiled against the South, then touched along Their outlines with a lambent fringe of fire; Yon slopes o’erbuilt with villages, and dark Beneath the plough, or bright with cereal gold; With these the stream that, lapsing westward, flashed Through yon high woods with massive turrets topt, And spires, grey rising into the calm air, Or flaming up against the reddening clouds That isle-like hung beyond the western edge Of yon bare down, powdered with flocks as now, And drawing with a semicircling sweep Its dun and rolling outline, like a sea, Against the dusky North;—all these at once Swam in their various aspects into one Vast picture, on the wide dilated eyes Which spoke that moment’s rapture!—and I cried Be thou, sweet Tree, of this my favorite haunt The future elegance! And with my growth 37 It grew, luxuriant,—but to fade thus soon. Ah, blasted witness of departed joys! No more shalt thou, what time the cuckoo’s heard, Breathe an imparadising perfume round, And with thy blushing families compose A manifold gem conspicuous in the fresh And lovely crown of Spring! nor he who then Fostered thy beauty, be so glad again: For Time who brought the blight to thee, hath cast A canker on his being, which hath seared Each bloom of fancy and each smile of heart.

To be added to the third Monologue.

Thus, in the morning of my life, I have No happiness rooted in the earth, to hold My nature to the actual. All my hopes Are hurled away,—out of the world, to seek Such solace as may be derived from far And lonely flights of Faith. Yet even these Only divert, not satisfy, my soul. Still when her wings refuse them, wearied out By so wild will’d a voyager as I, 116 Having no nearer comfort, ev’n as now, I meditate their influence, though foregone, And trace them upward in their heavenly track Through the similitudes of many things. As through an ocean of uprotting mist Amid the morning Alps, a morning bird Keeps soaring, trustful of the risen sun, That then is turning all the mountain tops To diamond islands washed by waves of gold That shatter as they surge,—keeps soaring, till It shoots at length into the cloudless light, And gleams a bird of fire: So Faith upmounts Through the Earth’s misty tribulations,—up Into the clear of the eternal world; Unfainting, fervent—till with happy wings Dispreading full amid the rays of God, It glories, gleaming like the alpine bird. But wearying in its flight, even Faith returns, As does the bird—returns into the mist 117 That shutteth down all less adventurous life: But stronger for the mighty vision left, And livelier for the warmth upon her wings.
