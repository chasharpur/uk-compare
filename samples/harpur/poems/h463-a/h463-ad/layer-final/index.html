III.

Milton.

[His poetical Style.]

In this what a grave beauty claims our praise, Through the long march of his stupendous Lays. How full—how fit the epithets; how terse Each phrase, as built into the solid verse. Then in his management of tune and time How richly can we spare the oil of rhyme. What melody of measures; flowing still Into one lengthened harmony—until Some fine, up-rolling redundancy shall fill 57 (Even like a heaping wave when surging near) With doubled thunder the astonished ear! Which thence more soothable, is soothed once more By cadences as silvery as before. Such graces these of Style, that wanting such, Even true poesie will lack too much.

But of his “stiffness” what made Johnson bawl, So that the charge is grown proverbial? A rigid narrowness of taste, combined With a tripodic tunelessness of mind, That just a see-saw couplet’s limbs could scan, Filed up and jingled on the Grub-street plan; Or a spruce quatrain, alternating nice— But not the numerous peals of the Lost Paradise! For these, indeed, he had nor ear nor soul, He could not meet mete their interflowing roll— Each being (try them, all who yet may doubt) A piece of “linkëd sweetness long drawn out.”
