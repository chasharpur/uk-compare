ORIGINAL POETRY.

THE SNOW CHILD.

A SWISS LEGEND.

BY CHARLES HARPUR

Beware child, in your gambols, Of the fatal rift below:— Thus warned his boy, a Switzer, Amid the piles of snow.

The snow will never harm me, Replied the wayward child, As back upon his father He gayly looked and smiled.

And forward then he started Like a spirit through the drift, And many a wild bound ventured, Unmindful of the rift.

Till in his heart the father, Said with a feeling wild, Can you be, my own Herman, So like a phantom child?

And then he looked a moment, To where, with thund’rous jar, An avalanche had bounded From a glittering Alp afar.

He had looked away,—a moment Did hardly intervene; And lo! the little Herman Was nowhere to be seen!

With a great nameless horror, Crushed in upon his heart, He stood;—then shook the glaciers With an upward shuddering start.

As o’er the snow he shouted, The little Herman’s name— But from those dreary echoes The only answer came!

Still, ‘Herman!’ cried the father, As he madly rushed about— But only some wild cavern Responded to the shout.

And all his kindred sought him, Day after day in vain; The wayward little Herman Was never seen again.

And never on the mountains Did his sire again rejoice, Filling their icy gorges With his bold and manly voice.

His little Herman’s image, There buried in his pride, Lay frozen on his vision, Until the day he died.

And aye till then he sought him, Spring after Spring in vain— The little Herman’s body Was never seen again.

But often the bold Switzer, Amid the snow storm wild, Sees through the white drift bounding A lovely phantom child.

Bounding through tha white drift As with a frantic joy; And straightway he bethinks him Of the hunter’s wayward boy.
