SONGS, EPIGRAMS, NOTES, AND OPINIONS, &c., BY CHARLES HARPUR.

No. 8.

1.

Valedictory Bosh.

Some Jack the Tinker’s “going home,” And straight his friends, Nick, Tom, and Neddy, Must to a public banquet come, With all their jawing tackle ready, To tell the whole world, as the knack is, The very splendid Tinker, Jack is; And what a loss will be his going, Through dishes, platters, trumps, and pannikins, (Just as the need for them is growing) To all our wives, and all our manikins.

Note.—Public breakfasts and dinners to persons departing from our shores, are certainly preferable to those grosser testimonials of character, from the vogue of which, their hint is taken. Yet, at the bottom, and behind the scenes, what set, low-motivated, and got-up affairs, are even these? They are deliberately concerted butterings of the departing individual, at which, his friends are to hold themselves pledged and ready, to daub him with their praises here, as a social passport elsewhere—and, it may be, a shove in high quarters. This is their almost acknowledged object—their inspiration—and hence, the speeches on these occasions, are fulsome and nauseous to a degree: the breakfast or dinner which the several speakers have just eaten, coming back again, through their teeth, could scarcely be more so! In short, the whole matter of them, is a most pitiful ovation of the smallest things—a fussy godification of the inherently petty. One of the speakers would show how wise and good a man, their friend must be, by parading him before them, as his conscience keeper; another would celebrate his fraternal large heartedness, by relating the benevolent manner in which he gave him—the speaker—without fee or reward—an infallible remedy for the finger-ake; a third would prove his public spirit, by referring to the readiness with which he accepted every social honor that was offered him; a fourth would remind them of his liberality, by re-uttering his well known and most magnanimous sentiment— namely, that though a man should differ from himself in religion or politics, he would hardly, for that reason, like to have him brained; a fifth would do justice to his unbounded munificence, by informing all the world, that he had been known to give a penny to a poor blind man, and subscribe a pound to the testimonial of a rich one, would could see very well:—and so on, ad nauseam. Or he is praised, and that highly, for having evinced the mathematical precision of a spider, in the weaving about him of the most selfish gain traps: or for having deliberately chosen to work, rather than starve; or for having passably performed some public service, any marked neglect of which, would have drawn down upon him, both ruin and dishonor; or, for having preached a couple of sermons per week, conamore, for super-excellent pay:—and so on, ad flummery. A man of thorough dignity and refinement, would as leave be spat upon, as be lauded in a manner so set and barefaced. And, after all, such things will only go, for what they are worth— at least, with the judicious. The man of true mark and likelihood, needs no such credentials of character; and knowing the clap-trap nature of their origin, and the countenance they are calculated to give to shams, he would assuredly decline them. He would feel that all they could confer upon him, would be as purely putative as the brass plate which is worn by some supposed aboriginal chief, and which is often bestowed by the Government—or by individuals—upon some poor creature, who, by character and in fact, may be one of the least influential persons in his tribe. He may be, and most probably is, by family a Hibbi, whence the foolish assumption of his royalty, on the part of the donor of the brass plate, aforesaid—the most apt emblem I can conceive of these public testimonials to the illustrious obscure, or to the high and mighty, and altogether marvellous magnates of Liliput.

Moral.

As heathens (pious though they seem) The more they bow to gods of stone, Are thence the less disposed to own A spiritual God supreme; So we (though in a better state) The more we set our eyes to see The moral Small, the blinder we Become unto the moral Great. And when dwarf merits, much we gild, Not only are they pandered to, But merits of a loftier build Are shrunken to their standard, too.
