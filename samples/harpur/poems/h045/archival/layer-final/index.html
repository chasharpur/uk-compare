Original Poetry.

[HUNTING THE LIGHT KANGAROO]

When the day-star has sunk o’er the hills of the west And the morning sky is blue; We’ll off to the sport that we all love best— To chase the kangaroo. For he must be chief of a dastardly crew, That fears to go hunting the light kangaroo.

E’er the break of the day then away to the plains, The plains of the swift emu, For the chiefest of pleasures that this life retains, Is chasing the kangaroo. E’en the child of the mountain, though sable his hue, Loves dearly the hunting the light kangaroo.

Give the miser his gold, give the spendthrift his wealth, Their lot I’d not covet it’s true; But where is the man would not envy the health, Gained by chasing the kangaroo. E’en the sports of the ancients, for all that they knew, Fell far below hunting the light kangaroo.

In the land of the North, let them boast of their deer; Give the Indian his tiger-hunt too; The Southern wants nothing his gay heart to cheer, Save chasing the kangaroo. No bugle for him, but his own wild halloo, As he speeds o’er the plains for the light kangaroo.

Then spring to the saddle boys, blithely and free, And we’ll bound it o’er the dew; And the motto of each native lad shall be, The lively, the gay kangaroo. For I hold that true pleasure, though known but to few,, Lies ever in hunting the light kangaroo.
