The Poet’s Wife.

He.

Mary, before us swells the sea Of Life, at best a changeful state; Then think, ere venturing forth with me Where Love alone can master Fate, How many new and daily cares, And troubles, born of worldly strife, Must be to her as nought, who dares To be the Poet’s Wife.

A self-devoting self-control Must speak her boundless faith in Love; And in the temper of her soul The lioness obey the dove: A mind above Wealth’s golden snares, With trust in Providence through life, Must be her heritage, who dares To be the Poet’s Wife.

She.

Why paint Life’s perils thus to me? I know it is a changeful state; Yet will I venture forth with thee, Where Love, you say, can master Fate. Then speak no more of golden snares, Of lowly toils or worldly strife; They daunt not one /like me/, who dares To be the Poet’s Wife.

He, again.

Then come, my brave confiding Girl, A hero I with such a mate! What though the future round me whirl The most injurious waves of Fate? No more I faint; no more despairs May drown the spirit of my life! Buoyed heavenward by her love, who dares To be the Poet’s Wife.

I was earnestly imaginative; simple of mind, and single of heart. Hence, though not void of ambition, the material magnificence of mere authority had no lures for me: if harmlessly prescriptive, I was indifferent to it; if specious, I despised it; but if absolutely wrong, I hated it aloud. Yet I could greatly venerate what- 31 -ever I could believe in, as being righteous and true. I was affectionate to all-forgiveness— perhaps to weakness. I was proud to my sorrow. I was impolitic to my loss: that is, in worldly matters, for in things spiritual there is nothing in the end to be gained by policy. I was naturally social, though solitary in my habits from wounded good will, and from a shiness superinduced by the ever- -lasting evilness of my circumstances; knowing that men in general can only value the fortunate. I was /as I am still/ an enthusiast in the cause of human liberty and progress; liberty in all directions and progress infinite, though my first faith in the perfectability of the race had been considerably discountenanced by my worldly experience. But however bedimmed by the earthy exhalations of low-motived crowds, while amongst them, it still burned brightly in the clear solitudes of thought. Though of no sect, I was not without religion; it was ever resident in the depths of my nature, like truth in the well—and was drawn thence not unfrequently.
