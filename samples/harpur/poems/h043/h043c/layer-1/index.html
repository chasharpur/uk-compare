I will conclude with a couple of Poems exhibiting that kind of beauty which is so far self existent as to ask only to be perceived. No internal—no mental process is needed to elaborate, or to chasten it into beauty the occular appearances which they chronicle,—for they are themselves the external robes of the Beautiful. And I would here venture a few remarks rebuttive of the notion, that ‘mere descriptive poetry’, as the [unclear] goes, is but a secondary product of the art. The fact is, there is properly no such distinction of kind in poetry. Or rather, all poetry is descriptive;—descriptive of natural appearances, of imaginary or real events, of persons, passions, feelings. Is not even dramatic poetry descriptive? No: say the Critics—And why? Because in dramatic poetry, all kinds of characters, situations, and semblances are made as it were to describe themselves. Their distinction, methinks, must finally come to this; and if so, of what real value is it? That the poetry which is merely descriptive of material appearances nature does not affect us so powerfully as that which depicts intelligent passion, is no doubt true; but the difference even here is one of subject, not of kind. All poetry is descriptive, and of one value as to kind, apart from subject and treatment: and some of the finest passages even in Homer, are certain of his descriptions of natural appear- -ance. And indeed, notwithstanding all that men who are run away with by high German notions, may hold to the contrary, the visible 721 poetry of the world as manifested in a sunset, a leafy wood, a green pasture, a flowing and stream,—is “beautiful exceedingly” in its mere self, and well worthy of artistic appropriation, even to the occasional exclusion of all other and deeper poetical matters.

Dawn in the Forest.

It is the Morning Star, arising slow Out of yon hill’s dark bulk, as born of it. Then glides she forth into the dim sky, leaving A whiteness in her wake that whitens still As she ascends, till all the gloomy woods Are touched along their multiformous outlines By a faint gleaming azure, that instills With a soft clarifying sequence, down Through their dense fleeces of dark heaping leaves And[unclear] branches,—as away beyond them now The dayspring strengthens momently, to spread Into the mightier Morn. Meanwhile the stars, Those golden children of Eternity, From its eclipsing growth are all withdrawn Into the Unapparent. Stronglier still, Though with a gradual increment, works out And downward, even to the grassy ground, That skiey gleam and azure prevalence Which first bespake the Dawn: till all the trees Eastward disposed,—against the brightening east Clearly defined to their minutest sprays,— Stand in unspeakable beauty. Moist with dew, And glinting all with a dim silveriness, The leaves curve out or tremblingly depend From the remote tracery of the boughs, 722 While interfused through all, the clearing light Keeps steeping all in a diviner glow— Diviner every moment;—for beyond The ruddy face of Morning more and more Is breaking into smiles, to fill the world To overflowing with the joy of brightness; And the great soul of Man with a relief Surpassing joy, as thereby given afresh To feel the presence of that greater Soul Which makes all nature, and of which it is Itself an effluence, however far Projected, and devoured by tract of time; Even as a sunbeam’s fountain is the Sun, Whether it hit the earth or glance away Into Infinitude,—shooting on forever.
