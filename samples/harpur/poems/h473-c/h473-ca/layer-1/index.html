Coleridge.

His General Characteristics.

A wizard Poet! One who found too late The wild weird region of his true estate: Weaning, at first, the public ear to wile With Pixie tinklings that provoke a smile, Or by the might (through all his early lays) 353 Of tumid tortuosities of phrase.

Bu

t he grew giant-like, whenas, at length, His soul grew conscious of its pent-in strength— Pent in and irked by the too rigid Real, And thence urged forth into the wide Ideal, To sail its unknown seas, thereon to find Through wild vague agonies of heart and mind, That all seen things are but external shows At whose hid roots true absolute being glows, And thence infer, how from one fountain rains One tide of kindred through all living souls; Human or brute, the mighty and the small, Children alike of God, the loving Sire of All.
