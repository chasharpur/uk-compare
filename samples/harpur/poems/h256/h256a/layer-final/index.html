Songs of Passion.

I.

[A LYRICAL LOVE STORY 1:1]

Yonder’s my Chosen One—look boys!—yon Creature Sphered like a star in the light of her grace! How rosy each ghesture! how gentle each feature! And her figure’s the twin-born delight of her face.

Look in her eyes how the lightning of beauty Keeps mellowing into the azure of love! Till Passion in gazing is tamed into Duty— An eagle turned vassal as twere to a dove.

Then ‘neath her kerchief—O heaven!—behold you, What love-apples, filled by her breathing, are there! Give heroes their glory, and misers their gold too, Of riches and fame be yon Maiden my share.

Purists and fanatics of all kinds will be disposed to quarrel with the warmth of the foregoing songs of Passion. I have no doubt of that. But the purist or fanatic is never a philosopher; neither has he ever much sound worldly wisdom; though is often credited with the possession of it by the acre. There is, however, in this naughty world of ours, even such a thing as irony as well as warmth of passion. But to deal only with the last: It should be cherished as openly as the warmth of the body; being the health of the inward as that is of the outward man. It is the fever heat of both which is alone dangerouse; and a secret indoor sort of suppression is the surest way to engender this in both regards upon any excitiable occasion. The notion which originated in the spurious half of Christianity, 143 that we should war upon and utterly subdue—nay even crucify our passions, is, I verrily believe, the procreant cause of fully two thirds of the moral disease, obliquity, and secret uncleanness, at this moment existing in Christiandom. Who complains so much of the “thorn in the flesh” as the fanatic? Who, even upon his own confession, is so often goaded into sin by it? [When in some conventacle, we see a mistaken creature of this kind, with blazing eyes and flushed cheeks, and distended nostrels, holding forth in the same breath of love and damnation, if we ascribe his paroxism to a burning fervour of pure piety, we shall be very presently quite as far in error as himself. His mild excitement is more probably the torture of suppressed or unnaturally diverted animal passions, howling for vent through the sentiment of veneration itself diseased by the predicament. The crying of his dungeoned human desires smite against the outraged heart of Nature—and lo, they are avenged! [The passions then are not to be subdued, but wisely directed: They are not to be subdued, but wisely directed: They are not to be tyranised ever, but paternally governed. Then free play, in all legitimate directions, is as necessary to our moral heartiness as the out-door action of the joints and muscles is to our physical. Above all, to hold them in perfect and constant health, their due warmth must be openly cherished, both by the wisdom of the philosopher and the rapture of the poet. They are never so liable to an undue warmth as when fresh from the cold cell of the fanatic, or the temptation-proof closet of the purist, or from the moral shower-bath of an over-wise grandam of either sex. In all such cases re-action is inevitable – for it is natural; and if violent, a greater or less degree of fever – fever at all events, must be the consequence. But if purists and fanatics, as I suppose, will be disposed to quarrel with the spirit of the foregoing poem, what will they say to the matter of this note to them? I care not. Human nature has been dwarfed and deseased and strait waistcoated but too long by their wisdom, and it is high time therefore that it should be both philosophically and poetically exploded.

C.H.
