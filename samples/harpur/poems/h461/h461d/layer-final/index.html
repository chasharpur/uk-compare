Prefatory Note.

The style of these Rhymed Criticisms may not be quite pleasing to all readers. Those especially who still think that Pope and his associates were our model versmen, will be apt, I doubt not, to pronounce it loose. Yet I have chosen it advisedly. It is intended to be something betwixt the heroic rhyme of Dryden and that of Cowper, with also a running dash in it of the more open manner of our elder masters of English verse. Hence, though I have partially avoided the super-precise balance of Pope’s couplet, as being in itself, if con- -tinuously adhered to in a poem of any length, a most restrictive and wearisome, though mellefluous, monotony; I have yet considered that somewhat of such a balance should be maintained recurrently, or upon the whole, in our regular heroic rhyme. In other words, this balance, or somewhat approximating to it, should only be frequently varied—by 4 unexpected breaks in the metres, for the sake of a stronger emphasis, or by occasional runnings over from the rhymes, into a more impetuous cadence. But in thus giving freedom and room for invention to the formal manner of these pieces, I have been careful, on the other hand, not to slide too deeply into that blank-verse-rhymed style (if I may coin a characteristic term for it) which was so much used by both Shelley and Keats; because it was just such a failure as might be expected to follow from the meeting of extremes, however well it mo served to indicat, as it were schismatically, the prior existence of an opposite abuse.

Chaucer.

Outstanding from the twilight of his days In bold relief, he takes the student’s gaze, As some old crumbling Abbey glooms apart, A failing wonder to less primal art. And one so intellectual for his age, And studiously happy in the sage Delights of Poesy, most surely claims High mention on the roll of everlasting names.

A well of English undefiled, is he, Said Spencer—but he’s almost Greek to me. And what most moves me while I spell him o’er, Is that great simple faith, which, evermore, Makes him see all things through the Muse’s lore; Together with a crowing, frank delight In primal nature’s beautiful and bright Though daily objects;—for it is the creed I carry always with me as I read, That whoso had for a seer’s eyes Was more than commonly both pure and wise— 6 Was one in whom an earnest joy in good More than redeemed the errors of his blood— Was stored in spirit with a portion rich Of that transcendent seraphism, which, Of all things most,—designed to intimate Man’s elevation o’er the brute’s estate,— Declares the music of his life a chime Of orphic efficacy, whose sublime Emotion dies not from the ear of Time: But virtual in the world’s prime features still— In sun, mooon, star, wild wood, and skyey hill, And winding vale with all its green array, Clear stream, and broad sea billowing wide away;— A music that the Present with the Past Connects by these great chords, and which, at last, May constitute the Beautiful a whole,— One conscious flow of thought—one mighty stream of soul.

Note.

We often hear Chaucer referred to, as being a well of undefiled English (the phrase coming, I think, from Spencer), and I would note first on this head, that our words of Saxon origin, though always somewhat harsh, are yet for the most part singularly expressive, and certainly not the less so for that very reason. The word think, for example, appears to me to possess in its mere sound, an spontaneous consonance with the cerebral sensation—the intense brain-throb—that mostly accompanies the act of deep and sustained reflection. And no less expressive of their objects are the words smooth, rough, slow, swift, thrill, thunder, wrench, crash—but this last is supposed to be from the French ecraser, though I for my part, demur to the supposition. But whether so derived or not, the acoustic force of crash is remarkably greater than that of ecraser. Then there are slip, glide, flicker, rattle, rumble, shout, flight, spill, sudden, and a multitude of others that are equally echoes as it were of the ideas 8 attached to them. Or rather, they are the native utterances hailing sympathetically the things they stand for, and this in a tone unmistakably akin to them, whether rough or smooth, strenuous or feeble. And this excellence in the language is no doubt due to the earnest and straight- -forward character of the several Northern races by which it was founded.

Even Pope, though in the main so unfaithful (from classicist prejudice) to his native perceptions, was quite alive upon certain suggested occasions to the verbal proprieties here adverted to. See, for example, in his version of the Iliad, the whole description of the felling and drawing of timber in Mount Ida—part of which I will transcribe.

First move the heavy mules, securely slow, O’er hills, o’er dells, o’er crags, o’er rocks they go, Jumping, high o’er the shrubs of the rough ground Rattle the clattering cars, and the shocked axles bound But when arrived at Ida’s spreading woods, x x x x x x x 9 Loud sounds the axe, redoubling strokes on strokes, On all sides round the forest hurls her oaks Headlong. Deep echoing groan the thickets brown, Then rustling, crackling, crashing, thunder down.

In this passage, so remarkable for the agreement between sound and sense, at least four fifths of the words are of the Saxon and its kindred tongues. And in the following lines from his Essay on Criticism, which are yet more remarkable perhaps for their verbal and sentimental concordance, there are only some seven or eight altogether—even including the two proper names—that are not of the same growth.

When Ajax strìves some ròck’s vàst wèight to thròw, The lìne toò làbors and the wòrds mòve slòw. Not so when swift Camilla scours the plain, Flies o’er the unbending corn and skims along the plain.

But some suppose that the verbal or acoustic proprieties 10 here insisted upon, are mere associative results—that is to say, are wholly derived from, or dependent upon, a chance relation which has long subsisted in our minds between the articulate structure of certain vocables and their objects or ideas; that the sound of the word slow, for example, if it had been thus mentally associated with rapidity of motion, would be just as expressive of it as that of the word swift in which we have the very air-cutting hiss as it were of a passing arrow. But such a supposition is obviously erroneous. The sound and sense do not agree in this case, and in others like it, because of our having constantly concocted in our mindsa certain sound with a certain act or object, but the association itself has manifestly obtained because of a certain primordial congruency subsisting between them in their conjoint relation to a perceiving and inventive organism. The nature of the thing to be signified threw some mystical image or analogue of itself into the nature of the rightly applying sound, and they were joined together accordingly by the mind of man, as the intelligent witness 11 of this impictured agreement, or intershadowing tendency of like to like, the sensible after-tests of which were immediately centreed in its own auricular and vocal faculties.

To deny the correctness of this view altogether, were tantamount to asserting that sweetness, or harshness, or sorrowfulness, or jollity, even in music (the science and spiritual impassionment of sounds) is solely a consequence of the mood in which we may happen to hear it, and not a superinducement, as it actually is, of a peculiar and corresponding condition of soul.

Nay, this primative kinship between actual things and their verbal symbols obtains in a very evident degree even in regard to qualities of strength and magnitude. The word huge, for example, seems to me to be one of the most natural explosions that could break from the broad chest of an ardent and original man, upon his 12 first beholding somewhat vast beyond expect- -tation—somewhat enormous.

In fine, but for some such pre-existing agreement between the sound of words and the sense of them, as is above asserted, the poetical canon in that regard would be utterly useless. Their brightness or dark- -ness, or beauty, or ugliness, to the eye, in relation to their objects, is more, though certainly not entirely, a matter of foregone mental association.—But to return to Chaucer.

To estimate the worth of his genius aright, we must take into account the crudity in all respects of the age in which he flourished. We must make large allowance also for the mutations which our language has since undergone; and in respect of his versification especially, we must be satisfied with just so much of grace and beauty as are here and there indicated in those passages which can be restored in the greatest measure to their metrical integrity. 13 In this spirit I, for one, have handled his writings; and I do not hesitate to accept them, as offering abundant evidence throughout of being the lingering auroras—the lights yet on the horizon as it were—of a great though long departed poetic Luminary.

Of these writings themselves, however, considered apart from their author and the age in which they were written, I do not think so highly as some profess to do. I value them more as exhibiting un- -mistakeable indications of a great poetical spirit, in its workings within itself, and in its walkings abroad in a comparatively rude world, than as absolutely containing in themselves any consummate fruit of poetry. And of their value thus far, the most cursory study of their ivy-mantled lineaments—of the Gothic arches, crum- -bled though they be, and pillared aisles (to speak allegorically) of this first great temple of English Poetry—will convince any one having ideality enough in himself to be able to appraise them heartily and 14 with a right critical instinct. For no such reader can fail to acknowledge the fine strokes pervading these olden rhymes in passages like the following, or hesitate to assign them. if not to the highest, yet to a very high order of poetical perception.

A Perfect Knight.

And evermore he had a sovereign praise, And though that he was worthy he was wise, And of his port as meek as is a maid.

How finely robust is the expression here “a sovereign praise”—a perfectly original one in Chaucer. And how otherwise could the poet so well have impressed us with an idea of the unaffected modesty of a perfect Knight, or gentleman, as by the allusion in the last line. He who is at once brave and refined is always thereto forbearing and gentle.

“A Lover and a Lusty Bachelor.”

Embroidered was he, as it were a mead 15 All full freshest flowers white and red; Singing he was, or fluting, all the day— He was as fresh as the Month of May.

Here we have all the Springtime joy and blossomy freshness of humand life and rural nature, bound up together as it were in one immortalised nosegay.

A Wealthy Yoeman’s Table.

Withouten baked meats never was his house, Of fish and flesh, and that so plentïous, It snowéd in his house of meat and drink.

Good cheer in abundance, and the sensuous gust of it, were never more strikingly put, I think, than in that last line. The figure in itself, however, as applying to other than a Northern Counry, and but for its suggestive reference to the good cheer of a Christmas set in the midst of Winter, might perhaps be objected to, as being somewhat out of keeping with the geniality of the subject.

How happily is the religious simplicity of the Prioress here indicated by the quality of her greatest oath! It is in subtle real-life seizures of this kind that one of the chief excellencies of Chaucer lies. Then he is not content with telling us that the gentle and good Nun has a very scrupulous conscience and most tender heart, but with the full moral faith of a great Poet in his great moments, he will have her “all conscience and tender heart.”

Of a piece with this nice seizure of certain 17 real-life characteristics which conduce so much to the imaginative realization of his persons, is this faither habit in Chaucer, of gathering about them, as with a simple dash of the pencil, the remotest wonts and most romantic circumstances of their foregone being. If, for example, he has to do with the frosty beard of an old sailor, he is sure to bethink him of how often it must have streamed, like a meteor, upon the mighty breath of the storm

“With many a tempest had his beard been shaken.”

Or in his description of some fair far-travelled pilgrim, his imagination is not satisfied to take her simply as she stands in the present, but it must glance away into the past track of her fortunes, and become intensified with the thought of how “many a strange stream” she had already crossed in her pious wanderings.—But to proceed with our analects.

A Good Priest.

He waited after neither pomp nor reverence, Nor makèd him no spiced consciënce, But Christ his love, and his apostles twelve, He taught,—but first he followed it himselve

That “spiced conscience” is exquisite. Under the fair front of a pleasant sin he was for detecting its leprous ultimate, and any wrong, however perfumed, was gilded on the out- -side, was no less a wrong in his simple sense of it.And the other stroke of it in italics is also a thoroughly home one. There is nothing to the bold and open spirit of the true Poet more admirable than sincerity in all things, while from the same cause, all hypocricy and pretense are especially abhorrent to it.

A Poor Scholar.

Of study took he the most care and heed, Not a word spake he morè than was need, And that was said in form and reverènce, And short and quick, and full of high senténce. 19 Sounding in moral virtue was his speech, And gladly would he learn, and gladly teach.

What a noble draft is that! And with what a fine couplet—both as to sound and sense—does this passage conclude. It is the true English heroic couplet, not composed, like Pope’s, of two lines having each a cesural balance, halving them nearly in the middle, but flowing freely forth, and yet at the same time open to every variety of modulation. And notwithstanding all that has been dinned abroad to the contrary, I cannot help thinking the true English heroic verse the best of all kinds of metrical forms,—entire, compact, continuous, and yet admitting of all the harmonic pauses being located anywhere in it, from the first syllable to the last; while even the boasted hexameter, is not so much one prolonged determinate verse, however varied, as it is two demi-verses, somewhat awkwardly hinged together. In the style of the Iliad especially, this metrical duality in the form 20 of the hexameter is so far uniform and fundamental, that almost every verse of that poem might, with but little if any violence, be broken into two. And in full view of what is here affirmed, let the reader observe the decided oneness of every line of the ensuing passage—and this not- withstanding the remarkable variety of pause and cadence which it everywhere exhibits.
