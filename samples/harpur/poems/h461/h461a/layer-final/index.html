No. 3.

CHAUCER.

Out standing from the twilight of his days In bold relief, he takes the Student’s gaze; As some old crumbling Abbey glooms sublime, A failing wonder to the Modern Time.

And One so spiritual for his Age, And studiously happy in the sage Sweet mystery of Rhyme, most surely claims High mention on the Roll of Everlasting Names.

His English undefiled is said to be By Scholiasts—but ’tis almost Greek to me! And what most takes me as I spell him o’er, Is an all-hearty love of Mental Lore; Together with a crowing, frank delight In all of Nature’s beautiful and bright Yet daily Objects,—for it is the creed I ever carry with me when I read, That whoso had for These beloving eyes, Was more than commonly both pure and wise;— Was One in whom an earnest joy in Good More than redeemed the errors of his blood— Was stored in Spirit with a portion rich Of that transcendent Seraphism, which Of all things most, designed to intimate Man s elevation from the Brute’s estate, Declares the Music of his Life to be Something that cannot perish utterly: But, virtual in the World’s prime Features still, (Sun, Moon, and Stars; clear Stream and Vernal Hill) An effluence that the Present with the Past Connects transfusedly—and which, at last, Shall constitute Humanity a Whole, Onflowing through all Time—one mighty Stream of Soul.

CHARLES HARPUR.
