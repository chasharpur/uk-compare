Mr Mort’s Testamonial

674c

The true man values Virtue for itself, And not its accidents, however golden; The sham one for the influence or pelf Through which by him it is alone beholden.

674d Note I believe Mr Mort to be a very worthy man upon the whole. 676d I believe that when Mr Manning said that he “had never heard any expressions of him, in public or private matters, that did do him honor” he spoke sincerely, and was not altogether overpraising him. Nay, I even think that his permanent withdrawal from amongst us would be a social loss. 674b ⊙ And such, too, is my opinion of his good sense, that I think he would be glad to slip from under the impending honor, if the powers of Sham were to be balked in any other way than by treating them with contempt. 676e But with this much premised, I will now propound my query. Suppose Mr Mort to be in every respect just as worthy as he is: but suppose, at the same time, that instead of his having amassed immense wealth in his enterprising career, he had only been able, through the shortcomings of others or through some malevolence of fortune, to have just kept himself afloat; and with this fact patent to the community, were about separting from our shores—would the intending Testimonialists, whose names are blazoned at length in the public prints be altogether so ready to come forward with their valedictory incense to his worth and conduct? No not likely they would, and I think nobody else believes they would or that the Herald and Empire would keep several columns open to catch the drippings of the occasion. 675a Nay, many an honest fellow who shall read this, will be ready to assert, on the strength of his own Colonial experience, that some of them, in the reversed case I have supposed, would scarcely turn upon their heels to vouchsafe him a parting smile. and that ... But I care not myself to go so far as that: 674a x I would rather imagine that they have still amongst them a brazen half-moon on hand which they would fain confer on somebody. 675b To conclude knowing that they would not then do, all that they are now bent upon doing, is to conclude that the forthcoming Testimonial, whatever shape it may happen to take, will at best be only comparable to

675c

“A goodly apple, rotten at the core:”

675d that is, if we could but see it in its essence; inasmuch as it will be more of an offering to Mr. Mort’s wealth and material influence than to his intrinsic worth. But should any one ask what moral harm there can be in such like displays, I answer, a great deal. The mere suspicion of their genuineness, to which we are unavoidably led by the prevailing tone of our society, and by its ingrained mammonism, tends to demoralise our faith in the public expression of all social opinion however spontaneous in appearance. And in this, too, that they tend to perpetuate in the getters- up of them, an habitual hollowness and a self- complacency in pretences, of whose real nature and end they must very frequently be most meanly conscious. 675e “But you require men to be too sternly sincere, too loftily motived,” says some sleek apologist: “what you complain of is inherent in human nature.” But this is only putting a worse face upon the matter: for it is tantamount to declaring that human nature has a Midas-like curse upon it, and can only test virtue by turning it into gold—a doctrine I abhor immeasurably,
