TO THE REV. JOHN SAUNDERS, ON LEARNING THAT HE WAS ABOUT TO DEPART FROM THE COLONY.

[By Charles Harpur.]

If a large love of the whole human race, With charity that hopeth a meet cure For its worst evils, indicate the grace of Goodness, thine is such as will endure. And if our prayers to stablish what is pure In faithless hearts, pierce through the starry face Of Heaven, unlost in the dim glades of Space, Thine have been heard, and thy reward is sure. Farewell—my Country could be well content To part with much besides, if so she might Keep burning o’er her moral gloom, unblent With earthlier ardors, passionately bright, Thy steady flame of purpose in the right— Thy eloquent fervency of wise intent.*

* ”They had assembled for the purpose of ex- pressing their feelings of gratitude to that undoubted friend of the Colony, the Rev. John Saunders; and it would have been a stain on the community at large, had that gentleman been permitted to have left these shores without some demonstration of the high esteem in which he was held.”—From the speech of the Attorney General as Chairman of a Meeting for the purpose of subscribing a Tes- timonial to Mr. Saunders on his departure from the Colony.

“He (Mr. Saunders) came to this Colony at his own expense, about thirteen years ago, and his unceasing labors for the Colony were carried on without one farthing of expence to the Government —having been supported entirely by his own con- 452 gregation. As a religious man, he was of a pure Catholic spirit, embracing men of every creed, and hailing all as his brethren.”—From the Speech of Dr. Ross at the same Meeting.

To the spirit of these high Testimonials, far more precious, I doubt not, to Mr. Saunders than the mere ”silver and gold” of the material one intended, I have presumed to prefix my poetical mite as above.

C. H.
