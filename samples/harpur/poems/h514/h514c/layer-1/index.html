Chorus. Let us pity the poor Whiteman; No Mother has he to bring him milk— No Wife to grind his corn.”

African Song of Hospitality.

Part I.

Behold an Indian Isle, reposed Upon the Deep’s enamoured breast, Even like a royal bride be-rosed With passion in her happy rest. Or when the Morn is there disclosed, Or Eve is robing in the west, The Deep, as by that Isle embossed With central gauds of sumless cost, And else outspread in circuit—wide And round as heaven from side to side, Might figure to a fancy bold Like a vast shield of fretted gold, Dropt by some worsted Elder God, When on his track, where’er he trod, Jove’s chasing thunders rolled.

Or circling out beneath the moon, And sowed with all the stars of night, 83 Or in the broad Noon domed with heaven, A world-wide Temple’s marble floor, It seemeth, with one Altar graven From the rude mass of things terrene, By Time inspired with Eden lore: An isle-like Altar, sculptured o’er With craggy hills, and valleys green, And heaping forests hung between: By Time, with an old love enthralled, Wrought thus in living emerald; And after Nature’s earliest style Is all the work—that Indian Isle.

Or circling out beneath the moon, Or sowed with all the stars of night, Or And by the lamp-like planets strewn With long inflaming tracks of light, Might seem it to a fancy fond, Grey Time’s broad seal of diämond, Enchased by Nature, memory-taught, With one most rich and rare device— A haunting insulated thought Of her sin-ruined Paradise.

A Summer Island: there the trees Of glorious forms unseen elsewhere, Hang forth in golden congeries Their fruit through all the purple year; And flowers of every sunset hue, And peerless plants of stateliest stem, Fresh showered each morn with honey-dew, Voluptuously impaves and gem The pillary aisles of primal groves That skirt the sunny sea-board coves, 84 From Or hang in their umbrageous crowds From coasting slopes like verdant clouds: While from the craggy midland hills, Out of the gelid springs, the rills Leap, as exulting to be free, And thence in their bright liberty Through glades and cultured valleys vast, And many a wide pasture-lea, Come murmurously winding, fast And flashing to the san. There too, what birds on plain and mountain, The fairest creatures of the earth, As deepest dipt in Beauty’s fountain By their ultra-summer birth, Swarm round, and vividly unfold Their fulgent wings of feathery gold, Bedropt with gem-like lustres, which All interbeaming in their flight, Break, as they pass, into a rich Flame-vision on the sight. Thus swarm they, and with splendors rare Emblaze the warm and genial.

Such is the Summer wealth and worth Of that bright Isle I’d picture forth: Nor wants it fields that well afford The yellow grain and mellow gourd; Nor many a cultivated plain Prolific of the luscious cane, And mealy root: for all things there Are bounteous in their kind, and fair, And genial;—all but the bad mind Of recreant Man! and which hath made Its very beauty seem designed 85 To deepen Evil’s deadly shade (As to such contrast there confind); And given its repute to be, As borne abroad by every wind That wafts a white sail o’er the sea, Even like the savor, tho damp with doom, Of some o’ergorged though costly Tomb! O learn how like a Upas Tree, (Not fabled) his dread cruelty Can make a scene that else might tell Of Paradise, a type of Hell!

Part II.

‘Twas Eve—and a long tremulous cry Had sunk to low sobs, thin and dry, (Even such as are the poor relief Of weary Eld when clothed in grief) After the loud and dinning spite Of oaths and whips had ceased to fright The soft airs that were ruffling through The greenest crops that ever grew, Save in that Isle so summer-bright! When though an orange hedge unclipt And old and haggard Negro slipt With feeble haste, as he in fear Would hide from some pursuer near, More dredfull to his thought than death! But unpersued and takeing breath, Thus with his story did he stir The soul of a pitying Traveller.

Master 86

Master thou seemest sad to see These bleeding stripes—my misery! Are they deserved because no more My failing vigor what it bore So freely once, can bear? Nor ply with these age-shrunken arms— Now that the blood no longer warms This withered heart of care, The wearying hoe as I was wont When younger in despair? How dare you cruel men account That God hath made me man? and they Being men, yet mock him day by day— Him and themselves, in loading so With direst wrong and bitterest woe— With every suffering that they can, A man—an old and broken man?

Thou pitiest me—and I have longed In vain for pity many a year: Tis very precious to the wronged, And to the slave tis doubly dear: Tis sweeter to his heart than rest Is to his weary bones, oppressed With love through the long scorching day, And gentle-hearted Sir, since they, My drivers, will no more molest My wasted nature with their spite So terrible, till the morrow’s light Couldst thou accord the time to one Thus abject,—now, even while the sun Drops o’re the burning waves,—to thee I’d tell how grew this misery.

87

I thank thee Master;—sit you here Upon this bank, I am very old, And dead and is gone is many a year (Their lost count must remain untold) Since in my own fair land anear My little orphan children twain, I sat before our hut of cane, Which clung as twere to the old wood, Where once my Tribe’s cheif village stood— And yet; with me it marked the scene Where that gay village once had been,— Full in the pleasant evening breeze. That hut my dwelling long had been; Twas joisted in four living trees That met aloft high one the roof In domes of solid shade sun-proof; And from its wicker door the sheen Of a clear brook might just be seen, And, if you listened—just be heard Its mellow bubble, where twas stirred, Or where it made a murmurmous moan Over a ledge of mossy stone. While from between two trellised lines Of large leafed intertwisting vines, On which bright cooling berries grew Like clustered drops of golden dew, Far one a white beach-bordered green— Far as might range the wandering eye, The vast upriseing Man was seen To meet the bending sky.

Twelve moons before my wife had died, And I at length had grown to have No hope, nor wish, nor joy, nor pride, 88 Save what my living children gave My heart, that was their mothers grave And as I thought of this, with tears— The showers of a serene distress, Fast trikling from my palm hid cheek— (Oh; there’s a greif itself endears By its own very sacredness, And love is aye both strong and weak:) Behold, upon a way-side stone Within the unbroken shadow thrown Far outward by our household trees A seaward coming, Whiteman, moiled With travel, and and with dust besoiled, Did seat him in the evening breeze; And shaking out his sweat-damp hair He seemed to hunger, sitting there.

And so I led (as closed the day) My wondering children forth, that they With one might for the stranger feel, And bid him to our evening meal. We have enough for all, (I said) And some to spare besides, to stay Thy need th[r]ough all tomorrows way. Dilay not—come, and still with bread The hearts moan when ’tis hunger’s prey. With kindly hospitable pride My girl shall wash thy fretted feet; In soothing murmurs at thy side My Boy shall sing a ditty sweet, How once a wanderer had died Of famine in the desert wide, But that his Mother found him there And saved him with her gentle care. 89 Learn hence my Daughter here to let The stranger pass in his despair, If help it, thy best welcome can— Thy Brother may be wandering yet A solitary man. And know that I in days of yore A wandering was and alone, Thus sat me near thy mothers door While from the brook she heaved her can— Even thus upon a way-side stone, A solitary man.

The Whiteman at my bidding rose, And followed whilst my children went Before, the wicket to enclose, On hospitality intent: Arriving there, the girl and boy Shouted for charitable joy; Then turned them, our regard to win, And smile their Fathers guest within.

Part III

Now plenteous was the meal I spread Before him, pleasant in my care; And heartily the stranger fed, And heartily he praised our fare— And when he was sufficed, I threw Our softest mat upon the floor, That he might stretch him there and rue That long days weary-load no more. And 90 And flattered much were we to here The comfort and content that then His jaded being seemed to shroud As with the a shadowy summer cloud; Till breathing yet more deep, and deep, He sunk, and sunk away to sleep: When softly in my bended ear My daughter breathed those words of fear:

“The Whiteman sleeps: his face how fair! Yet something dark seems ore it hung, The shadow of crime or care, Or secret hate. He looks but young, And yet his strange unfrizzled hair, In long flakes from his forehead flung, Is flecked throughout with lines of grey (Like darkness at the dawn of day;) And see at times—yea, even now, How fiercely knotted grows his brow.”

Thus she: and then my Boy, as nigh The couch he watched, this lullaby Sang softly—”Rest thee, stranger, rest! Thy need is past and freinds are near; No longer by the heat opprest, Sleep, nor consent to dream in fear Of the remorsless tiger here! Sleep, till the first shot glance of morn Inflame the dews on every thorn, And from the sun-kindled sea The earliest breeze comes braceingly”

Then I:—”And when thou dost depart Again upon thy desert track, 91 Three ample meals to cheer thy heart Shall store the wallet at thy back; And still before thee, strong in love, May the Good Spirit’s shadows move, Preventing every shape of wrath And danger on thy lonely path. And when thou hungerest again Upon the dry unyeilding plain, Even to thy wish, may’st thou discry Some hospitable village nigh: And whenso’ere thy strength shall flag From thirst, may there fresh breezes bring About thee a cool murmuring Of water, from some fissured crag Down slipping into mossy tanks, Or brimming from its shaded well, Or winding out of some dim dell Into a rill with willowy banks.”

But when we too on mats spread out, In Sleeps descended cloud reclined, Strange faltering fears,—a shapeless rout,— Kept peopling my prophetic mind. And then I dreamt of her I’d loved Who stealthily, methought, draw nigh And fixed me with her mournful eye, Till, with a mighty sorrow moved, I wept, though dimly wondering why.

Still as I slumbered, many Forms Moved round me, or kept hovering ore; And voices that belonged to yore To kindred gathered to the worms, 92 Even when I started from my dream Did from the dark yet sounding seem. And closer too, than wont, my Boy Clung to his boding Sire that night,— His little dreams were not of joy, For he would shudder with affright, Whene’ere in louder gusts the breeze Swept through the overdoming trees.

Part IV

At length I slept too soundly!—Cries Were shrilling in mine ears, And yet I could not wake! mine eyes Seemed loaded with a thousand years:— Till rude hands grasped my throat! and then I woke to see;—my children bound! And then six tiger visaged men Were feircely pressing round! And he,—our Guest, was standing there, The unmasked leader of the crew! Yes, he, in payment for our care, They wrong they did us, bad them do, In vain I showerd his feet with tears, He dashed them in my face, till they Were red with blood! whereat his sneers Were levelled as he turned away, And bade his villains drag us where His lurking vessel lay Like a sea-monster in its lair Within a mangrove shrouded bay,— Distant seven weary leagues or more; And where, like wolves, the day before He and his gang had prowled for prey

93

The rest is brief: they sold us here— They tore the parent from the child! And curst them for their shreiks so drear, Or scourged them for their greif so wild! Nor have I, all the weary years That I have drencht in slavery s tears, Learned wither went my Boy, nor where My Daughter dwelt in her despair, Nor wither, unredeemed by death, They yet may draw life’s painfull breath.

But he, our base Enslaver! (this His shipmates shuddered aye to tell, As if their memories then did hiss Within them, like the snakes of hell!) Next visiting my native coast, One morning, gastly as a ghost Rose raging, murder mad, and slew With sudden onslaught half his crew! And though the rest with chains of strength Did bolt him to the deck at length Amid the murdered in their gore, Vain were all fetters! With a roar Wild as a wounded pard’s, he wrencht His gybes apart, the hoop unclencht From round his trunk, the bolts uptore Leapt from the ship, and to the shore Though by the thundering surf deep drencht, Swam shouting! made the beach and fled Into the jungles, where, tis said, Even the mighty tiger blencht Before the howling fratricide, As day and night, with eyes of fire, 94 On, he roamed nor seemed to tire, Till hunger clung him and he died.

Fit end, though terrible and sad, For one so violently bad Even in his wits,—if ever sane We may account the sons of Cain. And he,—for so the story ran, Was born amonghst a pirate crew, And cradled in their bark; And evermore, from boy to man, More wildly fierce his nature grew, His deeds more deadly dark! Boldly he compassed what he planned, And boldy could the bad command, For nothing in the world thought he Or worth his heed or rivalry But wrong and riot on the land, And storm and battle on the sea.

My tale is told:—nor more for me Remaineth, but to hunger more Than fleshless famine does for food, And more to thirst than even he Who journeyeth, tongue swollen, o’re Some wild unwatered solitude, For that—even that apppointed day Which soon shall break my bonds away! It comes! and all my misery vast Shall be but mist before the blast! It comes!—ah, why doth it delay To give me to the pangless Past.
