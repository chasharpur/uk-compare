The Slave’s Story.

It is an Indian Isle, reposed Upon the Deep’s enamoured breast, Even like a royal bride berosed With passion in her happy rest. Or while the morning’s there disclosed, Or evening robes her in the west, The Deep in one wide circuit rolled, And midward by that Isle embossed As with a stuff of rarer cost, Seems a vast shield of fretted gold, Dropped by some warrior God as he Passed on in his eternity.

Or domed with heaven in the broad noon, A world-wide Temple’s marble floor It seemeth with one Altar, hewn By Time in love with Eden lore: An isle-like Altar sculptured o’er With craggy hills and valleys green And heaping forests hung between: By Time, with an old love enthralled, Wrought thus in living emerald, And after Nature’s earliest style Is all the work—that Indian Isle.

Or in the radiance of the moon Stretched out, and sowed with all the stars, Or by the lamp-like planets strewn With broad inflaming bars It seemeth to a vision fond A mighty seal of diämond, Inscribed by Nature in a trance With one most rich and rare device— A far, far backward isle-like glance At her sin-ruined Paradise.

A Summer Island: there the trees Of glorious forms unseen elsewhere, Hang forth in golden congeries Their fruit through all the purple year. There flowers of every sunset hue, And peerless plants of stateliest stem, Fresh showered each morn with honey-dew—, Luxuriantly impave and gem The pillary aisles of the dense groves That skirt the sunny scaboard coves, Or hang in their umbrageous crowds From the high slopes, like verdant clouds: 650 While from the craggy midland hills, Out of their gelid springs the rills Leap, as exulting to be free, And thence in their bright liberty Through glens and cultured valleys vast And many a wide pastured lea, Come murmurously winding, fast And flashing to the sea. There countless birds on plain and mountain, The fairest creatures of the earth, As in the sun’s alchymic fountains Dipt by their summer birth, Swarm round, to bid the eye behold Luminous shapes of wingëd gold, Bedropt with gem-like lustres, streaming In their swift flight, and interbeaming Thus swarm they, and with colors rare Emblaze and warm the stainless air.

Such is the summer wealth and worth Of the bright Isle I’d picture forth: Nor wants it fields that well afford The yellow grain and mellow gourd; Nor many a cultivated plain Prolific of the luscious cane, And mealy root: for all things there Are bounteous in their kind, and fair, And genial,—all but the bad mind Of recreant Man! and that hath made Its very beauty wear the shade Of hate, and given its name to be,— As borne abroad by every wind, Even like the sav and the doom Of some o’ergorged though costly tomb! Learn how his kindless cruelty Can turn even scenes of heaven that tell, Into as many types of hell!

‘Twas eve—and a long tremulous cry Had sunk to low sobs, thin and dry, (Even such as are the poor relief Of age and sickness clothed in grief) While also the loud dinning spite Of whips and oaths had ceas to fright The breezes that then were ruffling through The greenest crops Save on that Isle so boon and bright; When through an orange hedge undipt An old and haggard Negro slipt 651 With feeble haste, as he in fear Would hide from some pursuer near, More dreadful to his thought than death! But unpursued, and taking breath, Thus with his story did he stir The soul of a pitying Traveller.

Master, thou seemest sad to see These bleeding stripes—my misery! Are they deserved because no more My failing vigor what it bore So freely once, can bear? Nor ply with these age-shrunken arms— Now that the blood no longer warms This withered heart of care, The wearying hoe as I was wont When younger in dispair? How dare you cruel men account That God hath made me man? and they Being men, yet mock hi him day by day— Him and themselves, in loading so With direst wrong and bitterest wo— With every suffering that they can, A man—an old and broken man?

Thou pitiest me—and I have longed In vain for pity many a year: ‘Tis very precious to the wronged, And to the slave ’tis doubly dear: ‘Tis sweeter to his heart than rest Is to his weary bones, oppressed With toil through the long burning day. And gentle-hearted Sir, since they, My drivers, will no more molest My wasted nature with their spite So terrible, ‘till the morning’s light Couldst thou accord the time to one So abject,—now, even while the sun Drops o’er the burning waves,—to thee I’d tell how grew this misery.

I thank thee Master;—sit you here Upon this bank. I am very old, And dead and gone is many a year (Their lost count must remain untold) Since in my own far land, between My little orphan I sat before our Hut of cane, Full in the pleasant evening breeze. That Hut my dwelling long had been; 652 ‘Twas joisted in four living trees That met aloft, high o’er the roof, In domes of solid shade sun-proof; And from its wicker door the sheen Of a clear brook might just be seen, And, if you listened, just be heard Might its clear bubble, pebble-stirred, Or where it made a murmurous moan Beyond a ledge of mossy stone. While from between two trellissed lines Of large leafed intertwisting vines, On which bright cooling berries grew Like clustered drops of golden dew, Far o’er a white beach-bordered green— Far as might range the wandering eye, The vast up rising Main was seen To meet the bending sky.

Twelve moons before my Wife had died, And I at length had grown to have No hope, nor wish, nor joy, nor pride, Save what my living children gave My heart, that was their Mother’s grave. And as I thought of this, with tears— The showers of a serene distress, Fast trickling from my palm hid cheek— (A Father is both strong and weak, And there’s a grief itself endears From its own very sacredness) Behold The showers of a serene distress, Fast trickling from my palm hid cheek— (Oh! there’s a grief itself endears By its own very sacredness, And love is aye both strong and weak:) Behold, upon a wayside stone Within the unbroken shadow thrown Is far then by our household trees, A seaward coming Whiteman, moiled With travel, and with dust besoiled, Did seat him in the evening breeze; And shaking out his sweat-damp hair He seemed to hunger, sitting there.

And so I led (as closed the day) My wondering Children forth, that they With one might for the stranger feel, And bid him to our evening meal. “We have enough for all, (I said) And some to spare besides, to stay Thy need through all tomorrow’s way. Delay not—come, and still with bread The heart’s moan when ’tis hunger’s prey. With kindly hospitable pride My Girl shall wash thy fretted feet; 653 In soothing murmurs at thy side My Boy shall sing a ditty sweet, How once a Wanderer had died Of famine in the desert wide, But that his Mother met him there And saved him with her gentle care. Learn hence my Daughter ne’er to let The Stranger pass in his despair, If help it thy best welcome can— Thy Brother may be wandering yet A solitary Man. And know that I in days of yore Wearily wandering alone, Thus sat me near thy Mother’s door While from the brook she heaved her can— Even thus upon a wayside stone, A solitary man.

The Whiteman at my bidding rose, And followed; whilst my children ran Before, the wicket to unclose, On hospitality intent: Arriving there, the girl and boy Shouted for charitable joy; Then turned them, our regard to win, And smile then Father’s guest within.

Now plenteous was the meal I spread Before him, pleasant in my care; And heartily the Stranger fed, And heartily he praised our fare. And when he was sufficed, I threw Our softest mat upon the floor, That he might stretch him there and rue That long day’s wearyload no more. V And flattered much were we to ken The comfort and content that then His jaded being seemed to shroud As with a shadowy summer cloud; Till breathing yet more deep and deep He sunk, and sunk away to sleep: When softly in my bended ear My daughter breathed these words of fear.

“The Whiteman sleeps: his face how fair! Yet something dark seems o’er it hung, The shadow or of crime, or care, Or secret hate. He looks but young, And yet his strange unfrizzled hair, In long flakes from his forehead flung, Is flecked throughout with lines of grey (Like darkness at the dawn of day;) And see at times—yea, even now, How fiercely knotted grows his brow.”

Thus she: and then my Boy, as nigh The couch he watched, this lullaby 654 Sang softly—”Rest thee, Stranger, rest! Thy need is past and friends are near No longer by the heat opprest, Sleep, nor consent to dream in fear Of the remorseless tiger here! Sleep, till the first shot glance of Morn Inflame the dews on every thorn, And from the sun-enkindled sea The earliest breeze comes bracingly.”

Then I:—”And when thou dost depart Again upon thy desert track, Three ample meals to cheer thy heart Shall store the wallet at thy back; And still before thee, strong in love, May the Good Spirit’s shadow move, Preventing every shape of wrath And danger on thy lonely path. And when thou hungerest again Upon the dry unyielding plain, Even to thy wish, mayst thou descry Some hospitable village nigh: And wheresoe’er thy strength shall flag From thirst, may there fresh breezes bring About thee a cool murmuring Of water, from some fissured crag Down slipping into mossy tanks, Or brimming from its shadded well, Or winding out of some dim dell Into a rill with willowy banks.”

But when we too, on mats spread out, In Sleep’s descended cloud reclined, Strange faltering fears,—a shapeless rout,— Kept peopling my prophetic mind. And then I dreamt of her I’d loved, Who stealthily, methought, drew nigh And fixed me with her mournful eye, Till, with a mighty sorrow moved, I wept, though dimly wondering why.

Still, as I slumbered, many Forms Moved round me, or kept hovering o’er, And voices that belonged of yore To kindred gathered to the worms, Even when I started from my dream Did from the dark yet sounding seem. And closer too, than wont, my Boy Clung to his boding Sire that night,— His little dreams were not of joy, For he would shudder with affright, Whene’er in louder gust the breeze Swept through the overdoming trees.

At length I slept too soundly!—cries Were shrilling in mine ears, And yet I could not wake! mine eyes Seemed loaded with a thousand years:— Till rude hands grasped my throat! and then, What saw I then?—my children bound! And that six tiger-visaged men Were fiercely pressing round! And he,—our Guest, was standing there, The unmasked leader of the crew! Yes, he, in payment for our care, The wrong they did us, bad them do! In vain I showered his feet with tears, He dashed them in my face, till they Were red with blood! whereat his sneers Were levelled as he turned away, And bad his villains drag us then To where his lurking vessel lay Like a sea-monster in its den Within a mangrove shrouded bay,— Distant seven weary leagues or more; And whence, like wolves, the day before He and his gang had prowled for prey.

The rest is brief: they sold us here— They tore the parent from the child! And curst them for their shrieks so drear, Or scourged them for their grief so wild! Nor have I, all the weary years That I have drencht in slavery’s tears, Learned whither went my Boy, nor where My Daughter dwelt in her despair, Nor whether, unredeemed by death, They yet may draw life’s painful breath.

But he, our base Enslaver! (this His shipmates shuddered aye to tell, As if their memories then did hiss Within them, like the snakes of hell!) Next visiting my native coast, One morning, ghastly as a ghost, Rose raging, murder mad, and slew With sudden onslaught half his crew! And though the rest with chains of strength Did bolt him to the deck at length Amid the murdered in their gore, Vain were all fetters! With a roar Wild as a wounded pard’s, he wrencht His gyres apart, the hoop unclencht From round his trunk, the bolts uptore, Leapt from the ship, and to the shore, Though by the thundering surf deep drencht, Swam shouting! made the beach, and fled Into the jungles, where, ’tis said, Even the mighty tiger blencht 656 Before the howling fratricide, As day and night, with eyes of fire, On, on he roamed nor seemed to tire, Till hunger clung him and he died.

Fit end, though terrible and sad, For one so violently bad Even in his wits—if ever sane We may account the sons of Cain. And he,—for so the story ran, Was born amongst a pirate crew, And cradled in their bark; And evermore, from man to man, More wildly fierce his nature grew, His deeds more deadly dark! Boldly he compassed what he planned, And boldly could the bad command, For nothing in the world thought he Or worth his heed or revalry But wrong and riot on the land, And storm and battle on the sea.

My tale is told:—Nor more for me Remaineth, but to hunger more Than fleshless famine doth for food, And more to thirst than even he Who journeyeth, tongue swollen, o’er Some wide unwatered solitude, For that—even that appointed day That soon shall break my bonds away! It comes! and all my misery vast Is but as mist before the blast! It comes!—ah, why doth it delay To give me to the pangless Past?

Yet grieve not, Master, that mine eyes Are now thus wet;—these tears are sweet, For from a soothed heart they arise As thanks for thy sweet kindness meet. And never—never mayst thou need Ere God shall shield thee in the grave, Such pity as thou hast shown indeed So freely to a Slave.
