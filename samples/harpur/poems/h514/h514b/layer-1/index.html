African Song of Hospitality.

Part I.

1.

Behold an Indian Isle, reposed Upon the Deep’s enamoured breast, Even like a royal bride be-rosed With passion in her happy rest. Or while the Morn is there disclosed, Or Evening robes her in the west, The Deep in one wide circuit rolled,— 51 And midward by that isle embossed As with a stuff of rarer cost, Might figure to a fancy bold Like a vast shield of fretted gold, Dropt by some elder God, as he Passed worsted on in his eternity. Jove’s chasing thunders rolled.

2.

Or in the broad Noon domed with heaven, A world-wide Temple’s marble floor It seemeth, with one Altar graven From the rude mass of things terrene, By Time inspired with Eden lore: An isle-like Altar, sculptured o’er With craggy hills, and valleys green, And heaping forests hung between: By Time, with an old love enthralled, Wrought thus in living emerald; And after Nature’s earliest style Is all the work—that Indian Isle.

3.

Or circling out beneath the moon, Or sowed with all the stars of night, 52 And by the lamp-like planets strewn With broad inflaming bars of light, Might seem it to a fancy fond Grey Time’s broad seal of diämond, Enchased by Nature, memory-taught, With one most rich and rare device— A lingering insulated thought Of her sin-ruined Paradise.

4.

A Summer Island: there the trees Of glorious forms unseen elsewhere, Hang forth in golden congeries Their fruit through all the purple year; And flowers of every sunset hue, And peerless plants of stateliest stem, Fresh showered each morn with hony dew, Voluptuously impave and gem The pillary aisles of primal groves That skirt the sunny sea-board coves, Or hang in their umbrageous crowds From coasting slopes like verdant clouds: While from the craggy midland hills, Out of their gelid springs, the rills, 53 Leap, as exulting to be free, And thence in their bright liberty Through glens and cultured valleys vast, And many a wide pastured lea, Come, murmurously winding, fast And flashing to the sea. There too, what birds on plain and mountain, The fairest creatures of the earth, As deepest dipt in Beauty’s fountain By their ultra-summer birth, Swarm round, and vividly unfold Their fulgent wings of feathery gold Bedropt with gem-like lustres, streaming In their swift flight, and interbeaming. Thus swarm they, and with splendors rare Emblaze the warm and genial air.

5.

Such is the Summer wealth and worth Of that bright Isle I’d picture forth: Nor wants it fields that well afford The yellow grain and mellow gourd; Nor many a cultivated plain Prolific of the luscious cane, 54 And mealy root: for all things there Are bounteous in their kind , and fair, And genial;—all but the bad mind Of recreant Man! and that hath made Its very beauty seem designed To deepen Evil’s deadly shade; And given its repute to be, As borne abroad by every wind That wafts a white sail o’er the sea, Even like the savour (thick with doom) Of some o’ergorged though costly tomb! Oh! Learn how a kind-less cruelty Can make a scene that else might tell Of Paradise, a type of Hell!

Part II.

1.

‘Twas Eve—and a low tremulous cry Had sunk to low sobs, thin and dry, (Even such as are the poor relief Of age and sickness clothed in grief) After the loud and dinning spite 55 Of oaths and whips had ceased to fright The soft wind that was ruffling through The greenest crops that ever grew, Save in that Isle so summer-bright! When through an orange hedge unclipt And old and haggard Negro slipt With feeble haste, as he in fear Would hide from some pursuer near, More dreadful to his thought than death! But unpursued, and taking breath, Thus with his story did he stir The soul of a pitying Traveller

2.

Master, thou seemest sad to see These bleeding stripes—my misery! Are they deserved because no more My failing vigor now can bear With agile step and willing air The load it once so freely bore? Nor ply with these age-shrunken arms— Now that the blood so thinly warms This withered heart of care, The wearying hoe as it was wont 56 When I was younger in despair? How dare you cruel men account That God hath made me Man—and they Being men, yet mock Him day by day— Him and themselves! in loading so With direst wrong and bitterest wo— With every suffering that they can A Man—an old broken man!

3.

Thou pitiest me—and I have longed In vain for pity many a year; ‘Tis very precious to the wronged, And to the Slave ’tis doubly dear: ‘Tis sweeter to his heart than rest Is to his weary limbs, opprest With toil through the long scorching day. And gentle Sir, since they, My drivers, will no more molest My wasted nature with their spite So wanton, till to-morrow’s light Shall re-awaken Grief and me, Couldst thou afford the time to one Thus abject,—now, even while the sun 57 Drops o’er the burning waves, to thee I’d tell how grew this misery.

4.

I thank thee, Master;—sit thou hear Upon this bank.—I am very old, And dead and gone is many a year, (The lost count must remain untold) Since in my own far Country, near My little orphan children twain, I sat before our Hut of cane, Which clung as twere to the ‘ancient wood Where once my Tribe’s chief village stood:— Methinks I see myself again There sitting with my children twain, Full in the pleasant evening breeze! That Hut my dwelling long had been; ‘Twas joisted in four living trees That met aloft, high o’er the roof In domes of solid shade sun-proof, And from its wicker door the sheen Of one clear brook might just be seen, Or, if you listened, just be heard Its mellow bubble, where ’twas stirred 58 From underneath by pebbles strown About the bed, and thence upthrown The margin reeds between; Or when it made a murmurous moan Over a ledge of mossy stone, And which, so lonely ’twas and low, Did ever, to the heart’s relief, Touch Memory with some gentle grief, As always in its hidden flow ‘Twere babbling of the long ago: While from betwixt two trellised lines Of large leafed intertwisting vines, On which bright cooling berries grew Like clustered drops of golden dew, Far over slopes of forest green— Far as might range the wondering eye, The vast uprising Main was seen To meet the bending sky.

5.

Twelve moons before my Wife had died, And I at length had grown to have No hope, nor wish, nor joy beside What now her living loved ones gave 59 My heart, that else had dwelt in gloom As buried in their Mother’s tomb. And as I thought of this, with tears,— The showers of a concealed distress, Fast trickling from my palm-hid cheek— (Oh, there’s a grief itself endears By its own very loneliness, And love is aye both strong and weak!) Behold, upon a wayside stone Within the unbroken shadow thrown Far outward by our household trees, A seaward coming Whiteman moiled With travel, and with dust besoiled, Did seat him in the evening breeze; And shaking out his sweat-damp hair, He seemed to hunger, sitting there.

6.

And so I led (as closed the day) My wondering Children forth, that they Might with me for the Stranger feel, And bid him to our evening meal. “We have enough for all,” I said, “And some to spare, wherewith to stay 60 Thy need through all to-morrow’s way. Delay not,—come, and still with bread The heart’s pine when ’tis hunger’s prey. With kindly hospitable pride My Girl shall wash thy fretted feet; In soothing measures at thy side My Boy shall sing a ditty sweet: How once a wanderer had died Of famine in the desert wide, But that his Mother found him there And saved him by her gentle care. Learn hence my daughter ne’er to let The stranger pass in his despair, If help it thy best welcome can— Thy brother may be wandering yet A solitary man. And know that I, in days of yore, When wandering weary and alone Thus sat before thy Mother’s door While from the brook she heaved her can— Even thus upon a wayside stone, A solitary man.”

7.

The Whiteman at my bidding rose And followed; while my children went Before, the wicket to unclose, On hospitality intent. Arriving there, the girl and boy Shouted for charitable joy; Then turned them, our regard to win, And smile their Father’s quest within.

Part III.

1.

Now plenteous was the meal I spread Before him, with a pleasant air, And heartily the Stranger fed, And heartily he praised our fare. And when he was sufficed, I threw Our softest mat upon the floor, That he might stretch him there and rue The long day’s weary weight no more. And flattered much were we to see The comfort and complacency 62 Which seemed his jaded sense to shroud As in a shadowing summer cloud, While breathing yet more deep and deep, He sank into the depths of sleep: When softly in my bended ear My daughter slid these words of fear.

2.

“The Whiteman sleeps: his face how fair! Yet some dread shade seems o’er it hung— The token or of wrong or care, Or secret hate. He looks but young, And yet his strange unfrizzled hair In long flakes from his forehead flung, Is flecked throughout with lines of grey Like darkness at the dawn of day; And see, at times,—yea, even now, How fiercely lours his knotted brow, As through his dreams were thundering o’er Dark memories of a stormy yore!”

3.

Thus she: and then my boy, as nigh The couch he watched, this lullaby 63 Sang softly:—”Rest thee, Stranger,—rest! Thy need is past and friends are near; No longer by the heat opprest, Sleep, nor consent to dream in fear Of the remorseless tiger here! Sleep, till the first shot glance of day Inflame the dews on every throrn, And from the sun-enkindled sea The earliest airs come bracingly.”

4.

Then I—”And when thou dost depart, Thus rested, on thy desert track, Three ample meals, to cheer thy heart, Shall store the wallet at thy back; And still before thee, strong in love, May the Good Spirit’s presence move, Preventing every shape of wrath And danger on thy lonely path; And when thou hungerest again Upon the dry unyielding plain, Even to thy wish, mayst thou descry Some hospitable village nigh; And 64 And when from thirst thy strength shall flag, May fresh-wing’d breezes round the bring A cool and constant murmuring Of waters from some fissured crag Down slipping into mossy tanks— Or brimming from their shaded well, Or winding out of some dim dell Into a rill with willowy banks.”

5.

But when we too, on mats spread out, In Sleep’s descended cloud reclined, Strange faltering fears,—a troublous rout, Kept peopling my prophetic mind. I dreamt of Her that I had lost, Who stealthily, methought, drew nigh And fixed me with her mournful eye— Till on a sea-like trouble tossed, I wept, though dimly wondering why.

6.

Still, as I slumbered, many Forms Moved round me, or kept hovering o’er, And voices that belonged of yore 65 To kindred gathered to the worms, Even when I started from my dream, Did from the dark yet sounding seem. And closer too, than wont, my boy Clung to his boding sire that night— His little dreams were not of joy, For he would shudder with affright, Whene’er in louder gusts the breeze Swept through the overdoming trees!

Part IV.

1.

At length I slept—too soundly! Cries Were wildly shrilling in mine ears, And yet I could not wake; mine eyes Seemed on their loaded lids to bear The burthen of a thousand years Of weariness and care! Till rude hands grasped me! Woke I then— Alas! to see my children bound! To see six tiger-visaged men All fiercely pressing round! 66 While he—our Guest, was standing near The unmasked leader of the crew! Yea, he, in payment for our cheer, The wrong they did us, bad them do! In vain I knelt in suppliant fear, And bathed his feet with many a tear; He dashed me ruthlessly away And turning with a demon sneer, As bleeding at his He bad his villains drag is where Within a mangrove shrouded bay, Like a sea monster in its lair His lurking vessel lay,— Distant three lonely leagues or more, And whence, like wolves, the day before He and his gang had prowled for prey.

2.

The rest is brief: they sold us here— They tore the father from the child! And cursed them for their shrieks so drear, And scourged them for their grief so wild! Nor have I, all the weary years That I have drenched in Slavery’s tears, 67 Learned whither went my boy, nor where My daughter dwelt in her despair, Nor whether, unredeemed by death, They yet may draw life’s toilsome breath.

3.

But he—our base Enslaver! (this His shipmates shuddered aye to tell, As if their memories then did hiss Within them like the snakes of Hell!) Next visiting the Afric coast, One morning, ghastly as a ghost, After a dread debauch, wherein Wild Riot led the dance of Sin, Rose raging, murder-mad, and slew With sudden onslaught half his crew! And though the rest with chains of strength Did both him to the deck at length, Amid the slaughtered in their gore; Vain were all fetters! With a roar Wild as a wounded pard’s, he wrenched His gyves, as they had been no more Than yarns of flax;—the hoop unclenched From round his trunk—the bolts uptore— 68 Leaped from the deck, and tow’rds the shore, Though by the thundering surge deep drenched, Swam shouting!—made the beach and fled Into the tangled jungles spread Immensely inland,—where, ’tis said, Even the mighty lion blenched Before the howling fratricide, As day and night with eyes of fire On, on he roamed, nor seemed to tire, Till hunger clung him and he died.

4.

Fit end, though terrible and sad, For one so violently bad Even in its his wits—if ever sane We may account the sons of Cain! And he (for so the story ran) Was born amongst a pirate crew, And cradled in their bark; And ever, up from boy to man, More wildly fierce his nature grew, His deeds more direly dark! Boldly he compassed what he planned, And boldly could the the bad command; 69 For nothing in the world thought he Or worth his heed or rivalry, But wrong and riot on the land, And storm and battle on the sea.

5.

My Tale is told: nor more for me Remaineth, but to hunger more Than withered Famine doth for food, And more to thirst than even he Who journeyeth tongue-swollen o’er Some wide unwatered solitude, For that sure heaven-appointed day Which soon shall break my bonds away! It comes!—[unclear] all my vast Heaped misery, shall, like a grey And spectral mist before the blast, Melt scattering!—Why doth it delay To give me to the pangless Past?

6.

Yet grieve not, Master, that mine eyes Are now thus wet;—these tears are sweet, 70 For from a soothed heart they arise As thanks for thy sweet kindness meet. And never—never mayst thou need Ere God shall shield thee in the grave, Such pity as thou hast shown indeed So freely to a Slave.
