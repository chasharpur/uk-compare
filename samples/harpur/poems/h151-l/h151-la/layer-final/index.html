[GENIUS LOST. THE SORROWS PART FIVE: LIFE’S FIRST AND SECOND BIRTH]

Oh, the wild joys of Boyhood! when our life Is an impulsive portion, as it were, Of all the open beauty, gladness, truth, And energy of unadulterate Nature: When even our veins are little brooks that flow Within us, as from a like source with those That gush amongst the hills; and all our thoughts Come breathing and alive with the sweet breeze And glad light of the morn, as born with them. Then but to see the Sun uprising glorious Is shouting rapture; for his golden crown Hath taken yet no damp familiar stain From the world’s sordid breath: the scent of flowers Is Mirth’s true wine, unpurchased; every note Of joyous bird, heard far or near, finds still A musical respondence in our hearts, Itself a morning bird uncaged by care, Untutored by convention.

77

To the Boy’s Quick fancy, a gay dreamer, yon far hill Hath Fairyland beyond it. Yon dark wood Is a wierd region, by the teeming faith Of unshocked Wonder peopled with strange forms And likelihoods of life. The horse that starts As from an outburst of internal fire; The lowing kine; the flock that up the vale Spreads, whitening it all over with a quick And intervolving motion;—these are all New creatures of a new bright world; a world Whose freshness yet hath not been bruised away By Trouble, trampling down the growth of joy And even of Love, into the dust of death, Nor grind through the defects of the soul By impious conclusions.

Then a roll In the green grass, how grateful to the Boy’s Primeval instincts; while a perilous slide Over a mossy bank or slippery ledge, Is utmost glory. Or in changeful sport To fancy his small self a mighty horse, A rampant bull, or even a fearful sheep, 78 Is absolute bewitchment! yea, a joy Exceeding even the Poet’s, who hath set In golden phrases gems of fancy, meet To braid Apollo’s brow:—it being in truth His firstling flutter on poetic wings, And going forth in glad Imagination Out of the trite and personal

Thus the day Wears onward in continuance of delight, One flowing round of joy (marr’d, if at all, Only by the dry task and dryer mien Of some starch pedagogue:) and when the sun Hath sunk into the tree tops seeming A moment ere it sets all barred across 79 And sectioned by the branches, as it were A diämond-pan’d window looking forth From heaven into the world;—then Evening mild Meets him, less gladly boisterous, but no less A centre for all natural delight— All primal beauty, and so melts with all Her glowing hues into his happy heart. Then is that little child of Man indeed A spirit of the season; yea, himself A many-colored cloud of dreamy love And changeful wonder. And when Night at length Shuts over him, with all her stars, he glows A star amongst them, for he feels,—though he Conclude not,—that he is immortal too.

Such are the joys of Boyhood. But I am A Boy no more, though one in years. My heart Is hoary, like the world, whose cold grey shade Hath fallen too soon upon it. I can now And evermore taste only in a dream Of Life’s first joys: in such a dream as that 80 My spirit knew last night—a happy dream! Which from its crimson chariot set me down In the clear morning depths of other days, Grateful, though fruitless, and thick tombed with hopes. So nice was the illusion blending these The things of Past and Present, that the tones Of mine own voice, lifted in laughter, seemed As uttered by another, till, methought, Having perused myself, and as a test Recrost with wonted bound the rushing brook. Where, ‘tween its narrowed marges, like a shaft Of pearl it glanced,—and next, my stature tried Under the low arms of familiar trees,— Uprooted since, or that storm-stricken now, Preach eloquently in the shrill sharp blast Of Time and Change,—I grew at last assured Of a peculiar being, pleased to be The solitude-loving Schoolboy much remarked For the pale cheek and burning eye, alone Wandering at eve: ☆

Wandering alone, to joy Over the dying strains of some old Bard, Or treasure in the future shapes of love And beauty, garmented with all the wealth Of his most affluent heart; and chide those days, Though happy, for their leaden pace, that still So long forbad my willing feet to thrid The mazes of the glorious World!

But now Much of that future hath become the past! And I, descended from my mount of vision, Now thrid the mazes of the glorious World! Alas! Reality smites me on the face: Scared at her stony presence, far away Blankt Fancy fleeth like a frighted dove! Hope languishes, and, dying, putteth off Her mortal weeds, to seek her native heaven, Unmindful of her charge, as of the earth Earthy, and due to death,—and all is changed Within me and without:—all changed!

Than thus Could it be otherwise? Is not the world, So brave and beautiful in prospect, charged With deeds of blood and spoil? with injuries Of mortal hate? with shouted blasphemies, Oaths and obscenities, to taint or blast The lone and delicate flowers of Innocence: 1 ++++ Obscenities so vile that even their names Are Gorgons, turning into solid sin The purest air that shudders to their sound? 82b With the oppressors threatenings, and the groans Of the oppressed? with dirges of despair Wailing the obsequies of murdered Truth, And martyred Faith—and which, where sometimes met By snatches of a fearful happiness, More haggard, while they make such contrasts sound Heartless and strange? with throes of Shame and guilt, Of error, and untimely death? with sobs Of pale Philanthropy, to find the page 83 Of each day’s history so wildly blurred With shapes of madness, penury & pain, The houseless widow and the breadless orphan, Exiles and wanderers with furrowed brows And broken hearts,—who starve awhile, and die, And are forgotten? for all they, perchance, Who might have told their story with a tear, Like doom had withered at an earlier stage, Or individual interest estranged, Or calumny divourced; or sounding seas Divided, till new scenes and other ties Had overlaid each image in their hearts That pointed to the Past, and sighed in thought The charge—’Remember’!

This then was the World I sought so soon, and found. But chiefly whence Is all the evil thus empoisening it? It cometh not by fate, nor does it come By nature—but because of a self willed Defect in Man; the error he adopts; 84 The mischievous policy his craft, installed In wisdom’s office, calls the rule of right; The dread necessity of standing well Amongst his kindred, as a genius, shown By ever beating upward in the world, Not wisely onward, in the track of Peace; His folly, and the excusive tenderness With which he blames it, and thereby abets, Because it is sheer folly—and his own; Th avoidable baseness he consents to;—these Are the great causes that o’erflow his cup Of Life with misery!

Or thus it is, Because a privileged Few must bloat themselves With the abundance of the Earth, and waste In pomp and riot what were happiness, Rightly dispensed, for All! O surely, God Never intended this! As surely, Nature, Through the indignant and complaining hearts Of countless millions, speaks from age to age 85 Her motherly abhorrence of a state So slanderous of her justice! Yes; and yet The wise and brave of all thus wronged, defer From age to age, to arise at once, and doom By word and deed this universal fraud To its meet dungeon in the depths of hell, For ever and for ever! +

Ah!, my heart—my so untamable heart Is like a lump of fire within my breast! Bursting into a flame of burning blood! I would avoid this agony, albeit I do believe it righteous:—and perhaps It yet shall largely profit me that thus Matters evolve; seeing my habits thence Are shaken from their too tenacious hold Of Time and sensual things.—And come, thou meek Religious trust, that sometimes to my soul Fliest friendly, like a heaven-descended dove With wings that whisper of the peace of God, Come, and assure it now, that all thus seen 86 Of evil, by the patience of the One Almighty Master of the universe, Is but allowed to dash all vain repose On Time’s foundations, and all mad belief In Human consequence; that finally, Amid the death of expectations fond, Discoveries diurnal that the pomps And pleasures of the world, are but bright mists Concealing ’mid its heights of glory and fame Its depths of degradation,x we at length, Even this subdued and chastened, might be led To look between the thunder-bearing clouds That darken over the blind face of this Mysterious ball, for better things beyond Its flying scenes of doubtful good, commixed With certain evil: yea, conclude at last That whereso in the universe of God Our better home may be, it is not here; Then here why build we?

Yes, thus let me learn To school my stricken heart; and with an eye Towärds contentment in this troublous life, From bygone disappointment strive to draw 87 A rigid satisfaction, while I deem These loftier moods of thought, for all the gust Of uninformëd Hope, and all the dreams Of a too wild & venturous Fancy, blenched By the stern strictures of unsparing Time, Meet compensations—yea, their proper fruit, Though forced, and moral end.

O then, farewel Fancy and Hope, twin Angels of the Past! Then, Fancy, chiefly, as of my younger heart The spiritual spouse,—farewel! and all thy pictured equipage: the Shapes sublime Of Universal Liberty and Right Dethroning Tyrants, and investing Worth Alone with power and honor; and with these, Those Visions that come shining to the heart Like evening stars from a serener air, Of Generosity in rapture high At rival excellence; of Charity Living in secret for her own sweet sake; 88a Of Mercy sparing still a fallen foe; Of Pity yearning o’er the child of Shame; Unselfish Love, and resolute Friendship,—all, Even to common Trust—farewel! these lights May never burn along my path again! No more! no, never more!

Ah! I am sad: Yet, if my spirit I explore aright, Gainfully sad; for I do feel succeed A freshness of the heart,—a breezy freshness Shed from the memory of those earlier days,
