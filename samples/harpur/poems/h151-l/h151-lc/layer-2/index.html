Life’s First and Second birth into the World.

Oh! the wild joys of Boyhood! when our life Is in impulsive union, as it were, 275 With all the open beauty, gladness, truth, And healthfulness of unadulterate Nature: When all our veins are happy brooks that flow Within us, as from a like source with those That gush among the hills, and all our thoughts Come breathing and alive with the sweet breeze And glad light of the morn, as born with them. Then but to see the sun uprising glorious Is shouting rapture: for his golden state Hath taken yet no dull familiar stain From Custom’s sordid breath. The scent of flowers Is Mirth’s true wine, unpurchased. Every note Of joyous bird, heard far or near, finds still A musical respondence in our heart, Itself a morning bird, uncaged by care, Untrammelled by convention’s formal packs, Untutored by pretenses that forbid All natural self-freedom.

To the Boy’s Quick fancy, a gay dreamer, yon far hill Hath Fairy-land beyond it. Yon dark wood Is a weird region, by the teeming faith Of unshocked Wonder peopled with strange forms And likelihoods of life. The horse that starts As from an outburst of internal fire, When challenging forth his mate, to prove his speed, And tread up thunder from the shaken glebe; The lowing kine; the flock that down the vale Spreads, whitening it all over with a quick And intervolving motion;—these are all New creatures of a new bright world; a world Whose freshness yet hath not been braised away By Trouble, trampling down the growth of joy, And even of love, into the dust of death, 276 Nor shadowed, through defections of the soul, With impious conclusions.

Then a roll In the green grass, how grateful to the Boy’s Primeval instincts; while a perilous slide Over a mossy bank or slippery ledge Is utmost glory! Or in changeful sport To fancy his small self a mighty horse, A rampant bull, or even a timid sheep, Is absolute fascination! yea, a joy Engrossing as the Poet’s, while he sets In golden phrases, gems of fancy, meet To braid Apollo’s brow;—it being in truth A firstling flutter on poetic wings, And going forth in glad imagination Out of the trite and personal: wherein full Fruition rests not, even with a child,— But seeks the dreamy distant.

So may pass The pleasant morn: but even the hot noon To him is pleasant; for the stream is near; And hark, the headlong plunge: the bright wave clasps The swimmer, and, with a cool bubbling swirl, Closes behind him sparkling; being both A minister and figure of the glee That ruffles then the course of his young life Into as bright a tumult.

Thus the day Wears onward in continuance of delight, One flowing round of joy (marr’d, if at all, Only by the dry task and drier mien Of some starched pedagogue:) and when the sun Hath sunk into the tree-tops, and is seen A moment ere it sets, all barred across 277 And sectioned by the branches, as it were A diamond-panëd window looking out From heaven into the world;—then Evening mild Meets him, less gladly boisterous, but no less A centre for all natural delight— All primal beauty, and so melts with all Her glowing hues into his happy heart! Then is that little child of Man indeed A spirit of the season; yea, himself A many-colored cloud of dreamy love And changeful wonder. And when Night at length Shuts over him, with all her stars, he glows A star amongst them—yea, a spirit-star Yet brighter; while he feels, although he may Conclude it not, that he too is immortal

Such are the joys of Boyhood. But I am A Boy no more, though one in years. My heart Is hoary, like the world, whose cold grey shade Hath fallen too soon upon it. I can now And evermore taste only in a dream Of Life’s first joys: in such a dream as that My spirit knew last night—a happy dream! Which from its crimson chariot set me down In the clear morning depths of other days— Days, fair, though fruitless, and thick-tombed with loss. So nice was the illusion blending then The sense of Past and Present, that the tones Of my own voice, lifted in laughter, seemed As uttered by another, till, methought, Having perused myself, and as a test Recrossed with wonted bound the rushing brook, Where, ‘tween its narrowed banks, even like a prone And slender shaft of fluted pearl it glanced Shooting along,—and next, my stature tried 278 Under the low arms of familiar trees— Uprooted since, or that storm-stricken now, Preach eloquently in the shrill sharp blast Of Time and Change—I grew at last assured Of that peculiar state of by-gone being My soul had reassumed, well pleased to be The solitude-loving Schoolboy much remarked For thought-pale cheeks and burning eyes, alone Wandering at eve: and saying in his heart What shall prevent me, if I will that Homer, Chaucer and Shakespear, Milton, Dante,—all The soul’s great potentates and world-renowned Poetic Cæsars do in death become My spiritual kindred? What prevent The attainment, if I will it, of a mind Replenished with all high and lovely things, All genial energies, that play like thoughts And feelings in a lively countenance, Over the frank and open face of Nature; And which, in mental combination, yield That nourishment which makes the outward growth Of Genius, and the primal life of Art? Man cannot, and God will not—this I feel! And therefore wed me to a lofty Muse, To be of all my future life the sole Inseparable Spouse; to walk abroad In the dull world with me, a daily joy, And nightly, at the failing of the lamp,— A last look taken at the unquenchable stars And beautiful-thron’d moon alive in heaven,— Sleep with my soul. . . . .

So all to me was then The Poet! the one sovereign lord in Nature! The Exalter! who, by royal right of mind, 279 Upon the ugly and dwarf life of man Conferred the build and stature of a God’s! The Beautifier! who, by might of thought, Did make the aspect of the common hills, And lowly fields, and unromantic woods, Hold of the soul a glory,—a rich light Clothing them with a splendor not their own, Though constant to its cause; as round about The opal an ensphering lustre clings, But which is, in itself, a lent display, Born of the Sun. . . .

Wandering alone, to joy Over the dying strains of some old Bard, Or treasure in the future shapes of love And beauty, garmented with all the wealth Of his most affluent heart; and chide those days, Though happy, for their leaden pace, that still So long deferred his willing feet from threading The mazes of the glorious world!

But now Much of that future hath become the Past! And I, descended from my mount of vision, Now thread the mazes of the glorious world! Alas! Reality smites me on the face: Scared at her stony presence, far away Blencht Fancy fleeth like a frighted dove! Hope languishes, and dying, pulleth off Her mortal weeds, to seek her native heaven, Unmindful of her charge, and of the earth One wholly due to loss Within me and without;—all changed!

Than thus 280 Could it be otherwise? Is not the world, So brave and beautiful in prospect, charged With deeds of blood and spoil? With injuries Of mortal hate? With shouted blasphemies, Oaths and obscenities, to taint or blast The lone and delicate flowers of Innocence: Obscenities so vile, that even their names Are Gorgons, turning into solid sin The purest air that shudders to their sound? With these, the bloody sweat of wrongful toil and blistered hands, And ever aking thews of wrongful toil By which a few, not they who work, arise To eminence, and lord th land? With threatenings of oppressors, and the groans Of their oppressed? With dirges of despair Wailing the obsequies of murdered Truth, And martyred Faith,—and which, where sometimes met By snatches of a fearful happiness (Fearful because so mortal,) are but thence More haggard, while they make such contrasts sound Heartless and strange? With throes of shame and guilt, Of error, and untimely death? With sobs Of wan Philanthropy, to find the page Of each day’s history so wildly blurred With shapes of madness, penury and pain, The houseless widow and the breadless orphan, Exiles and wanderers with furrowed brows And broken hearts,—who starve awhile, and die, And are forgotten? for all they, perchance, Who might have told their story with a tear, Like doom had blasted at an earlier stage, Or interest or disparagement estranged, Or calumny divorced; or sounding seas Divided, till new scenes and other ties Had overlaid each image in their souls That pointed to the Past, and sighed in thought The charge—’Remember’!

This then was the world 281 I sought so soon, and found. But chiefly whence Is all the evil thus empoisening it? It cometh not by Fate, nor does it come By nature—but, because of a self-willed Defect in Man; the error he adopts; The mischievous policy his craft, installed In Wisdom’s office, calls the rule of right; The dread necessity of standing well Amongst his fellows, as a genius,—shown By ever beating upward in the world, Not wisely onward, in the track of Peace; His folly, and the excusive tenderness With which he blames it, and thereby abets, Because it is sheer folly—and his own; The avoidable baseness he consents towhen The world’s example shields it; Are the great causes that o’erflow his cup with wrong and misery

Or thus it is, Because a privileged Few must bloat themselves With Earth’s abundant bountihood, and waste In pomp and riot what were happiness, Rightly dispensed, for all! O surely, God Never intended this! As surely, Nature Through the indignant and complaining hearts Of countless millions, speaks from age to age Her motherly abhorrence of a state So slanderous of her justice! Yes; and yet The wise and brave of all thus wronged, defer From age to age, to arise at once, and doom By word and deed this universal fraud— This heart-repudiated, infamous lie, To its meet dungeon in the depths of hell, For ever and forever!

But ye the most— 282 Ye mouthy system-mongers do I hate! Ye preachers of the nothingness of time And filthiness of sense, say, are not ye Time servers, and hog-bellied to a proverb? Do not your greedy lives make manifest To all who dare to see, that you, yourselves, Believe not in the Heaven and Hell you teach? But what you do believe in, none may doubt; ‘Tis gold, and purple pomp, and mitred power, The Holy Trinity of your constant worship, Save for an hour of sundays, set apart For saintly seeming. Crafty, but unwise Are all your motives: for of this be sure, In great concerns, all craft is folly—yea, The pitfal of itself. And if indeed, The lowly Nazareen be very God As ye affirm, not even the brutal Herod— No, not even Satan is more surely damned Than in the end be ye! O there’s none Of all the frauds by Superstition spawned In the dank cloisters of a mystic caste, So fatal & insidious as the guile Of impious Churchmen, when they list to clothe Their naked lust of privilege and power With the great name of God! But yet awhile, And the stern breathings of indignant Truth Shall gather to a hurricane, and sweep For ever from the altars of the earth All Priestcraft—sweep it like a mist away!

Ah! my heart—my so untameable heart Is like a core of fire within my breast, High blown and bursting into flames of blood! I would avoid this agony, albeit I do believe it righteous:—and perhaps 283 And with a spirit that not alone endures The evil by a foregone lapse upthrown, But shapes as well the destiny that cures Earth’s ailment, and her day of health ensures, Arise at once from zone to zone And claim all sovereignty for Mind alone! Arise to the great hour of moral need; In with the plough, and sow the better seed— And reap your own!
