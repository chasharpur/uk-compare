IV.

The Incumbency of a habit of Labour.

Whatever in life to mankind may befall, There’s a spirit of fortune at work through it all; Yet the brave, if they win not its favour alway, Can at least hold its evilness sternly at bay

Note.—The advice “Put thy shoulder to the wheel,” is all very well, when a man happens to have a wheel to put it to. ”Well then,” says Mr. Advice Gratis, following suit and swelling with the largest imaginable conceit of his own wisdom, ”if a man have no wheel to put it to, he should make one.” But it is not every one who can make a wheel. And though some particular man, may have skill enough, he may want the necessary materials; and having these, he must have certain tools as well, which can only be produced by the skill of several other men: while the skill of others, in the present state of society, is only commandable by ready money: which again, is a commodity that, in any capital measure, is in but few hands. The truth is, a wealthy independence is more derivable from certain happy runs of chances—or from some native fertility in our social position—or from the favourable disposition towards us of other persons, who may possess several wheels, and who are ready therefore to lend or give us one of them, than from all the abilities and industrial energy of any individual, however great these may be, and however well and perseveringly applied. Still every man in health, can work—and to some purpose; and all that foregoes this fact, only makes a sturdy habit of labour, for the sake of a noble self dependence amidst all sorts of casualties, but the more rigidly and heroically incumbent upon every one of us.
