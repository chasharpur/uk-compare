The Terrors of Beauty.

Thy fairy feet and sylphic waist, And those twin innocents, as they Fill up like doves whose nestward haste Had wearied them; those lips that play Apart so clovenly and rich; Those pinky cheeks, and—thou sweet Witch! Those eyes, the loveliest in the world, And that fair forehead, over curled With tresses in their darkness bright, Like darkness of a starry night, And massing inward round a neck Blue vein’d, but guiltless of a speck, And throatward full, for Melodies Having within like honey bees, To store it with live sweetness;—these All fill me with such fond dismay, That still, for looking though the more In love,—for Love I’ve less to say Than when I looked before.

Note. The Heroine of these verses was an Imagi- -nation—not a Fact. In other words, the effusion was but a piece of poetical statuary—a sort of verbal beau ideal—designed (to use a figure) for the univer- -sal temple of Feminine Beauty. Not but that I have seen an actual woman as lovely in person as any thing I could conceive of the female form by the most elaborate combination of ideal limbs and features. But alas! where was the angel mind, and soul of love, and heart of truth which should have accompanied ”so fair a piece of well formed earth”? This otherwise so beautiful being—this physical perfection, had never so much even as dreamed of them. 468 Hence her beauty, upon lengthened acquaintance, became so far animalised in effect as to be almost dis- -tasteful. It had a marred, —incipid,— palling inconsequenc[e] very like the sensuous disappointment which we experi- -ence from a fine and otherwise enjoyable air, when attached to silly or meaningless words. Such is mere outward loveliness.
