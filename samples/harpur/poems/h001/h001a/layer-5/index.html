Poems by Charles Harpur

I

Abed Ben Houran.

Abed Ben Houran the magnificent! The wealthiest of his people! So for long Men styled him;—but, alas! a hideous change Had darkened o’er the fortunes of the man. Wind, water, fire, with all their accidents, Warred on the garners of his hoarded wealth. His harvests fell with blight; of murrain died His cattle; and the hinds that erst had served So rich a master with obsequious heed, Fled from him now,—and from the mighty woe That like the shadow of a terrible cloud Had settled on his home, and even his name, As from the wrath of Heaven. And when at length, Of all forsaken, in the deadly dark He stood alone with Ruin and Dismay, Thus, in his soul, with scrupulous piety, He moralised a matter long foregone.

“Once on a time while supplicating heaven For mercy, notwithstanding many sins, And much that asked long-sufferance divine, I raised my hand against a venomous fly That stung me on the forehead as I bowed, And crushed it! Woe is me! at such a time To slay that creature, though so base and brute, Was in the moral import of the act (As now I see it in the light of thought In all the dread significance wherewith It pointed to the heart’s habitual state) 542 Most flagrant, as most ignorant of God, The merciful Father of all sentient life: A sin including many sins; and thence Of mystic magnitude enough to draw Behind it, scorpian-like, the deadly curse Which hath so surely followed me and mine!

“What though the Prophet nowhere hath declared Aught like it reprobate, or of a kind To incur signal wrath of heaven, and start The storms of Fate;—yet, as at such a time Committed—yea, by one bowed down in prayer For mercy from the Merciful! the wild And impious fierceness of the act itself Should have been even to my natural mind So patent and pronounced, as to have kept The flames of anger, though provoked indeed, Yet innocent of life!”

In spirit, thus, He moralised that matter long foregone: Showing this only,—that his life had been Pious & pure, and that he had withal But little absolute wrong upon his soul. And making this, too, patent x (since ’tis plain A man so principled could ne’er have been Accursed by Providence)—that all the ills Whose rage had whelmed his worldly weal, and still Bent on his head, were missioned so to rage Not against him and his, but had their course Pre-channelled by inevitable drifts In Nature’s sequence—even that course, wherein He and his fortunes happened then to be.
