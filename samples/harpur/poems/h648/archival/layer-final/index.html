A Truth little understood, and which is recommended to the particular study of William C. Wentworth, Esq., and his political Colleagues:—

A sentence of the sage, or of the bard A simple verse, which flxes in humanity One new and Spiritual Feeling, or Refines in it one exquisite sympathy Domestic, social, sexual, or friendly, Is of more absolute value to the world Than ten mechanical inventions, though Amongst the greatest that have been; or ten Mere cities (from their worth in men abstracted), Though towered with all the palace pomp that mocks The fleeting gaze of those who, yet untaught Pride’s nothingness, build [gap]abels to it yet! Of how much greater value then, than all The sheep grim Wentworth, or smug Windeyer, has? Or Boyd, or Nicholson, can hope to have?

CHARLES HARPUR.

Pyrmont, July, 1845.
