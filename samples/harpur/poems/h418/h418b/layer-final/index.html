I.

On first walking in the Fields with Rosa.

Why tower my spirits, and what means this wild Sweet tumult at my heart?—this dreamy chase Of far-off joys glowing like stars in space? Now, even to Death I were half reconciled, For all seems spiritual! Hills up piled And rude, are cheated into beauty; grace Suffuses the dull forest’s fruitless face; The river that, like a pure mind beguiled, Grows purer for its errors, and the trees That fringe its margin with a dusky shade, Seem robed in fairy wonder: and are these Thus raised transcendently, because surveyedBy 40a By One who near me sweetens the sweet breeze x For the first time;—an almost stranger Maid?

40c

x In the previous copy of the Sonnet, this line ran thus: By Her who sweetens at my side the breeze. Here, however, I have thrown forward the word sweetens, for the purpose of locating upon it a more marked emphasis. By this alteration the line has lost somewhat in mere smoothness: but a certain strenuous sweetness is always to be preferred to that.—I do not make 41 this Note from any distrust of my own ear, or musical instinct; but from being aware of the necessity there is for guarding the general reader against the hum-drum cavils of critical pretenders.
