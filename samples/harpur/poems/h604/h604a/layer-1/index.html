To Beatrice.

Though orbed are thine eyes, like the moon,—and as bright As the glad-gushing fountains that flash in her light; Though thy lips as the core of a cherry may be That with ripeness hath burst as it hangs on the tree; Though refulgent thy locks as the tresses that streak The forehead of morn ere the sun’s on the peak; Though like gardens thy cheek be, whose borders enclose The delicate lily and exquisite rose;— 40 Yet, as a sharp thorn in some dew-jewelled bloom Forbids the fond bee for its nectar to come, A disdain in thy mien warns each Youth to discard The hope that might foster a tender regard. Oh, be that but subdued with a generous endeavour, And the veriest Saint might adore thee forever! x

x The Belle on whom these lines were written (called Beatrice allegorically) retorted on reading them: “that she would become all the Poet wished her, on condition of his becoming the saint who might adore her forever.” This was an un= expected poser. I felt at once that for me to attempt to put on the saint would be only to put off the attempt; and that everlasting adoration was just as great a difficulty spread over a greater surface. So I did nothing but nudge the Muse, to sing smaller; the end of the affair being, a lapse of the conditions on both sides, I rema[i]ning of the wicked, and she remaining—a shrew.

C.H.
