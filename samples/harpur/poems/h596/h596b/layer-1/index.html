XLIV. X

To a Friend, on the Death of one of his Children.

I know not what it is to lose a child, But I can feel what such a loss must be; Can apprehend how the heart waileth wild At the death-parting with a creature we Have loved so largely—to our misery! And how all objects that before have smiled In union with it, must be thenceforth soiled With the damp shadow of mortality.Still, 272 Still, though thus hurt, let us no less adore God’s ways. Our loss is only a remove Of the Belovëd to some heavenly shore, Whither she trails the clue her spirit wove Up to her better home. The strength of Love Might teach us this much were it all our lore.
