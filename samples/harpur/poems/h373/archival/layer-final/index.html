PARADISE.

(From “The Miltoniad,” an unpublished Poem.)

And now from Heaven descended, to the bower In Paradise we’re led, where Raphael lends At Adam’s board his presence, ‘till the hour Of even warn him thence.[CHECK .] Like that of friends Their converse: now we list the Angel tell Of that wild warfare which in Heaven befel, When his black standard Satan there unfurled, Defying God!—and, listening, apprehend A rushing turmoil, such as might attend The starry frame of this majestic world In ruin—system against system hurled! And when the Heavenly Historian’s voice Suspended is—gaze we around; and lo, All animals, immortal all,[CHECK ,] rejoice In mutual innocence: the wolf and roe, The lion and the lamb, the hound and hare, Minim and elephant—an equal troop, The largest as the least—with serpents, rare In loveliness as beauty, feed or play Under one spreading tree, whose branches droop With edible gold, unsubject to decay;— Whilst flowers and blossoms of unfading hues, Breathing in dreams of their own sweetness, spring Beneath or bloom above; and warblers, whose Bright plumes are by perfection painted, sing Amid them, or like sainted souls, diffuse Their songs in flight;—whilst insect myriads near— Shard borne, or volant on expanded wing— That, glowing in their gorgeousness, appear Incarnate ardours—all in concert ring Peals drowsy with delight; and, lapsing round, Nectareous streams amid the fruitful trees Keep cadence with their branches, as they bound To the invisible gladness of a breeze There weaving into one all Eden’s harmonies.

Now turn we to the bower again, to hear Adam his happy history relate; Into the affable Archangel’s ear Pouring the blest experience of a state Little inferior to his own, and dear Even as a brother’s welfare to a brother; Whilst near—unfallen Nature’s choicest smile, The garden’s [CHECK ‘] loving Empress—Eve, our mother, Is moving with such grace as to beguile Implicit homage from whatever wings The air, and walks the ground, and from all creeping things; Yea, quickened by a fresher gladness, while She gazes on them, are the flowers and trees; And, with her felt perfection, wins she still Melodious worship from each silvery rill, And mellow-gurgling brook—and from each passing breeze.

Charles Harpur.
