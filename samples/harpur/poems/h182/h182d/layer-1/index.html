XXIX.

The Hunter’s Indian Dove.

[The figures in the third and fourth lines of the opening stanza of this Poem, are made in allusion to the red cup—a flower (or rather the flower) of the American Prairies.]

Dark is her cheek, but her blood’s rich blush Comes through its dusk with a sunset flush, While Joy, like a prairie bee, slaketh its drouth At the red hony-cup of her smiling mouth, And her wild eyes glow, like twin stars,—there, ‘Neath the streaming storm of her night-black hair!

And ever I pride in my forest choice The more, while I list to her bird-like voice Warbling old songs in her own wild speech, 98 But aye with this new refrain to each: Who’ll pity—who’ll comfort the dark wood-dove, When the white hawk leaves her to die of love?

O then, by the artless tears that rise ‘Neath the downcast lids of her gleaming eyes— By the truthfully tender and touching grace That boding passion then lends her face— I swear by the very wild spirit of love, Never to part from my Indian Dove!
