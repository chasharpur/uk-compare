II.

As more Closely Rendered. in blank verse.

Thus having spoken, the swift-footed One Passed heavenward, and divine Achilles rose. And o’er his mighty shoulders Pallas threw Her many-fringēd ægis; and she put About his head a golden cloud, from which A shining flame went burning. And as when A smoke ascendeth from some island town Which foes besiege, who, from their own strong hold Marshalling out against it, all day long Contend in hateful fight;—till night fall, when The torches, blazing fiercelier, shoot aloft Such skiey splendors as may haply warn Old friends afar, who in swift ships shall come, Repellers of the war: so heavenward up That flame went burning from Achilles’ head.

Advancing then beyond the wall, he stood Beside the trench, nor mingled with the Greeks, For he revered his mother’s prudent words. And standing there, he shouted. Pallas too, 91 Shouted along with him, and thereby raised Amongst the Trojans a tremendous tumult! And as the clangor of a trumpet shrills, When blown by deadly foes against a town Which they beleaguer; so distinct and clear Did Æacus’s great descendant then Send forth his brazen voice: whereby all souls Were troubled, and the beautiful-maned steeds Backed in their chariots, as presaging woe. And all the charioteers were smitten through With shivering panic, while beholding there, That terrible indefatigable flame Which from the head of the great-souled Achilles Rose burning, as the blue-eyed Goddess willed, Even Minerva, who had kindled it.

Thus o’er the trench thrice shouted the divine Pelides; and as oft the Trojan host, With their illustrious allies, were whirled Into wild tumult, so that perished then, As hurt by their own chariots and spears, Twelve of their noblest captains.
