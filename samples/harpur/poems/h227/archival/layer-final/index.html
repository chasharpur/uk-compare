2e

SONGS, EPIGRAMS, NOTES, AND OPINIONS, &c., BY CHARLES HARPUR.

No. 6.

1.

Lord George.

Two Butchers, bluff fellows in aprons of blue, Met with—”Jim, how’s yourself?” and—”What, Sam, is that you?” And thus to confer, more at ease, o’er a dram, They walked into the “Dog and Duck”—Jim treating Sam.

And there, having talked much of mutton and beef, And the best way of sticking a pig:—to be brief: “Do you kill for old Chops, yet? asked Jim—”Oh, no; blow him!” Answered Sam, “I’m now let to Lord George: don’t you know him?”

“Lord George! now you’re joking, Sam! Aint you, though, raily? There’s no nothing like lords and great men in Australey!” “Ah, you’re soft!” rejoined Sam, somewhat tartly, though well he meant, “Aint men lords when they b’longs to the Nominee helement?”

“Oh, I see!” Jim exclaimed; then he threw up his eyes, And whistled that note which betokens surprise; While Sam thus concluded—”I say, Jim: do you Make the blunt, and mayhap they’ll make you a lord too.”
