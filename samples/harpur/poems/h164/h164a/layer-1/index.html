II.

Expediency.

Great Truths are plain but to the Few Who look from Freedom’s giddiest height, And thence in word and deed pursue, Not the Expedient—but the Right.

Note. In politics, as in morals, be should ever be wary of 77 the plea of Expediency. In the hands of a State-sophist it can be stretched into an apparent justification of any manner of measure; and in those of a tyrant, it is but Necessity in disguise. It is expedient, for instance, that the Franchise should be guarded by intelligence and property: therefore /it is argued/ none but educators men of education and material substance should be intrusted with the Franchise. But it is right that every sane citizen whose unforfeited liberty is to be legislated for, should have a vote in the choice of its senatorial representative. Again; it is expedient for the sake of unity, that the ministers of a particular government should be all of one Faith: therefore its offices should be elligible to those only who hold with the religion of the national majority. But it is not right that any man should be excluded from any social or political advantage or position on account of his speculative convictions. X And whenever Expediency and Right may thus fall foul of each other, we should habitually hold fast to the right, and kick the expedient to the devil. Nay, more: whenever Expediency is [unclear] pleaded in support of any measure, that should be enough of itself to warn us to examine it well from beginning to end; ay, to probe to the very kernel both it and the arguments adduced in its favor: for it is a plea that, in its mere speciousness, almost inevitably masks something that is either positively or latently unrighteous.
