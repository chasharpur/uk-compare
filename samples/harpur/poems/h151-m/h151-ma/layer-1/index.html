‘Tis late—for it is midnight when the Moon Thus through yon casement shows her dream-pale face. No wonder that they worshipped her of old, So mystic in its beauty is her look. How wild and lonely must his heart have been, Even as mine, who erst in vision brought Her nightly down, Love’s Visitant, to him Whose dreams she impassioned in mountain cave, The Carian boy, Endymion?

—Seems she not Heaven’s lonely Shepherdess, thus keeping watch Over the starry flocks that nightly graze 55 Its wide ethereal fields: now gliding forth From some bright-bordered tent of woven cloud, Or gliding in? was it strange, first meeting, thus to love A Being so lovely? And besides, there grew Something that yearned like interest in her eyes When turned on me,—increasing as she passed Away perhaps forever! Heavily then, And damp, or so to me it seemed, the air Closed on the blank space her departure made. I could have called her but I knew no name! I could have worshipt round her habitation, But none could tell me where the Stranger dwelt! Seas may roll ‘tween us now, for aught I know, Or many a mountain rise.

Yea at times, 60 Slow disengaging from the shaded depth Of yon recess, that Shape, unstepping, seems To float a moment forward, and then stand Solemn and still, against the narrowed points Of the dim taper’s rays;—wearing at first An air in which seems blended heaven’s repose And earth’s solicitude; anon a glow Of rapture, palp[a]bly effusing forth, Outlines its liniaments as with a fringe Of spiritual fire: while wholly swayed By such memorial witchcraft, and possessed By such sweet madness as Anchises once Concieved at sight of Venus in the shades Of moon-illumined Ida,—I avoid The admonitions of reality, And worship as some skiey Creature, homed In the Eternal, this fair statuary And portraiture of Memory’s dreamy art Etherealised by sorrow.

ore than once, 61 When soured by days of sordid toil, through which Advantaged nothing in my future hopes, Or present loneliness, I’ve thrown me down To lose in sleep dark thoughts; and sleeping, Fancy Stealing the color of my waking mood Hath thickened it with dreams.—And then I have seemed To wander lost under a starless sky, Where pitfals gaped and ragged ruins hung In threatening indistinctness. All so still At first the scene, that even Silence there Did tingle audibly, and press around, Intenser every moment,—yea, until So keen the sense of her still presence grew It grided to the brain: when, wondrous change! Through Heaven’s black concave a tremendous storm Hath bellowed in its strength, and I, as though Befated with a wizard’s insight, saw Its guiding Demons from the foremost clouds Unmask, and dart, with bare blue arms, abroad Ten thousand thunders—globes of writhing fire! 62 Till harrowed to daring by the sight, I smiled With a proud grief, to know I bore the brunt Of such wild work—alone! I smiled, and lo From a deep fissure in the cloudy mass That hung aloft its lurid canopy, The blue-eyed Stranger’s semblance, flitting forth, Hath hovered o’er me smiling! Suddenly The fiends were laid, the thunders hushed, and all The clouds erewhile so foul, receding, shone Like pendant fl of liquid gold, embossed With purple isles; and, to complete the change, Profluent rills mingled their murmurs round, Flowers sprung beneath, and, in as instant growth, The clasping tendrils of embowering vines Rustled above to songs of blithesome birds! Then, at her nearer presence, overcome With perturbations of a joy so rich— So honey-thick, that even its own wings Were clogged with its own sweetness;—thus o’ercome, 63 Methought I could not but—though restless— sink Into Sleep’s drowsy arms—and I have slept.
