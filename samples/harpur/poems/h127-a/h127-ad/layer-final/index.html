To the Editor of the Herald.

Sir,—As you have heretofore exhibited from time to time in your columns certain specimen translations (from the pens of Tennyson and others) of the famous Night Scene in the 8th Book of the Iliad, perhaps you will also give inser- tion to the ensuing attempts to transfuse into English verse as much as may be of the singular beauty which character- ises that truly Homeric draft, as it stands in the original Greek.

I am, Sir, yours, &c.,

CHARLES HARPUR.

Euroma, 23rd January.

[THE FAMOUS NIGHT SCENE IN THE VIII ILIAD]

I.

AS PARAPHRASED IN DACTYLIC HEXAMETERS.

Mouth-chafed and sweating, the steeds forthwith from their traces are loosened, And fed, where the cars, in a rank, make a long gleamy rent in the gloaming. Fat oxen and sheep from the town are brought in uncounted abundance, With bread and rich wine for the troops, who meanwhile have hewn with their axes A huge store of fuel, and kindled their red-flaming camp fires in hundreds; Whence soon to the night wind is given the steam of their savoury supper.

As when, vast and round, the full moon hangeth clear in the orient heaven, And through the wide fields of the air, the stars, with a manifold splendour, Throb, as if surely alive;—all around in the beautiful brightness, See the tall pinnacled rocks how they thrust themselves up into prospect, With the brows of the slopes, and the woods, that erewhile lay huddled in shadow; Till even the valleys, like rivers, are filled with a silvery glitter: While o’er them the firmament still seems to burst with a sur- charge of glory, And the signs of the year are all shown, and the heart of the shepherd rejoices.

So many and thick glow the fires before the proud city of Priam, Bringing its structures by fits substantially out from the dark- ness— Bastion and turret and tower, as evoked from some region Plu- tonian, And reddening far on its way the bickering current of Xanthus. And, by each several fire, watch a guard of full fifty bold Trojans, Whose furbished arms glare as they move; and the bright brazen studs of the chariots Keep scattering into the dark a broken and shadowy lustre: While near them the barley-fed steeds neigh loudly at times o’er their fodder, As if, with their masters, they longed for the white-coming steps of Aurora.
