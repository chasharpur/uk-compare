A BASKET OF SUMMER FRUIT.

BY CHARLES HARPUR.

Dedicated to the Mammon-trapped clerks of Sydney.

First see these ample melons!—brinded o’er With a green mingled brown, is all the rind; For they are ripe and mealy at the core, And saturate with the nectar of their kind.

And here their fellows of the marsh are set, Covering their sweetness with a crumpled skin; And here pomegranates, dull without, and yet With vegetable crystals stored within.

Then mark these brilliant oranges, of which A by-gone poet fancifully said, Hanging unplucked, the orchard they enrich Like golden lamps in a green night of shade.

With these are lemons, that are even more Golden than they—and that enrich our rhyme As the rough pendants of barbaric ore, Did, temple porches, in the olden time.

And here are peaches with their rosy cheeks And ripe transparency. Here nectarines bloom, And, mottled as with discontinuous streaks, Diffuse a fruity fragrance through the room.

With these are cherries mellow to the stone, Into such ripeness hath the summer nurst them The velvet pressure of the tongue alone Against the palate were enough to burst them.

Then see these plums, like large red rubies glowing, The language they of Summer’s fondest theme: Even through the skin how temptingly keeps showing Their juicy health, like a rich clouded gleam.

Here too are figs, pears, apples, (plucked in haste Our Summer treat judiciously to vary;) With apricots, so exquisite of taste, And yellow as the breast of a canary.

And lastly bunches of rich blooded grapes, Whose vineyard bloom even yet about them clings, Though ever in the handling it escapes, Like the fine down upon a moth’s bright wings.

Each kind is piled in order in the basket, Which we might well imagine now to be, Transmuted into a great golden casket, Entreasuring Pomona’s jewelry.

Note.—From this sylvan solitude, wherein God and Nature have at length so fitly provided for my life, I have thought to send you,—not indeed a basket of actual fruit, that were impossible,— but as it were the artistic essence of one, poetically expressed. For I know, my poor brothers, how little there is of Nature that is yours, and how needful it is that some of her benign influences should be made to pierce into those dreary cells—called offices— in which you are daily immewed like so mony serviceable rats, or even creatures less considerable:—those dreary cells attached to the palatial abodes and capacious ware-treasuries of our merchant princes, and merchant devils, or those somewhat more showy cages that are under the supervision of the great governmental drones called heads of departments.

There, with the eyes of my mind, do I behold you cribbed and confined from day to day and year to year—and yet what do you gain by such a natureless immurement? Not so much as a thousandth part of their gain,—the Mammonists for whom you toil? No, not even so much as that! Then, on the other hand, see what you lose by it: almost every health in-breathing bene- ficence of pristine nature, and with these all sturdy habitual freedom, moral and physical. Besides you are perpetuating a system which makes a few men rich by the blood and sweat of many other men; which ordains that a pound shall go into the pocket of A, the comparative idler, for every farthing that may by any chance find its way into that of B, the absolute worker.

Well then, be you men, and will that it shall be otherwise. Leave them, those inconscionable grasp-alls, to pack their wares and balance their ledgers for themselves, and come forth all of you, into this fertile wilderness, with resolute hearts and sharp axes, so that in process of time you may gather from your own orchards even such a Basket of Summer Fruit as I have painted for you above.

But alas! I am dreaming: our infernal Land System bars you back even in that direction! bars you back, and keeps you at the mercy of those merciless grinders of your faces.

And here a prophet of Mammon, one so habitually inspired by his peculiar afflatus, that it has even carbuncled his nose—you see him there, that swag-bellied fellow in the white cravat;—well he, on the principle of sluicing with water an already drowned rat, will of course have a fling at me here.

“And so, sir, (says he) by not looking as to whither you might be steering, you see you have run yourself very conclusively aground. Besides—a-hem! even supposing the outlet you have just been dreaming about were practically open, and that the whole clerkdom of Sydney should be mad enough to act upon your advice, would not the injury thereby done to trade be most disastrous—would it not be such a result as no good man would willingly conduce to?—a hem!”

To the latter clause of this imposing question, I answer decidedly—No. Then as to trade in itself, I object not to it. But I do hold it to be greatly more an evil than a good, when pursued, as it ever has been hitherto, in an utterly selfish spirit. To care therefore about crippling it, in a matter touching the enfranchise- ment of its victims, would only argue in me, as it assuredly does in all its wholesale advocates, a defective humanity, So much for your question. And now, Sir, as the champion of this boasted trade, answer me this:—Supposing it were reduced to one-tenth of what it is, and that nothing more luxurious than hominy were eaten in the land, and nothing more costly than tweed worn in it, but that it yet happened that the eaters and wearers of these homely things were all true, liberty-loving, sturdy, healthy men, would not the community be infinitely richer and worthier than it is?

“Hum—why—in fact—ahem.”

A most conclusive answer: and one that would make a nice little leader for the Sydney Morning Herald—such a leader indeed, as might awaken a distant hope, that the old lady, after all, was going to look up a bit, and grow logical.
