ALLAN OF THE MILL.

A BALLAD FOUNDED ON FACT.

“Come, daughter, braid thy glossy hair, And deck thy beauty gay; The Squire has sent a message fair, That he’ll be here today; And he has gold in countless store, And flocks on many a hill:”— Young Fanny heard—to think the more Of Allan of the Mill.

“Why droop thine eyes, my child? I wish Thy mother would not chide, But think how brave a thing it is To be a rich man’s bride! To play the queen with full a score Of servants at thy will;” She heard—but only thought the more Of Allan of the Mill.

She thought how true he was of heart, How generous and free; How to fulfil each manly part, The worthiest youth was he; How fair his front, his songs how sweet, While she his theme was still; And how her heart sprang forth to meet Young Allan of the Mill.

“Oh, mother, what can gems and gold, Or hoards of worldly store, With hearts dissatisfied or cold, But weigh them down the more? And willing vows, as pure and free As dens of heaven distil, Have woo’d me long the bride to be Of Allan of the Mill.”

“Nay, daughter, be no longer loth To yield to our desire; Besides, thy father’s made an oath, That thou shalt wed the Squire. Ere give thee to that landless youth I’d see thee childless still:”— She heard—to think upon the truth Of Allan of the Miil.

The Squire he came, a heartless elf, With eye so hard and cold, And ere he went, those two, for pelf, Their child to him had sold: And soon her voice, that late was heard As joyous as a rill, Grew plaintive, like a widow’d bird, For Allan of the Mill.

Her step grew slow, and pale her cheek, Her glowing glances dim, Until they named the hateful week Should tear her love from him; And then her step waxed firm again, Her glances brighter still— And she was heard no more to ‘plain For Allan of the Mill.

But when the fresh and jocund spring, In green and gold attire, Brought on the day, with breezy wing, That she should wed the Squire, Her father heard at dawn of light, Her mother screaming shrill— “Up, up! our daughter’s ta’en her flight With Allan of the Mill.”

That day, a horseman passing there, Told, on the over night, That he had met a youthful pair Who seem’d in hasty flight; And what the traveller had said, As both were absent still, Left none to doubt but she had fled With Allan of the Mill.

And now behold a mansion fair O’ertop yon woods so green! A matron beautiful is there, The angel of the scene; And as six cherubs in her sight Desport with merry skill, She blesses aye her happy flight With Allan of the Mill.

Charles Harpur.
