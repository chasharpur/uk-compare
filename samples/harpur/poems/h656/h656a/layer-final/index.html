No. 10.

1.

Ideal Vagueness, an element of Beauty in Poetry.

How oft has Song some beauty most divine, Which well we feel, yet cannot well define? Some yearning excellence, intense and far, Coming and going like a clouded star? Some awful glory we but half descry, Like a strong sunset in a stormy sky.

Note.—Ideal vagueness—such especially as arises from some descriptive epithet—is often an element of great beauty in Poetry. In the description of the horse of death, in the Apocalypse, the visionary effect of the epithet “pale,” is an example strikingly in point. Pale, as applied to the colour and substance of the horse of such a rider, is the word of all others, best calculated to impress us with an idealism at once vast and dreary, vague and terrible; and the more vast and terrible, because of its dreary vagueness.

In like manner, the dreadful sublimity of Milton’s description of Death sitting at the gates of hell, results mainly, if not entirely, from the terrible vagueness of the images: as the reader will perceive—

“———————— ————the other Shape, If shape it might be called that shape had none Distinguishable in member, joint, or limb, Or substance might be called, that shadow seemed, For each seemed either. Black it stood as night, Fierce as ten Furies, terrible as Hell, And shook a dreadful dart: what seemed its head, The likeness of a kingly crown had on.”

And afterwards, when he would incidentally characterize the same dread Being, at a stroke, by such epithetical figures (if I may so express myself) as the “grisly Terror,” and the “grim Feature,” the mighti- ness of the effect is made to loom upon us through the same mystic element—a certain ideal vagueness.

In a like view, and depending for its sublimity upon the same element of mysterious vagueness, is the following account of a Vision in the Book of Job:—

“In thoughts from the visions of the night, when deep sleep falleth on men, fear came upon me, and trembling, which made all my bones to shake. Then a Spirit passed before my face; the hair of my flesh stood up. It (the Spirit) stood still, but I could not discern the form thereof: an Image was before mine eyes—there was silence, and I heard a Voice, saying, &c.”

But it is not only in sublime poetry, that this ideal vagueness constitutes an element of beauty. Milton’s Lycidas is an example, almost throughout, of how exquisitely it may be employed in subjects purely pathetic. The impersonation, in that poem, of the river Cam (for instance) contains an example in point—

“Next Camus, reverend sire, wont footing slow, His mantle hairy, and his bonnet sedge, Inwrought with figures dim.”

Now in this instance, no doubt the poet refers, in fact, to the filamentous tracery, which, on close inspection, were observable in the sedge, composing the bonnet of the river God; but the subtle manner in which he so alludes to this unobvious charactery, as to work out of it, a farther poetic effect—namely, an ideal apprehension of a certain vague and mystic beauty, is very noticeable, and no less remarkably efficient.

Vagueness, as a poetical quality, is, no doubt, akin to remoteness; but it is identical with it only so far as ideal distance has its analogy or material counterpart, in real— both alike tending to “lend enchantment to the view.” The following sweet passage from Coleridge will, in some degree, explicate this position. It owes everything, but its music, and that, partly, to the visionary mystery of its subject:—the mere remoteness of the mentioned localities—the maiden’s country, and the mountain of 6d which, she sings—being wholly merged in the vague isolation of her ideal advent—

“A damsel with a dulcimer, In a vision once I saw: It was an Abyssinian maid, And on her dulcimer she played, Singing of Mount Abora.”
