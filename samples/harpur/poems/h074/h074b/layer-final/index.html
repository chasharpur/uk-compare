Consolation.

Mine heart is heavy with an ancient sorrow, My head is aching with a clinging grief; And if I seek to smooth away the furrow It plougheth in my soul, in the relief 159 And balminess of song—the cheat is brief! One feeling still, from which the Past did borrow Exceeding light, reminds me that the morrow Must drag me further from its lost belief. For solace therefore I would dive with Truth Into the depths of her remotest lore: Somewhere in Nature’s motherly heart, there’s ruth Yet for her Child, though wounded to the core! Though Life’s first objects may beguile no more, And Misery clothe her with the dreams of youth.

Note. The Poet, Keats, somewhere remarks, that the imagination of a boy is healthy, and that so also is the imagination of a man; but that the interval of life between boyhood and manhood is strangely filled up and begloomed with the miscreations and disappointments which are incidental to a certain morbid state of thought and feeling. His remark is perfectly correct; and I append it, in substance, to the above Sonnet, because I refer its conception, if not its actual composition, to the perilous interval to which he alludes. The solace, however, which is proposed in it was the rational remedy. Nature is healthy: and hence a profound contemplation of the beauties and truths which compose her great constitution, is so happily self-abstracting, that 160a it will cleanse ere long, in a majority of cases, the most morbid imagination, and console also, or purify, the most dissatisfied heart.
