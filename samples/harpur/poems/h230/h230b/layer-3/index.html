Lost in the Bush.

Lost in the Bush—the night approaching fast, And around loudening a cold-breathing blast; Fancy the thing! Then mark you Settler Will Severely conning yon peculiar hill; To learn at length this lesson of dismay, Told bluntly—”William, thou hast gone astray!” At this he backward hastes, intent to find Some prospect more familiar to his mind. How puzzling tis! still stranger seems to grow The backward scene o’er which he hurries so: Whereat Will mutters, as he scans the ground, “Dang it! the world is surely twisted round!” Then, with a sudden jerk, the anxious swain Turns, just to contemplate that hill again. Which was the first to warn him, as he thought, That his bush-instinct had been napping caught, But! which is which? A dozen summits lie As like as eggs against the twilight sky, And it amongst them. With a tart surprise, Will opes his mouth at this, and eke his eyes! Squats on his hams, and strains his neck, thus bent, And this way bobs his head, and that,—intent To put the pleasant fact to final test:— Which done, he draws a long breath, muttering,—”Well, I’m blest!”

In short he’s lost: and seeing that the night Will be a wild one, he (as well he might) Scratches his head, and much that member loads With execrations: “What! forsake the roads For shorter cuts, so near the day’s decline, ‘Mongst hills and brushes?—Drat this head o’ mine! The night is settling round as thick as mire; The Bush too’s roaring like a world on fire! I am not one much given to foolish frights, And far too knowing to believe in sprites; But such a night in such a cursëd place Might well nigh put one in a raving case: And somewhere here, for certain it is said, The Blacks have burial—Oh! this silly head! Besides, Peg may forget to bed the sow, Big Bess, that is so near her pigging now; Nor can she by herself, with all her care Secure the stack from that downdacious mare; Or pen those gallows calves up from the kine— I must regain the road!—Oh! this thick head o’ mine.

Thus bringing into catalogue his woes, On, dogg’d and desperate, he at random goes. Ah, Will, dame Fortune surely owes thee spite! Thy blazing hearth shall know thee not to night! Vainly thy Peg now watches for her Will, 155 To loom in sight upon the ‘customed hill, Thus saying to herself, with gesture grim, “He’s boozing somewhere—but I’ll give it him!” And just as vainly dost thou onward toil, Dismally cooeying all the weary while, And wish—how kind! that thou hadst there with thee Thy neighbour Wilson—just for company.

Now thickest glooms blank every forest path, And like a large Cyclopian eye in wrath, One only star glares redly down, as seen. Two huddled masses of dark clouds between. Those dark clouds streaking as he flaps along, The owl pours forward his funereal song; Sounds of strange waters through the turmoil vast, Of the dark woods, dash over in the blast; x or the long howl of the lank dingo, drear And wild, comes wailing from some thicket near Startlingly near, and spectre-like to see, Sharp-shrieking squirrels stream from tree to tree; Rocked high in the old gum, the opossum, sways, A nestlike lump, amid the hanging sprays Till craning outward through the gusty dark. He scolds his fellow in the ironbark; or the spare curlews, from all points that throng In nightly revel the dim glades among Their ominous cries give upward to the gale, As ghosts about some scene of murder wail; or the wild cat, with breath-suspending rush, 156 Whisks, imp-like, from some goblin-featured bush; Or startled kangaroos, dark-bounding high, Come like a troop of shadowy devils by! Ah, Will, such hour may well thy spirit grieve— Most woe-begone, unlucky son of Eve!

Worn out at last, he throws him down,—and sleep There finds him, coiled beside a bleaching heap Of drifted leaves, that patteringly alight From the hoarse blast—while loaden with its might, Huge trees sway solemnly o’er the unconscious wight.

But now, when Night, a limping hag, her shades Had westward drawn, and grey had grown the glades, Stung into partial consciousness, ‘twould seem, By the encreasing cold, he dreams a dream. A banished man, far and alone he fares ‘Neath polar skies, menaced by herds of bears! Gigantic fellows, shagg’d with ice, and grim, And fully bent upon digesting him! Begins the onslaught, horrible his fright Great his despair, yet he maintains the fight! But just as in the strife a leg is lost, /That luckless leg most bitten by the frost/ Sweating and wild, he wakes. His face forlorn First rubbed, he looks, and hails the wished-for Morn: Then scans the unknown scene with rueful eyes, And folds his arms, and shakes his head, and sighs: 157 When hark! distinctly although far away, He hears some watch-dog’s thrice repeated bay— Starts from his huddled posture at a bound, And joyfully pushes for the grateful sound. Nor long his search: for now the Morning Star Wheels from the eastern steep her shining car, And in her pallid but effectual glow A Hut and Clearing stand revealed below. Thither he hastens—and is welcomed there, Consoled and fed with hospitable care; And when the sun shall crown the wintry day, His kindly Host shall set him on his homeward way.

Note. The rough draft of this Poem was originally published in the Australasian Chronicle, when that paper was edited by Mr. Duncan, and was in substance nearly as it appears above, with the exception of the lines about the curlews. These were introduced on the following occasion. Mr. Thrum of Singleton and myself had been pigeon- shooting at the back of Castle Forbes, and being overtaken by the night, as we were 158 returning homeward, he observed, that the aspect and circumstances of the scene answered in many respects to the description in my Lost in the Bush. At that moment, however, a number of curlews began to muster in the dim glades about us, and send forth their “ominous” cries with a wild and startling effect; and in reply to his remark, I exclaimed: “But hark! I omitted to notice those strangely discordant gentlemen:—the Poem is so far incomplete.” The lines descriptive of their peculiar nocturnal orgies were immediately upon the “Muse’s anvil”, and had, ere we had reached home, the approval of my companion, as a finishing stroke to the Performance. I have noticed this circumstance, thus at length, to shew inferentially, the critical study I have ever bestowed upon all and every of my poetical pictures of Bush matters.
