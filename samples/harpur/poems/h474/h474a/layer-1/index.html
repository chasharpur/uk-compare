Shelley.

Beautiful Shelley! he who smote the lyre With wild hand, moved by an o’ermastering fire! Though much in his impictured page there be Of what is loveliest in Poetry, Robed with a radiance time can never dim— Ah! that so little should remain of him!

How deep his colors!—even the sunset shows None richer, nor that less substantial glows. How sweetly wild his spells!—in Tempè’s vale The flowers not sweeter, wilder, not more frail. Lovely his visions as the rare stars through Heaven’s depths first yearning in the twilight new, Or burning broadest in the vast of night— But ah! like them, so distant though so bright!

Yea, as in some broad water gladdening earth, Heaven’s starry face, Eternity’s bright birth, Glows mirrored,—so in his clear vision grew Transmuted into song, all Fancy’s view Perceives of beauty, or the heart can dream:— 360 A broad, abundant, heaven-reflecting stream, Embanked with mythic mountains, from whose caves Spirits of Love look out upon the waves, Or shower down into them, like sunny rain, The luminous raptures of some Orphic strain, Whereof his numbers in their eloquent ease Are but the soul’s responsive symphonies. Yet all is picture,—vivid, but unrife With the warm beat of blood and actual breath of life.

Thus making the Ideal his sole guide, He dared its ardors till they mystified. Yet was he gentle as a child, and knew To worship, martyr-like, the Social True, And what he thought its ethic lights should be, Freedom and love and hope and charity. Enough: alike is now our p[r]aise or blame To what remains of him;—a star-like name, And ashes urned in everlasting fame!

Note.

My admiration of Shelley’s poetry, simply as such, and taking it at the best, has in nowise diminished since these 361 critical rhymes were written. But on moral grounds, I have come to regard it somewhat differently. I would not now recommend his productions as an early study. They are too morbid in their beauty, too hectical in their loveliness, to be much dwelt over by forming minds without complexional injury. Under every distinctive attribute of Shelley’s genius—its subtle reach of thought and daring brilliancy—its thrilling mystical visionaryness tending to vagueness and obscurity—its passionate abandonment —its gorgeous aggregation of material imagery, as being the external and manifold robe of one world-wide spiritual Energy;—under and through each and all of these, there is constant evidence of moral disease, or of a sensibility so extreme as to be for ever bordering upon it. Even in his happiest effusions, there is almost always some strange and deathly contrast involved in the details which chills and depresses a mature imagination, while it facinates the partial unhealthiness of all nascent ones of a sensitive order; and it cannot do this but to their detriment. He seems to have craved for opportunities of shocking the reader by some ghastly opposition or interchange of image or sentiment, and absolutely riots in the power of appalling him with thought-forced connections of life and death, or of over-saddening him with the most far-fatched associations of beauty and decay. The pallor of a corpse (for example) in contrast with the glow of flowers, newly gathered and strown over it by the hand of surviving love, is just such an association of thingg as he delights to contrive—making it infinitely suggestive of misery. This will he be sedulous to do at one moment, turning capriciously away at the very next, it may be, to advance the positive happiness of a laughing child, or magnify the heavenly wealth of an Italian sunset.

Now all this is morally unwholesome. And if such are the general defects of even his happier pictures, what must be those of his sadder ones? They are altogether too sad. His pathos is not simply touching or painful;—it harrows—it lets blood: and what is i worse, it not unfrequently affects the loathsome. The pathetic in ideal art, to preserve its integrity, must deal reservedly with sorrow and want, and healthily even with disease and death. It is morbidly overshot,—as much so in Poetry as it would be in Painting,—when living hearts are anatomically searched as it were, however skilfully, or the rats and worms of the charnel are brought to near to us, as in the following

Still with merits of all sorts, and beauties numberless, the 364 poetry of Shelley is too vague in its drift, and too unreal or pointless in its objects. Even his wild po Song of the Cloud, for example, in which some of his critics thought he had struck into the true vein of his genius, is of a kind very questionable. It is too set an effort to think a thing into what it it is not; and much as we may admire the fine handling and flowing ease that are everywhere manifest in its composition, we cannot, for that reason, go wholly along with it. The human or ethical reference of such a poem, even in its most poetical aspect, is too far off and merely factitious. There is no real breathing purpose in it —no more than there is in a shadow apart from its substance. What sentimental value has it,—or what ideal actuality, place it in whatever light we may? Had it any of the semblative merits of an allegory, or aught of the typical significance of a fable, it were somewhat; but it is destitute of moral and mystical relevance. At the best it is an artificial flower—not a natural one; and at the worst, its subject, even with all the graces and sanctions of Shelley’s genius swarming like “golden bees” about it, must ever bear the impress of being chosen rather to show the “extent of human wit”, rather than the pot poetical potency and worth of it. And this brings me to the the gravest objection of all against the whole characte 365 species to which it pertains: namely, that it a misusing of the spiritual as a mere vesture for the material, instead of making all external and bodily beauty, what it only should be, the robe and ocular adornment of the spiritual. In other words, sensuous objects should only be thus personified for the purpose of giving local form and consistence to some abstract esoteric Ideal with an ethereal tendency, and not for the mere sake of enducing them, as it were on their own account,—with a strange unlikely vitality and most unnatural intelligence.

Taking the whole mass of his poetry together, it is chiefly to be esteemed as composing a vast chamber of imagery, intensely poetic and gorgeously primeval, but having too little of rational purpose and actuality to be permanently valued by solid-thoughted readers. In perusing it, readers of this order are perpetually aware, that its human reference is but a secondary, not a prime element; or that it is so remote or merely contingent, or so indirect and unsustained at the best, as to be without the pale of a legitimately earnest interest. With such readers therefore, to have a general and lasting regard for it, were like admiring a beautiful and many-clouded evening sky in the abstract: that is, without positive reference either made or makeable, to its being but a magnificent 366 canopy overarching the theatre by God appointed for Man;—to its being other than an empty spectacle, except as it associates with the habitable Earth, the cradle, the garner, and the grave of Humanity, and of which all its aerial glories are therefore but the appropriate drapery,— the curtain and the pall.

But it may be objected against this kind of estimate, that its spirit, if fully indulged, would be fatal to the purely and specifically ideal in poetry. But such is not the fact. When a poet is professedly mythological, like Keats in the Hyperion, or allegorical, like Spencer in the Faerie Queen, or creatively imaginative, like Shakespear in the Tempest, we are believingly prepared to go forth with him into the wonder-world of Fable, and are abundantly satisfied with our Hesperian fare. But when, after the manner of Shelley, the manifestly Unreal is substituted for the Real, or jumbled up incongruiously with matters of absolute fact,—why then we cannot help feeling that the mixture is a bad one, and of a nature to surfeit our poetic be- -lief, however large and enjoying it may happen to be.

It will be seen from the tenor of the early part of this note, that my present opinion of the ethical influence of Shelley’s writings is not very favorable. From his sexual moralities I dissent entirely. But I never- 375 nevertheless think that his notions about chastity in general have been greatly over censured. The rational of the thing is simple enough. It is only a virtue in so far as it is a duty,—a duty to one’s self and to one’s family in particular, and to society in general. In the abstract it is just what he had pronounced it—a nullity,

“The virtue of the cheaply virtuous.”

Nor is the religious spirit of his writings much more to my liking. I especially detest his atheasm, spiritual as it is in comparison with that of Miraband and others of the French materialists. It is somewhat redeemed by a belief in the immortality of the soul, and the divine prevalence of a Spirit of Good, that is destined in the fulness of time to become victorious over its opposite— the grim fact and feature of Evil. Thus the native health and truth of heart, endeavored to correct the morbid fancies and metaphysical mistakes of his head, by filling up and vitalising the dreary void of a Godless universe with a creative Goir of Love—the deified beneficence or apotheosis of itself.

Wish not the Poet perfect: were he so, What part in him were ours, I’d like to know?

The animal force and feeling of Byron, with the mental andsensuousness of Keats, the moral depth of Wordsworth, and the gorgeous ideality of Shelly, in equal proportions and intimitely blended in the constitution of one man, would make him, perhaps, a perfect Poet. I say, perhaps;—for it would be questionable whether a perfect poet were a possible character. It is also questionable whether it should be a desirable one: for, it would presend all raciness, —all idiosyncrasy; and manner, the fit audience from this cause, and partly from sheer elevation, the “fit audience” would be “few” indeed, if not altogether wanting. His poems would not tell: they would not hit hard enough upon the the coarse materials around them. All the salient points and angles of a general sympathy with an imperfect race would be rounded away from them by the perfection of their author. In a word, they would not cut into common wear, and would never, for that reason, come into pepular appliance: for the human can only heartily appreciate the human —not the angelic.
