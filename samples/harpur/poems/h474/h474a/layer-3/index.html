Beautiful Shelley! he who smote the lyre With wild hand, moved by an o’ermastering fire! Though much in his impictured page there be Of what is loveliest in Poetry, Robed with a radiance time can never dim— Ah! that so little should remain of him!

How deep his colors!—even the sunset shows None richer, nor that less substantial glows. How sweetly wild his spells!—in Tempè’s vale The flowers not sweeter, wilder, not more frail. Lovely his visions as the rare stars through Heaven’s depths first yearning in the twilight new, Or burning broadest in the vast of night— But ah! like them, so distant though so bright!

Yea, as in some broad water gladdening earth, Heaven’s starry face, Eternity’s bright birth, Glows mirrored,—so in his clear vision grew Transmuted into song, all Fancy’s view Perceives of beauty, or the heart can dream:— 360 A broad, abundant, heaven-reflecting stream, Embanked with mythic mountains, from whose caves Spirits of Love look out upon the waves, Or shower down into them, like sunny rain, The luminous raptures of some Orphic strain, Whereof his numbers in their eloquent ease Are but the soul’s responsive symphonies. Yet all is picture,—vivid, but unrife With the warm beat of blood and actual breath of life.

Thus making the Ideal his sole guide, He dared its ardors till they mystified. Yet was he gentle as a child, and knew To worship, martyr-like, the Social True, And what he thought its ethic lights should be, Freedom and love and hope and charity. Enough: alike is now our p[r]aise or blame To what remains of him;—a star-like name, And ashes urned in everlasting fame!

Note.

My admiration of Shelley’s poetry, simply as such, and taking it at the best, has in nowise diminished since these 361 critical rhymes were written. But on moral grounds, I have come to regard it somewhat differently. I would not now recommend his productions as an early study. They are too morbid in their beauty, too hectical in their loveliness, to be much dwelt over by forming minds without complexional injury. Under every distinctive phase of Shelley’s genius—its subtle reach of thought —its daring brilliancy—its mystical vagueness and obscurity—its passionate abandonment —its gorgeous aggregation of material imagery, as being the external and manifold robe of one world-wide but impersonal spiritual Energy;—under and through each and all of these phases, there is constant evidence of moral disease, or of a sensibility so extreme as to be for ever bordering upon it. Even in his happiest effusions, there is almost always some strange and deathly contrast involved in the details which chills and depresses a mature imagination, while it agrees with and even pampers the partial unhealthiness of all nascent ones of a sensitive order; and it cannot do this but to their detriment. He seems to have craved for opportunities of shocking the reader by some ghastly opposition or interchange of image or sentiment, and absolutely riots in the power of appalling him with thought-forced connections of life and death, or of over-saddening him with the most far-fatched and melancholy associations of beauty and decay. The pallor of a corpse (for example) in contrast with the glow of flowers, newly gathered and strown over it by the hand of surviving love, is just such an association of thing as he delights to contrive—making it infinitely suggestive of misery. This will he be sedulous to do at one moment, turning capriciously away at the very next, it may be, to chronicle the positive happiness of a laughing child, or magnify the heavenly wealth of an Italian sunset.

Now all this is morally unwholesome. And if such are the general defects of even his happier pictures, what must be those of his sadder ones? They are altogether too sad. Their pathos is not simply touching or painful;—it harrows—it lets blood: and what indeed is worse, it not unfrequently affects the loathsome. The pathetic in ideal art, to preserve its integrity, must deal reservedly with sorrow and want, and healthily even with disease and death. It is morbidly overshot,—as much so in Poetry as it would be in Painting,—when living hearts are anatomically searched as it were, however skilfully, or the rats and worms of the charnel are brought to near to us, as in the following

364 poetry of Shelley is always too vague in its drift, and too unreal or profitless in its objects. Even his wild Song of the Cloud, for example, in which some of his critics thought he had struck into the true vein of his genius, is of a kind very questionable. It is the offspring of too set an effort to think a thing into what it is not; and much as we may admire the fine handling and flowing power that are everywhere manifest in its composition, we cannot, for that reason, go wholly along with it. The human or ethical reference of such a poem, even in its most poetical aspect, is too far off and merely factitious. There is no real breathing purpose in it —no more than there were in a shadow considered apart from its substance. What sentimental value has it, or what ideal actuality, place it in whatever light we may? Had it any of the semblative merits of an allegory, or aught of the typical significance of a fable, it were somewhat; but it is destitute of moral or even mystical pertinence. At the best it is an artificial flower—not a natural one; and at the worst, its subject, even with all the graces and sanctions of Shelley’s genius swarming like “golden bees” about it, must ever bear the impress of being chosen rather to show the “extent of human wit”, than to set forth its poetical worth and potency. And this brings me to the the gravest objection of all against the whole 365 species to which it pertains: namely, that it has its genesis in a misusing of the spiritual as a mere vesture for the material, instead of making all external and formal beauty, what it only should be, the ocular robe and envelopement of the spiritual. In other words, sensible objects should only be thus elaborately personified for the purpose of giving local force & outlet to some covert Ideal,—some subtle tendency of feeling, or some ethereal conception of Love, and not for the mere sake of enducing them,—as it were on their own account,—with a strange unlikely vitality and most unnatural intelligence.
