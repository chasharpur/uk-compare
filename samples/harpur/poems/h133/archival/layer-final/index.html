Original Poetry.

FIRST-LOVE AND COURTSHIP.

With my Idalia, Oh! how soon The rapture-pinion’d moments fly. ‘Tis morn—we scarce seem met—and moon And stars are in the sky. Then, ere for the long night we part, Fondly-unsatisfied of heart, I turn, and oft I turn again, With dazzling eye, and eddying brain, To strive—how vainly!—to discover How much, how faithfully I love her.

And when is past the dull long night, She seems to me, again discern’d, As Heaven might unto Gabriel’s sight From this low world return’d! What startling raptures through me roll, Absorb my heart—o’erflow my soul— Whilst with a pang, most dear to know, My sight, awhile, and hearing, go— How vain the strife then, to discover How much, how faithfully I love her.

Oh! were to words new meaning strong, An all-informing radiance given— Or did I know the blessed tongue That seraphs speak in heaven— Or could , with limner art, my eye Give colour to th’ out-gushing joy, Give, starlike, in its orb, to burn, The silent spirit’s nameless yearn— I then might—scarcely then!—discover How much, how faithfully I love her.

ADDED STANZA.

‘Tis years since this wild lay I wove, And little, ah! did then I deem, The grateful glowing star of love Should only gild a dream. The maid was false—I know not why, My truth proved but a golden lie. Hence truth is battling from my head All—all the dear romance it bred; And caution whispering, “Don’t discover To your new love, how much you love her.”
