A Friend in need is a Friend indeed.

An o’er true story.

I was needing—not needy, but just at a rise In my aims, where a lift would ensure me a prize, And so I at once asked a Friend who of old Had vowed that he treasured my friendship like gold, 201a X And that to establish me, he, for his part, Would hold even cheap the red blood of his heart. Will you lend me a hundred, to spare me the ten Percent they extort in the Usurer’s den? 201b And what did this excellent son of his mother, Who loved me (these words were his own) like a brother?

197c

Why, after a sigh full of exquisite feeling, And a look at the floor, and then one at the ceiling, He replied, “Will I lend you a hundred, you say? Alas! my expenditure’s just that per day, In completing the wheel, as I yet hope to do, Of a fortune like that of Old Rothschild the Jew. And besides, I’ve just given a hundred at least, To a Church I deny and the War in the East: For my charity’s none of your home-centred sort, But begins far abroad, where the other falls short. And moreover I mean to give fifty or so In rozen to Hawser divine fiddle bow; Or to build a fine monument carved in relief 198 To that Housemaid heroic, who strove with the thief For a bundle of small clothes, till slaught[ere]d!—O grief! My heart at the thought gets so large in my breast, It will burst if I pause not, and hush it to rest.”

Here my Friend’s noble visage grew terribly gloomed; Yet his eyes, to my thinking, were strangely illumed With some roguish bye-meaning—At length he resumed. “This is bunkum, you’ll say; so it is, to a tittle; But the thrivers by bunkum must pet it a little. In short, I can’t aid you—I would if I could: I’m a trump—but I’m not in the cash-lending mood. Yet be you not downcast, my very dear fellow, Since your health is so good, and your pipe is so mellow; And the Visions of Genius, all lovely and rare, Are with you, no matter how rugged your fare! And your appetite also, I doubt not, is fine, While a girl of sixteen might think meanly of mine.

Ah, my friend, in the health of your Sylvan abode, Think of me in my counting house pent like a toad; With marrowless bones—a system quite shaken— And a skin like the rind of an old flitch of bacon. Hence, though you’re so needy and I am so rich, Which is luckiest?—ah, there is no knowing which! So, good bye—and (aside) go and die in a ditch.

This muttered—my Friend made a hasty removal, With a grim covert look of intense self-approval.

In the teeth of Belief be this better fact hurled, There’s a natureless spirit abroad in the world: For they would lend only who’ve nothing to lend, They only be friendly who cannot befriend; While ’tis ever the wealthy whose fingers most itch To be scraping still self-ward the hoards of the rich; And the beggar on horseback is he, be you sure, Who in riding to Hell would ride over the poor, And yet, there must here and there flourish a true man, Or God would abolish a race so inhuman!

From all I experience—all that I see— Some fatal conviction comes ever to me; 200 And the story I tell is beginning to hiss Like the head of a Fury with one:—it is this: My old heartless Friend, were he tougher than steel, Shall never succeed in completing the wheel Of the mighty concern (as he yet hopes to do) That’s to make him as rich as Old Rothschild the Jew: For the man who is false to a Friend in such need As relieved, would but give him to prosper with speed, Must have clubb’d with the Demon of ruin that day, And have shock’d, by the deed, his Good Angel away!

And though all the rivers of Charity ran Through my heart, should I spare thus to picture this man? This Friend, by the means of good rendered so bad, And through the success of his sanity,—mad? No! one who hath outraged my faith in mankind So vilely, is Rottenness, body and mind! With me he could scarcely smell worse were he dead, And his Species henceforward hath “Skunk” for its head.[※]

201c

※ Skunk is the name of an American animal, the most stinking in creation. If I remember aright, it is a sort of hybrid, being closely allied to both the weasle and otter.
