XV.

Critical Reviews of the last Age.

In yon dark corner, dusty as thy cell, Oblivion! and which therefore wombs them well, By gone Reviews lie huddled:—snappish all, And eke dogmatic—that is, critical. Prompt not so much to foster as abuse— The grim-faced Ogres of each youngling muse! Authorities whose strangely great dominion Rests but on little circles of opinion! Mental parade-grounds of restrictive rules,— Or rather, literary dancing schools, In which an author’s summoned to go through Certain set steps, but aim at nothing new, Unless he’d bring on half his life at least The saintly hate of some reviewing priest, Or make new quarrels with the much-scarr’d backs Of scape-goat editors and party hacks, Those foredoomed sages in whose palms the seer Finds stripes retributive in lines severe, And in whose horescopes (as well it may) The sign of “Horsewhip” holds celestial sway.

From those I learn that Author’s in our hearts Now horned, were wanting both in grace and parts: That Byron, even, a Poet grew to be From credit at the bank of Memory, And not from those born tendencies that claim Unpurchased freedom in the Bowers of Fame: That Shelley’s solemn bursts, and gushing gladness, Wild as the skylark’s, with his musical sadness, Were but the floodtides of—what think you? Madness! That Keats was a mere driveller; and poor White Strangely misled in thinking he could write; And Hunt’s nice hand, so fondly prompt to nurse Young truths of Song, but fit to cut a purse, Or lime a twig amongst Whitechapel’s sharp Bird catchers;—steal it might, not strike the Harp! That Wordsworth’s muse was windy to a jest, And her afflatus backward blasts at best: That Bulwer was a blackguard high in vogue, Whose villainous passion for a dashing rogue Wherewith to spice his Novels, might perchance Yet give this motto to some flash Romance: “Living he greatened Newgate’s great renown, And swung in glory when his star went down; That is, a highway Pegasus perforce 381 Bore him headlong—until hanged, of course!”

But on the other hand I’m told that wit And worth were one with some forgotten cit, Like Cottle, and his verses all but holy writ! That every Muse who without fault could sing Was held by Milman on the same bright string; And every power of humor fresh and clear Wedded to—Someone—when “bemused in beer”: That Gifford, with his small keen soul, was one Whom even Apollo might have hailed his son With cloudless front, so god-like was the blaze That lightened round him in Baviad days; Or that sleek Bowles, with several friendly Blues, Trod earth no doubt—but in seraphic shoes, And stockings dyed in heaven’s divinest hues! Or that the Northern Bard, whose lays show bright, As shallow streams do, from no depth of light, Wrote Epics that might turn a Homer pale With envy—seeing (I suppose) their sale, And those transmuted Heleconian dews (Coins with milled rims) it showered upon their Muse.

All this in those t once-famed Reviews I find, Which sway, even yet, the mediocre mind; 382 With more that’s worse; and thence feel honor-bound To sell them to some grocer—by the pound.

But of their Writers? Many were of note Amongst the Coteries for which they wrote, And living thus by—lying, truth to tell, Lived long, dined bravely, and digested well.

Note.

As a comprehensive commentary upon these three and seventy lines would ask the compass of a volume, I can only refer the reader to the British Monthleys and Quarterlies contemporaneous with the authors whose names are therein mentioned. And if the literary verdicts contained in any five or fifty numbers of each, taken promiscuously, when tested by the reversive justice of even an immediate posterity, do not choke up as with rotten drift timber the channel of his faith in a sub umbra Magazine Aristarchi thenceforward for ever,—why he himself must be an incorrigible Zoilus by nature, or have become one by the very process which is here suggested.

When I spoke in the text of Hunt’s “most dainty hand”, it strikes me now that it must have been more with reference to his poetical criticism than to his poetry;—though even that is very often as dainty and sweet, and beautifuly writ, as youthful womanhood. But in a certain genial, delicate, racy appreciation of the spells, and golden veins, and “spicëd airs”, in the writings of other men of genius,—men after large hearts, like his own, and believing imaginations,—he has, to my thinking, but few equals. And he can set all this forth in a prose style that is, both in its structure and bloom, always beautiful, and sometimes perfect.

Still he has a critical failing. He is too nice; too fastidiously prone to point out in his favorite poems, as artistic proprieties, certain alliterative and other minute felicities, which so far from being designed as matters of art, were never, it is likely, even so much as critically perceived by their authors themselves. No doubt such happinesses are often the self-approved results of a nicety of perception in those writers in whom they most abound. But he is sedulous, if I mistake not, to point them out, as direct evidences of poetic art; whereas, at farthest, they may be, and mostly are, but incidental showings of a peculiar turn of poetic nature. And here looms the evil. The critical petting of such matters may lead in the future to a vast deal 383 [of] literary foppery: that is, the habit to which I refer approximates so nearly to an abuse in Hunt himself, that it is perilously likely to become one all out in his critical imitators—and he is sure to have many such; and also in the poetical imititors of the Authors he has so super-subtilely criticised

For example, how fondly in his “Fancy and Imagination,” does he pet the stray beauties, few and far between, of certain old trashy authors?

“The things we know are neither rich nor rare, But wonder how the devil they got there.”

They are of little or no value in themselves; but then they are so refreshing on account of the surrounding barrenness. And then again, what a delicate critical segacity of nose does it require to detect the odor of a few poetic flowers in the midst of a whole wilderness of unpoetic weeds? And here, I say again, looms the evil. “On this hint” we shall have, if we have them not already, a whole class of nice-nosed critical piddlers, for ever smelling after stray and doubtful beauties—and this to the marked neglect of such as are manifestly “native to their soil”, like the roses of Sharon, or indisputably empireal, like the stars of heaven.

This judgment in the text upon thy muse of Sir Walter Scott will no doubt be dissented from by many. Still, I believe it to be, in the main, a just one. With much of the fire and strength of poetic genius, he had scarcely anything of its ideal subtlety and grace. He could outline the poetical face of things with considerable freedom and boldness—but the spiritual express- -ion of the “living soul” that lies under and yearns through it, escaped him almost wholly. Take away the proper names and merely local associations from even the best of his ballaad epics, and you absolutely reduce it to a picked bone. Nor would this reductive method in its his case have its analogy in that of the Frenchman who once said of the view of Windsor Castle: Take away the Thames and it it were nought! for the inherent beauty of such a river, which would still remain a “glorious feature” in any prospect, were everything else in it reduced to insignificance, is the very type, not of mere names and traditions, but of that central poetic prevalence itself, which is so primely a-wanting in the rhymed Romances of Scott.
