A Musical Reminiscence.

Here, as I wander where the Hunter draws Her deeper pools as by a glittering cord Of narrow rapids, running loud along; From the veranda of yon Cottage, clumped About with fruit-trees, and thence girdled wide With maize-fields, all in tassal,—hark, as once I heard it breathe o’er such another scene, Where the blue Hawksbury in long reaches draws Its broad abundance,—do I hear again The long lost air of an old rude-rhymed story Of early and hapless love! Forth how it swells, Voiced by the eloquent flute—the very same. A wild sad thing, in which the Python spirit Of ill-starred passion mingles mournful tones Even with such as, in triumphant starts, Depict the certainty of present joy! 128 Wild mournful tones, prophetical of change And of disparaged or recanted vows.

When first I heard it, I remember how It boded to my fancy; but my heart Did not its oracles interpret then So closely; for ’twas fond and confident, Nor versed in change, nor schooled to feeling’s vile And worldly fallings off.

But ’tis not this That moves me most, as I stand listening here To yon recurring sounds. It is the swift And subtle pace with which my spirit-self Hath hurried backward to its dim abodes In other years and regions. Strange! that those Most simple sounds, engendered of mere air Breathed through a reed-like tube, should rap me thus With their recurrence, o’er ten thousand bars Of intervenient feelings, objects, deeds, And seat me at once as on a wayside stone In the remotest distance of the Past!

This is it that most moves me; and in this Is there not more indeed, howe’er we reason, Than mere association, the quick craft Of Memory? Yea, infinitely more! Nay, what indeed if Time and Space, and all Which they enrobe,—the solid-seeming earth, And starry dome bent o’er it,—all be one In mystic being with that rare, unseen, Swift essence, the sole-absolute Spirit of Thought? The ultimate of Nature—yea, a rill From that great source which is the mind of God 129 And fountain of all secular Appearance: For there is nothing in this mighty world, But feeling and perception (which are but The darkling roots of Thought) do make it that Which to the most it seemeth in itself.
