BYRON.

A vast Imagination, and a free Fancy were his, but wanting subtlety Of Taste, his Verse, so vividly it glows, Lacks richness, and the beauty of repose: The beamy strength of polished steel behold, But where’s the serene pleasantness of gold?

Darkly magnanimous of soul, but proud And desperate of heart, he spake aloud The terrible mistrusts that from within Stained Life with a fatality of sin! And thus insure of moral faith, the strength Of his whole nature fell in twain at length; Balancing so, irresolute of choice, The love of Virtue and the lust of Vice, And oft in thought confounding them,—until From a strange insincerity of will, His better tendencies, subjected, waned Even as his worse a growing mastery gained.

And if a daring fervency of soul Whate’er he wrote made noble as a Whole, Yet Admiration’s self dare not exempt Too much in Byron from entire contempt! That sneering ’mid familiars at each feeling Wherewith to the world he seemed all wildly reeling; Making us doubt that with an Actor’s art, And not a Poet’s, he but played a part! That blending of the pure with the impure So vilely in his Woman portraiture! A scorn-masked envy of the luckier great! The littleness that hurled a Rival’s hate At Thought’s own oracles in Wordsworth’s strain! A nursing cherishment of foul disdain For what in others was but weak or vain, Or simple; flowing from their caste, their creed, Their social mode;—with much besides, indeed, That, howe’er masterly on the world’s mere plan, Was mischievously ignorant of Man.

His Conrads, Laras, Alps, and pallid Giaours— What is there like them in this Life of ours? For the dire ravin of the Tiger kind, In hunger there’s sufficient cause assigned; But if for gainless rapine, Men could be Like Byron’s—then indeed, a gallows tree Might symbolize Humanity for evil In the most classic short-hand of the Devil!

Now, let such airs as prompt the Opera dance In sensual Italy or shameless France, Announce his Women! all with large long eyes Darkly resplendent, like the midnight skies; All ripe and ready too for Love to dish them, And warmly wishing even as Lust could wish them! Yes; maugre the poetic draperies Thrown round his Parisinas and Haidees, How naked in their passions are they all, How earthly—how merely animal!

But wronged in Julia most hath Woman been: Portrayed at first as lovely in her mien As the new risen Moon; piously pure, Fervently gentle, thoughtfully demure; And all for what?—to show how soon she can Put on the shameless, scolding harridan! If this be true to Woman’s nature—then, There was no truth at all in Shakspeare’s pen.

But let me leave to the censorious ease Of well-paid Moralists (my betters) these Defections—such they are, even to the shame And detriment of all poetic fame! And only, as becomes me, tilt the while At some unsightly peccancies of Style.

Here must admit the most admiring one, That there is much to censure and to shun. The oft paraded mannerism quaint; The big no-meaning, and theatric feint; The pinning word on word a line to make, With the affectedly mysterious break So imitated by each rhyme-sick ass— As, “Well—I might have been—but—let it pass!” Even when he’s most felicitous and terse, We catch too frequently the make-weight verse; Great Homer’s style hath moles, ’tis said,—but these Are fistulous, and indicate disease.

Yet, I repeat, his fervency of soul Whate’er he wrote, made mighty as a Whole. Not more resistless in its downward will The Rhone pours thundering from its native hill, Than from his solitary spirit burst Its gathered load, by stormy passions nurst, To broaden on eternally in Song, So wildly naked—so supremely strong.
