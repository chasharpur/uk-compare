Part III.

Ranged are the nearer hills, and scoured Those vales o’er which too oft are poured From Wealth’s full hives amid the woods Their fleecy swarms, for wild-born broods Such as they only knew of yore To won about them as before: These ebbing back, as those intrude, On some yet trackless solitude; Even such as shall ere long unfold Its game-grazed scope to those Hunters bold, Making the gladdest gladder.—See, Merry men I trow they be! Or straggling out—or side by side— ‘Tis theirs, thus in the Forest green, To mix with manhood’s vigorous pride The glad surprise, the relish keen, And all the unresisted glee Of boyhood’s large sincerity! Mark with what fire each eye is filled— The inbreathed soul of the ancient wild! Mark with what life each motion’s willed! Were ever the nerves of the City child Thus, like a harp’s chords, thrilled?

Yon grassy nook, of forest bare, But belted round with cedars high, And clumped with brambles here and there, A famous place in days gone by For game was wont to be, And thitherward now all merrily hie The Hunters—half habitually: Recounting, as they view the grounds, The former feats of favorite hounds— Who as their masters tell the same, Wistfully catch the blazoned name, 110 And, as if conscious of the praise, Prance round them with a brawny grace.

That nook is passed—its pillary wall Of bordering wood now hides them all, As under its leafy arches they With echoing tread pursue their way: And soon the birds of a wilder scene Are starting to see them loom between The boles of mighty trees that ne’er Quaked at heart with dim dismay To hear the axe loud-ringing there. And, if because all bootless they Have hurried on as yet, Some one should thoughtlessly essay To make his hound curvet, Or to beguile the idle time Give lusty voice to a hunting rhyme, Right quickly shall another chide The trifler—he who, sharper eyed, All on a sudden hath heedfully spied In the grey dust laid by the heavy dews Fresh seeming slots;—each closely views The bushes near, as well aware How surely it may be seen, If brushed by aught, in its passing there, Ever so lightly they late have been, In the midst of their glistering fellows they’ll stand Like unadorned hinds a-near Lords of the Land. (1.)

But strictliest there is speech forbid Where deep inshelves some craggy lid, For if the Spirits of the Cave Be fully roused, unchecked they’ll rave— Visiting each rocky den That doth a viewless Sister hide, Till every Echo from glen to glen Take up the alarum to drive it wide O’er scenes where yet, close couched and warm, The game-flocks doze unweeting harm. 111 There, as they pass below the crags Whence wild figs, rooted ‘tween their jags, Slant forth against the sky, The eagle, watching o’er his brood, Views with spread wings and angry cry The invaders of his solitude.

Nor moves the tightest tongue beside The creek that sliding from some ledge, Where the mountain springs abide Then lists yon fern-flat’s slopeward edge, For yesterday there, at eventide, If shepherd John said truth,—as home His flock he drove in the gathering gloom, A solitary flyer (2.) was spied. Obsequious to the signed command, The hounds all come to a sudden stand, Or slink to their masters’ side, Whose practised eyes explore the place Before ’tis crossed with their wonted pace.

At length a belt of cedars tall In a broad scoop (3.), and undertwined With brushwood of each gadding kind, And with the rankest vines,—is all That, interposing, hides a scene Where hunter never hath bootless been. ‘Tis entered—and a sound upsprings Of lashing boughs and rushing wings! In a desultorious throng, What birds their varied plumes display! Flashing upward and along How Many and beautiful are they! The dove on burnished pinion strong Hurries afar in sounding flight, And the yerowalas (4.) unfold Their pointed wings of verdant gold, Fleetly flaming to the light 112 Each one singly hits the view Like a volent crysolite. And the rosella glories through The leaves;—and with its green, and blue And orange checquering on the sight, It seems a creature of rainbow birth, A little tarnished by the damp Of its after life on earth; Or the scarlet satin bird (5.) Hangeth like a golden lamp Where the berried bough is stirred: While haply the king parrot, showing Full aloft his broad red breast, Like a vast blood-rich ruby glowing, Near it boldly sits at rest. Or troops of rooks in loud alarm Mount the trees from arm to arm; (6.) While crowds of nameless twittering things With rich-ringed glossy eyes, and necks All streaked and interstreaked, and wings Spangled with rows of spark-like specks, And tails with broad barred blazonings, And crimson backs, and bosoms bright As the breast of a cloud in a sunset sky, Or glance afar in gleamy flight O’er leafy domes up heaping high, Or lighten through the nearer shade, Or sparkle aloft like stars new-rayed, Or stream in flocks, fright-disarrayed, Like shattered meteors by. (7.) With these the noisy yellow-bill, And the ground parrakeet (8.), screaming shrill As out of some high tussock near It starts at once into the clear, And seems while swiftly shooting higher Imped with plumes of subtle fire, So vividly it hues the light As thus it blazes forth in flight: And the whirring quail that skims the brush, 113 And the soaring pie, (8.) and the dodging thrush, Precede the Hunter as he pushes Through clinging vines and flaunting bushes, Or sternly chides the loitering dog That scents the coot in the hollow log, Or one that in pursuit would fly As oft the dooaralli (9.) rushes bounding by.

Soon as that lofty wood is past, While yet its margin shades the last, Comes swimming dim on the staggered eye As it lay loose against the sky, A prospect wide and vast! The more that morning’s mist yet keeps Cragging up in broken heaps Wherever the eye is cast. Here grassy slopes all greenly gay Save where embrowned with clumps of broom, Straggled wide, like clouds astray, Heave up, as they were pressed for room, Their broad breasts to the opening day; There steepy ridges loom Out from a sea-resembling gloom With peaks of iron grey, And bank yon glen that ‘neath their throng Of shadows, (10.) broken, broad and long, Winds like a giant’s path away. Full in its midst a rill’s deep course Leads on the eye to its flashing source Welled in yon Alp, that wedged between The valley’s lips, shuts in the scene. Its high bare back cuts half the blue Expanse of heaven sheer from the view, And thence, forth jutting bluffly,—lo, Its cliffs their time-hewn caverns show. Broods o’er the whole a dread repose That far a pleasing horror throws: Whilst Wonder deems, that in the glooms Clinging round those craggy domes, 114a And showing in their door-like rents That let to inward folding spaces, As they were mighty phantom tents, Pavillioning those lonesome places Wildest Spirits are wont to dwell, And brew the charm and mutter the spell That prosper those who meet them well. Ever she hears on such cavernous height A whisper by day and a moan by night, Though not the faintest breathing air Shall give one leaf to quiver there. Fittest site whereon to try The mental gold of Poësy, Or woo the mood that wings the soul And bears it to some visioned goal. There brightens Memory’s tearful eye And Sorrow herself forgets to sigh: While bardic rapture kindling clear As in its most primeval sphere, With a reflected insight rays Even Apathy’s else-stony gaze; And, to a nearer heaven, her prayer Religion best prefers,—a meek enthusiast there.

Notes to Part IV.

(1.) The appearance here described is often that of a closely couched flock of kangaroos when beheld at a distance of a hazy morning. But they are only to be surprised in such a position where subject to little disturbance, and where their pasture is plentiful. Be this, however, as it may, I describe what I have myself seen.

(2.) To show how little, or at least how loosely observant of the commonest natural appearances are the great majority of persons, one has only to refer to the old popular superstition about the sun’s dancing of an Easter morning. Where it otherwise, such a notion could never have obtained, even amongst the superstitious vulgar; because the newly risen sun will appear to dance whenever the atmosphere is in a certain condition, neither very hazy nor yet perfectly clear: or rather, he will appear to bloat and wabble unsteadily, somewhat after the manner described in the text. And even when the air is quite clear, there is always (while he is rising or when just risen) an apparent intervol[ve]ment of motion, or a seeming dilatation and contraction in the blazing mass of his disc, such as the eye of superstitious expectancy (being studious of this appearance only of an Easter morning) might easily magnify into an absolute manifestation of religious jollity. The old superstition here spoken of has been very fancifully pressed into poetry by Sir John Suckling in his Country Wedding, where describing the bride dancing, he says:

Her feet beneath her petticoat Like little mice stole in an out As if they feared the light: But oh! she dances such a way, No sun upon an Easter day Is half so fine a sight!

(3.) All the circumstances mentioned in the seventeen lines which compose this passage, must be supposed to pass almost momentarily—or at least in far less time than their narration unavoidably occupies. This observation bears also upon the other passages of the poem. Indeed, an allowance of the kind must be conceded as a matter of course, to any particular and minute description of several matters which occur all together, or even in rapid succession.

(4.) The men gain the glen as soon as the hounds by means of what is called “cutting off”—that is to say, by so adapting their course to the actual and anticipated deviations of the hunt, as in some measure to converge with it upon particular points. Where the ground is well known, this may be often done; and their manner of doing it is fully shown in the ensuing Part, of which their movements, in particular, form the subject.

(5.) This may appear to be what Pope wittily terms a “simile unlike”. But it is not. Amongst low bushes or high grass, a hound at the top of his speed, has a round or hooped appearance. The reason is this: his extremities are hidden, and keeping his head low, he gathers himself up so quickly in the act of bounding, that his back presents to the eye the semblance of a constant curve—the visible section of an orbicular body rolling rapidly away.

(6.) What is here called the woodvine is a shrub the boughs of which terminate in long leafy runners, exceedingly tough, and which clasp and climb, with many wild contortions, whatever forestry of a higher growth may happen to neighbour them. But when several of these shrubs stand together, apart from other trees, their runners have no resource but to intertwine for mutual support—thus forming a curiously wovencanopy over the parent stems; or weighing them down at length, in their ultimate luxuriance, the whole mass becomes a heap or clumb of singularly “implicated” living cordage.

(7.) Namely, two of her nearly full grown pups, which, after the manner of dogs—of the greyhound kind especially—put themselves, up to maturety, entirely under the discreet guidance of their respected dam; therein affording an excellent example to their assumed betters—us humans— during our nonage.

(8.) This is the error or fault on the part of the hounds which is referred to in the Preface, as being designed for the purpose of protracting the pursuit with a due regard to probabelity.
