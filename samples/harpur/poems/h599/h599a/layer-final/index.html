To a Lady

on hearing that she remarked, while censuring certain slips in the Author’s conduct, that “they would not be at all mindable if his circumstances were independent.”

Though, Lady fair, thy judgment’s more Than generally lenient, I’m irked to find its moral lore So mightily convenient.

My slips, it seems, fill Censure’s throat With proverbs sage as musty, Only because I fill a coat That looketh somewhat rusty.

But though, when so ’tis governed, I May only smile at censure, Henceforth, in self defence, I’ll try 48 To make my life more pence-sure:

Or pocket-virtuous, if the phrase May better give my meaning: And gain, by patching thus my ways, Mammon’s ‘well done’ for screening. x

x Men’s acts are judged of by society upon grounds al- -together too positional. Of course our outward circum- -stances should determine the amount of good or evil in particular acts, but not to the extent of making what is almost a virtue in one individual a vice in another. In a declaration of sentiment, for instance, what would be held the height of imprudence, if nothing worse, in a poor man, is often taken for an evidence of spirit and generosity in a wealthy one. Now this is pernicious, and clearly indicates that the present structure of society is miscalculated to give fair play to that bold individuality of character which best constitutes its moral harmony. C.H.

On reviewing the above note I would add a few words to it. I am no apologist for vice in any, myself included. At this moment, I am thankful to Providence, for all that I may have suffered for past misconduct. Still I affirm that society in censuring me at all, while it over looked the same faults in others, on purely positional con- -siderations, was acting meanly, and in a manner only calculated to draw upon itself the contempt and enmity of nine out of every ten delinquents who should make the same observation under the same circumstances. Even open profligacy is not the parent of so vile a brood of social evils as is a pretendedly God fearing Mammon-worship. Even the self-declared villain may have something noble remaining in him; but he who has once systematically arrayed himself in the conveniences of Pretence, is thenceforth ingrainëdly ignoble forever. C.H.
