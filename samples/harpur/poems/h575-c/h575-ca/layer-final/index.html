Thus having spoken, the swift-footed Iris departed; But Achilles, beloved of Jove, arose, And Minerva threw her fring’d ægis about his strong shoulders. And the divine one of goddesses Crowned his head with a golden cloud, Wherefrom she kindled a shining flame. And as when smoke goeth up from a city And reaches the æther from a far-off island, Which foes invest, who issuing from their own city, Contend all day in hateful fight; But with the setting sun torches blaze in succession, And the brightness thereof ascends, rushing up aloft, For neighbours to behold, and haply come with ships, As repellers of the war. Thus did the flame From the head of Achilles reach up to heaven. Advancing from the wall to the trench, he stood there, Nor did he mingle with the other Greeks, For he heeded the sage counsel of his mother. There standing, he shouted, and Pallas Minerva Made also on the other side of the trench a loud outcry, Which stirred up a vast tumult amongst the Trojans. And as the tone of a trumpet is very clear, 329 When sounded by deadly foes beleaguering a city, So distinct was then the voice Of the descendent of Æacus. But when they heard the brazen voice of Achilles, All were troubled to the soul. At the same time the beautiful-maned steeds Turned the chariots backwards, foreboding afflictions in their mind. The charioteers too were panic-struck While beholding that dreadful unresting flame, Which blazed above the head of the magnanimous Pelides: For the azure-eyed Goddess, Minerva, lighted it. Thrice over the trench the noble Achilles loudly shouted, And thrice were flung into confusion Were the Trojans and their glorious allies. And there perished then twelve of their bravest heroes, By their [own] chariots and spears.
