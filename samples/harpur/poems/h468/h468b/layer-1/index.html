VIII.

Collins.

A Genius caged in niceties of art; A full-souled Bard that should have thought apart, Creatively peculiar—not as taught By models which, however richly wrought, (Like polished jewels set in chastened gold) Have lost at length their birth-fire, and are cold.

317

Yet how shot through with beauty are the Lays His nice hand fashioned for the after days! Painting and Sculpture in his verse combine With Poesy; and breathe through every line In harmonised transfusion, they dispense The spirit of a triune excellence.

Note.

No sufficient critic, I think, will now venture to deny, that Collins walked too exclusively by the light of certain poetical canons which in his day were esteemed the arcana of all that is classical; but which, in fact, restricted Poetry to a mere foregone art,—for ever fixed and consummated,— to the disparagement and even displacement of a present and everlasting nature. But while admitting this, we have only to consult the Ode on the Passions, for ample con- -firmation of the critical veracity of the concluding lines of the text.

For example, how consummately picturesq as a group are the impersonations in the following extract, and at the same time, how sculturesq in their several individualities; while for the peculiar inwardness and life of poetry, let the reader observe the expressions in italics.

318

But thou, O Hope, with eyes so fair, What was thy delightful measure? Still it whispered promised pleasure, And bade the lovely scenes at distance hail. Still whould her touch the strains prolong, And from the rocks, the woods, the vale, She called on Echo still through all her song, And when her sweetest theme she chose A soft responsive voice was heard at every close: And Hope enchanted smiled, and waved her golden hair. And longer had she sung; but with a frown Revenge impatient rose; He threw his blood-stained sword in thunder down, And with a withering look The war-denouncing trumpet took, And blew a blast so loud and dread, Ne’er were prophetic sounds so full of woe! And ever and anon he beat The doubling drum with furious heat; And though sometimes, each dreary pause between, Dejected Pity at his side Her soul-subduing voice applied, Yet still he kept his wild unaltered mien, While each strained ball of sight seemed bursting from his head.

The tremendous line here,

Nere were prophetic sounds so full of woes,

appears to me to contain the suggestion, however remotely obtaining, of that grandly mysterious one in Coleridge’s Kubla Khan:

Ancestral voices prophesying war!
