THE ROSE TREE.

A matchless Rose Tree! From the shaded mould Banked tow’rds the garden wall, its bole twists up Massive and knarled in miniature, and whence, As it ascends, a hundred lesser stems Branch off, and so, contorting all, are led Upward and spreadingly against the stones: And thus it well beseems a fairy picture Of some vast vegetable wonder, such As only is seen, in mighty growth, to climb The face precipitous of some Indian Alp.* Then, midway up the wall, its wide-spread boughs No further stayed, lop outward in a mass, And by their weight of leaves, as still the sprays Keep tapering all, are curved into a dome: A leafy Heaven! from whose vernal skies A thousand glowingly consummate blooms, Pensile and round, gaze smiling down upon me! Even like the faces of so many Cherubs, Wing-frilled, and flushing with celestial health.

* The Alimucta is here alluded to. It is the most gigantic of all vines; and when it meets with nothing higher than itself to clasp, it is said to assume the form of a sturdy tree, its topmost branches indicating, how- ever, as they contort and tress onward in the air, their natural inclination to climb. A vine of this kind will sometimes festoon the whole face of a vast precipice.— See Asiatic Researches.

C. H.
