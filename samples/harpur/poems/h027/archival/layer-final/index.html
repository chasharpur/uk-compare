III.

The Ballot.

‘Tis cowardly, cried Mort, with aspect stout, To vote in secret upon any grounds; And so it were, in Voters hedged about, As he is, with a hundred thousand pounds.

Note.—A vote in itself is virtually the same, whether recorded openly or under the secrecy of the Ballot. It can suffer no damage, as an electoral sanction, from the mode in which it is given. But the holder of a vote may be so circumstanced, that the ill will of his landlord (for instance), of whose principles or party he does not approve, would be of power even to ruin him, in the event of his suffrage being sought, or rather demanded, by that dominant individual; and yet openly with- holden or given in favor of another. Now in a case like this, and in all cases analagous to it, unless it can be shown that the virtue of the vote itself is touched detrimentally, by the manner of recording it, the secrecy of the Ballot is not only a social necessity, but it were also a moral good. It were so, because an honest exercise of the franchise should not be made to imperil the welfare of any man who may or might be obnoxiously circumstanced. And hence, even he who by nature or by fortune, may be superior to an unconscientious dis- charge of his duty as an elector, under any mode of voting, should yet consent to the Ballot; or, refusing to do so, he should be able, in some other way, to prevent among his compatriots those social dilemmas and many moral infirmities which are so glaringly unfavourable to the popular integrity of all open voting. No other expediency can be so well pleaded for as the Ballot. That cannot be a wrong which displaces no right. That cannot be properly inimical to existing interests, which tends to conserve the faithful exercise of an existing privilege; and which can only work for radical change, through legitimate effects and progressive influences.
