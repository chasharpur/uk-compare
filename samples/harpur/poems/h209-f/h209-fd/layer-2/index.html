Part V.

See now upon the mountain side The dusky brakes among In lengthened race and scattered wide The Hunters burst along! Six hold the lead; two, sorely tried, Those follow; and two, afar behind, Ancle-shocked, through the game tracks wind Slowly and pained—and with angered look That tells how ill their plight they brook: While almost even with the van, But glenward more, a single man Comes on with eager pace— But vainly he so well hath sped, For soon yon gorge’s passless head Will wall him from the Chase! And far he’ll have to hurry back Ere he may then regain its track: I guess that so to have gone astray Will sharpen his wit another day.

But who are these that lead—on going With a pace so freely flowing?? Five are sons of this evergreen Land, Fleet of foot and strong of hand. The sixth is one with a foreign name Who o’er the seas a wanderer came. ‘Tis said, proud Fortune’s golden rays Were shed through all his earlier days; Till over him swept an evil wind, To leave a life-long blight behind. Though sad his brow and drear his eye— Though his breast oft labors aloud with a sigh, Yet to compete the challenged race Full prompt is he, and in the Chase Right eager—but, who hath seen the while 128 His shrunken cheek ever sunn’d by a smile? A daysman (1.) he, and his job once done, Moody and lone he wanders on.

But five are sons of this summery Clime, all quick with the soul of its pristine prime! Right able each one to fulfil the part Of a bold brave will and a generous heart. Nor brook they bigot Custom’s reign, Nor galls them old Oppression’s chain But creedless, here, they worship God, And freely roam the hills where ne’er The foot of a Tyrant trod, Or the head of a fellow worm might wear That frater-fraud—a kingly crown! And in the echoing woods, when down Thundering crash the primeval trees, What men can wield the axe with these? Or urging there the unwilling wedge, So strongly swing the ponderous sledge? In taming the stubborn steer to the yoke, Or guiding the root-obstructed plough— In Bending the harvest to the hook, Or plying hard the heavy hoe;— In the bound of hight—in the leap of length— In laboring through the long trial of strength; To rival these may few aspire: Their limbs are thunder, their souls are fire! (2.)

First, Mileham, like a rushing tower, Along yon steeping rock doth scour, Spanning the frequent rift withal Gaily contemptuous of a fall! Him the bard Linus follows fast, The slimmest there, but ne’er the last— A place amongst the foremost still His flying foot and fiery will Claim proudly, or on plain or hill: For never was there bard, but well 129 He loved in all things to excel.— Sweet is the woodland voice of Spring— But sweeter can young Linus sing: How bright a painter is the Morn? Yet brightlier can his light adorn Whatever he loveth—soul or thing. Hence can he, with creative hand, Fling glory and gladness o’er the Land Where stark and lonely most it lies; And make at will, with charmful stave, The spirits of the mountain cave Seem voices drawn from Paradise. Not always (as a mid-sea wave Unsettled) in these woods so lone And wild, hath roamed that dreamy one: Far from the scenes his memory loves— Far from his birth stream’s murmurous moan the scene of his childhood’s home, For his soul’s sake prized by none, he roves But filled with a pine that but seldom removes For the days that are gone: When by the might of song he gave Glad meanings to old Hawksbury’s wave, And peopled all the bordering groves With Graces and melodious Loves.

Where grass-trees (3.) fring the glenward edge Of yon long sloping terrace-ledge, Lo, Orland of the valley hies Right forward, with a dizzy heed; There, too, the graceful Ossian flies On, as with a wingëd speed; While Gregory of the doughty deed, With envious space-devouring eye Strains, body and soul, to pass him by! But vainly, Gregory, mayst thou try! Though mightiest in the village fray Thou hast a rival in the Chase, For never might beholder say 130 Young Ossian’s rearward in the race. ‘Mongst those with whom he loves to be Though gentlest of the gentle, he If needful ‘twere, might well command The warrior’s avenging brand: Witness that memory-haunting day, When whooping along in fell array, Dark savages, like demons dread, Amongst the backwood dwellings spread Dire death and sore dismay! Till, like a lion in his wrath, He met them on their wasteful path— A beardless war-god in the way, And smote them back, and as they fled Littered the forest with their dead!— And Ossian too is skilled With cunning hand to build [unclear] Rhymes beautiful as strong (4.) Hard eyes that had for many a year Been tearless, have run o’er to hear His true and touching song;— Old men have smiled as they smiled when young While he hath framed some ballad gay— Old men that smileless were for long, Until that day.

Yet higher upon the mountain side, Where kerrijongs, so brightly green, Out from the upper crags are seen Hitherward slanting; There Will of the Herd’s best mettle is tried By the dark Jobber;—stride for stride They burst the tangled brakes between, Eager and panting.

And as, like Creatures of the Wild, The Hunters all thus hurry on, Against yon precipice up piled Of mighty stone on stone, 131 Like giant ghosts their shadows glide, Thrown sheer across the gorge even where Tis full a furlong wide! A fearful sight to the warragl there As he slinks along to his mountain lair; And eke of wonder wild, no doubt, To the eagle himself, as he stareth out O’er the whole wide view with his eyes of fire From the topmost stone of yon craggy spire.

Oh! these, I trow, are woodsmen rare: Yea, such their ready sylvan skill, That where they once have known to fare, The hidden turns of the lonely rill, And the secret traits of each trackless hill, Drear valley, and cloud-thick jungle, still Are laid by their memories bare! And thence full well do they all foreknow Where the bent of the ground,—or high or low, Broken or plain,—shall tempt or force The fagged and desperate kangaroo To alter his instant course: And thitherward by a shorter route Straight tending, they shall keep in view The Hunt as it shapes about.

And lo, even now, with that intent Their course is suddenly westward bent, For northward, with a circling sweep, A rocky wall uprises steep Against the fugitive’s escape; And o’er those Hunters fleet shall fall The shadow of that rocky wall, Ere he can round its western cape: And there, as past the greyhounds strain, Well breathed, on with them shall they start again!

(1.) That is, a laborer who only hires by the day, or for the performance of a particular job.

(2.) It must be confessed that there is in this part of my poem a rather strong infusion of a quasi national glorification—must I so call it?—a quasi national glorification then. Nevertheless there is a bright bead of truth sparkling through the spirit of it, and mantling to the surface, like an evidence of good liquor. The physical completeness of my countrymen, as a race, is undeniable; and I believe their moral and mental capacities only require adequate culture to become equally allowed and remarkable. A good national system of Education, by effecting this, would eventually make Australia the mother of one of the finest people in the world. And for how long shall the bigoted fears and party covetousness of irrational or dishonest sectaries be allowed to prevent her of this prime desideratum? Or are the soul= =dwarfing antagonisms of “chartered” and unchartered priesthood to curse the future of Australia as they have cursed the past of Europe?

That the children of the South are naturally capable, speaking generally, of the highest moral and intellectual developement, is a broad fact attested by Phrenology;—this organic excellence being itself attributable to the genial influences of a surpassing climate, operating conjointly with a happy admixture of races. And thoroughly advised of this fact, I would even have my countrymen beget in themselves a wholesome contempt for the ingrained prejudices of old-world guides and alien hirelings. A good system of National Education, I say again, is the very Shiloh of their Country’s greatness. In preparing the way then, and making the path straight, for the advent of such a system, let them be true to themselves and to their children: let them be bravely mindful of the noble inheritance which Providence has allotted to them and their posterity. Finally, in devising their System of National Education, let them be full of faith in the divinity of the spirit of knowledge: for so shall all knowledge be ultimately eviscerated of evil. Let them also have entire belief in the religiousness of Science: for all her revelations, all her veritable teachings, are the indubitable scriptures and precepts of God!

(3.) The grass-tree must be well known to almost every person who has been a few miles out of Sydney. The cane that grows out of its tuft, is used by the aborigines for the shafts of those light spears which they dart by means of the hooked stick called a womera. Its stem and roots exude a sort of pitch, which is also used by the Blacks, to fasten the iron-wood heads of the spears above mentioned into the shafts: and when grubbed up and dried, these parts of the grass tree are excellent fuel.

(4.) Under this name, the author has characterised a very dear friend. In early life, he was wont (as might be inferred from such a nom de guerre) to take his mental inspiration mainly from the Muse; and many of his metrical pieces have been pronounced by competent judges, to breathe that subtle aura which, more than any thing else, distinguishes poetry from prose, and which may be, and often is, utterly absent even from verse, however polished and elegant, or otherwise meritorious we may consider it,—that is, when regarded on its true level, or only as composition.

Under the other names of the several hunters real persons are also represented. The names only are fictitous. And with respect to whatever of self-valuation may be very obviously reflected upon one of them in particular, the author has nothing for it, but to shelter himself behind a certain poetic prescription, founded on much glorious usage and ancient as the hills. To do this, however, should be to do enough. The Poet has a kind of vocational right to no small liberty of self-laudation; because, in him, arising as it does out of a generous desire of fame, which is but “love in disguise”, it is not selfishness; or because he has been pricked to it by neglect or by wounded good will;—and moreover, because it is often the only happy and grudgeless praise that he receives (this praise from himself to himself) during his sojourn in the world, for all the beautiful forms and lovely idealities which his spirit bestows upon it. Nevertheless, even in his case, when least thanked and most neglected, there must be, I suppose, a meet reward somewhere, or at all events bare payment; either in the final consequences of our being, or in the eternal tendencies of the soul.
