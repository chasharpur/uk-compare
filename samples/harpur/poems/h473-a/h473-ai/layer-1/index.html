2.

His Christabel.

Note.

I think I have, or have had, upon the whole, a greater admiration for the poetry of Coleridge, than for that of any one of his contemporaries. Yet, in imaginative sanity, it’s inferior to the poetry of Wordsworth. Nor has it the direct force and earnest passion of much of Byron’s; or a classic purity of expression equal to that which characterises even the wildest flights of Shelley. The best things of Keats too, exceed in a certain sensuousnes loveliness. And moreover, the most wonderful of all his productions, and in many respects the most beautiful,—Christabel and the Ancient Mariner,—both savour somewhat too strongly either of opium or indigestion. Their afflatus, their inspiration, even as we trace it through a consummate effect, was so manifestly morbid, that it yet touches them, so though in a manner remote and retroactive, with unpleasant intimations of foregone disease. Apart from their subject—apart from the wild weird drift of them, there is something strangely haggard in their causal 355 reference to the spiritual struggles and sufferings of Coleridge himself: as the lines in a prematurely worn countenance, however bright its present expression, are the too palpable revelations of an accomplished woe. And hence I deduce the opinion, that to be thoroughly agreeable under all tests, the preternatural in art must have always a healthy—a Shakesperian progeneration.

Yet, notwithstanding every conceivable drawback, there are strokes and passages all over his poetry, of an excellence so supreme, that we can for ever return to it, again and again, as with a whetted appetite.

How sweetly perfect in its beauty is the following picture.

It was a lovely sight to see The lady Christabel, when she Was praying at the old oak tree. Amid the jagged shadows Of mossy leafless boughs, Kneeling in the moonlight To make her gentle vows; Her slender palms together pressed, Heaving sometimes on her breast; Her face resigned to bliss or bale— Her face, O call it fair, not pale! 356 And both blue eyes more bright than clear, Each about to have a tear.

His early poems were too mouthy to be rightly musical, but his later ones are unique in the melody of their metrical movements. As an example of this, take the following stanza. If it were wholly meaningless, it would yet, in its metrical structure, and as a mere vehicle for the voice, be absolutely pleasing. But when, with this melody of mere movement, we drink in all its other exceeding beauty, what a piece of sufficing music it is!

It ceased; yet still the sails made on A pleasant noise till noon, A noise like of a hidden brook In the leafy month of June, That to the sleeping woods all night Singeth a quiet tune.

His poem called Love, to which I particularly allude in the text, is, to my thinking, the divinest erotic in our literature. Neither the exceeding and serene delights of a summer sunset, nor the ethereal pathos of a starry night, is a lovelier manifestation of the Spirit of beauty, 357 than is that poem; while to the heart of man it should be even dearer than they, being utterly a creature of human emotion. On the subject of Love, I know of nothing so lovely. Moore’s best amatory pieces are but Cyprianades in comparison. Yet is it in nowise deficient of passion—but it is passion blended throughout with those holiest lights of love and splendors of poesy, which seem quietly conscious, like the stars, of their own immortality

Finally, take the ensuing Jacobinical prophecy against England as an example of a sublimity almost unparagoned. So vast and terrible is it in its moral import, that its material analogue might only be looked for in the sudden up-thundering of an earthquake, or of an oceanic cataclysm,—or in the awful bulk and stormy grandeurs of some Mount Terror of the physical world, and which as thus seen is only half seen; is only the ocular shell—the mere outside of a measureless magazine of “storëd thunder” and latent destruction.

O abandoned of heaven! mad avarice thy guide At cowardly distance, yet kindling with pride— ‘Mid thy corn-fields and herds thou in plenty hast stood, And joined the loud yellings of famine and blood. The nations curse thee; and with eager wond’ring 358 Shall hear Destruction, like a vulture, scream! Strange eyed Destruction, who, with many a dream Of central fires through nether seas upthund’ring, Soothes her fierce solitude; yet, as she lies By livid fount or red volcanic stream, If ever to her lidless dragon eyes Visions of thy predestined ruins rise, The fiend-hag on her perilous couch doth leap, Muttering distempered triumph in her charmèd [unclear] sleep!

Thus wrote Coleridge in the “prime and manhood of his intellect”; and though he afterwards adopted a tone [unclear] in the conservative extreme, as being vastly more suitable to his worldly welfare—no matter. Nor does it much matter now, that the later manifestations of his character ran out into a spiritual cant and claptrap that were exceedingly deplorable. And though his metaphysical pretensions were always so preposterously bloated and merely bewildering, as to make even those who were disposed to admire him immeasurably, sometimes distrustful and even ashamed of him,—yet,

Take him for all and all When shall we look upon his like again?
