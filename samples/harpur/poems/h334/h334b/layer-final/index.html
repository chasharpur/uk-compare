THE NIGHT BIRD.

To cheer my lonely dwelling shed, Upon its humble roof is heard, As duly I retire to bed, A little nightly Bird: Hark! ever there the creature sings Its iterant but melodious things.

Beneath the silent shining stars, And while the moon pays down her light, Although no wiery thraldom bars The freedom of its flight, There nightly thus it ever sings Its iterant but melodious things.

And while I listen, there will come Dim memories of the past, that say Upon my childhood’s happy home A little shrilling lay Was nightly heard, like this that brings Those iterant but melodious things.

I know not if it were in kind The same with yon peculiar bird, But on my Father’s roof I mind, How often we had heard A voice like this without that sings Those iterant but melodious things.

But where is he, the parent sage, Whose Seven then met his nightly call? Where him, whose cheek of tenderest age Was fondliest kissed of all? Ask sorrow—while the creature sings Its iterant but melodious things.

And now too there’s a grave new made ‘Mid scenes to memory unknown, And there cold stranger hands have laid Our manly hearted John! I weep—while still the creature sings Its iterant but melodiouz things.*

* ”We were Seven”—and our family in its earlier years was pre-eminently a happy one. In fact, I have never since seen a greater degree of domestic peace and unity than was presented by my Father’s household for many prosperous years. But trouble at length made her abode in our midst, and a few evil seasons 7a were enough to break up the foundations of our family welfare, and scatter us, some in life and some in death, over half the world; beginning with Henry our youngest, who died a child. Then, after a hard but fruitless struggle in the desolate bye-ways of fortune, our father followed; and latterly our brother John, who died in early manhood in Seymour, a wild district somewhere beyond Geelong in Victoria, and was shovelled into his lonely grave (as we learned afterwards) without even a burial service. Of our youngest living brother, William,—if he be living,—who went to sea a boy, we have heard little for years:—and this is nearly the whole sum of the past and present fortunes of our family. Such is life—a fever dream ending his death. Such is the world—a great blind fortune trampling hearts into dust.

But should the reader infer from the manner of John Harpur’s burial that he could not have been of any “mark and likelihood” he would err considerably. He was a man of fine natural parts,— honest, generous, and brave. But he lived and died in the Australian Bush, and amongst those by whom a dead Crichton, if not richly related, would be given to the dust without a stone to mark the spot” I speak advisedly. Such would inevitably have been my own case, had I died amongst those with whom I was compelled to herd during the most intellectual years of my life (and this while Martins were creeping into the Legislative Council of my country, and Nicholsons being knighted.) Not that such a social state is aught to the dead: they are happily beyond its low- thoughted influence. But then it perpetuates the brutalization of the living.

Still, for myself, I do not now complain of the world, or or any part of it; by which term of ‘the world’ we mean, I think, that man-devised frame of society which rests upon the selfish passions and the compedative bias of the intellect. I care not now to com- plain of it; for I can contend with it on its own terms, and with its own weapons, and admit therefore, that it is well enough, even in its sum total this our poor, rich, polluted Australia. Aye, it is well enough; albeit an eternal verification of the old rhymes:—

[THERE’S NO WORLD LIKE THE ONE THAT WE LIVE IN]

There’s no world like the one that we live in, To spend, or to lend, or to give in; But to beg, or borrow, or come by one’s own, ‘Tis the very worst world that ever was known: And the sooner we slip from its folly and fuss The better for it, and the better for us.

And so be it. The two added lines in italics are mine, and I make a present of them to the spirit of the proverb.

Granbelang, 1854.

C. H.
