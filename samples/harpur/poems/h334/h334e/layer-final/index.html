The Night Bird.

To cheer my lonely dwelling shed, Upon its humble roof is heard, As duly I retire to bed A little nightly Bird: Hark! ever there the creature sings Its iterant but melodious things.

Beneath the silent shining stars, And while the moon pays down her light, Although no wiry thraldom bars The freedom of its flight, 25 There nightly thus it every sings Its iterant but melodious things.

And while I listen, there will come Dim memories of the Past, that say Upon my Childhood’s happy home A little shrilling lay Was nightly heard, like this that brings Its iterant but melodious things.

I know not if it was in kind The same with you peculiar Bird, But on my Father’s roof I mind How often we had heard, A voice like this without, that sings Those iterant but melodious things.

But ah! for him, the parent sage Whose Seven then met his nightly call; And him, whose cheek of tenderest age Was fondest kissed of all! I murmur—while the creature sings Its iterant but melodious things.

And now too, there’s a Grave new made ‘Mid scenes to Memory unknown, And there cold stranger hands have laid Our manly-hearted John! I weep—while still the creature sings Its iterant but melodious things.

1847.
