THE CLOUD.

AN ALLEGORY,

BY CHARLES HARPUR.

One sultry morn, out of the sea-waves wild A speck-like cloud arose, and as a child Playfully glided up the broad blue sky, And o’er the sun-parched hills all brown and dry.

Onward she glided, through the azure air Borne by the breeze without or toil or care, Till looking down in her etheral joy, She marked earth’s laborers at their hard employ. And oh! (she said) that I could somewhat do To ease the burthens of yon toil-worn crew, To give the hungry food, the thirsty drink— The thought of good is very sweet to think.

The day advanced—and the cloud greater grew, And greater still; and her desire to shew Some kindness unto mankind, greatened too: Greatened and warmed within her fleecy breast Like a dove fledging in its downy nest.

The heat waxed fiercer, until all the land Glared in the sun’s rays like a mighty brand! Ill could earth’s laborers their toil endure, Though still they toiled, for they were very poor. And many a look, with many a sigh, they sped Tow’rds that fair cloud;—beseeching looks that said We famish for thy bounty! For the sake Of pity, in a showery blessing break!

I feel, and I would help you! said the cloud, And tow’rds the earth her bounteous being bowed. But then rememb’ring a tradition she Had, while a child, learned from her native sea, That when a cloud adventures from the skies Too near the alters of the hills—she dies! Awhile she wavered, and was blown about, Hither and thither, by the winds of doubt. But in the midst of heaven at length all still She stood, and said within herself, I will! Yea, in the glad strength of devotion, I Will help you, though in helping you, I die.

Filled with this thought’s divinity, the cloud Kept greatening more, as earthward more she bowed. 258b O never erewhile had she dreamt her state So great might be—beneficently great! O’er the parched fields, in her angelic love, She spread her wings out like a brooding dove: Till as her purpose deepened into storm, So terrible in its grandeur grew her form, That men and beasts all trembled at the view, And the woods bowed—though well at heart all knew That near, in her, their destined benefactress drew.

Yea, I will help you! said the Cloud again: Receive me—lo, I die for you—in rain! Suddenly flashed then through her full grown form The glory of her will—the agony and the storm Of that contention with desire of life Which even in Christ’s devoted heart was rife? Flashed, seething out of rents amid her heaps Of lowering gloom, and thence with arrowy leaps Hissed jagging downward, till a sheety glare Illumined all the illimitable air! The thunder followed, a terrific sound Loud doubling and reverberating round, Till one wide rumbling intonation rolled Through all the shuddering mountains manifold; Strong was her will, but stronger yet the power Of love that now dissolved her in a shower, Dropping in blessings, to impregne the earth With health and plenty at one blooming birth. She bled in vain—bled even as martyrs bled; Bled unto death, and glorious was the dead!

Far as the rain extended o’er the land, A splendid bow the freshened prospect spanned, As the last greeting of the spirit pure Of love self-sacrificed, and thence made sure, Because in record on the Eternal writ In faith and hope, and to endure with it.— The rainbow vanished, but the blessing craved Long rested on the land the cloud had saved.
