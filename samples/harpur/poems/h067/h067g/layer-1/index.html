The Cloud. X

One Summer morn, out of the sea waves wild A speck-like cloud, the season’s fatal child, Came gladly gliding up the broad blue sky, And o’er the sun-parched hills all brown and dry.

Onward she glided, through the azure air Borne by its motion without toil or care; When looking down in her ethereal joy She marked Earth’s moilers at their hard employ: And oh, (she cried) that by some act of grace ‘Twere mine to succor yon load-wearied race, To give the hungry food, the thirsty drink— The thought of good is very sweet to think.

The day advanced—and the Cloud greater grew, And greater still; and her desire to do Some charity to mankind, more and more As the long arid Summer day on wore, Greatened and warmed within her fleecy breast Like a dove fledging in its downy nest.

The heat waxed fiercer, until all the land Glared in the sun’s rays like a mighty brand. Ill could Earth’s children their long toil endure Though still they toiled, for they were very poor. And many a look, with many a sigh, they sped Tow’rds yon fair Cloud;—beseeching looks, that said We famish for thy help! For pity’s sake, Oh, break thou! in a showery blessing break!

I feel, and I would help you! said the Cloud, And tow’rds the Earth her bounteous being bowed. But then rememb’ring a tradition she Had, while a child, learned from her native sea, That when a Cloud adventures from the skies Too near the altars of the hills—it dies! Awhile she wavered, and was blown about, Hither and thither, by the winds of doubt. But in the midst of Heaven at length all still She stood, and said within herself, I will! Yea, in the glad strength of devotion, I Will help you, though, in helping you, I die.

Filled with this thought’s divinity, the Cloud Grew world-like vast, as earthward more she bowed! 121 Oh, never erewhile had she dreamt her state So great might be—leneficently great! O’er the parched fields, in her angelic love, She spread her wings out like a brooding dove: Till, as her purpose deepened, drawing near, So god-like terrible did her face appear, That men and beasts all trembled at the view, And the woods bowed,—though well all creatures knew That near in her, to every kind the same, A great predestined Benefactress came.

Yea, I will help you! said the Cloud again: Receive me—lo, I die for you—in rain! Suddenly flashed then through her full-grown form The glory of her will—the agony and the storm Of that contention with desire of life Which even in Christ’s devoted heart was rife Flashed, seething out of rents amid her heaps Of lowering gloom, and thence with arrowy leaps Hissed jagging downward, till a sheety glare Illumined all the illimitable air! The thunder followed, a tremendous sound Loud doubling and reverberating round, Till one wide rumbling intonation rolled 122 Through all the shuddering mountains manifold! Strong was her will, but stronger yet the power Of love that now dissolved her in a shower, Dropping in blessings, to impregn the earth With health and plenty at one booming birth. She bled in rain, bled unto death; but died Only to higher being—Good beatified!

Far as the rain extended o’er the Land A splendid bow the freshened prospect spann’d, As the last greeting of the spirit pure Of Love self-sacrificed, and thence made sure, Because in record on the Eternal writ In faith and hope, and therefore one with it: One in duration, and in glory one, With all that God-ward out of Time hath gone. —The rainbow vanished, but the blessing craved Long rested on the land the Cloud had saved.
