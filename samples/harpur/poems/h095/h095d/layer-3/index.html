Dian.

A Huntress born to Nature Through a dream of olden lore, Lithely on bounding, ere long lightens past me, all Full, like the morning, of hurry and vigor, When I roam the mountains o’er— A Seer in my soul, like a Poet of yore!

Look! her bow!—I see it plain! And shafted quiver balanced nice! See her robe, blown back amain, Though looped in front with a sure device, Sweeping behind her, like the train Of a Bird of Paradise!

Or when the forest shadows Are dew-silvered by the morn, Lonely forth roaming, not long do I listen, till Suddenly sounding up, merry and mellow, Hark! I hear her mystic horn:— New bo to my soul, as in Greece it was born

Then her buskined limbs how white They glance, as past she boundeth there! See her bow—a curve of light Across her arm upraised and bare! And see how backward streameth bright The torrent of her hair!

Or when the hills are sleeping In a silver silence deep, Musefully strolling, not long am I lonely, for Mystical harmony, billow on billow, Wells from every echoey steep: And thence o’er my soul with a confluent sweep:

Then an arc of rainbow light Sudden spans some viny glade, And there, behold her, boon and bright, Disclouding from the deeper shade, All girt with dancing Nymphs, in white And festal robe arrayed.
