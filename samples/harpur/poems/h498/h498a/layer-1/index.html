Shepherd Tear-away’s battle with the Devil, as related by himself to his much-believing Flock.

I.

The Devil came to my bedside last night, Believe me, my Friends! And said he—So you’re there, my diamond bright! Foh! but you’re now in a deuse of a fright, Though, o’ Sundays, I’m told, from your pulpit’s height You boast you could “lick me like nothing” in fight, To compass your ends— With your holy, roly, poly, O.

II.

Avaunt, I exclaimed, O thou Wicked One! Believe me, my Friends! When out came his tongue, a full yard by the run, And he asked—How I “liked my ‘taters done”? And then added—At whom did you “poke your fun” Last year? and then he went off like a gun At one of his ends— With his coaly, roly, poly, O.

III.

My wrath, at this, like a furnance ‘gan rise! Believe me, my Friends! So I whipt from the sheets to his sudden surprise, And standing before him, Mendoza-wise, With the right hand of zeal, right between the two eyes I fetcht him a lick—Oh! I’ve napt it, he cries, On the best of my ends— With your holy, roly, poly, O.

IV.

But Satan is not an unready Foe, Believe me, my Friends[!] And soon in the side, where my pocket is, lo, With his fist full of gold, he returned me the blow, And seeing I could n’t but double up, so, He dabb’d his hoof on my naked toe, To come by his ends— With his coaly, roly, poly, O.

V.

At this, alas! I was over swayed! Believe me, my Friends! And as to the floor I now fell, half dismayed, He threw on me what seemed a most beautiful maid— Like our sister there, all in gingham arrayed— But feeling the thorn of the flesh—I prayed! To baffle his ends— With my holy, roly, poly, O.

VI.

I prayed—and upsrung like a grig to the fray, Believe me, my Friends! And at once with the mawlies of Faith made such play, That the Thief stood aghast—see what ’tis to pray! And then, from a hole in his horn, this way, Taking snuff—I’ll be damned if I longer stay, Says he, for my ends— With my coaly, roly, poly, O.

VII.

725

So, blowing his nose in a soot-colored clout, Believe me, my Friends! He thought to bow himself quietly out; With his tail ‘tween his legs, like a dog in a rout; But as at the doorway he whisk’d about, I there caught him fast by the horns and the snout, And so far gained my ends— With my holy, roly, poly, O.

VIII.

And now, to complete the victory, Believe me, my Friends! I took of my last printed Sermons three, Which I roll’d into the jolly great pills, d’ye see; And with these then so physicked the Enemy, That, think you, he long held his own?—not he! But fell sick at both ends— With his coaly, roly, poly, O.

IX.

But, foh! I now smelt such a damnable smell, Believe me, my Friends! I could hold him no longer—So, kicking him well, I told the foul villain to—”Go to Hell”! Whereupon through the door like a soot-bag he fell, And then up and away, with a horrible yell! And, thus the tale ends— With a holy, roly, poly, O.
