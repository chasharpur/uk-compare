III.

A Roguish Epigram.

The most accomplished rogue is one Who best can mask the roguery done; Not he who plans with greatest skill The means whereby to compass ill.

Note—There is nothing like putting a bold face on a ticklish matter; a fact that is well exemplified in the following story:—During the early colonial times, in consequence of the non-arrival in due course of certain provision ships, there was well nigh a famine in the land. So pressing was the want on all hands, that it had begun to manipulate the ribs of even the otherwise favoured soldiery. Well, one morning, a settler who dwelt in the vicinity of the Windsor Stockade, found himself minus of the very identical half-grown pig that he had resolved the over night to sacrifice forthwith to the importunate cravings of his own inward man: and for some good reason, no doubt, suspecting the soldiers of the theft, he popt in upon them just as an old veteran of the name of Goodall had slung a huge pot on the fire, and was quietly seating himself beside it, to superintend, as it seemed, some culinary operation of unusual magni- tude. Now the settler remarked all this, and, approaching Goodall, he opened the business of his visit, by stating first, that he had lost a pig, and secondly, that he believed the thieves thereof were not quite a hundred miles off at that precise moment. “Well, Mr. What’s your name.” said Goodall, looking more bluntly innocent. perhaps, than the honestest man in the world could have looked, under an imputation of the kind: “if we stole your pig, I suppose we stole it to eat it?” ”I don’t suppose you stole it to brush its coat, and ‘list it for a soldier,” retorted the settler, with an attempt at wit, that really did his humour credit on such an occasion. “Why no,” laughed Goodall assentingly,” ”in course we stole it to eat it, if we did steal it at all: and look you, friend, there’s the pot—satisfy yourself with your own eyes.” “No,” said the settler, with a dubious glance in the direction of the fire, “I don’t exactly say you stole it: though some people’s eyes are wider awake than some other people may think they be. All I say is, the pig’s gone!” and so saying, he left the stockade, with his suspicion transferred to the abode of a hungry neighbour

And now for the sequel. Aye, let us have it, says the impatient reader. It is soon told. Had the settler looked in the soldier’s pot, he would have found a part of his pig.
