A ROGUISH EPIGRAM, EXEMPLIFIED BY A ROGUISH STORY.

The most accomplished Rogue is one Who best can mask the roguery done; Not he who plans with greatest skill The means whereby to compass ill.

So runs our epigram, and now for our story in exemplification. During the early colonial times, in consequence of the non-arrival in due course of certain provision ships, there was well-nigh a famine in the land. So pressing was the want on all hands, that it had began to manipulate the ribs of even the otherwise favoured soldiery. Well, one morning, a settler who dwelt in the vicinity of the Wind- sor Stockade, found himself minus of the very identical half-grown pig that he had resolved the over night to sacrifice forthwith to the importunate cravings of his own inward man: and for some good reason, no doubt, suspecting the soldiers of the theft, he popped in upon them just as an old veteran of the name of Goodall had slung a huge pot on the fire, and was quietly seating himself beside it, to super- intend, as it seemed, some culinary operation of unusual magnitude. Now the settler re- marked all this, and, approaching Goodall, he opened the business of his visit, by stating first, that he had lost a pig, and secondly, that he believed the thieves thereof were not quite a hundred miles off at that precise moment. “Well, Mr. What’s your name,” said Goodall, looking more bluntly innocent, perhaps, than the honest man in the district could have looked, under an imputation of the kind; “if we stole your pig, I suppose we stole it to eat it?” “I don’t suppose you stole it to bush its coat, and ‘list it for a soldier,” retorted the settler, with an attempt at wit, that really did his humour credit on such an occasion. “Why, no,” assentingly, “in course we stole it to eat it, if we did steal it all; and look you, friend, there’s the pot—satisfy yourself with your own eyes.” “No,” said the settler, with a dubious glance in the direction of the fire, “I don’t exactly say you stole it; though some people’s eyes are wider awake than some other people may think they be. All I say is, the pig’s gone!” and so saying, he left the stockade, with his suspicion transferred to the abode of a hungry neighbour.

And now for the sequel. Aye, let us have it, says the impatient reader. It is soon told. Had the settler looked in the soldier’s pot, he would have found a part of his pig.

C.H.
