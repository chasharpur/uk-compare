VII.

Grey.

The loud, apt epithet, applying sure; The dim-drawn image, artfully obscure; The perfect stanza, framed of words as choice And round as pearls, yet liquid to the voice; A pith of phrase, and musical array Of numbers;—these are the prime charms of Grey.

The naked majesty and open wonder Of true sublimity heaped in lines of thunder; That artless grace wherewith the olden time 314 Dandled the happy infancy of Rhyme; That negligent melody which shames the trick Of wire-drawn verse, and verse-drawn rhetoric: These in our rich old Bards abound; but these To Grey were literary heresies.

Note.

Before making my last copy of the lines, I re-read Grey, with a view of finally testing their justice, and saw no reason to alter a word they contain. His finish is no doubt exquisite, even to fastidiousness; and he exhibits, moreover, a quick evoking power and art of personification, that sometimes reaches after the sublime. All this must be acknowledged (and it is much)—but beyond this, I cannot see the vaunted wonderfulness of his productions. Nay, though thus alive and populous with the figurative spells and graces of poetry, they nevertheless seem to me to be deficient of its diviner breath—of that subtle something (akin to the ideal suggestiveness of the highest music) which we cannot precisely describe, nor always wholly apprehend, but which, if faithful to our more earnest poetical instincts, we can always miss. Still the poems of Grey will ever be held in high scholarly esteem, and deservedly so. Their precision 315 and elegance as compositions, together with the fire and energy of their conduct, will for ever secure to them a standard rank in English Literature.

I speak in the text of the style of the elder English poets, as compared with that of our modern songsters of the order of Grey, in such a manner as to imply a preference of the mode and tone of the former. Oh, those glorious old penmen of the Muses! Which amongst the so-called classical minors of the “after days” has written anything of a like kind and compass, which is at all comparable with the following Epitaph—not by Ben Jonson, as is generally supposed— but by Browne, the author of Britannia’s Pastorals? In every thought, in every line of it, there is the dreamy earnestness,—the ideal abandonment of the right poetic faith, with all thesimple, unforecasted, yet sufficing music of real poetic passion.

Underneath this sable hearse Lies the subject of all verse— Sydney’s sister, Pembroke’s mother: Death, ere thou hast slain another Fair and good and learned as she, Time shall throw a dart at thee.

Marble piles let no man raise To her fame for after days: 316 Some good woman, born as she, Reading this, like Niobe Shall turn to marble, and become Both her mourner and her tomb.

No doubt these stanzas may be, and have been, cavilled at, as concluding each of them with a conceit. But were conceits always, or even generally, so exquisitely pathetic in spirit and sculpturesq in form, as are the things here that may be so called,—then were they, indeed, amongst the loveliest graces of poetry.
