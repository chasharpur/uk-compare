Postscript.

As the foregoing poem is on[e] of considerable length, and written mostly in blank verse, I will even annex to it, by way of postscript, a few remarks on English unrhymed heroic metre. Though partly written for and applied to the present occasion, the matter of these remarks has been long gathered and digested in my mind; and if I throw them together with but little of set method, it is to avoid being tiresome, without perhaps being much the less instructive.

One reason by which I am induced to append this Postscript, I may as well state plainly, without, however, desiring to be offensive to any one. It has often been my 122 fortune to hear my own and others’ blank verse most vilely entreated even by supposed good readers—even by such readers as a very little study of the subject would have adequately accomplished. Now on occasions like these, an author’s feelings are in nowise enviable; in short they are much akin to those by which we must suppose Handel to have been moved, when, on hearing one of his own compositions agonising as it were under the ursine manipulations of some common scraper of cat-gut, he ejaculated interrogatively “Ma Got! is dat ma moosic?

Moreover, the relief of a little criticism at the end of such a poem may not be considered even by those most affected by it as an undesirable and obstrusive matter, but, on the contrary, 123 as somewhat opportune and seasonable.

Though in my ordinary bill of poetical fare—in common, perhaps, with most readers,—I prefer rhyme to blank verse, I yet believe the latter to afford far greater advantages to a just metrical harmony. It evolves, no doubt, when freely written, the “golden cadences” of a tuneful style of line more streamingly, because more naturally; and is open also to a more strenuous organ-like majesty of modulation; to all of which the recurrent checks in the flow of a passage that are occasioned and demanded by like endings, must be more or less inimical. Those swelling and falling prolongations of measure that may not unaptly be figured as musical waves in orderly commo- -tion, inter-heaving and undulating along 124 the bright sea beaches of the Ideal, are harmonic diversities native only to prime blank verse. Still it labors under peculiar disadvantages. A poem in blank verse, when written in the best manner, requires to be read fully as well as it is modulated, to secure to its numbers anything like their due effect,—nay, to prevent them from falling into confusion, and becoming “like sweet bells jangled out of tune.” And what is more rare than a good reader of continuously va- -ried blank verse? or indeed of any kind of verse whereof the melody is achieved through other than the most obvious and ordinary cadinces?

Still the eye of a musical-minded reader, so far as he himself is concerned, will sufficiently correct any incapacity of voice or manner—sufficiently at least 125 to beget in him a growing appreciation of the marshalling cadences, however varied, of well-constructed numbers, when unshackled with rhyme; and eventually, in most cases, a decided relish for them. But there is a precisely balanced kind of blank verse, like Young’s, for example, or Addison’s, that is well nigh intolerable to a judicious ear. It wants all the compensations of rhyme, while lacking, at the same time, all that variously interflowing melody of cadence which should be primarily distinctive of unrhymed numbers, and which alone constitutes their proper harmony. There is also another species of blank verse, like much of Byron’s, that is obnoxious in several respects to the same objection: though in its linear arrangement it may be adroit and flowing enough, and when at the best, may somewhat 126 atone by a certain eloquent, or rather glib profuseness, for the gradational defects of its rhythm. And this last is the kind most likely to be preferred by a literary novice with an ordinary musical instinct.

The blank verse of Milton can- -not be surpassed for variety of modulation. But it is too artistically difficult in the execution, to use a phrase hitherto only used in reference to the sister art of Music, to be ever very popular: being indeed, so exceedingly numerous, that it requires to be studied, like a complex movement, ere all its harmony can be arrived at and adequately fulfilled. Who, for instance, but a well practised reader could trippingly evolve, yet so as to distribute them distinctly, the measures of a passage so variously paused as the following, 127 which I take almost at random from the Paradise Lost?

The eagle and the stork On cliffs and cedar tops their eyries build.— Part loosely wing the region—part more wise In common—ranged in figure—wedge their way— Intelligent of seasons—and set forth Their aery caravans—hìgh over seas Flying—and over lands—with mutual wing Easing their flight;—so steers the prudent crane Her annual voyage—borne on winds;—the air Floàts as they pass—fànned with unnumbered plumes.

Hence it is that so few persons can read the blank verse of Milton with absolute effect. Its only fault (if it be a fault) is that the interflowing measures of each rhythmically dependent passage or series of lines, do not often enough terminate 128 in one unbroken or continuously profluent verse.
