The Hours in Final Chorus.

Night Hours. Where’s the young Bard Who sang of loneliness yesternight In such strains as, when heard, Drew a cloud o’er the rising moon’s light? Day Hours. Hark, Echo cries Where? He hath passed from the world as that cloud from the sky; He hath fled to a rest where his once sleepless eye Glares no more with despair. Night Hours. We have sojourned in every clime Having name in the annals of Time, From the gates of the West to the portals of Morn, And in all to be poor was a crime! From sorrow and scorn For the poverty-born, Say, then, where this refuge may be? Day Hours. He’s dead!—he is dead! Gone to his death bed, Though not in the shade of the old willow tree He longed to lie under when dying forlorn, And where no one but Misery crieth—Ah me! For the loveliest child that e’re died on her knee! Where none, save herself, mourns for Misery’s child. 315 Night Hours. Ah! that he should, Though heart-wild with loneliness yesternight! And for why? though he could Not rejoice in the rising moon’s light. Day Hours. He asked but to toil For the proud and the wealthy, and met with rebuke! He strove to be humble—but how might he brook Their contumely the while? Night Hours. But alas! until Error is hurled From his throne in the heart of the world; And till Love over Wrong hath the victory sure, And the standard of Right is unfurled, Such scorn aye endure Must the lowly and poor, No matter how worthy they be! Day Hours. Even so. He is dead! Gone to his death bed, Where sleeps he as well as if under that Tree His dying thought saw by the runnel so wild And though no one but Misery crieth—Ah me! For the loveliest child that e’er died on her knee! Though none, save herself, mourns for Misery’s child. 316

Postscript.

The foregoing Poem was written in my youth, and was suggested by the character and fate of the “marvellous boy”, Chatterton. It should be observed, however, that I did not derive either my inspirations or materials from any regular Life of Chatterton; for prior to the composition of the Poem, and for some years afterwards, I had never been able to meet with any work of the kind. On the contrary, what may be comprehensively termed the characteristics of these Sorrows, were drawn from incidental notices in old newspapers and magazines, of Chatterton’s genius, of his literary habits and worldly misfortunes, and in no small degree also, from certain popular traditions of his singular being which were then floating about the dim reefs of the last passed century, vague and unauthentic, like the drift wood of a wreck.

In the design of the Poem, the monologues are a psychological, step by step, developement of the lonely conceptions and peculiar sufferings that are but too surely leading such a wild and unfriended, though noble nature, to a disastrous, self-inflicted end; while the Chorusses annexed to them, are spiritual cognitions of these peculiar moods and sufferings—but they are also raised and illumined throughout by prophecies of a general good in predestined reservation, and thence, by inference, of a better era for the lowly born children of Genius: and moreover, they are pointed and practicalised, as it were, by frequent suggestions of such intellectual efforts and means as might be conducive to the speedier advent of such a desirable era.

Such being the origin and drift of the Poem, of course there are opinions broached and feelings indulged in and developed in the conduct of its composition, which are not to be imputed to myself personally. On thes head, I hold myself strictly and directly chargeable with responsible for the soundness and sanity of those ethical views and positions only, which are embodied in the several Chorusses. Nor would I have it in anywise inferred from a certain passage in the Poem, that I am 317 inclined to justify Chatterton’s attempts at literary imposition. In my own light, I am at once disposed to scorn all attempts of the kind. Yet, in his case, I would be understood to excuse them. Chatterton’s circumstances were painfully peculiar, especially in their literary aspects. He had “set his life upon a cast”, and one, perhaps the chief, object of his palming certain of his poems on an old suppositious author, was doubtless to seduce the literati of the day into a ready notice of productions which, if honestly fathered, he—even he, young as he was—knew by experience, were likely to be received, if noticed at all, with the coldest indifference. And as he had much reason for believing that these poems were well worth (as such things go) the price to be paid for them, in the event of their publication, his poverty might have argued, that beyond this, the interest of the buyer of the articles, in relation to that of their manufacturer, should not be much considered or very nicely respected.

Now this reasoning is not altogether just. But would it be going too far to assert, that under equally desperate circumstances, such reasoning would be sufficient to confirm in a similar design, many who at the present moment—being comfort- -ably provisioned both immediately and prospectively—imagine themselves fortified against every possible assault of the Tempter by an uncleaveable panoply of (self) righteousness? Nay, let all those who are the readiest to condemn his memory, ask of themselves, whether they have not, at some period of their lives, given in to the lures of a far worse design,—more selfish, more earthy, and for ends every way more base and ignoble? And if they answer this question truly, though only to themselves, it will either improve their charity, or give a sterner emphasis to their virtue.

With respect to such readers as would have been allured to the purchase of Chatterton’s books, solely by the supposition of their being “curiosities of literature,” it may be added, that as they were of that class of humans of which Shakespear has somewhere said “When they will not give a doit to relieve a lame beggar, they will lay out ten to see a dead Indian”,—the device that 318 aimed at converting to the benefit of a present writer the expense they were only disposed to throw after a past one, if not praiseworthy, was at least pardonable. Or if we look at the matter simply in the light of an intended hoax upon black-letter Antiquarianism in general, it was an indulgence that any literary humourist, which whether young or old, had wilful right to, however dangerous it might prove to himself, or professionally offensive to the antiquarianism aforesaid.

His suicide was infinitely worse. But he was stung to the quick by neglect, and rendered reckless and defiant by the apparant blindness of fortune. His excentricities, too, had tarnished what little reputation had been awarded to his talents, while his poverty exposed him to constant con- -tamination and contempt. And when we consider all this, surely even his suicide—that most faithless of crimes! may now be forgiven to the unhappy memory of one so sensitively and proudly constituted, so friendless, and so young.

I will add a few words having a self-reference. It is for the reader himself to determine how far, and in what way, he may be affected by this Poem; but to me, its author, I can most truly affirm, it has been an exceeding benefit. It proved the occasion—undesigned indeed—of the purging away of much “perilous stuff” that weighed upon my heart and life up to, and during, the period of its composition. The working out of its details enforced me to gather into a certain measurable, and thence more healthy consistence, all the vaguer and wilder feelings and fancies, and darker gropings, of a young and sensitive, and, it may be, over-speculative nature; affording them, at the same time, a most opportune and artistic outlet. And moreover, its composition became to me the means (especially the Chorusses) of balancing the account of suffering—whether real, as affecting myself, or imaginary, as attributed to Chatterton—with instinctive prophecies of that sure, though distant, reversion, which is for ever taking a wider hold upon the destinies of the human race, through the self devotion and recorded thought experience of its more gifted individuals.
