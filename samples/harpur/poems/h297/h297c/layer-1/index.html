Obituary Lines.

I stand in thought beside my Father’s Grave: The grave of one who, in his old age, died To late, perhaps, since he endured so much Of corporal anguish, sweating bloody sweat; But not an hour too soon—for his bruised heart 429 Had long resigned his times, and all his hopes, Even the lowliest, and the latest had, Like faded leaves deciduous By blast on blast of sorrow and mischance, Had one and all been, like deciduous leaves, Blown from their vernal hold on what we here Call nature: he but followed in their wake. Nor was there in the lives of those he loved, Even had he been susceptible of cheer, Enough of fortune to warm into ease, A little longer, ere he passed away, The remnant of his chilled humanity. Wet are mine eyes, and my heart akes, to think How much of evil ridged his course of time And earthly pilgrimage;—enough to have slain In most hearts all reliance on the sure Though still reserved supremacy of Truth! For few are they, who, on the stormy ball, Can live a long life full of loss & pain, And yet through Doubt’s dull cloud, uplooking, see In that wide dome which roofs the apparant whole Without or seam or flaw, a visible type Of Heaven’s intact infinitude of love! Yet died he a believer in the truth And fatherhood of the Holy One—a God Help-mighty, nor unmindful of mankind! Yea, in the heavenward reaching light of faith His soul went forth, as in a sunbeam’s track Some close-caged bird from a long bondage freed, Goes winging up—up through the open sky! Rejoicing in the widening glow that paths The final victory of its native wings! And whether all was triumph as went Careering up and up, or whether cloud 430 Of penal terror gathered in the way, Not less must Death the great inheritor be Transcending all Time’s highest lore, all Life’s boasted values;—must be, at the worst— Even at the worst! a grimly grateful thing, If only from deliverance from doubt, The clinging curse of mortals!—In the present, What know we but the present? All that lies Beyond it, lies or in the iron-poor world Of human needs;—or worse, at the wild will Of a demoniac Fortune! But the Dead Have this immunity at least—a known, And measured portion—a fixed lot within The gates of the Eternal! For the past Is wholly God’s, and, therefore, like himself, Knows no reverse, no change,—but lies for ever Stretched in the sabath of its vast repose.
