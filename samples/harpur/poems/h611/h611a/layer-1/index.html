II.

To Henry Parkes on reading his Sonnet beginning “Who would not be a Poet?”

“Who would not be a Poet?”—thus I read In thy proud Sonnet, my harmonious Friend; And then the streamy lines still led My utterance on—but at the end The question gave me pause, and I let pause The Poet; for that aye, alas! A Man of Sorrows he is doomed to be. Yet thus far my assent was given: There is not under the wide heaven Aught happier in itself than the witch Poesie.

What is not spell-touched by her winged Powers? See those happy creatures, Flowers, 12 Even when they sweetest be Blest into beauty by the genial Hours— Then as they breathe into the day Their soul of sweetness, should but we Name them in verse, are not even they Yet sweeter for the breath of raptured Poesie?

Yea, Love itself is earthly, till betimes, Upon the bosom of her mystery We lay it, like a wayward child, Hushing its nature wild With honey flowing rhymes, Distilled from all things sweet that be, And all that most sublimes In Nature, hand in hand with passionate Poesie

But “who’d not be a Poet?”—there I pause Forebodingly; because, To see “all beauty with his gifted sight,” To love like him with all the soul, To be when life is bright, The very Creature of Delight— Delight beyond control! Is ever still to be Even in the same degree, To sensitive of loss and images of dole Of loss and slight and all the weeping shapes of dole.

There was an earnest-thoughted Boy, that still Had to himself proposed thy question, till His swelling heart and throbbing head Flowed with the tunefulness they coveted. He loved to stray alone, this studious Creature, Yet was not solitary, but apart; For to his eye and ear and heart, Each prospect, tone, and trick of Nature, Was still a grand, or sweet, or lovely feature; And, temperingly with these, 13 Her veriest simplicities, Even with his body’s growth, nourished his spirit’s stature.

And thus accomplished only, to the fight Of Life’s dishonest battle did he come, To meet instead of Valor mean Despite; To see unblushing Fortune doom To some repute unholy, Or to some miserable estate, All such as would not trample on the lowly And glorify the falsely great; Or for a hireling’s sordid pay Defend the oppressor and belie the oppressed: And so at last he turned from all away, Even those he loved the best, To brood with a proud bitterness apart O’er dead yet lovely Hopes—a quick yet broken Heart.

x

When these Rhymes were written, my outward circumstances were much better than at the time of my composing those preceding them (the Poet.), yet inwardly I was even more dissatisfied than then; being obliged to associate daily with persons whose natures could value nothing in mine that I valued myself; whose souls indeed had no faith for anything in the Universe that was not, in their own vile phrase, “money’s worth.” Thus circumstances, when I reflected what “miserable men” almost all our Poets had ever been through the unfitness of similar compulsory associations, I had nearly armed myself in the resolutions of destroying as far as I could, all poetical record of my being: and of living mentally apart from my kins for the remainder of my life, dying eventually, like the wolf, in silence. But as time slid on, I gradually thought and felt myself out of this bitterness of love—for such it was; love of the Beautiful, crushed into bitterness by the burthen of its own incommunicable wealth. This analysis of feeling may be sneered at by the prosaic, but a “fit audiance though few” will understand it better, by virtue of a lovelier experience. C.H.
