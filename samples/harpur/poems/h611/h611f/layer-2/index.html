To

“Who would not be a Poet?”—thus I read In thy proud Sonnet, my poetic Friend; And then the streamy measures led My utterance on—But, at the end, The question made me ponder, And out of mind let wander The Poet, seeing that, for ever, he A Man of Sorrows is foredoomed to be: Yet unto this much my assent was given— There is not, cannot be,—under all heaven, Aught happier in itself than the witch Poetry!

What is not spell-touched by her dream-wing’d Powees? See those happy creatures, the Spring flowers, Even where they freshest grow— Even where they richest glow— Blest into beauty by the genial Hours! There as they blisfully breathe away There odorous spirits, do but we While strolling near them leisurely On some fair holyday, Their worth rehearse In eloquent verse, O are not they then—even they, Yet fresher for the might, And richer for the light, And sweeter—howe’er sweet they be— Sweeter for the angel--breath of raptured Poetry?

Even Love itself is earthy—till betimes Upon her bosom, in its mystery, We lay it like a wayward child, 36 Hushing its nature wild With lullabies drawn from its own history Through all the heart’s most golden climes: Fond lullabies, set forth in free And fancy-honeyed rhymes. Rich fit rhymes—the meet Wing-plumes for love-born Melody When Wooed abroad by airs and odors sweet, And with all morning things that freshest be Glossed and dewed over, as with jewell’ry; And eke (its orient glory to complete) With every hue the setting sun sublimes Touched, like a cloud,—and resonant inwardly With every flowing tone that sweetliest primes The peans of Nature, who herself we see Smile always happiest when her music chimes With the music of passionate Poetry.

But—”who’d not be a Poet?” Here I pause Forebodingly, my Poet-friend,—because “To see all beauty with his gifted sight,” To love, like him, with all the soul, To be when life is morning-bright The very Creature of Delight— Delight beyond control; Is still to be in a like degree, Too sensible of misery, And loss, and slight, and all the weeping Shapes of Dole.

There was an earnest-thoughted Boy, Who with a secret thrill Of lonely but hopeful joy, Had to himself proposed thy question—till His swelling heart and throbbing head 37 Flowed with the tunefulness he coveted. He loved to muse alone, this studious Creature, Yet was not solitary, but apart, For to his eye, and ear, and mind, and heart, Each aspect, every tone, and trick of Nature Was still a grand, or sweet, or beautiful feature: Yea, all!—her veriest simplicities— Bird, insect, beast,—brooks, flowers, and trees— All in their final influences, Even with his body’s growth nourished his Spirit’s stature.

And thus accomplished only, to the fight Of Life’s dishonest battle did he come; To front instead of Valor, mean Despite, Envy aye in Emulation’s room; Blotting Heaven’s sacred light! To see unblushing Fortune’s minions doom To obloquy, through some repute unholy Or to some vile and miserable estate, All such as would not trample on the lowly And basely glorify the falsely great; Or for a mental hireling’s devil-pay Defend the oppressor and belie the oppressed: And so from all he turned at length away Even those (the noble few) he loved the best, To sorrow in proud silence and apart Over dead Hope—and find a vast unrest Even in the loneliest ways, through weariness of heart.
