III.

RHYMES TO HENRY PARKES, On reading his Sonnet beginning—”Who would not be a Poet?”

“Who would not be a Poet?” Thus I read In thy proud Sonnet, my harmonious Friend; And then the streamy lines still led My utterance on —— But at the end, The question made me ponder and let pass The Poet, seeing that for aye, alas! A Man of Sorrows he is doomed to be: Yet unto this much my assent was given— There is not under the dome of heaven Aught happier in itself than the witch Poetry.

What is not spell-touched by her dream-wing’d Powers? See those happy creatures, the Spring-flowers, Even where they freshest grow— Even where they richest glow— Blest into beauty by the genial Hours! There, as they blissfully breathe away Their odorous spirits, do but we Their wealth rehearse In happy Verse, O then are not even they—even they! Yet fresher for the might, And richer for the light, And sweeter—howe’er sweet they be— Sweeter for the breath of raptured Poetry?

Even Love itself is earthy—till betimes, Upon the bosom of her mystery We lay it, like a wayward child, Hushing its nature wild With fancy-honied but truth-tempered Rhymes, Dewed with all things fresh that be, Hued with all that most sublimes In Nature, who herself we see Smile happiest, when her music chimes With the music of passionate Poetry.

But, “who’d not be a Poet?” Here I pause Forebodingly, because “To see all beauty with his gifted sight,” To love like him with all the soul, To be when Life is morning-bright The very creature of delight— Delight beyond control; Is still to be In a like degree, Too sensible of misery, And loss, and slight, and all the weeping shapes of dole.

There was an earnest-thoughted Boy, who still Had to himself proposed thy question, till His swelling heart and throbbing head Flowed with the tunefulness he covetëd. He loved to stray alone, this studious creature, Yet was not solitary, but apart; For to his eye and ear and heart Each aspect, tone, and trick of Nature Was still a grand or sweet or lovely feature: And temperingly with these Her veriest simplicities, Even with his body’s growth, nourished his spirit’s stature.

And thus accomplished only to the fight Of Life’s dishonest battle did he come; To meet instead of Valour mean Despite; To see unblushing Fortune doom To some repute unholy, Or to some vile and miserable estate All such as would not trample on the lowly, And glorify the falsely great: Or for a hireling’s sordid pay Defend the oppressor and belie the oppressed: And so at length he turned from all away, Even those he loved the best, To grieve in silence and apart O’er dead yet lovely hopes, with a quick but weary heart.
