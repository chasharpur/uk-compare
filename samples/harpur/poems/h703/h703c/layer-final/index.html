No. 3.

THE WORLD’S VICTIMS.

“They tell me He in riot now, For whom I would have died, Hath sunk all memory of the vow I made to be his Bride: But then the happy past will swim Before mine eyes again, And one wild thought of love and him Seems rooted in my brain.

“I would not trust him more, for nought Could mend his broken vow; Nay, were the false one hither brought I would not name him now! And yet from one long tearful gaze How might I then refrain, With this wild thought of happy days So rooted in my brain?

“He surely sometimes thinks of me— But can he love me yet? Alas! this mood must sinful be— My part is to forget! I feel the bright deceit is o’er— That I have loved in vain; And yet this wild regret the more Keeps rooted in my brain!”

Thus, day by day, a maiden pale, And oft the long-long night, Did, in her soul’s despair, bewail Her lover’s broken plight; But, ah, her cold and cruel friends, Regardless of the truth, Had thus, for base and selfish ends, Belied the absent youth!

And much they urged that maiden wan, When now a year was past, To wed another—richer man, And they prevailed at last! But then, to show how vile their part, Her lover came again! Alas! it broke his manly heart— It turned her weary brain!

And then, in hopeless grief, away He wandered o’er the sea; And soon at rest that pale girl lay Beneath the churchyard tree. All heart-truth shall the world thus doom To ruin evermore? And Love’s most p[r]ecious flowers but bloom To perish as before?

Note.—”The moral of this poem is wrong,” saith the mere economist; “egregiously wrong! All questions are money ques- tions; and the friends of young ladies act wisely, when they would induce them by all means to wed the richer of any two men: the result of a broken heart being, indeed, just one of those purely romantic ideas that should weigh nothing against the real presence of a whole purse.”

And having thus delivered himself, he may well look profound, for he is profoundly mistaken. There is more in the matter—aye, a whole world of things more, than is ever dreamt of in his phi- losophy. The mere gain of a bargain is not always its wisdom; nor does hardness of heart imply solidity of head.

“But reason, Sir, reason”—is an excellent thing; nay, more, an essential one. And yet no men have differed so widely from one another, and from all others, in their moral systems, as mere rea- soners have. “When men begin to reason (merely),” says Rous- seau, “they cease to feel“—and herein lies their liability to err. They do not give fair play to their whole nature, which is three- fold; two parts being compounded of sentiment and passion. But is not reason the proper guide and governor of these? It is; but it should guide and govern them through a knowledge and feeling of their principles, and not according to theories drawn exclusively from its own; for if too dogmatically relied on, it becomes the mere egg-bag of heartless conventions. And at this point, I be- lieve, we stand over the greatest anomaly of human life—thatperpetual warfare betweeen the wisdom of the heart and the wis- dom of the head.

But to dip deeper into the subject. The dry logical definer of human proprieties has rarely any moral or mental progressiveness. Neither does the mere laying together of the dry bones of a social argument require much grasp of intellect or harmony of feeling. Hence, a merely reasoning utilitarian seldom makes a lasting im- pression upon the great heart of mankind. He may present uswith an outward frame, a skeleton of truth, anatomically put together; but it asks genius, in addition, to “create a soul underits ribs of death.” Besides, if a mere reasoner commences in error, he is ever afterwards hopelessly astray. Dealing only with the successional links of his subject, the further he goes the further he errs. A man of a largely feeling as well as thinking intelli- gence, on the contrary, seldom affects the beaten and outside logic of things. He dashes rather into their principles. He yearns more after communion with the order of their inner spirit than with their material congruities. Hence, such a being is never entirely lost to truth. He will often leap into the full blaze of its light over the very fences of error. The real and living consonance of human passions and interests, rather than their apparent and subjective agreement, being his object, becomes progressively more and more his possession.

To arrive then at a just and comprehensive idea of the harmo- nious in society, we must give intellectual sea-room to the whole man within us. In such a course resides the reason of the truthful influence of genius; and out of such a course is evoked the pro- phecy of the “good time coming.”

Still I must not be mistaken. I am no advocate for imprudent love-matches as such. All I would urge on that head is, that when once the affections are engaged or pledged, then their plea should be held paramount to all selfish considerations; then a single worldly reservation is an inlet to dishonour and in devotion— from which good can never come.

Marry for love, and labour diligently together afterwards for the good things of the world, is both a wise and a hearty motto for the young of both sexes. For love in the married state will ever be found the best promoter even of those very prudential and industrious dispositions which most conduce to its ultimate worldly prosperity; while without it, there is always a conjugal dissatis- faction of passion, disposing its victim more or less, according to temperament, to practical excesses in some direction. At all events, the want of it, I say, as in the case of the long unmarried, must naturally originate some vague craving for social compensation, the more insidiously unfavourable to constancy of conduct from its very vagueness.

The heart, then, is the most central thing of human life as well as of the human body; and it will never, therefore, rest contentedly in the meagreness of purely conventional results. Add to this, that the merest propensity has its sacred rights; and that to make it a rebel, either openly or in secret, little more is needed in a 8b majority of cases than to gall it by some inappropriate restriction or denial. And then, let us ask ourselves, why, in the solicitude of our worldly wisdom, we do not fathom more attentively these under-currents of humanity, in order to fetch thence a necessary, because an elementary, addition to its present inadequate capital?
