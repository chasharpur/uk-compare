Original Poetry.

EPIGRAMS, NOTES, AND OPINIONS, &c. BY CHARLES HARPUR.

I

Educational Mottoes.

It is not Systems—it is Men who teach: The men who love to practise what they preach. Rules are but lifeless bones. till through the whole, The destined Teacher breathes a living soul.

A Teacher may have learning. method. wit, And yet be missionless—and thence unfit. Is there a child’s heart beating in his breast? A truthful answer’s the unfailing test.

“I beat you for your good,” the Master cries; “Then spare me for your own,” the Boy replies. There’s reason here on both sides, for at school, The mean (as elsewhere) is the golden rule. Undue severity but hardens more, And Birch-’em’s temper may be quite as sore, As Dunce’s buttocks, ere the flogging’s o’er.

Note.—The art of effective teaching is much rarer than the faculty of acquiring knowledge. Hence a man may not only be very learned, but excellently versed also in various methods of education, without being a good practical educator. The apt schoolmaster has a cordial sympathy with his scholars— that is, with children. He has a sort of second consciousness in their regard, and can thereby enter at once. and with ease, into the operations of their nascent minds: perceiving intuitively—at a glance—their peculiar difficulties. and the best means of smoothing them away. In short, his mind’s home, with the whole interest of his heart, is in their little mental world—at least for the time.

Nor does his sympathy with them end in the schoolroom. Even their pastimes afford him a fresh and perennial pleasure. They are the delightful accessories of his vocation: in which, with him. there is no “crude surfeit.” And it is through this, his general sympathy with the young, that he deserves the art of mentally communing with them, as an instructor. In fine, Education—the Art of Teaching—is his born business—his characteristic idiosyncrasy, and providential mission: and it is also his ultimate reward—or how else could he bear so patiently the couuteracting dogmatism of parental ignorance and stupidity? How else could he submit to the insolent interference of clerical prejudice? or survive upon thepitiful remuneration that is now so grudgingly awarded to his all-important office? Above all, it teaching were not its own reward to him, how might he content himself for a moment in the low—the almost despicable—rank which is at present assigned to his priceless labour?

But there is a “good time coming,” even for the school- master, when he will not only be abroad, but comfortably at home. And to discover and establish in the profession, such teachers as may best answer to the above draught of a good one, is the great educational desideratum of the future, and not the multiplication of mere forms and systems of tuition.

Then as to Educational Systems—the less they are confined (beyond their first principles or elementary framework) to mere parade, like details. the better: more scope being thereby afforded to the really clever or destined teacher for improving his pupils, according to their specific and individual capacities. And hence the pertinacity with which our Educational Boards have been wont to stickle for the mere classing and marching routines of their several systems, is only significant of their want of knowledge in the grave and weighty matters over which they preside. But in this country, as in England, and every colonial offshoot of England, society is officered throughout by old women: a fatality-boding fact that is mowing and grimacing at us wherever we look!
