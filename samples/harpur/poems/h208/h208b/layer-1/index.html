The Kangaroo, A Ballad for Little Boys.

(This piece (inculcating a helpful deed of pity) was written expressly for the amusement and instruction of my own little Boy, Washington; and as it pleases him abundantly, I suppose it will be equally agree- -able to other little boys, and to that end publish it.)

A pretty playful Kangaroo, By a river side dwelt he, There drinking of its waters And bounding in his glee.

And there he dwelt for many a day, Till he grew large and fair, For the grass he cropt was growthful Because of the moisture there.

So to himself one day he said, In a proud & pleasant mood, No Beast more beautiful than I E’er bounded through the wood!

And I have gentle wife, and she Hath borne me a gentle child, So that never a happier Creature Was free of the Forest wild.

Just then the bay of a Hunter’s dog Was heard on the mountain side; It smote him through with terror And dashed his Forest pride!

And into sight as suddenly Six hounds came looming then— Came looming and leaping in their might, Cheered on by hunting men.

He fled away in mad dismay, And the Greyhounds followed fast, While from a nook his wife & child Beheld them as they passed.

And sorely then the wife did grieve, And to her child complain: My pretty one, we’ll never see Thy hunted sire again!

For yon destroying Foes that kill The quiet of the Wood, Will hunt him to a cruel death, And lap his glowing blood!

And ever as the Kangaroo Fled for his life that day, He thought how lonely these would be Should he be reft away.

Still he hurried—on and on, And the Greyhounds followed still, Along the stream, and down the vale, And up the shadowy hill:

A long, long way—till he grew faint, And turning in his flight, Came hurrying back upon his track, Till the River wound in sight;

When rushing down the bank, he plunged Neck deep into the broad stream, Whose silver calm, thus troubled, Threw out a broken gleam.

And when the hounds plunged in as well, Still thirsting for his blood, With his fore paws he’d clasp them, And drench them in the flood.

But they were many, and his strength Was wasting fas[t] away: It seemed his doom, full surely, To die that very day!

But just then youthful Nobleheart, (A yeoman’s son was he) Came riding to the River, And saw his jeopardy:

And his heart melted at the sight, To pity ever true, And he at once determined To save the Kangaroo:

Saying, fear is sobbing at his heart And glaring in his eye, And God who made me made him too, And would not have him die.

So into the broad stream he dashed And drove the Hounds away, And carrying the spent creature out He bore him home that day:

Upon his saddle bow astride, Disposed with happy knack— While bootless to their masters The dripping hownds went back.

And the Beast felt he was his friend, And to his feeling true, Said in his heart, I’ll love him, And be his Kangaroo.

And now he goes with him about, And comes at his command, And drinks from out his pannikin, And eats from out his hand.

And thus he is as Creature’s happy As any thing might be, So wildly born as he was born And once so wildly free.

But his forest wife so gentle And the lovely child she bore, Are lonely in the Wildwood Where he shall come no more.

Unless the good lad Nobleheart Should learn where that may be, When straightway he would take it there And set the Creature free.
