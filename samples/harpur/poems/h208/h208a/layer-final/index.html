The Kangaroo. A Ballad for little Boys.

A pretty playful kangaroo By a River side, dwelt he, There drinking of its waters, And bounding in his glee.

And there he dwelt for many a day, Till he grew large and fair, For the grass he cropt was bountiful Because of the moisture there.

So to himself one day he said In a proud and pleasant mood No beast more beautiful than I Ere bounded through the wood

And I have a lovely Wife, and she Hath borne me a lovely child, So that never a happier Creature Was free of the Forest wild.

Just then the bay of a hunter’s dog Was heard on the mountain side, It smote him through with terror And dashd his forest pride.

And into sight and suddenly, Six hounds came looming then, Looming and leaping in their might, Cheered on by hunting men.

He fled away in mad dismay, And the Greyhounds followed fast, While from a nook his wife and Child Beheld them as they past.

And sorely then the Wife did weep And to her Child complain: My pretty one, we’ll never see Thy hunted Sire again.

For those destroying things who kill The quiet of the Wood Will chase him to a cruel death, And lap his glowing blood.

And ever as the kangaroo Fled for his life that day, He thought how lonely these would be Should he be reft away.

Still on he hurried—on and on, And the greyhounds followed still, Along the steam, and down the vale, And up the shadowy hill:

A long long way, till he grew faint, And turning in his flight, Went hurrying back upon his track Till the River came in sight.

When rushing down the bank, he plunged Neck deep in the broad stream, Whose silver calm, thus troubled, Threw up a broken gleam.

And when the hounds plunged in as well, Still thirsting for his blood, With his fore claws he’d clasp them, And drench them in the flood.

But they were many, and his strength Was ebbing fast away, It seemd his doom full surely To die this very day.

But Just then little Nobleheart (A yeoman’s son was he) Came riding to the River And saw his jeopardy.

And his heart melted at the sight, To mercy ever true, And he at once determined To save the kangaroo.

x Saying pain and fear are at his heart And horror in his eye And God who made me made him too And would not have him die

So into the broad stream he dashed, And drove the hounds away, And carrying the spent creature out He bore him home that day.

And the beast felt he was his friend And to his feeling true, Said in his heart, I’ll love him And be his kangaroo.

And now it goes with him about And comes at his command, And drinks from out pannikin And eats from out his hand.

And thus he is as happy As any thing might be, So wildly born as he was born And once so wildly free.

But his forest Wife so beautiful And the lovely Child she bore, Are lonely in the Wildwood Where he shall come no more.

Unless the good Boy, Noble-heart, Should learn where that may be, When straightway he would take him there, And set the Creature free.

Note. This piece (inculcating a helpful deed of pity) and expressly composed for the amusement & instruction, of my own little boy, Washington, and as it pleases him abundantly, I suppose it will be equally agreeable to other little Boys, and to that end publish it.

C.H.
