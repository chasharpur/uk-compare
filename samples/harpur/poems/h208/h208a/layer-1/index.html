The Kangaroo. A Ballad for Washington.

A pretty little kangaroo Dwelt by a River side, There drinking of its waters, And bounding in his pride.

And there he dwelt for many a day, Till he grew large and fine, For the grass he cropt was green & fresh As it waved in the bright sunshine.

So on a time to himself he said In a proud and happy mood No beast more beautiful than I Ere bounded through the wood

For I have a lovely Wife, and she Hath borne me a lovely child, So that never a happier Creature Was free of the Forest wild.

Just then the bay of a hunter’s dog Was heard on the mountain side, To fall on his ear like a sudden knell And dashd his forest pride.

And into sight and suddenly, Six hounds came looming then, Looming and leaping in their might, Cheered on by hunting men.

He fled away in mad dismay, And the Greyhounds followed fast, While from a rock his wife and Child Beheld them as they past.

And sorely then the Wife did weep And to her Child complain: My pretty one, we’ll never see Thy hunted Sire again.

For those destroying things who kill The quiet of the Wood Will chase him till they slaughter And lap his glowing blood.

687a

And still as the poor kangaroo Fled for his life that day, He thought how lonely these would be Should he be reft away.

Still on he hurried—on and on, And the greyhounds followed still, Along the steam, and down the vale, And up the shadowy hill.

And now the kangaroo grew faint, And turning in his flight, He hurried back upon his track Till the River came in sight.

When rushing down the bank, he plunged Neck deep in the broad stream, Whose silver quiet, troubled thus, Threw up a broken gleam.

And when the hounds plunged in as well, Still thirsting for his blood, With his fore claws he’d clasp them, And drench them in the flood.

But they were many, and his strength Was ebbing fast away, If no aid come, it is his doom To die this very day.

Just then a Boy named Washington (A poet’s son was he) Came riding to the River And saw his jeopardy.

And his heart melted at the sight, To mercy ever true, And he at once determined To save the kangaroo.

So into the broad stream he dashed, And drove the hounds away, And carrying the spent creature out He bore him home that day.

687b

And the beast felt he was his friend So generous and true, And said in his heart, I’ll love him And be his kangaroo.

686a

And now follows him about And comes at his command, And drinks from out pannikin And eats from out his hand.

And thus he is as happy As any thing might be, So wildly born as he was born And once so wildly free.

But his kangaroo Wife so beautiful And the lovely Child she bore, Are lonely in the forest Where he shall come no more.

Unless the good Boy, Washington Should learn where that may be, When straightway he would take him there, And set the Creature free.

Note. This piece (inculcating a deed of mercy) and composed for the amusement & instruction, of my own little boy, Washington, and as it pleases him abundantly, I suppose it will be equally agreeable to other little Boys, and to that end publish it.

C.H.
