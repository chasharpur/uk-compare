Forward Ho!

Forward ho! forward ho! soldiers of Liberty! Hope on! fight on! till the whole world is free! Doubt not the day of your triumph must come— Doubt not, the Earth that has grown old in sorrow, Shall grow young again in the light of that morrow Which maketh her breast the Republican’s home.

Forward ho! forward ho! lovers of truth and good! Think on! write on! till the Earth’s herohood Rise up at once under heaven’s high dome; And shout, to behold all the hill tops adorning, Like a banner of purple, the light of that morning Which maketh the world the Republican’s home.

Note.—Methinks I hear some budge philosopher of the old school, denouncing the above, as the “Forward ho!” of a mere nineteenth century enthusiast: his own profound dicta being, that men will never advance much farther than they have already; and that if particular communities do occasionally bound forward beyond a certain limit (which he never defines), it is only to gra- vitate back again into their old or a worse position. Such anti progress notions, however, only show how readily monarchists and aristocrats can believe in what their natures and interests induce them to desire. But I do not envy even the crowned head that can repose on such a belief. Men so disposed can have little benevolent feeling for their kind, and no sympathy with the higher manifestations of moral and mental greatness. Neither can they, if they think at all, have any merciful sense of those traces of solemn agony with which Despotism has entrenched the pale face of the Past. They can have no fraternal apprehension of those accusing ghost- voices which, to the ear of a rightly constituted mind, are for ever surging upward against the spirit of wrong dominion, from the untimely graves of forgotten generations.

But they are mistaken! God is just, though long- suffering, and his mercy is over his works. The heaven- beating agonies of millions of widows and orphans —made such by the wars and wrongs of oppression:— the gathered cries of numberless multitudes of men who perished in the insanity of their king-worship: gathered from the flight of ages, and accumulated as the sound of many waters upon the present, must be heard at the throne of the Almighty; and thence I believe, is derived that wise daring by which the Franklins and Channings and Lamartines of the race are impelled to evoke and elucidate the principle of Perfect Liberty. For though apparently of the growth of the Present these principles are pressed into it from the Past. They are of the present only as the present is the immortality of the past.

The writings of a Channing, wherever read, would a

lone render impossible anything approaching to a moral and mental stagnation for the future—not to speak of retrogression. Folly might elsewhere fight (as in the Crimea) for kings and emperors, and even for queens,— but not there: for they are instinct with a power trans- cending all the physical force in the world, and are of more real value to it than all the war material it contains, costly as that may be. “Heaven buries madmen in the heaps they raise:” it says ‘ruin to ruin,’ and desolation covers forever both wrong doer and his deeds. But the works of the truly wise and righteous come not thus to nought. They are baptised into eternity, & become everlastingly enwrought with the constitution of the world.
