[DESPAIR]

Yet were safe from aught of further soil As though he filled his grave. He hath forgot The taste of fear [unclear]—the faculty hath lost To feel vicissitude;— events to him, Whether by Fortune brightened or begrimmed, Being hueless as the noiseless tide of Time On which they hurry past.

Even such art thou, Despair! and such I felt thee at my heart For many a day and night.—But to arise With a new sense of strength, from visions new Of Love and Truth and Freedom, and through these A promise that Humanity, so much By error now deformed, shall yet become A creature as divinely-browed again As the first father, Adam;—to arise With a fresh-swelling vigor thus derived In solitary thought, and shake thee off! Even as Shakspeare’s lion, in the Bard’s Rampant conception of the act, did shake The dew-drop from his mane—into “thin air”.

Note The leonine image in Shakspear which is here alluded to, occurs in the following passage from Troilus and Cressida, Act III. Sc.3.

—Rouse yourself; and the weak wanton Cupid Shall from your neck unloose his amorous fold, And like a dew-drop from the Lion’s mane, Be shook to air.
