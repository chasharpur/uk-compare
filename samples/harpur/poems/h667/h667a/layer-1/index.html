The Wanton.

How bitter oft is Truth.—Be sure of this, There’s nothing in the world, not even the winds, The ocean waves, the fell-fires’s fickle flame, So faithless as a Wanton:—one in grain. 673 I loved a creature of this sort,—yea, loved her.! Being lured into her thralls by the fond thought (For so it seemed) that she did first love me. Yet even in the rapture of my heart’s Most fatal facination, strange and wild Suspicions of her worthlessness would flash Like fire into my brain. For in the mode And temper of her love, I felt a want Of something that should indicate, as ‘twere, Its hidden truth:—a subtlety of joy, A rosiness of passion conquering pride And maiden shame, and yet accusing aye Its own sweet wilfulness;—all these were wanting.

These lines were the commencement of an intended tale to be founded upon one of the most bitter and painfully humiliating passages of my life, which I may yet complete—that is, if the muse herself does not also prove a wanton, and forsake the forsaken. I make mention of the fact, however, merely to show how much my poetry, even to a scrap like the above, has grown out of my actual experience: how much it has been pressed out of my own heart by the heavy crush of the real, however idea[l] it may seem in itself.

C.H
