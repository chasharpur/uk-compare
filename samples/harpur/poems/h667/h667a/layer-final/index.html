The Wanton.

How bitter oft is Truth.—Be sure of this, There’s nothing in the world, not even the winds, The ocean waves, the fell-fires’s fickle flame, So faithless as a Wanton:—one in grain. 673 I loved a creature of this sort,—yea, loved her.! Being lured into her toils by the fond thought (For so it seemed) that she did first love me. Yet even in the rapture of my heart’s Most fatal facination, sudden and fierce Suspicions of her worthlessness would flash Like fire within my brain. For in the mode And temper of her love, I felt a want Of something that should indicate, as ‘twere, Its hidden truth:—a subtlety of joy, A rosiness of passion conquering pride And maiden shame, and yet accusing aye Its own sweet wilfulness;—all these were wanting.

These lines were the commencement of an intended tale to be founded upon one of the most bitter and painfully humiliating passages of my life, which I may yet complete—that is, if the muse herself does not also prove a wanton, and forsake the forsaken. x I thank God, however, now, even for that trial, believing that hither my moral nature has alw[ays] been the stronger for past weal[.] And I make mention of the fact, merely to show how much my poetry, even to a scrap like the above, has grown out of my actual experience: how much it has been pressed out of my own heart by the heavy crush of the real, however idea[l] it may seem in itself.

C.H

Woman

Now for the philosophical set-off. Though constitutionally disposed well nigh to worship Woman and to stickle for her rights also with an ultra liberality; I kn[ow] at the same time, that Man is her natural superior—her bor[n] head. Of this fact, their comparative persons & dispositio[n] with every sexual, social and domestic relation susbsisting between them, is unmistaka[bly] demonstrative. His bodily structure is fir[m] and angular, hers yielding and round. He is adventur[ous in] spirit and out-seeking in habit; she is unspeculativ[e] and retired. He is endued with the higher fortitude of soul for their material defence; and with the greater physical energy and endurance for their joint manual support; whe[n] the mere results of their conjugal intercourse are often such a[s] to render her utterly helpless and dependent upon him. Nay[,] finally, in the very perpetuation of the species, he is the cause, and she only the procreative medium.—Th[e] poetry above was written in my youth; this note to it in my manhood.

C.H.
