THE BABYLONIAN CAPTIVITY.

(PSALM CXXXVII.)

BY CHARLES HARPUR

By far Euphrates’ stream we sate, A weary band of hearded slaves, And over Judah’s fall’n estate We wept into the passing waves.

Like massed up clouds, before us rose Proud Babel’s distant structures,—where Her shadows in their vast repose Lay dark within the watery glare.

On willow boughs that o’er us bent Our once glad-sounding harps were hung, That but the wild wind as it went Might grieve their wailful chords among.

But they who spoiled us—even they Who wasted us with daily wrongs, To make them [illeg.], did asking say: Come sing us one of Zion’s songs?

How can with us the will remain To strike the harp with fettered hand? How can we sing a joyful strain, As captives, in a foreign Land?

And when, Jerusalem, we let Thy memory pass on Time’s grey wing, May our right hands at once forget Their mastery o’er the sounding string!

Yea, let our tongues to song be dumb, As in a dull and voiceless dream, Till to thy courts again we come, And thy redemption be the theme!

Then, Edom, thou shalt get thy wound, For thou, on Zion’s evil day, Said Raze her beauty to the ground, And captive drive her sons away!

And lo, yet fiercer visions rise! The day is fixed—the hour is known— When, Babylon, thy fateful skies Shall rain red wrath and ruin down!

Till by thy towers all overthrown, Thy daughters, desolate as we, Each with her shame shall set alone— With Shame and wild-faced Misery!

And then may the Avenger dash Thy children’s heads against the stones; For so, ’mid Zion’s falling crash, Didst thou with all our little ones!
