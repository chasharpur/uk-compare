I Would Get Me a New Love.

I would get me a new love, the old fire is out! I would get me a new love ere Spring comes about; That as in her warm arms the fields all re-bloom, My lone heart may awaken from coldness and gloom.

O there’s nothing in nature like love when ’tis new; And there’s nothing in fortune like love when ’tis true! But my passion for Meg was a fire that’s burnt out, And I’d get me a new love ere Spring comes about.

111a

I would get me a new love, less Custom’s cold slave; And I’d get me a new love more earnest and brave! To pure to mistrust me, too noble to doubt— I would get such a new love ere Spring comes about.

But if such a new love I nowhere may get, I’d as lief be thus lonely—nay, lonelier yet! Than have one like the old love, just playing a part, With a flare in her fancy, but ice at her heart.

Final copy.

110c

As the above piece was composed to serve for the Marriage Proposal of a poetic friend, it strikes me now, that it is in some measure incumbent on me, to tail-piece it in prose with a sufficiently unromantic Sequel. The heroine of the poem—or rather who might have been the Heroine of it, was a very excellent young person. But so shocked was her prudence–so suddenly galvanised, as it were into super-vitality and insight— by its rigid statement of terms, that she at once backed out of whatever engagement there happened to be between my tuneful friend and herself. Leaving him “all alone for to die”, she afterwards wedded a well-doing bachelor;—had her gig; had her servants; had her table supplied with the best of “boiled and roast”; bore several butcherlings; did a deal of gossip; and finally pushed up her super-prudent existence at the age of twenty eight: her mortal ailment, according to the doctors, having been caused by over- feeding—a malady that could hardly have been so privileged to circumvent her, had she been a Poet’s wife, instead of a butcher’s. Still it will be seen 111c that she was no fool, as she used to say of herself, in reference to a certain “stuffy old affair”, and as her friends used to say of her when discussing the same matter; immediately followed in the text is the pen while the poem that im- mediately follows in the text is the per contra account, and gives the feeling in which my Poet Friend looked at that particular defection of hers.
