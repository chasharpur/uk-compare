* JONES THE IMMIGRANT.

At Auburn lived Jones, in a little thatched cot, In a quiet, secluded, and beautiful spot, With scarcely three acres to plough and to seed, And from them his five rosy children to feed!

Yet his father and grandfather both had lived there, And had brought up their children; then wherefore despair. He strove hard and spared much, still to cling to the place, And to look, like a man, all the world in the face.

And there was the Church where his Jenny he won,— And there the Churchyard where he buried his son,— Where his father and grandfather both lay at rest; And of all the wide world he loved Auburn the best.

He strove, but in vain;—times press’d hard on the poor, And, with tears, he abandoned his loved cottage door, Yet with trust in his God he passed over the sea, In Australia, a living to make, and be free.

And that he and his family too might grow rich, And not die, as at home, like mere dogs in a ditch, A liberal soul, a great squatter, agreed To give him, as wages, twelve pounds, and his feed.

And no doubt Jones was happy—he had no regret, He had nothing, but misery at home, to forget; While his family to keep in great comfort and cheer, He has here a bare ration, and twelve pounds a year!
