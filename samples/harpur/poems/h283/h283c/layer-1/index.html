The Lass of Eulengo.

Let those who worship Fashion sigh At titled Beauty’s feet; Let him whose God is Mammon try A golden Bride to meet; Let all whom Affectation charms Frequent the city show— But give unto my happy arms The Lass of Eulengo.

If Beauty that hath bloomed apart Be loveliest to behold; A spotless name, and truth of heart More excellent than gold; 97 If sparkling arts that lure to sin Be nought to virtue’s glow; Then matchless were my portion in The Lass of Eulengo.

I see her in my dreams by night, And hear her gentle voice; And when in morning’s golden light All living things rejoice, Each skiey tint of rarest grain— Each fresh delight below— All bring into my thoughts again The Lass of Eulengo.

O never may one frown of Fate Upon her future lour, Nor villain Guile there lie in wait To wound so sweet a flower! But happiness for her abound, And joy forever flow, A bright and gushing stream, around The Lass of Eulengo.

Note. I once hoped and believed that the heroine of this Poem was destined to become my Wife; but fortune was permitted to will it otherwise. I was poor, nor was she rich, and “prudence was her o’erword aye”. I would have been content—I would have been happy to have had to have toiled for her welfare every day of my future life and

“The world was all before us, where to choose Our place of rest, and Providence our guide.”

98

But she would not: her alledged reason for finally breaking with me, being certain imperfections in my worldly welfare. She was advised to this course by her friends. But I never pretended to be a moral saint—even to her; and the model-men, the suggested preferences, of her friends—they, in all moral regards, were even less saintly than myself. They were, however, considerably richer.

My notions and tastes may be peculiar, but I have small patience, and as little respect for, overmuch prudence in love, and as little relish for a cold passion as for cold soup. And what is now the fruit which she has gathered from these? A loveless future /supposing her love for me to have been genuine as far as it went/, and a regretful—perhaps an accusing past. For the first evil, what can compensate? And secondly, if she valued me much, despite the chilling admonitions of her mere prudence /as I believe she did/, there is at least so much against the self-referent wisdom of her conduct, in finally rejecting me.

I write this Note in the spirit of regret—not of complaint. Nor she now, can have aught to complain of. For, to be phlosophical, if her prudence has been a genuine thing—a moral and not a selfish principle of conduct, the satisfaction of having thoroughly carried it out, is her proper, and should be her sufficient recompense. In acting upon it, she has been simply fulfilling, in her appointed place, her appointed duty.

But for myself,

“The wearied Bird, blown o’er the deep, Would sooner quit the shore, Than I would cross the gulf again That time has brought me o’er.”

1847.

P.S. (1850.) The Lady-subject of the above Note was not so very prudent after all. At all events, she is at this present smiling over my shoulder as Mary Harpur: smiling magnanimously, as having just consented that the said 99 Note, with all its faults, should remain wedded, even like myself, to the “Lass of Eulengo”. To this consent, of course she has been moved by my most dutiful request, made in all humility, under the impression that the matter of it is very characteristic of a peculiar turn in my bygone batchelor disposition.
