[Pope]

[His Satirical Mastery.]

In this supreme! with what a Jove-like ease He crushed the Titans of the times’ disease! “Bared the mean heart that lurked beneath a star!” And even with brazen Dulness waged victorious war.

O, could I wield so well, in current song, The searching scourge of the sateric thong, How the bold visaged Vices of the day Should crouch, and slink, like beaten curs, away! But chief, the Lust of Gold, that hell-lit rage Which eats into the Spirit of the age! That Mammon-worship which makes outward show The prime morality where’er we go! Befrills Religion like a jade in vogue! Besainsts a usurer—if a “pious” rogue! Enslaves even Justice, till she dare not see In its high places palpable villany! Brands honest Need with shame! till money’d Hate Sees but a brutedom in a poor estate! Deems Learning beggarly, and Wisdom vain, Devotion weak, Philanthropy insane Impairs in souls once elevate and brave, All noble scorn of every prosperous knave! Taints even the heart of childhood! and distils A social poison that all friendship kills!— O, I would lash it to its native hell, Thenceforth with Devils; not with Men, to dwell. x

x Though Pope’s poetry is the very perfection of its School (the French- -English School, which permitted nothing beyond the precisely beauti- -ful, and a rhetorical imitation of the sublime—not the sublime itself), his best pieces, as poems, are unspeakably inferior to those of the elder English Muse, beginning in Chaucer, and last seen in its thoroughness in Crashaw, until its reappearance in the present century. Still, I say, his poetry is perfect in its kind and degree—but this very perfection, nationally considered, was its blame; because it became therefore the fashionable cause of the exclusive cultivation in England for nearly a century, of an exotic refinement, not in verse only, but even in the prose of popular 65 novelists and flashy divines. See, for instance, the writings of Hervey. Take away his French-English prettinesses of phrase and anthetical exclamations—and what remains? Positively next to nothing: these bearing about the same proportion to the body of thought, as the tendrils of the largest kind of our bush creepers do to the handfull of root which attaches them to the soil☉.—That Pope did but open a vein of mental silver at best, is evinced by the fact, that during the whole of the period of his continuing in prime vogue, his style and manner to a hair, became the poetical small-change of everybody, from a “rhyming peer” to a sentimental footman. Nay,

Even leaden Pedentry, by working hard In verbal joinery, became a Bard.

As to his mere style, even Walker the orthöepist, who was nearly as tuneless as an ox, could hit it like winking; and pronounce upon the strength of this, the following lines from Denham “as rug- -ged and as little musical as almost any in the language.”

Though deep/ yet clear// though gentle/yet not dull; Strong/ without rage// without o’erflowing/full.

It will be evident from the tenor of these remarks that I do not relish Pope so heartily as when I criticised him in rhyme (see my Poem). I do not: and think the ensuing strictures on his style, by Lowell, eminently just “Pope had treated the English language as the image man has served the bust of Shakspeare yonder. To rid it of some external soils, he has rubbed it down till there is no muscular expression left.xxx He is forever mixing water with the good old mother’s milk of our tongue. xxx He makes Homer lisp like the friar in Chaucer, and Ajax and Belinda talk exactly alike.”—Nevertheless Pope has great and peculiar excellences. His sense is always clear and good, his fancy elegant, and his wit keen and beamy as the sun-blade of Apollo himself. But he wanted simplicity of faith in Nature, and idial abandonment in Art, and should never therefore be the sole Gamaliel of a young Poet.

C.H. 1847.
