Part V.

“Again I lived—again I felt. But now The winds of heaven seemed under me, and I Was sweeping,—like the Spirit of a storm That bellowed round me,—in its murky glooms, All heaving with a wide and manifold motion That seemed yet mightier than the darkling swells Of ocean, wrestling with a midnight gale! The wild winds tossed me; I was drenched throughout With heavy moisture, and at intervals, Amid the ragged gaps of moving cloud, Methought I caught dim glimpses of the sun Hanging aloft, as if in drear eclipse. But gradually my senses cleared, and I Grew conscious that some new change was upon me. Then glancing at my limbs, I saw that they Were clothed with plumage; at the same time, too, I felt the sharpness of long-taloned claws, As they worked closing with a fierce desire And sudden hungriness for blood and prey! An impulse to pursue and to destroy Both on the earth and in the air, ran quick Out from my heart and shivered in my wings: And as a thing more central yet, I felt Regnant within me, throned o’er all, a lone And sullen, yet majestic, glow of pride.

“‘Twas plain that I who had aforetime been Crushed out of human being into that Of a Wild Beast, had thence again been crushed 210a Into the nature of some mighty Thing That now swept sailing on wide van-like wings, Amid the whirls of an aerial gloom, That, out extending, in one mighty cope Hung heaving, like a black tent roof,x o’er all The floor of Africa.

“Still on I swept, And still as far as my keen vision went, That now was gifted with a power that seemed To pierce all space, I saw the vapours roll In dreadful continuities of black And shapeless masses, by the winds convulsed; But soon in the remotest distance they Showed different, as touched with sunny splendors; And nearing still to these, I saw them dash, Like the wild surges of an uproused sea Of molten gold, against the marble sides Of heaven-high mountains which, though far below My flight, yet pierced up through them all, and stood With splintered cones and monster-snouted crags, Immovable as Fate. Beneath me, lo, The grandeur of the Kingdom of the Air Was circling in its magnitude immense! A dread magnificence of which before I might not even dream. I saw its quick And subtle interchange of forms and hues That more and more astonished; saw its black And solid-seeming reservoirs of rain— Its laboring forges of the awful thunder.

“At last, as onward still I swept, above A milky mass of vapour, that spread flat Like a vast floor of purest alabaster, Behold, reflected in its quiet gleam, I saw a Shadow that swept on with me, Reversed as was the Lion’s in the well: With van-like wings; with eyebals seething fire; With taloned claws, and cruel down-bent beak: The shadow of a monstrous Eagle—such As seemed indeed, the mightiest that had sailed The seas of space since Adam named the first.

“No doubt was left me as to what I was: My fated soul had passed into the form Of that huge Eagle which swept shadowed there! My first impression from the thing I saw Was a cold horror! I was once again Imprisoned in the being of a Brute, Albeit a noble one; and had again To live for ages—centuries it might be— In the base being of a nature, yet Inferior, by what infinite descent! Even to that remnant of intelligent mind Which still kept with me,—like a put-back soul Burningly conscious of its foregone powers,— Its inborn sovereignty of kind,—and yet So latent, self-less;—had to live again A life of carnage, and to sail abroad A terror to all birds and gentle beasts That heard the hurricane rushings of my wings! A royal Bird indeed—but thence alone: Whose inaccessible pride of place was still The solemn loneness of the mightiest hills, And of the highest heavens.

“But in truth, Not much, for many periods, had I need To search for prey; for countless hosts of men Were mustering forth o’er all the face of Earth, Casting an iron gleam o’er trampled leagues Of golden corn, or as they onward marched Leaving behind them seas of raging fire, In whose red surges cities thronged with men, And happy hamlets, homes of health and peace, Isled in the scopes of plenty-bearing glebes That rang erewhile with rural thankfulness, Were whelmed in one wide doom, and from the earth All but erased. Or to their wish at last Confronted upon some set field of fight, Their sullen cloud-like masses, in their strength, Would charge each other with a dreadful roar— A roar that shrunk the furious yells of beasts Into mere make-believe;—would rush to wounds, And sumless slaughter, with a madness such As could have issued only out of Hell! Or else about fenced cities they would pitch Their crowded camps, and leaguer them for years, Sowing the fields about them with a slime Of carnage, till their growths were plagues alone! And finally beating them so flat, that lo, Their very names were thenceforth heard no more. —What is the ravage made by brutes on brutes, To man’s on Man?

“

With liesure hence derived, (For ever when I hungered, close at hand Some fresh-slain man lay smoking in his gore) And even scandalised by what I saw Of wholesale murder in the ways of men, 213 I said—My realm is large, and I will keep My state in all its regions; and though still The savage instincts of Eagle life Were fierce within me, not the less I felt That now my lot was mollified, and cast Into a mould even capable of joy: Safe as I was from all pursuit—from thirst, From famine, and from wounds; for what wild fool Would aim an arrow at me as I soared Above the populations? It was plain That solaces, though few and far between, Were added to me; and I thence derived Even in the musings of my Eagle heart, A feeling that was daily more confirmed, That not for ever was my soul condemned To suffer in the body of a Brute! For though the memories of the towering crimes And matchless lusts, that filled my whole career While human, worked within me evermore, No longer did they shed about my life That scorpian venom whose so subtle fire Once burnt like blight. Nay, I remembered well How often I had looked with longing eyes Up at the clear Egyptian heavens, and watched The wings that clove them, envying every bird That soaring in the sunshine, seemed to be Exempt from all the grovelling cares of men. Oft I remembered how, even in my most Triumphant period, I had wished to exchange My being with an ibis that I saw Floating away, upon his speckled wings, Tow’rds Memphis, at a height in heaven so safe— So inaccessible to hate or hurt! I thought how oft, when with my hunting train, 214 In that rude region spread about the Cataracts, I had watched the eagles as they rose aloft Into the lovely blue, and saw them turn As through the colored clouds still up they went Against the sunset, into feathered flags Of royal purple edged with burning gold.

“

These fields of space were now my Kingdom—why Should I then not enjoy them? Nay, the faculty Alone of motion in a world so rich Was something noble: but to move at will Upward or forward, or in circles vast, Through boundless spaces with a rushing speed No living thing’s might rival, and to have The majesty of the everlasting hills Perpetually beneath me, with the green Luxuriance of the myriad-peopled plains,— Broad rivers, and the towery towns that sate Beside their spacious mouths, with out beyond The lonely strength of the resounding seas— This liberty began to move my pride As somewhat godlike; and, in moving, made A sure impression that kept graining still Into the texture of my brute estate— Yea, graining in through all its animal lusts And savage wonts.

“

Hence ever more and more The temper of a better spirit grew Within me, as from inkling roots, and moved Even like an enbryon in its moist recess: A sensibility to beauteous things As now I saw them in the heavens displayed, And in the bright luxuriance of the earth; 215 Some power of just comparison—some sense Of how a man would rank them, could he see Those secular grandeurs from that sovereign height Whence I beheld them. And with this a wish To commune even with the human race, And pour the loftier wonders of my life Into their ears, through a rich-worded song Whose golden periods in their mellow flow Should witch all ears that heard them—even old men’s, Even jaded monarch’s; not to speak of theirs, Those spirit-lovely Ones—yea, Moons of Love That rise at first in the Circassian hills, As they should tingle all like tiny shells Of roseate whiteness, to its perfect chords.

“

One day, upon a royal visitation, Low stooping ’mid the Mountains of the Moon, Behold a sudden storm had gathered up Out of my view, hid by a neighboring height, But which, thence wheeling with terrific force, Wide tossed me with its gusts—aloft, and then Downward as far; then whirlingly about, Even like a withered leaf. My strength of wing Availed me nought, so mightily it raged! Then suddenly, out in the dun distance, lo, I saw as from the storm’s Plutonian heart A mass of white-hot light keep writhing forth With horrible noises, into a vast orb! When for a moment by its dread light shown, The dubious figure of a withered man Seemed dropping headlong through the lurid clouds; While full within its radiant disc, again The conquering Son of Paradise appeared Up towering in his amplitude sublime; 216 On whose divine brow I might yet discern Some sign of wrath, though of a cast less dread Than erewhile. Onward with tremendous speed The mighty Vision rushed, right tow’rds me, orbed In that white issue of miraculous light! The blasting influence of which ere long Enlapped my frame. I felt a stiffling heat; One cruel pang I felt, and headlong then Fell earthward—dead: a plumb descending mass.
