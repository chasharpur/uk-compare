The Carouse of Despair.

[A Lay of Passion.]

Come Alan, I will flood the bowl, And thou shalt sing that Song of mine Which dates the storm that dates my soul— Which fits me for the fiery wine! Yes, often may I lift it up, So parching is my sense of wrong, Ere I can drown in this deep cup The thought of Her I loved so long.

As comes the Moon when rising bright, To one who treads a foreign shore, She rose upon me like the light Of something I had loved before: The record of a former tie Seemed written in my heart and brain— A destined thrall foregone and by, Seemed cycling into act again.

I loved the genial smiles of Day, With every voice of these that told; I loved the Night, in her array Thus spangled with celestial gold; I loved all mediums too, through which The soul of Genius breathed;—but She Of these, of all became one rich— One final Ideality!

And was I loved?—Pour high the wine! Behold her!—to our tryste she trips! Again I feel the fire on mine That passioned from her burning lips! But there was treachery in her vow, And future blight in every kiss; With all the infamy that now Is in the consciousness of this.

And yet I grieve—remembering still How that wild passion sphered my soul! 159 How soveriegn ’twas for good and ill Or to impel me or control! Its dream was scattered,—but it left A blinding fire within my brain! And strange, of such a dream bereft, I wished its madness mine again!

For daily then, the morning flowers That flush the fields with joy’s excess; With all that makes the evening hours Instinct with thoughtful happiness: The gleaming waters of the earth— The glowing wonders of the skies— Did but encrease that mighty dearth That burns and blinds my loveless eyes!

And since, such sounds within the breeze Of bird or brook, far floating by, With that rare music, mixed with these, Which filters from the stilly sky; Seem, as Regret in every place Stands listening with me when alone,— Seem to have lost all native grace, And taken up a [unclear] tone.

Pour high the wine! I know too well Thy soul is dark—even dark as mine! And hath a tale of wrongs to tell That might excuse excess in wine. But met we not, my Friend, to quaff In Agony’s defiance? Yea, Untill our bitter hearts should laugh All memory of the Past away!

There’s one rare tone by Passion heard, Alone on Life’s wild-sounding shore, Which nothing to the heart it stirred But after suffering may restore: 160 And this, to night, keeps dirging by, A mystery of regret to thine; And this it is, even now, that I Would stifle in the fiery wine!

Ah, wear not thus that piteous smile! The wounded eagle highest flies! And be it ours to reach awhile That madness wherein misery dies! Yet when I think of times when we Were gladsome as the morning light, I weep to know that hearts so free Thus desolate were to be to night.

Fill high again the potent bowl! That thought is stinging like a snake! Despair is clouding from my soul Like mist from some benighted Lake! A shapeless Gloom—but fiery tongued With fierce regrets that now should be To one, by Love so direly wronged, The buried things of Memory.

But Alan, you forget. Come sing That Welcome to an Early Grave; It soothes despair, as Zephyr’s wing Might lull some storm-begotten wave: And while its measures gather up Love’s dying tears, to crown the song, I’ll pledge a last—a [unclear] [unclear] To that False One I loved so long. x

x I will transcribe here, by way of Note to this Lay, an extract from a speech I delivered in the Singleton Debating Society (1842) on the question: Which are the greater, the woes of the unfortunate debtor, or those of the unfortunate lover?—”When life is new to us—and before the heart’s young trusts are hackneyed away in the selfish struggles of worldly competition, or withered by the noxious breath of conventional distinctions, we know by common experience, how 161 how lavishly it is wont to sprinkle the veiled face of the Future with the bright dews of delighted hope. It cannot believe that envy there, and detraction, stand ready to dog our steps with the pertenacity of hired assassins, and to dash even the cup of success with poison -drops of injustice—the bitterer for being so mingled. No; the warm heart of youth cannot believe this, but is even too resolute to regard that shadowy Future as the grand rewarder of all honest endeavour, and the maternal ratifier of all legitimate enjoyment; and having given it this character, and feeling in the wise language of Scripture, that “it is not good to be alone”, it immediately gazes outward after some fair being with whom to share all these beautiful anticipations of success and happiness. A courtship is the natural consequence; and it may be, that an actual or fancied congeniality in tasts, habits, and sentiments, speedily induces on one side—we will suppose for the occasion, on the man’s side —a fixed and ardent attachment: such indeed, as shall make even the shadow of the chosen one mysteriously dear, and gift her lightest wish with a sway more imp[e]rative than that which is con- -veyed by the decree of an Empress. In fine, such an ascendancy over his whole being shall his passion have at length acquired, that “he would not let even the winds of heaven visit her face too roughly.”—But alas for human love! just at this crisis, by a sudden accession of wealth, or by some other equally fortuitous event, the lady is lifted far above the worldly sphere of her lover; and at a subsequent interview, she informs him, with an air of studied coldness, that Mr. Know-the-world, her uncle, or Mrs. Ways-and-means, her dear friend and adviser, have both decided in her behalf, that it would be monstrously improper for M Somebody such as she is now become, to unite her destiny with that of a Mr. Nobody. Her lover—poor devil! [unclear] very properly takes the Mr. Nobodyism to himself: and after a long last look, in which the crushed spirit of hope and the black bitterness of wrong, attempt in vain to disguise them- -selves under a mask of ineffable disdain—he bangs away in a huff; and bang, too, goes the door behind him!—But really it is no laughing matter. What a melancholy change is thus wrought in this discarded being’s destiny—— wrought in an hour! ‘Tis true, the pregnant future is yet untried before him—the same avenues to success and distinction are there—the same hills and dales of prospective enjoyment;—but she who was to sweeten every honor by participation—to exalt every pleasure by sharing 162 it—to inspire him with an energy equal to every necessity and pursuit—she is cruelly lifted forever out of the reach of his affections! He may affect to despise her. He may resolve to forget her. He may even have brought himself to speak of her with the coldness of contempt. He may mix with seeming heartiness in the noisy crush of social pleasure, or in the giddy whirl of fashion- -able folly. The fresh face of Nature may yet win him into rapture, and Beauty again attract him: nevertheless, his position thenceforth amid the dry realities of the world, will be such as to justify him in exclaiming, with all the bitter pathos of the poet:

“On me no more—no, never more on me The freshness of the heart shall fall like dew.”

C.H.
