IV.

A TOO COMMON REGRET.

When the Father who toiled for our welfare is dead, And the Mother who bore us is laid in her grave, What would we not give, if we never had said, Or done in our frowardness, aught that e’er gave Those hearts that once loved us, as none can again, One pulse of disquiet—one moment of pain?

We loved them—but ah! in a measure that fell Too short of the one they deserved on our part; We served them sincerely and honoured them well, But the motive, though truthful, was cold,—and we start With a feeling of loss that recurs evermore, And a failure of peace that we knew not before.
