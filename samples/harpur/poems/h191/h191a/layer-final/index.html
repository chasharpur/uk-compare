Impromptu, on reading in an article of the Melbourne Argus the names of Julius Ceasar and Mr. XXX coupled together.

What fanfarronade ’tis When a mere drudge of trade is Thus class’d with some bold Empire-builder of old! Why, this XXX, whom I know to a hair, sir, Is no hero—not he; and I’d sware, sir, In no cause he’ll be hearty Without a strong party And well-buttered bun: By my soul, I declare, ‘Twere as fit to compare The forge of some pot-brazing Tinker With the blaze of that star-moulding swinker, The Sun!

Note. Now I vow, upon my conscience, that I have no ill feeling to the party upon whom the above is written. x I even blank his name, to show that my quarrel is not so much with him as with a false standard. Nay, I entertain perhaps a higher true opinion of him than most persons. Yet on reading in the Argus the paragraph referred to in the Inscrip- -tion, the satire would out of me. And why? Because it is my confirmed 653 belief that false estimates of men and things are the social infirmities and and moral baulks of the day. Society is now compounding as it were for its long rest in direct shams, by clubbing with and running after indirect ones. But until it shall turn its back upon all shins and shams, direct and indirect, it will be unworthy of the respect, and even of the forbearance, of any whole-hearted thinker.
