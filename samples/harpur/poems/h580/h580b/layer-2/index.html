The Temple of Infamy: A Satire, by Charles Harpur.

“———you may kiss— Exactly as you please, or not, the rod But if you don’t, I’ll lay it on by G—d.”

Byron.

Preface.

The following Satire is the first step in an attempt to expose, and root up if possible, the “thousand and one” Infamies that are everywhere depraving the morals and debasing the intellects of the rising generation of this Colonial Public. The wise and the good, the decent and the gentle, have nothing therefore to fear from the application of its caustics, but much to hope, if there be any truth in the social principle, that the directest means for permanently establishing the righteous, arise out of the signal overthrow of the wicked.

To these expressions I will only add, that it was written under a keen sense of private injury ※ and the public necessity of such a production; and that the two vacuities indicated by asterisks are so left open for the introduction, in a future edition, of new characters, both singly and in sets—the Satire thus possessing the prospective advantage of hanging in terrorem, as it were, over the heads of such evil doers as may not yet have been publickly admonished of the infamy of their ways.

1846

※ I would guard this admission by a few distincte remarks. I can only be affected within myself by the opinions of such persons as I conceive capable of properly estimating any merit or demerit which they may applaud or blame in me. Of those of whom I conceive otherwise, I cannot regard, if I would, either the censure or the praise. I cannot, I say, because it is in my nature only to value what is intrinsic in other 356 natures. Then why, it may be asked, do I resent injuries coming from such unworthy quarters? Because, though not at all moved thereto from within, I am yet, unfortunately, somewhat affected from without. I mean with regard to the means of life. Had I but a certain provision of one loaf per day, with a suit of slops twice a year, I should be utterly regardless of all such creatures as they who are the literary subjects of this Satire could do and say against me, even though my individual disparagement should be their whole study, and their constant employment.

C. H.

The Temple of Infamy.

Weary of life almost, to think how Wrong And Infamy waxed daily still more strong In my own sunny Land, which else the sweet Abode might be of all things just and meet, In beauty excellent, in grace complete; I sought a lonely place—and there, the spell Of a stern Vision on my spirit fell.

A beauteous Creature, more than Woman fair, With eyes like two keen stars, and sun-bright hair, Came to my side and said—Fear not! thine eyes Behold in me the Soul of Truth—arise! For I have much to open on thy view, Touching the Ills thou dost so justly rue.

There is, as thou wilt see, a Temple, where The brood of Infamy in spirit repair Daily to worship—a delusive Den, Yet gorgeous to the unpurged eyes of men.

Follow. I followed.—Through a dreary Wood That shining Guide conducted, til we stood Before a mighty Temple,—all of gold In seeming, and, most glorious to behold.

We entered—and within all showed as bright And costly as without—a marvellous sight! Wherefore I questioned, how that such might be The Temple of the tribes of Infamy?

Truth turned, and answered,—touching first mine eyes,— Now look again! Then straight a new surprise Burst on me: all that there before had shone Like gem-starred gold, seemed now of coarsest stone, Decayed, and half with clammy moss o’ergrown.

Such is the change in all things evil, She Continued, when with gifted eyes we see; 358 And in exact reverse, the lowliest home Of Virtue then appears a glorious dome, And is such; for the smile of God is there, The one true source of all that’s truly fair.

Now to the purpose for which thou art here: The votaries of Infamy draw near; Of whom, as my vowed Champion, it is thine To see all such as with the rod divine Of great Apollo, thou must drive to light, And scourge, unsparing, in thy Country’s sight.

Stand with me here apart: and when Life brings Round thee again the scope of usual things— Then faithfully relate, despising fear, All that thou hearest and beholdest here.

Vacant the entrance yet a space was seen, But coming steps were heard, and, these between, Two coarse yet simpering voices high in glee— Their theme, the sweets of Taste and Poesie.

The colloquists soon entered, and then stood Linked arm in arm in loving brotherhood: Stood gazing, and as struck with rapture, then They kissed each other, did those girlish men, And fondly wept, a piteous pair to see, Brimful of friendship, sentiment,—and tea!

The Genius, with ineffable disdain Curling her lip, remarked then of the twain: Two thus in folly joined there’s no untwoing! I think you know them both—’tis H–ll and Ew–g.

The pitifullest Scribblers, surely, these, That ever dabbled in the mysteries Of either Prose or Rhyme! For H–ll, there may Be offered one excuse—the bite of R–e. (1.) 359 And what to Infamy hath brought him most, Is,—having horrified as Tarquin’s ghost The Muses into fits; and overthrown Of rhyme and reason every canon known, In his burlesque upon Napoleon,— That thus by his own tastelessness disgraced, He still should dare to lecture upon Taste!

But for this Ew–g’s crimes, the only plea Besides incipient insanity, That might avail, him’s a confirmed excess In mental pap,(2)—and thence, that windiness Which he, God wot! mistaketh for some fine Eructive voicefulness of thought divine.

She ceased: and I—What moveth most my mind, In reference to yon Scribblers, is to find An Institution of a kind most dear And hopeful to thy heart, O Truth severe! Divested so, as to be prone to yield To such Pretenders an exclusive field! What Man of sense should silent be, who sees Its ends debased to Mountebanks like these? If such are its prime lights in Taste and Song, Henceforward be it called, (or for so long As comes through such the knowledge it imparts) The School of Charlatans—not School of Arts.

I ceased: and now the Friends resumed their walk, Two lovely lilies on one tender stalk; So passed within, still tearful, but serene, Repeating the deep woes of Madeline. (3.)

I looked again, and saw within the gate A Wight come skipping, with a pygmy pate, And face of studied smiles, which else were seen In all its mental larceny of mien. 361 So entering, he turned, and beckoned straight To one who followed, limping in his gait, And smoothing as he came, with decent care, The majic ermine that proclaimed him Mayor. (4)

Then thus my Guide—Of this same two, I see One only is a child of Infamy; The other, a good easy man of leather, But follows ass-like, led by Flattery’s tether.

But who and whence, is he who foremost goes, Leading this civic Magnate by the nose?

He! answered Truth, the foremost of the two Regarding with a look that pierced him through! Of Scottish Scribbleshire a native he, A province betwixt Prose and Poetry. There had he hungred still, on crowdy fed, But that he squibbed a Baillie—so he fled! Fled from his lovely classic mists, to be (As Shame decreed at his nativity) The ball-room Ercildoune of Botany Bay, (5.) And Corporation Laureat—piddling R–e!

But ere installed thus in the “pride of place,” ‘Twas his to figure ’mid the scribbling race That fly-blow in Australia’s sunny clime— Man-midwife-general of rascal Rhyme! (6.) Even H–ll declares he never should have shown The ass’s ears, had he been left alone; And the sad Shepherd, having tailed his sheep, Damns R–e and Rhyme ere he in peace can sleep.

But let them pass—though truly such a pair Was never made before,—a Poet and a Mayor!

Behold who follows, and, in passing by, Half masks the lurking hatred of his eye! Slander has set upon his life her brand; And lo, the Atlas damnifies his hand!

I looked, and saw a low-browed Creature pass; A sort of mule, twixt human fox and ass! Half to his face he held a mask, and so Arranged a broad-sheet sneeringly and slow: For genuine malice and true love of trick Are sly and never in appearance quick.

You know him, Truth resumed, but none but he Himself, no man might credit that a slave And yet retain no Man’s likeness!—Let him sneer; ‘Tis the vile solace of a vile career.

Just loosed from school, and learned enough to string Rote-beads from Horace, he dished up a thing Y-clept a Book (7.) by custom, as the one Wide term of dog takes in each bitch’s son: Full of mere words,—without one glow of joy, One gush of love, though written by a Boy! ‘Tis dead long since:—and now, by Faction hired; That is, by just two pounds per week inspired— Lo, peeping from the mask that erst were Lowe, He churns and spit venom as he goes. On all whom his volponic spirit knows By intuition, are his human foes!

Next entering, a solemn Wight I saw, Reciting with the voice of a macaw Some verses, which, his earnestness alone, Horribly pompous! had declared his own.

My bright Companion laughed outright, and said Alas! ’tis time poor Poesie were dead! Since every graceless witling thus is grown Familiar with her garter as his own! Since ’tis proclaimed, for all the world to know it, That Portmacquarie had its special Poet! (8.)

‘Tis time indeed! indignant and ashamed, Henceforth I will forswear I! I exclaimed. To be but named with such I do not choose; Even Jenner Merritt has his blue-winged Muse! (9.)

Forswear her not, my Guide resumed; this haste Of feeling is not wise. Because that paste Is made to mock the diamond’s blaze, yet sure The gem when genuine’s not a whit less pure. Small Wits, by Nature but for lackeys meant, When literary, must be pestilent! Forswear such Poets as I show thee now, Whom Poesie must scorn as much as thou. (10.)

Still yonder Wight is something in his way— The worthiest Singer in the choir of R–e. He might have been perhaps a Man of worth, If Bachus had not “stole him at his birth.” Even yet, the things of Mind he holds as dear, Or nearly, as the smaller things of Beer. And when for a nice Critic he would pass, Having kept sober for the nonce, he’ll class (His judgment smacking of his darling sin) Shelley as brandy; Moore and Byron, gin; Keats as prime port:—then, drouthy with delight, He’ll off to Tipple street, and there, in spite Of all the devils (black and blue) who scour His haunts for “copy”—every Tavern o’er— Be lost to Reading (11) for a week or more! And thence or thither, even now, he’s reeling, O’ercome with treble X—and too much feeling

But who now at his heels comes following hard? Arcades ambo! ’tis another Bard! And in the same disguise; but every gesture Proclaims him—the self-glorified S–l–v–t–r! The vaguest slanderer of the Nine is he That e’er mistook mad prose for poetry; Or hunger’s gripe, unknowing where to dine, 367 For the afflatus of the art divine. Not even the jangle of Saint Mary’s chimes Is more incongruous than his tag-rag rhymes! In which, unerring Shakspear’s moonlight sleeps Not on a bank—but o’er the restless deeps Of Ocean; and the deathless stars, since “wave” Must chime of course to something, watch the “grave.” (12)

I interposed, amused—No ship at sea, Under a gale, might pitch more desperately! How wild his looks too! He is charged I ween, With something besides wine of Hippocrene. Had we not best retire a space? I fear Another lurch will cast him on us here!

But making I then rhyme aloud to dry, He took an offing tack, and staggered by.

Whilst yet I looked with piteous regard Upon the sad sub-editorial Bard, (A woeful sight he was, so dull and glum, And deadly pale, through poetry and rum!) All suddenly assailed my ears a din Of thronging feet, and I saw huddling in A motley gatherum from the needy tribes Of briefless Barristers and shirtless Scribes. Mean hearted things! the natural tools of Wrong! So came they, huddling furtively along, As urged by one behind, who, whip in hand, On straddled with a look of fat command, And upstart vulgar pride, as he should say— On—on, ye dogs! I have you all in pay! What if the H–d should be late to press?

Meet driver for the driven, you may guess, Said Truth, half smiling. How the fellow brays! You knew him in his timber-weaving (13) days? But as no strength of breeches might be made To hold his buttocks at that straining trade, He took to deviltry (14) with patriot Hall, And learned to edit as he learned to scrawl: 369 And passing soon that craft did he attain, Which makes the broad-sheet a mere rag of Gain: Yea, fattening on such clap-trap year by year, He launched at length into a wider sphere; Where still his fattening—ever fattening thus, Even to the Typoes seemed miraculous! And ’tis a wondrous Fellow in his line: For he, as by some instinct, can devine Who in such odour with the Bar may be, (When he would make a show of honesty) As, though with Libel’s doubly-venomed dart He wound them, they, still unredressed, must smart: Can nose a puff along a mile of street, When wrapping a bank-token—as ’tis meet; By the same process too, as deftly can Dog up the party-cause, or party-man, That has ten pounds to spare, when his paid sages Shall butter it, or him, in their pure pages.

But O, continued She, her glances bright Suddenly strengthening with indignant light! How long shall thus the godlike Press be made The very cess-pool of dishonest Trade, And shameless, faithless Party; Party void Of common countryship—the Clan of Boyd? How long shall it be made the means whereby The vulgarest miscreants lift themselves on high? O when shall such prime Intellects arise, Australia, like the planets in thy skies— Such sons of Light as may true kindred claim With Milton in the Family of Fame— Arise, and missioned for the dearest deed Whereof thy unborn myriads shall read, Purge thoroughly, with Herculéan stress, The Augean Stable of thy Public Press?

Now let the trumpet of Pretension proud Bray welcome! Be its blast, like thunder, loud! For El–n enters—lo, with portly tread; Green in his very grizzliness of head! Hard dining Luxury, like an o’ergrown Grace, 371 Reigns in the bacchic splendors of his face! And but for this, the gullish world might think As he would wish,—that his peculiar drink Was wine of Hippocrene, poetic ink! Mark how he turns, with patronising pout, To look for D–nc–n whom he left without, (Not brought within, a Demon of the place Had clapt the portal in his honest face) Having just told that modest Follower, he A second Beckford most desired to be; (15.) And asked him—”In how near his taste and state Had approached his whom he would imitate?” Which question Truth shall answer—In so near As a blown bladder to a perfect sphere Approaches! Beckford thou dost imitate In nothing, El–w–n, that made Beckford great! His sire-like love of Genius and its things— His charity, that fanned with healing wings The fevered brow of meritorious Want— And that high scorn of literary cant, Which from the squabbling Living shut him fast Amid the nobler Spirits of the past;— All these thou wantest—but thou hast instead, His luxury of heart; his pride of head; His niceness; his exclusiveness;—yea, all Of Vanity that made even Beckford small! Thou imitate, forsooth, his taste and state? What! by just showing that thou canst translate? Hoary pretender! Mammon’s lettered slave! Thy Metastasio scorns thee in his grave! (16.)

But hark! what hubbub now is this that comes Straight for the portal? Oaths and threats, like lomes Exploding, tell that in full force and feather March the Squatocracy, with their bell-wether! Even thus, insane of hope, the Prince of Evil Marshalled his luckless followers—to the Devil!

First come the Magnates—mark their Leader, he, The would-be Tell of the Fraternity! His state is that, so infamously sad, When Talent hath through selfishness run mad. In his well-masked displays of by-gone years, With democratic wrath he tore the ears Of Sydney’s wealthy groundlings, being then Thwarted and snubbed by Darling’s party-men! But now behold him in his native hue, The bullying, bellowing champion of the Few! A Patriot?—he who hath nor sense nor heed Of public ends beyond his own mere need! Whose Country’s ruin, to his public fear, Means only this—the loss of Windermere! And by the same self-legislative rule, Australia’s growth the growth of W–tw–th’s wool! Her rights—her liberties, for number one A universal ten-pound right of Run! A Patriot?—he from whose statistic care All that his Country’s general homes should bear Of mind and happiness, is thrust by that Which by some process may be turned to fat, And, duly barrelled and exported, then Return in wine for grazier gentlemen!

Such is yon Man! and not a whit belied! A Patriot? Let him “doff that lion’s hide”! Well may these Landsharks call him their prime Gem, For bound thus to himself, he’s bound to them. (17.)

And who, said I, is he that pace for pace, Strains at his side with emulative face?

A rival champion, Truth replied; of shame Equally reckless, and his ends the same. A Scribe whose hackneyed head, and callous heart, Can play, indifferently, a counter part: Who through all human principles can run, Unfixing any, and regarding none, Save such indeed, as for the time may guide The prurient instincts of his legal pride. 375 [Who] for his turn, uncaring which, would hoist The standard of the Bible or the Zoist! And thus accomplished, now would sell his soul To be the first name on his Faction’s roll. (18.)

The House of Commons Envoy follows next, And somewhat doggedly: perhaps he’s vext Even with himself, for having bared his mind More freely than (when sober) he designed, At the New England Dinner. From that inquiet Faction turning then, I saw in pour another heap of men, With glowing looks, all holiday and smiles, New shod, new coated, and with bran new tiles. But now when all the sons of Shame and Sin, That I had need to see, were come within, I turned to Truth, enquiringly, and said— Tis strange, but of the many I saw tread This perilous Hall, I know not whither one Went finally;—and outlet seems there none! For still, by the new comers, was my view Turned from the passers of that various Crew: And now where are they all? That shalt thou see, Replied my bright Companion—follow me.

Straightway I followed, and She led me then Up that enormous Hall, to where a den Scooped in the structure of the further wall, At last conducted to an outlet small, Through which she bad me look.—Alas, how drear, I cried, and shrank like an embodied Fear! Sure ’tis the region of some final death Which spreads beyond, devoid of light and breath; Yet thronged with painful Shades, ‘mongst which I see Pale Likenesses of all whom erewhile we Saw enter at the Gate of Infamy! Drifting like straws—hopelessly drifting out! Ah, yes! K–p’s width prevents the willing doubt!

Doubt nothing there, said Truth;—thou lookst on thus The final Limbo of the Infamous: When run their earthly race, forever there Their Shades thus float in limitless despair! 382a Then turning from that dolorous prospect, she Back drew me, woe-struck so much woe to see! Till, by the virtue of her touch, a ray Of grace renewed me—when she said, Away! Lest breathing longer the unholy air Of this bad place, thy mental health impair. All hast thou seen, the vilest of their kind, ‘Gainst whom the lightning that shall strike them blind Thou hast to launch. Be resolute. For know, As well might roses upon thistles grow, As those of Truth and Beauty ’mid their hate And envy, flourish uncontaminate! Nor fear thou Villainy in high estate, Even though the unthinking world esteem it great: The noblest cast of mind is that which can But venerate the dignity of Man; And not the mere puffed dignities of men Which oft, in fact, are but a social wen. (26.) This sword of Scorn, unsparingly to wield, I give thee;—sweep them from the public field! To such thou must be cruel, to be kind To thy wronged Country’s morals, means, and mind.

She ceased: and hastily we then withdrew: “To morrow to fresh fields and pastures new.”

(24) Since this was written, the pretensions of the squatters, in the hands of some of our Legislators, have assumed even a more frightful complexion than at first. It would actually appear that they consider themselves as in permanent possession of their Squattages! Even the Herald, reviewing the debate in Council on the Land Question, goes so far in affirmation of this, as to ask—Who would purchase these Squattages, seeing they would have to fight for their possession afterwards? Good God! are we then (the colonists generally) and our children, to be thus robbed of whole provinces by a few adventurers, who occupied them in the first instance for mere purposes of private gain? Is the mere going into the bush, under motives no nobler than such as point to the bettering an individual fortune, a merit of such magnitude, that it should be rewarded with an infant princi- -pality? and this too, to the detriment of unborn millions? The scribes of the Herald will not look at the question in this light; their feeling being—very evidently—that it matters little how these immense tracts are held, nor by whom, so long as plenty of wool is grown upon them—such political earthworms always identifying social progression with the vulgar “go-a-head” principle. But let them not be trusted. Even in Australia, and at present, we are going a head too fast; and in the particular of large landed estates especially. Immense inequalities of this kind are the arch curses of the Old World. A splendid is never a happy land. Wherever there are palaces there are hovels. It should be our wisdom then in Australia to have as few of either as possible Wherever mansions are numerous, and gentlemen (in the useless sense of the term) as “plenty as blackberries”, there also, poor houses abound and paupers starve in heaps. Such universally is the condition of the old world: and our present and future legislators should perpetually propound to themselves the question—Whether the same national mockery—patrimonial pomp on the one hand, and hereditary destitution on the other—is to be extended into this portion of the new. By keeping this constantly at heart, they will assuredly grow up to something better. I say above, this portion of the new world, because America, having fallen away from her first love, is but a second edition of European polity, with the royal frontispiece blusteringly expunged. A land of mere worldly impulses—of mere go a head selfishness— is unworthy of the graves of a Washington, a Henry, and a Channing!
