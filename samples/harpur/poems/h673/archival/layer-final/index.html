WENTWORTH’S CONSTITUTIONAL MYTH

[I]NTERPRETED BY A PARODY ON THE LINES QUOTED BY HIMSELF IN THE LEGISLATIVE COUNCIL FROM HIS OWN PRIZE POEM.

BY CHARLES HARPUR.

And O, Britannia, shouldst thou cease to have One born Obstructionist one titled Knave; Should ere thy Treasury only Truth employ, Nor longer hire brute Hessians to destroy; Should e’re arrive that bright auspicious hour When thou shalt abdicate all wrongful power, And not in seeming, but in substance free, To God, as monarch, only bend the knee; May this thy last born daughter then arise A barbarous Britain under other skies, And Australasia spread, with flag unfurled, All thy worst features through a wider world.

Note.—This parody of mine will not only serve to illustrate certain political differences, but it will show at the same time how far preferable a straightforward style is, even in poetry, to a viciously inverted one. Mr. Wentworth’s lines are given below: and it will be seen that the constructural salvation ef some of them depends in toto upon the unmerited grace of a crucified comma. See, for instance, the third line. And besides, when tried by the true poetical logic, at least half the passage is pure, stilted non- sense[.] The figures of Britannia’s attributes are worked togetherby no sufficient rule, and hence the result is one of the queerest things imaginable—a never before painted or paintable monster! a ship, a lion, and a kneeling biped, done up ugly into a stranger fish than Caliban in the Tempest. Mr. Wentworth never had in him even the promise of a right masterly intellect. No doubt there is in the mental being of the man a certain clumsy, cart- horse sort of power—and hence his influence over the intellectual pigmies of our present Council[.] But that is all. The only things in the old fellow that I myself admire are his nerve and wild-fire, or what he himself calls his “lava.” He is a true fiery Son of the South so far. Otherwise, however, I do not value him as a coun- tryman (leaving his political profligacy out of sight) at a fig of colonial tobacco.

His lines are these:

“And O, Britannia, shouldst thou cease to ride, Despotic Empress o’er old ocean’s tide; Should thy tamed lion, spent in former might, No longer rage, the terror of the fight; Should e’er arrive that dark disastrous hour When bowed by luxury, thou yield’st to power; When thou, no longer free’st of the free, To some proud victor bend’st the suppliant knee; May this, thy last born daughter, then arise Another Britain under other skies, And Australasia float, with flag unfurled, A new Britannia in another world.”

When I was a very little boy I used to think the above very sublime, and that its author was not only a great patriot, but a great poet as well. But since then a great change has passed over both him and me. He has descended as it were into hell, and I— have not followed him at all events.

5th Sept., 1853.

C. H.
