No sleep! no rest!—’Tis now the dead of night, Sightless and solemn. One continuous cloud Of thickest texture bloats from east to west, 92 Like some huge breathing miscreation, charged With slumbrous life—a fatal potency Ready to burst in thunder o’er the world! The Moon is in the ocean. Not a star Studs the vast cope of heaven—for that black Cloud Hangs from it now, in circuit, like a pall Funereal: its innumerable host Of sometime shining mysteries seeming all Quencht, and apparently as beingless, As do those countless myriads of mankind, Whose living souls adown the tract of time Looked glowing, or from caravan or camp, And thronging caravans, and mustering camps, Or from the towery cities of the earth, Long centuries ago. Full Silence broods With universal night-black wings outspread, Over the sleeping city—broods unruffled, Save while some steeple clock with sullen stroke Records the midnight hour. 93 Looked glowing,—clustered over populous plains, And thronged in caravans, and crowding camps, And the grey towery cities of the earth,— Long centuries ago. Full Silence broods With universal night-black wings outspread, Over the sleeping city—broods unruffled; Save while some steeple clock with sullen stroke Records the midnight hour.

Now is the time Supposed, when from the dreary vaults of Death Pale spectres rise, and striding on the blast, Lift in deep fitful moans and breathless shrieks Their fatal voices. Now’s the time too, when Those living ghosts, the broken hearted,—pale And desolate on the neglected couch Where sleep comes seldom, make their desperate plaint Secure from casual ears; and while they list The ghostly din without, feel o’er them steal A wild desire to join the viewless troop— 94 To be that shadowy incorporeal Thing The happy fear.

The night is drear indeed! But not to me—at least it is devoid Of moment, such as once it might have held, When the mute slumbrous dome of Darkness gave A meet though solemn shelter to a soul Drows’d with sweet thought; for then I could rejoice In the return of light: but now the chords Of glad or strange surprise on which the change Of days and seasons struck, within that soul Lie broken all, and voiceless. Yea, to me What hath this night of dreariness or dread? To-morrow, let the sun shine ne’er so bright, The birds sing ne’er so gayly, and the flowers Bathe gladlier in the dews, and breathe abroad Unusual sweetness; let the freshest winds And greenest woods that ever tangled sounds 95 Into all measures, mingle near; and like Star drops suspending, let the daintiest notes E’er flung by Eölus into the lap Of Silence, hover over,—seeming well Supernal anthems filtering from the skies;— And let the Spirit of the seasons too, On every sunny hill and valley, write In characters less mystic than his wont The promise of abundance; yet, Tomorrow Will be as drear to me!

For I have felt Out of the light and dark hours of the world An Influence pass—a Presence that was wont To robe me with a shadowy trust in days And nights to come; and ’mid the wants and cares Of this unyielding present, used to bring From out those future depths a smile wherewith To freshen this pale cheek,—a dream of love To still the ineffable yearnings of this heart Love-withering in its loneness! But ’tis gone 96a Forever! Could I fail even now to feel,— Now, through the medium of the thin dry air— The suffocating emptiness of each gust That hits my aching brow as I look out Into the dark of this so desolate night, Its absence? Or in the instinctive dread To meet the morrow in the stern cold face Of the unfriendly world, which even now Presses around me like a tangible gloom, A deadly proclamation fail to read Of its departure from the pathless wild Of all my future life? And all in vain In the swift bark of introverted thought, Far up the gleaming reaches of my soul I voyage—inland, where the mountains rise Of mystic wonder, holding springs that flow From the Eternal; for my Faith is dead, And buried in the Past. 154 From the white snows of the Eternal Past, Voiceless and cold;—no comfort cometh thence, For Faith, as smitten through my mortal need, Is starving too, and the keen quietitude And cold-bright countenance of the Inane, But pinches her the more.

Why should I live? Is not my portion servitude and rags? My doom, to slink about this excellent world Unknown, uncheered, untrusted and unloved? Whilst others, oft the vilest of her sons, Entrenched behind hereditary state And more hereditary impudence, Play the God o’er me!—yea, even while they stink 97 Within the nostrils of all temperate men, Worse than a pauper’s corpse of their abused And swoll’n mortality! while to the Angles And Eons of the spiritual world, The evil stench of their vice-sweltered lives Must reach beyond the moon!

But these extort My scorn, no more! and I, perhaps, by living, Could somewhat in my generation do To beat their impositions into dust, Or in the fiery crucible of mind Inflamed beyond all mitigable art, Resolve them into air!

But ah! even they Who bear my name, as bound me to me by ties Of kindred,—do I unto these impart My bolder thoughts, or my ideal draughts Of Man’s true magnitude; or some great hope Of what the wronged World yet may be, when roused By Truth, as by a trumpet, she shall start 98 Even as a wakened lion from her trance Of ages, shaking her Oppressions all Like hornets from her mane! and all that now Is darkly sacred held—the pious fraud That blinds with terror the bewildered minds Of millions, till they lick the chains of thought– Denouncing Superstition; chains at first Forged from old rhapsodies by her craftiest scribes The vulgar to enthrall, and strengthened since By the subjected to subject in turn— All these shall laugh at, as the mischievous Hallucinations of her babyhood, And give them to the Past:–but when to these I thus unfold myself even these the while They seem to admire, half shudder; and then turn Looks of such strange conjecture hard upon me, Or of trust clouded with such dubious heed As fastens on my brain a wilder sense Of mental loneliness!

They too,—they 100 At whose suggestion first, I strictly gave My fortunes to the thankless Muse, and whom The more to please, I struck with bolder hand Into the mightier meanings of the Lyre, Condemn its tones as far too thundrous now For their nice ears; the spirit it would start As all too stormy wing’d for their calm minds To follow in its flight! And there are some Who sought me when I cared not to be found And lack flattered forth my friendship—but to fall Coldly away—how soon! And why was this? I wanted but their friendship, not their aid! Nor ever did aught vile that they should plead Dishonor, in excuse—and yet they shun me, Or meet me with a frost upon their looks That freezes to my bones!

O be it told From thy unfathomable darkness, Night, Why were these burthens, huge as mountains, piled 101 Horribly up, and crushed thus on my soul? And ye, mysterious voices of the blast, Why doth the ministry of Fate thus turn All sympathy away?

Oft have I seen A flower, as forewarned by the stilly touch Of Nature, fold against the coming blast Charged with the vapours of a blighting change, Its tender leaves; and can it be that thus Some instinct, or some fatal prescience, shuts Men’s hearts ‘gainst mine—open to all but it? Some feeling that within me there resides A direful influence on their household ties, Their worldly welfare, or those high-sown hopes That burn as fixt stars in the heaven of God? Could it be worse, even did my presence give A death-damp flavor to the ambient air? Funereal accents such as wail abroad From haunted caverns, to the passing breeze?—

102

Alas! these thoughts are drenching all my soul With madness—yea, the madness of despair! And though my reason, lifting up its strength, As desperately confronts them, just as well Might he—the lonely shipwreckt mariner, Who dripping stands on some wild sea rock, then preach Obedience to the breakes surging round That perilous point, as I (in this wild gloom) Strive to subdue them—and why should I strive? No: rather let them come and howl like wolves Within my failing brain! and knaw, and tug At my sick heart, their bitter food! for they Will help me sooner to my one desire, Which,—having nought to live for,—is to Die!

No dread prevents that wish! For I have made This many a day the bone-built door of Death 103 In prospect so familiar, that I feel Stung by a consequent necessity, To turn the key on all its mysteries, Despite of all its terror!

And have I not The means at hand? What interposes?—Ah! ‘Twas madness!—but ’tis past! And now, whilst yet Life’s painful breath remains me, I will sing— Here, on this desolate death bed, will sing My proper Dirge, if but to shaddow forth The weary mystery that hath slain me thus, And give my being, through its heat, to slip More easy down the icy steep of Death!

Yea, to pass At once into that shadowy aisle which leads M To the great dome of the Eternal! Time And Sense, the clogs of this corporeal frame, Could fool me then no more!—

I was weary, I was weary Of the day and of the night; They had swept to darkness and to death Each vision of delight: The day was but a charnel grown Where Love and Fancy shrouded lay; In the still night my soul had heard Young Hope arise and flee away.

I was weary, I was weary Of the songster and the grove; Of the rising and the setting sun, And the moon’s wild look of love: The sunny stream—the wild bird’s song— The breezy voice of the moonlit tree, But swell’d me haunting stave of Fate,— “The wide world hath no place for thee!”

I was weary, I was weary Ev’n of the gust of woe; Of each cold decency of life— Of every friend and foe: My body less alone shall be Among the white worms of the tomb, Than my wild spirit ever was Even in my very childhood’s home.

O so weary! O so weary Of all beneath the sun! Weary, weary, weary! But My race of life is run. O for a grave some brook beside, Or under a weeping willow tree: O for a grave on some wild cliff That hangeth o’er the sounding sea.
