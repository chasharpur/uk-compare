ORIGINAL POETRY.

FROM GENIUS LOST, A PSYCHOLOGICAL POEM, FOUNDED ON THE TEXT OF WORDSWORTH:

“I thought of Chatterton, the marvellous Boy, The sleepless Soul that perished in his pride.”

BY CHARLES HARPUR.

[...] I was weary—I was weary Of the day and of the night; They had swept to darkness and to death Each vision of delight: The day was grown a channel, where Love, Faith, and Fancy shrouded lay— In the still night my soul had heard Young Hope arise and flee away!

I was weary of all around me, And weary of all above; Of the rising and the setting sun, And the moon’s lone look of love: The wild bird’s morn and even song— The breezy voice of the moon-lit tree, But swelled one haunting stave of Fate, Mourning thus in my spirit’s ear early and late: ‘What matter how lovely—how wide it may be, No place hath the World of the Present for thee!

I was weary of thought, and weary of joy, And weary even of woe; Of each cold decency of life— Of every friend and foe! Much less my body alone shall be Amongst the white worms of the tomb, Than my wild spirit was ever and aye— Ever and aye, by night and by day, Even in my happier childhood’s home!

Oh, so weary—Oh, so weary Of all beneath the sun! Yea, weary yet—albeit I know My race of life is run. Oh, for a grave in the bend of a brook, Or under a weeping willow-tree! Oh, for a grave on some lonely cliff That hangs sheer over the passing skiff, And throbs to the wide-moaning beat of the sea!
