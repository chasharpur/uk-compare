The World’s Opinion.

As a down feather by the wind Is this way whisked, and that way hurled; So is the man that trims his mind To suit the judgment of the world.

Who heedeth much the world’s opinion, Will be as much its facile minion.

Note.—No one but a fool or a designer, could set great store by the current good opinion of the world. Considered otherwise than as a conduit to something beyond itself, it is morally valueless, for it is always more or less in error. If the wise do not despise, they at least always distrust it. They know how vain and hollow a thing it is: how worthlessly it is often acquired, and how lightly lost. They know, moreover, with what little merit it may be regained, however ignomi- niously it had been before withdrawn, to be again lost, perhaps, because of a great deal of merit. So hollow a thing is the good opinion of the world: and it is capricious as it is hollow. If it can find no other fault with Aristides, it will revolt at his inevitable justice. Nay, it is an absolute fact, that one man may enjoy it on account of something, which, considered in its essence, or in its ultimate effect, is exactly that thing which has cost it to another. Hence the man of great designs, to fulfil them, must hold it cheap, or altogether forget that it is babbling round about him. The good man, to maintain his integrity. must habitually throw his conscience into the scale against it; and if he do this, he too in the end will hold it in no great esteem. The artist may form it if he can: but in doing so, he must ever bear in mind the Fable of the old man and his ass If he endeavour to please all the world, he will succeed in pleasing no part of it, and lose his art into the bargain. The poet, if he regard it at all, it must be only in the spirit of satire; for it is in his destiny to be thought righ either a fool or a madman for a long time by the world, and even when it is led no longer to think of him thus dis- paragingly, it will be just as far as ever from understanding him aright. On the other hand, he who would always retain it, and is able to do so, may have little vice, but he can have no virtue. Neither can he have any distinctive character, good or bad,—a thing it can bear with only on the stage; for in real life, all self strength is too apt to jostle and override it, to be in any measure tolerable to the instinct of its own inherent invalidity. This at least is the case, until character actually does override it, and then, whether it be good or bad, it can hold it at its mercy for a time, and it may be mould it to its object And why is all this so? It is so, because the world is envious of all high individual merit, and loves to countenance mediocrity; thus applying, both upwards and downwards, its own universal standard. And farther, it is so, because our moods of the moment, our social furors, and not our settled beliefs and rational conclusions, are the usual upgetters of that whirligig of opinion which is called the favor of the world.
