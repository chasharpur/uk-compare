XXXV.

The Wrongs of Poland.☞ ☞

Since every drop of wrong-shed blood that cries Out of the lapse of ages, at the throne Of God is heard, for! Since the lies Of fraudulent Power are mortal, and the groan Of patriot grief smites not as against stone At the world’s heart, though hard; and since the sighs Of exileld love roofed only with the skies, Must weary Mercy with perpetual moan: And since all Rights, though earth-born, doth belong To Heaven as well, bowed Poland yet shall cast That thraldom off which she hath borne so long; Calling retributive thunders from the Past, To urge her cause, and swell the trumpet-blast Blown forth by Freedom, trampling prostrate Wrong!x

47d

x Of the spirit of this Sonnet, as it applies generally to the sacredness of the rights and pieties of nations and of races, I still wholly approve. But my later readings have much altered my sentiments as to the national worth of the Poles, & their so called wrongs as a distinct People. And, as it appears to me now, their absurd Constitution itself with its single Nie pozwalam veto, and other such stultifications, led more than anything else to the final Partition of their territories between Russia Austraia and Prussia:—led to it, I say, and even made it a sort of political necessity.
