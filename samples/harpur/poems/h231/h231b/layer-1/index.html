Charles Harpur’s Wild Bee of Australia, A Series of Poems, With Prose Notes.

The Lost Voice.

Out of the day and night A joy has taken flight.

Shelley.

A voice forever sweet and low, Like the fluting of a brook; Richly familiar, Like a loved song given to flow From some loved book; Day by day was at my side Amid the hills where I was born, And if it went at eventide, It came again with morn; And ’twas the last that blest me, when I parted from my native glen.

A wand’rer long,—again I hear Just the streams that then I heard; Wildly familiar, Youth’s glad dreams seem warbling near 474 In each glad bird: Yet the more I feel alone That round me these known things rejoice, Because I miss in every tone The once according Voice, Which I must listen for in vain In all the weary world again!

Note. There is nothing sweeter to me in the world than the voices of those I love. This, of course, is the case more or less with all persons; but in all cases less so, I think, than with myself: that is, the voice in particular of any beloved party is not, I think, so especially grateful to them. And in connexion with this peculiar tendency, there is a painful mystery at my heart with regard to the remembered voices of the dead, and of those unapproach -ably separated from me—and of the lost. They haunt my sense of the past in a manner I cannot explain to any purpose, perhaps. I listen for them, involuntarily, with a pang like that which results from the partial consciousness of our state when lapsing into delirium. Or I seem to hear and yet miss them. And the feelings which struggle from under the burthen of this mystery, are ever and anon (as above) pining for expression in my poetry.
