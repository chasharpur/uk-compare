The Emigrant’s Vision.

As our bark dashed away o’er the night shrouded deep, And out tow’rds the South I was gazing, O’er my soul first there gathered a dimness like sleep, Then the light of a Vision amazing. As rises the moon, from the white waves afar Came a Goddess, it seemed, of love wisdom and war, And on her bright helmet, encircling a star, Behold there was graven—Australia.

Her robes were as green as the mantle of Spring, Newly spread by the streams that so mildly Elapse through yon flock-legioned plains, or that sing 471 ‘Mid those blue-ranging mountains so wildly; Her locks were as bright as the lustre that lies At morn on the seas of the South, and her eyes Were as deep in their joy as the clear sunny skies— The clear sunny skies of Australia.

O Stranger, she asked, hast thou fled from the home Which thy fathers have bled for so vainly? Or does shame for its past thus disgust thee to roam? Or dispair of his future constrain thee? In the far sunny South there’s a refuge from wrong, Where the Shiloh of Freedom, expected so long, To Genius already has shouted his song— ‘Tis the evergreen Land of Australia!

There Truth her abode on the forest-clad hills Shall establish, a dweller for ever; And Plenty rejoice by the thence-flowing rills Aye wedded to honest endeavour: Till a numberless People the future shall see, All equally noble as equally free,— And the God they adore their sole monarch shall be! Then come; build thy Home in Australia.

This said tow’rds the South she passed brightly away, And at once as from slumber I started: But the cadences sweet of its welcoming lay Yet breathed of the Vision departed. And when o’er the deep these had fadingly spread, The swell of my heart, as it throbbed to my head, Seemed to break into words on my tongue—and I said Be the home of my hope then, Australia.

Note. I speak in the Text of plenty in this country being ever wedded or weddable to honest endeavour. This is true enough. Any industrious man, if he be sober and healthy as well, can better his material circumstances in Australia: that is, supposing them bad at starting, and comparatively so at other periods, 472 given progressively. And yet, in the present state of its society, as of all society perhaps, so mortal hard is it to get together the first,—the nest egg of our fortunes, that the heart, however good at first, is ruined in the process. In making this nest egg,—that is, the first hundred, or thousand pounds, we have so to toil, and pinch, and screw, and lie, and haggle, that all which was primatively genial in our social being,— the “milk of human kindness”,—is utterly strained out of us. Our hearts have passed, as it were, through a cheese-press; not to be thereby bettered, as cheese is, but to be thoroughly hardened, like stones. Hence the worldly inexorableness of all our elderly men; their cold, cruel, Sat... Satanic want of faith in everything but money. But it will be their mortal doom to find, each and all of them, in the end—that money is not every thing. It can buy much indeed;—but it cannot buy enduring honor; nor truth (for an unrighteous end;) nor a good conscience; nor love in its first integrity, glowing and unselfish as a wingèd thing from heaven; nor more than the semblance of friendship,—not the substance; nor intellectual power; nor even a fine capacity for the enjoyment of mere bodily pleasure. And thus then we see, that every thing of supremes value,—even now, even here,— is unpurchaseable by money.
