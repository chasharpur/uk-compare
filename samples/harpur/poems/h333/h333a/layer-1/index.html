A Nice Child.

Give me a Child that is yielding though shy, With a half wilful half innocent eye, And with nothing at all in its speech of the looby; With flaxen hair, A full forehead and fair, And a round red mouth, like the moist-looking ruby I once saw,—cut into a double-lobed button; With strangers mild, But with friends rather wild, Yet trusting of heart and easily put on; And lastly, as fond of one’s kisses almost, As of lollies, or peaches, or butter and toast.

Note. “I bless God”, says the charmingly maternal Mary Hewitt, “that he has so stored the world with flowers and little children.” And may God bless her (and he will) for the saying, which is as sweet as her own heart ”suffer little children to come unto me, and forbid them not”, said the divine Jesus, “for of such is the kingdom of heaven.” And I can verily believe it. For myself, I have had more than enough of men, and almost enough even of women; but with a little boy or girl at my side or in my arms, the world still, for the time, seems fresh and loveable; life has yet a laughing spirit of gladness, and a green sha spot, upon its rough road side; and even full grown, fate-hackneyed human nature (even my own), as it then appears to me, has gathered something back into itself of the “original brightness”, and transpa- rency of the angel. Yet there is often, upon reflexion, a touch of ominous sadness in the very joy that “little children” give me: for a few years hence, and they into needy and grasping men and women – even such, perhaps, as I myself am in daily competition with, and who are as constantly stinging or shaming me into a state of feeling with regard to them, which were way-like, but for this child-lovingness,—very like misan- thropy.

The touch in the Text about the double lobed button may appear at first sight a “simile unlike”. But let the recur to it a moment, and try to bethink him of anything more thoroughly resemblant to a healthy child’s mouth, when held up and put into order for a kiss, than a full red button grooved horizontally across the centre. I, at least, cannot fancy anything more so.
