Personal and other Sonnets.

On the Death of a Boy.

“Whom the Gods love die young”, the ancients said, And in the saying there’s a subtle truth. Sorrow we for the living—not the dead; And age may envy those who die in youth. For what is Life?—at best a bright deceit, Of which the happiest the soonest tire; Where all that’s sweetest is as frail as sweet, And even our hearts before ourselves expire. And knowing well this evilness of Life, Ah, who could wisely wish alive again The Child that, dying, hath escaped its strife, And even its pleasure so commixed with pain? Then weep no more, ye Mourners! You deplore A virtual mercy;—Mourners, weep no more!
