Sonnets.

The Sonnet is perhaps the best metrical form yet invented for the terse embodiment of any truth which is spherical by reason of its comprehen- -siveness, or which is great enough to be simple. Nor do I know of any better form for the musical setting forth of any striking, yet individual, idea, or fact, or love fancy. It is also eminently suitable as a vehicle for literary compliment or biographical incident; and when inspirited with the influence of some memorable event, it may indeed, as in the hands of Milton, become “a trumpet,” thence the medium and blowing “soul animating strains.” upon the startled attention of the world. x

235a X 2nd. Par: But apart from its subject, there must in the mere construction and vehicular capacity of the Sonnet, be always exhibited a manifest mastery over materials. This is an indispensible requirement, admitting of no incompleteness either of thought or expression,—no slip- slop,—no inversion purely for rhyme’s sake,—not even one superfluous word. The subject must be so handled as to make its earnestness granted; and its earnestness must be such, either absolutely or apparently, as to make directness of treatment a requirement inevitable. A perfect Sonnet will be thus as it were a microcosm,—whole in itself, both in form and spirit: the spherical mould and integument of a spherical subject,—the one exactly enclosing the other without either diffusion or over compression, and shutting it up in “measureful content.” 236

Moreover, the untransgressible limits of the Sonnet have often the effect of superinducing that closeness of expression and that sublimation of image which are proper to the highest kind of enduring poetry—namely, that kind which is suggestive rather than descriptive of things, and which, while it amply recompenses the imagination of the reader, exercises it as well, and thereby strengthens it for conception upon its own account. And having as I think this tendency, of course these exact limits prevent in an equal degree, that verbal dilution of the sense, which is the besetting weakness of most modern writers, both of prose and verse. Not but a well-phrased amplification of a thought may be sometimes advisable. If novel, or very comprehensive, it may even demand a double draught of expression: but to flood it with words, however well chosen or elegant in themselves, is not to clarify, but simply to drown it. Nay, though in itself as vivid and nimble as a sunbeam, it may be dulled and deadened, like an ill-mixed potation, by a few irrelevant or superfluous words.

Ordinary readers object to the Sonnet on account of its shortness and individuality. It is not long enough, nor many-featured enough, to produce or minister to popular excitement. With such novel-pampered readers, it is only a poetical “nobler”—an apolo[g]y for a “dram”. It has not grip enough for their coarse mental cravings—and apropos of this:— A country Englishman was partaking of some fruit with the members of a family of my acquaintance, which consisted of two sorts—some nectarines of exquisite flavor, but singularly small, and a quantity of large slip-stone peaches of so bad a quality as to be almost tasteless. But after demolishing two or three of the nectarines at a mouthful, the countryman was observed to give them up with a look of some disappointment, and devote himself exclusively to the peaches. He was asked the reason—for, said his questioner, the nectarines are excellent but the peaches scarcely eatable. “Ess,” replied the clown, “the nectons be main good, loike— but then there be no grip on ‘em”. Quantity rather than quality was evidently the “ticket” for him. And the literary taste of those 238 readers who cannot relish the Sonnet merely because it is short and one-thoughted, is every whit as rustical—not to say vulgar—as was this fellow’s bodily appetite, all their pretensions to refinement notwithstanding.

In most of the ensuing Sonnets, the arrangement of the rhymes is somewhat different from the run of them in that form which has been adopted from the Italian poets by many of the best writers of the Sonnet in English. But in devising this different arrangement of the rhymes, I have been actuated by no mere desire to innovate upon a usage so imposingly established. In the pure Italian form, the reduplication of the rhymes is upon the whole, as it appears to me, not only too distant but too couplet-like in the irrecurrence, to tell thoroughly well; and to follow out this form so far as to rhyme theninth and fourteenth lines (as is often done by Wordsworth) is tantamount in English to not rhyming them at all. It is perceived to be a rhyme only by the eye— 239 and by the eye itself only upon trying back. On the other hand, the old English or Shakspearian mode of con- -structing the Sonnet, is obviously defective in oneness, both as to artistic appearance and musical effect; it being indeed, but a series of three separate quatrains based upon a closing couplet. Hence the rhyme-arrangement in this form is manifestly an unsuitable one. It is not sufficiently carried through, inter-recurrently, to impress either the ear or the eye with a feeling of wholeness and homogeneity, however one-thoughted and individual in itself (when critically after-considered) may be the subject of the poem. Now the form in which the ensuing Sonnets are for the most part written, partakes somewhat of the manner of both these exemplars; and after trying it carefully by my own ear, I do verily believe that it is a better compacted one than either, and altogether fitter for the English Sonnet—or rather for the Sonnet in English.

X On the Sonnet.

Why slight the Sonnet? Though one-thoughted, it May show us,—as by the minutest star The world’s bright order is evinced most far,— Through how much truth may pierce, with mastery fit, 240 One subtle, searching beam of mother-wit Shot from a Poet’s regal brain! Besides the value of no Thing on earth Is in its bulk—but, purely in its grain: As man’s small body doth more soul engirth Than doth behemoth’s, big as it may be; Or as the tiniest pearl’s of greater worth Than any boulder washed up by the sea. Wherefore I say, to slight a thing of mind (So long as it be happy in its kind) Because, by verbal steps, to climb it— Or, with watch in hand, to time it, May not ask at least an hour, Is just as tasteless as it were if we Should look with scorn upon a lovely flower Because ’twas not a Tree.
