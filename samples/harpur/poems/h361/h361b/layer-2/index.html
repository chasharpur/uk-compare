The Sonnet.

Why scorn the Sonnet? Though one-thoughted, it May show us,—as by the minutest star The world’s bright order is evinced most far,— Through how much truth may pierce and flit One subtle beam of wit Shot from a Poet’s regal brain! Besides the value of no Thing on earth Is in its bulk—but, purely in its grain: A man’s small body doth more soul engirth Than doth behemoth’s, big as it may be; And so the tiniest gem’s of greater worth Than any boulder washed up by the sea. x Wherefore, to slight a thing of mind (So it be happy in its kind) Because to climb it, Or to time it, May not take at least an hour, Is just as tasteless, as it were if we Should look with scorn upon a lovely flower Because it happend not to be tree.
