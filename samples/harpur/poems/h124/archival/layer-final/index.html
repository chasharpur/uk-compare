SONGS, EPIGRAMS, NOTES, AND OPINIONS, &c., BY CHARLES HARPUR.

No. 18.

I.

“Every True Fact is a Law.”

To say that every Fact’s a law Is just about as true, in fact, As that some very madman sees, By virtue of his mere disease, A reason for his maddest act.

Note.—I take the sentence which heads this epigram from a lecture by Doctor Woolley; and as the sense of it pplies to the constitution of physical nature, it is no oubt true. It is a true fact that flowing water will eek its level, and an exact statement of the fact itself, ill also comprehend the true mode of the law by which it does so. But amongst the free agencies of the moral world the assertion will no longer hold its own as an axiom, and yet the Doctor has italicised and put it as though it were of universal application. So far, however, from every true fact that occurs in the moral world being a law, it is often either a violation of law, or an evidence of its absence. Truth—right—good—is the only source of law; but then Error—wrong—evil—also exists; and the recorded facts of both are equally true as facts. But are they equally laws also? To hold that they are, is, in effect, to hold them of one essential value: and to reason from them, as being equally essentials, would be to divide the moral world between Good and Evil, as between two supremacies, and thereby nullify its righteousness—in so far at least as mere logic could do so. No doubt one violation of right may beget another, but one is not therefore the inevitably reciprocal law of the other[.] Both are but passing abuses, that may never recur, and the known truth of them, as facts, records and signifies no more. For the simple reason, that they are not laws as well as facts, they may fail to recur, whereas it is the immutable nature of a moral law to be of everlasting effect, and to involve or necessitate its own conditions. They may fail to recur, because Evil has not, like Good, an essential and indestructible root in all being, but is merely a factitious, sin-engendered prevalence, which, though it be forever coming into the world, is also forever dying out of it. But even to admit that they would recur under precisely the same conditions, is not to compromise the argument against their being true laws as well as facts, unless it can be shown that these con- ditions themselves lie whole, and by a reserved necessity, in the pre-order of Nature.

And now let us apply the import of the assertion to some specific moral delinquency. It is a true fact that men have committed murder; and if this fact be indeed a law as well, they will always continue to be murderers upon occasion. In other words, there is in the moral nature of man a law of murder, inasmuch as it is a true fact that men have heretofore committed that offence. But the very terms of such a statement are a sufficient confutation of it.

Again: Suppose a hungry man to have stolen an exposed loaf, and eaten it. Now, if the fact of this theft is also a law, the same man in the same condition would always be ready to do the same thing. But it is not so. The fact of his having eaten the loaf, because he was hungry, would always recur, because the conditions which make it a law are inevitable in his constitution. But the fact of his having stolen it, because he was hungry, is not a true law, because the conditions of the mere fact of the theft are not inevitable, nor even perdurable, in his moral nature, and may have been annulled by his subsequent experiences, habits of thought, and by a thousand other purely moral influences. And hence the self-same man, under precisely the same physical temptation to commit the offence, would yet die of starvation, it may be, rather than repeat it.

Again: (to treat the matter humourously) a wilful, incompetent lapidary may pronounce a pebble to be a diamond. The fact of his doing so may be true enough, may be past dispute; but wherein is it a law? The pebble, notwithstanding, is as far as ever from being a diamond; and here we have one true fact exploding the law of another true fact. The one, because it comprises a law, explodes the other, because it does not. Nor would the wrong judgment which makes the first of these facts, arise out of a law of wilfulness and incom- petency, but merely out of their perversity, their lawless- ness; for another lapidary, every whit as headstrong and ignorant, but not so whimsical, as the first, shall yet declare the same stone to be—what it really is—a worthless pebble.

Again: It may be a true fact that Mr. Martinet Moralman was drunk on the occasion of the Queen’s Birthday. But this fact may be the expression of no law in the said Mr. Moralman’s mental nature, having, for its conditions, a certain pressure of excitement in the presence of a certain quantity of come-atable strong waters; for that gentleman may never have been a bacchanal before, and may never be again. Nevertheless, the fact of his drunkenness on that auspicious occasion, was indeed the expression of a physical law, having, for its conditions, too much wine or French brandy, with a concoctive organ, like a stomach, to work in.

The sum of the matter is this: In the natural world, every true (established) fact comprises a law that is of everlasting effect under like conditions. But in the moral world, every true (authentic) fact is not also a law, because it may only indicate a tendency, which may be so traversed by other tendencies, as to annul the possibility of a recurrence of those conditions that were sufficient to make it a fact, but not to make it a law. Or the conclusion may be stated thus: Good or righteous- ness is, by God, ordained law, and therefore subsists fundamentally in the nature of things—the periodical rough workings of which are not properly evil, because the sure end of them is either restoration or advance- ment. But moral evil or wrongfulness arises purely out of perversions or violations of this law of righteousness, by the free action of moral agents. It is therefore by sufferance, not by ordinance: and having no funda- mental hold upon the substances of the universe, it must forever be lapsing from those conditions, through which it may nevertheless present itself as an historical fact.
