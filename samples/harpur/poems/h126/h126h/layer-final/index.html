Poetry.

TWO SONGS. (BY CHARLES HARPUR).

I.

FALLING AWAY.

The wintry wind is numbing, When blowing keen and high, It drives the dull and drizzling clouds Across the sunless sky; But ah! how much more chilling Is it when, on Friendship’s death, Some old acquaintance greets us—with A coldness in his breath.

It is cheerless—very cheerless To miss some well known way, Because the damp and darksome night Hath quenched the golden day; But ah! yet more disheart’ning Is it when, we know not why, Some old acquaintance fronts us—with A strangeness in his eye.

It is not that we’re losers Of aught the sordid prize; And scorn breeds scorn, and memory, wronged, Will teach us to despise: But oh! the waste of heart-faith, When to Friendship’s cold disgrace, Some old acquaintance meets us—with A frost upon his face
