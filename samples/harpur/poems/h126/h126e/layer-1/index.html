XX.

Fallen Away.

The wintry wind is numbing, When blowing keen and high, It hunts the dull and drizzling clouds Across the rayless sky; But ah! how much more chilling Is it when, on Friendship’s death, Some old acquaintance greets us With a coldness in his breath.

It is cheerless—very cheerless, To miss some well-known dome, 83 Because the golden light is quenched In night-shades rising black; But ah! yet more disheart’ning Is it when, we know not why, Some old acquaintance fronts us With a hard ignoring eye.

It is not that we’re losers Of aught the sordid prize; And scorn breeds scorn, and Memory, wronged, Will teach us to despise: But oh! the waste of heart-faith, When to Friendship’s cold disgrace, Some old acquaintance meets us With a frost upon his face.

Still no pleadings, no reproaches, When a Friend thus falls away; But let him go—to Jerricho! And keep thy own proud way: Then, though his failure darken O’er thy spirit’s trust for long, Every honest pang it costs thee Shall but tend to make thee strong.
