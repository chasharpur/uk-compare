A Storm in the Mountains.

A lonely Boy, far venturing from his home, Out on the half-wild Herd’s dim track I roam: A lonely, Truant, numbering years eleven, ‘Mid rock-browed mountains heaping up to heaven! 100a Here huge-piled ledges, ribbing outward, stare Down into haggard chasms; onward there, The vast-backed ridges are all rent in jags, Or hunched with cones, or pinnacled with crags! A rude peculiar world, the Prospect lies Bounded in circuit by the bending skies.

Now at some stone-tank (scooped out by the shocks Of rain-floods plunging from the upper rocks), Whose liquid disc in its undimpled rest, Glows Like a mighty gem brooching the mountain’s breast, I drink, and muse,—or mark the wide-spread herd, Or list the tinkling of the dingle-bird; And now tow’rds some wild-hanging shade I stray, To shun the bright oppression of the day: For round each crag, and o’er each bosky swell, The fierce refracted heat flares visible— Lambently restless, like the dazzling hem Of some else-viewless veil held trembling over them.

A change is felt—a change that yet reveals A somewhat only that mere instinct feels. Why congregate the swallows in the air, And northward then in rapid flight repair? At once Unsettled, and all roaming slow With heads declined, why do the oxen low? With sudden swelling din, remote, yet harsh, Why roar the bull-frogs in the tea-tree marsh? Why cease the locustsx to throng up in flight And clap their gay wings in the fervent light? Why climb they, bodingly demure, instead, 101 The tallest spear-grass to the bending head? Instinctly along the sultry sky, I turn a listless yet enquiring eye, And mark that now, with a most gradual pace, A solemn Trance spreads creaming o’er its face— Slow, but inevitable—past all doubt, On all hands from the South effusing out: Yon clouds that late were laboring past the sun, Reached by its sure arrest, one after one, Come to a heavy halt;—as travellers see In the wide wilderness of Araby, Some pilgrim horde at even, band by band, Halting amid the grey interminable sand. Thence down descending, its dull slumbrous weight Sullenly settles on the mountains’ great Upheaving heads, until the airs that played About their rugged temples—all are laid: While drawing nearer far off heights appear, As in a dream’s weird prospect—strangely near! Till into wood resolves their robe of blue, And the grey crags come bluffly into view. —Such are the signs and tokens that presage A Summer Hurricane’s forthcoming rage.

What in this country we call the grasshopper is no doubt the “locust” of the Bible—the locust that John the Baptist made his dinner of in the Wilderness. The Blacks often eat them, as a food; and even the native dog not only affects, as a dainty but seems to thrive upon them. In the right season, and in places where they happen to be plentiful, he may sometimes be seen jumping after them into the air, and snapping them up, for hours at a stretch. On the other hand, what we call “locusts,” of all kinds, are but various species of the cicada.—And while on this note, I may as well mention, that just before a rain-storm, the locusts or grasshoppers inhabiting such flats & basins as are liable to be suddenly flooded first, will be all seen mounting to the tassells of the reeds and high grasses—to be out of the reach of the water—should the matter come to the worst.
