The Losses of the Past.

I.

The world is emptied of that mystical gladness Which, dwelling round my childhood, o’er it came In every murmurous game Played by the winds!—and ever in my sadness I look out over its unfriendly face In vain for some fresh place, Which should to Memory seem fit habitation For that new light of love wherewith afar My soul rayed, like a star Beaming sweet influence from its heavenly station— Rayed tow’rds all beauty, through the boundless joy Of the then happy Boy.

II.

‘Tis gone too—that frank trust is gone for ever, Wherewith I afterwards essayed the world, And first the flag unfurled Of my own individual endeavour! Youth’s unworn bravery I strive in vain To gather back again, And know, however briefly, all the feeling That went then from my heart, as some wild rill Leaps from its native hill! But ah! instead, a sense of loss comes pealing Out of the sunless Past—like a long wail Heard in the midnight gale! A sense of vain regrets, and hopes yet vainer, And rich affections wasted upon such As could not prize them much! He only of young life’s an absolute gainer Who carries with him its unperished bloom Into an early tomb.

III.

And yet, though coming to this sick conclusion, My weary soul, methinks, derives some gain Of strength even from the pain 230 Attendant upon Memory’s transfusion Over a life so lossful;—strength to know The moral end of woe: That measurement of suffering to a tittle, Which makes us in our central self assured, As to its brunt inured; To feel besides, the future can bring little That was not typed in what time brought before— And to expect no more.
