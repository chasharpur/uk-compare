The Sorrows.

I.

[GENIUS LOST. THE SORROWS PART FOUR: LOOKING BEYOND]

(make this the third)

Yes, it is well amid the rigid, cold Realities of the world, to steal an hour For meditations unconfined to this Dusk point, the Earth;—to die as ‘twere awhile In body, and with all the powers of mind Thus freed, to bridge with mighty beams of thought The spirit’s perilous essays to span The depths of the Eternal. Even on me Such mood sometimes descends, the precious gift Of pitying Urania, wherewithal 14 To wing me for a free far flight beyond The scope of natural things;—to range at large, Perhaps, in middle Paradises, where The once earth-treading votaries of Truth Reside in spirit, till the promissed end Of Time shall open Heaven. But by these I mean not such as with sour faces boast The negative goodness of our purists,—those Blind moles of Fear, who think they honor God, By offering up on outraged human wills, As upon blood stained altars, every gay And genial sentiment, and rosy wish That sweetens human hearts. And who, convened In their dull tabernacles, all at once Behowl the Deity as dogs bay the moon, Or deprecate his wrath with grovelling rites, And boisterous groans, that from stentorian lungs Come forth in moist explosions through the nose, Even as a herd of swine, with upturned snouts, Snort at the coming gale! Oh, no: for such 15a The Paradise of Fools full wide extends Her dismal gates. ≠

I speak not thus in scorn For Scorn’s indulgence, or detractively. The vulgar satirist I abhor! The mean Detractor, with his wintry-hearted mate, The sneering, misbelieving miscreant, who Hath not of generosity enough To say a good word even of himself! These are, of all the reptile things that crawl Loathsome and poisonous ‘twixt earth and heaven, What least from impulse and the bent of thought I would be neighbour to. But when the rights Of Man are trampled on; when villains sit In the high places of the land, and scoff At all the just hold sacred; when mere pelf, No matter how acquired, alone can win The ‘well done’ of its Nestors, and the sleek Regard even of the saints; and when Religion 115 Itself, is ever in a bad extreme,— A bloated pomp, or coarse insanity Vile as a beggar’s raiment;—then the scorn Of indignation;—then the brave disgust Of righteous shame and honest hate, put forth In tones that burst abroad like God’s own thunder, Are things most worthy:—scoundrels never feel them.

15b

Enough of that! The good Are they, I deem, who leaving vain disputes On things unknown, to empirics, attend To those which God hath given them eyes to seen Strewn o’er their daily being; and of such Rightfully choosing, and to fitting ends Well shaping all, upbuild with honest pride The fabric of a useful life. Nor thus Employed alone; but in the frequent jar Of national factions, they as well, despite Of frowning kings and banning priests, afford Unflinching aid to Freedom: who, though oft Dungeoned by tyrants, aimeth not the less To write in starry characters on all Exalted hearts, the sacred text by Heaven First given to her dreams, as in the shades Embowering Eden, she reclined,—That all The goods of this fair Earth—fair, although now 16 Pale in her beauty, and weighed down with bonds, And haggard with dismay!—were from the first Designed by God to be dispensed alike Freely to all her children.

Such are they Rightly the virtuous called: and such as oft In spirit I encounter, ranging through Far regions of the Blest: Elysian Fields Pavilioned o’er with domes and temples vast Of diamond, and each lustrous stone, high arched, On shafts of living gold. And fast by these Nectarious rivers flow, now lapsing bright Amid palm-clouded hills, whose sunny green Gives them to glow like giant emeralds, Then lost or checkered seen in thickest shade Of trees the growth of Heaven, transplanted there; Thence breaking forth in steep cascades, they lace With shining tracery smooth slopes and meads And far extending campaigns, paven o’er With flowers more rosy than the rose, more white 17 Than whiteness, purpler than the purple of morn! And various more in form than are Earth’s sands; Of shape diverse, while all their odours breathe Of their celestial origin: —all these, Temples and domes, and rivers, hills and plains, And flowery shades, reflecting ‘neath a cope Of uncreated, intermittless, yet Undazzling light.

O ye immaculate Scenes, Second alone to those of Heaven! ’tis good Your denizens in vision thus to see, Or bathing in your streams, as round they pour Immeasurable delight; or gladly grouped In shades embellished with immortal fruit; Or wreathing their resplendent Shapes with flowers On hill and plain; or gathered in your domes— Your lustrous domes! in frequence full, to use Some love-completing rite, and feast on cates Ambrosial, shed from Heaven; and which, the while, Suspending o’er like a golden hail, 18 Obey the instant wish. High food! which still, As eaten, founds a growing gift to know, Not only the stupendous mysteries Within and bordering on the starry round Of this once unintelligible World, But those which lie, swathed in eternal night, Beyond it, to the walls of Heaven itself. And thus they reap an ever ripening sense Of still unfolding being,—till dieted To the full measure of their state, they smile In all the consciousness of God’s regard, And their own immortality!

And now My dreaming eyes behold them, risen all, In mazy measures moving to the tones Of heaven-wrought harps! or my intense regards, Turned from the general multitude, embrace Companies uttering joy with claspëd hands, While thronging round some new arrived, in whom Is recognised a father or a son, 19 A mother, daughter, sister, brother, friend, Or One more dear—dear as their blessëd lot! And whom the wild winds of Vicissitude Roughening the ever fluctuating deeps Of Fortune into thunder roaring waves, Even from the earliest passages of this Their mortal pilgrimage, and thence throughout, Had beaten from the haven of their hope In weeping Love’s despite! But lo, they meet Never to sunder more.

Here having long Expatiated,—finally I grow Intoxicate with Beauty; and thence stung With thirst of farther quest, even as a steed Rushing to battle at the trumpet’s voice, I catch a bolder daring from the loud Thick throbs of my own heart, to soar away— Away beyond all bound!—through starless voids, And vague eternities of imagination, Peopled with Terrors, dim and shadowy Shapes 20 That blindly drift along their destined way, Forever onward!—but to burst at length Into a world-west avenue of light Outpouring from the glorious gates of Heaven, And even there find entrance! Yea, I tread, All mortal as I am,—in vision tread The many-mansioned vestibules that lead To God’s palatial rest, a marvellous dome, Sun-rooft, and sacred to Himself: and then Encouraged by a prevalence of love Ineffably benign, I institute Free converse,—free, but grave and elevate, With Essences and Natures that have been From an eternity, and shall remain Through all eternity uncircumscribed By Pain, Fear, Doubt and Death, the tyrants fell Of this material ball!

But who might long Such converse entertain? O’erwrought at length And thought-bewildered, I perforce resign 21 The Vision—though, to find myself again Alone on the hard earth! where, when I would Rejoice in lore so high, there’s none to share That visionary pride: and I must feel Even like some Crusoe, who with pains hath reared Fast by the confluence of bright-winding brooks That babble of their pleasant intercourse, His Island Bower, but who, reclined in it, As neath a bloomy heaven, can yet, alas! Grieve only that so fair a shelter then Is unpartaken—is but known to one.

Yet will there come a day,—though not to me, When excellence of being shall be sought Not only thus in vision, but within The actual round of this diurnal world;— A day whose light shall chase the clouds that viel Upon the mountain tops of old repute, The imaginary gods of wrongful power, And pierce thence downward to the vales of toil, Healing and blessing all men: The great day Of absolute Freedom,—for truth must keep pace With Knowledge,—when the accident of birth, Or emptier imposition of mere pelf, Shall neither arrogate to any man Distinctions as of right, nor qualify Any, by a sole influence, for power Over his fellows; but all men shall stand Up proudly, under the wide roof of heaven, As God-created equals,—each the sire 156 Of his own worth, and the joint sanctioner Of all political pertainment, all Moral and social honor.

Yes: for such Is Freedom’s Charter, graven on the heart Of capable humanity when rid Of the foul scroff of Time, and on the heart Built godlike, when disclouded by God’s light Of a too old distemper’s fatal rout Of arrogant, hell-suggested Superstitions, And Customs born of Error.
