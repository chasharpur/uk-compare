III.

Prose Poetry.

On edgeless, tuneless lines, how sadly Ringing rhymes may wasted be; And blank verse, oft, is prose that madly Wishes to be Poetry: While Prose, that’s craggy as a mountain, May hold the well-spring of a fountain, Bright as that of Castaly.

Note.—A ready method of trying whether rhyme be indeed poetry, or not, is to turn it into prose. This may be done by getting rid of the rhymes, by substituting their synonyms, or by supplying all words understood or implied. And if, on being passed through this process, it appear thoroughly dispirited, it could never have been poetry proper; and if it retain no golden cadence—no profluence of expression—no bar of invulnerable melody, it could never have been true verse. Very little of our recent English poetry, wil[l] bear this test, both ways. Should it still retain enough of soul to vindicate its ideality (and it will rarely do this); then, as verse, in nine cases out of ten, it will be utterly condemnable. In the matter of versification, especially, our living poets, and even their critics, with but few exceptions, have assuredly adventured upon a voyage to the dogs. A glib prosaic wash of words, is mistaken for metrical ease; ruggedness, for strength; uncouthness, for nerve; and utter lawlessness, for something even better than the sweet olden violence, that always had method in its madness, and poise in its wildest indulgence; and, lastly, the absence of all congruity of rhythm for a very perfection of it. That, at all events, cannot be a masterly well-compacted rhythm, the prosodical identity of which, hangs and oscillates, as it were, between good and bad reading, and which is often missed, even in the best, or lies wholly beyond its redemption. In fine, no rhythm can be good, much less perfect, which does not, of itself, carry out the voice of the reader, and set it swimming, clogless, and buoyant, as upon a flowing river of melody; or which has not, when wilfully wild, some concealed artistic restriction, underlying and counterpoising its very wilfulness: as in the following example from Cole- ridge:-

“The òne rèd lèaf, thĕ lást ŏf ĭts clàn, That dàncĕs ăs òftĕn, ăs dànce, ĭt càn, Hànging sŏ lìght, and hàngĭng sŏ hìgh, On thĕ tòpmŏst twìg thăt loŏks ùp ăt thĕ sky.
