33

The Glen of the Whiteman’s Grave.

1.

I had well-nigh fared through a summer day O’er rugged mountains that round me lay Far as a wonder-keen eye could strain Range upon range,—like a dusky main Of monster breakers flowing high And wide along the shoreless sky. But some upconed, as holding sway O’er all that round them nearest lay In tribe-like groupes,—upconed away And alone in their greatness, like old-world Kings; High as soar the eagle’s wings— High as the fleecy clouds that play In the open face of a sunny day.

2.

Such had been my course—and when The day was well-nigh ended, Into a deep and gloomy Glen The mountain track descended: 34 A gloomy Glen that far was seen Winding hugely on between Two craggy walls that showed the trace Of many a tempest’s wrath— Hugely on, like a time-hewn path For the giant Pan of so wild a place.

3.

And wondrous was the backward view Thence seen from where the gorge came through. Huddled together and up-piled, There was all that’s rudely wild; Nature’s most enormous freaks, Hunching cones and snouted peaks Ruggeder than e’er were dreamed, And towering till the blue sky seemed Torn into fragments by the jags Of the giant shapen crags.

4.

In this melancholy Glen Far my feet had fared not, when In the pathway I might see Right against me as I came, 35 Carved deep in the bark of a grand old tree A fatal-fortuned name!

5.

The name of a murdered man! and I Many a time by the bush-fire side, Had heard his story with a sigh, And a boding look o’er the dim descried And savager haunts that, haply, lay Where the wild prospect, cloud enskied, Dropped as it were away. Wherefore, I turned me from the road With a sympathetic load At heart, and with a rueful face, To find his Grave in that lonesome place.

6.

That evening! never knew I one More solemn browed. The great round sun O’er the lit summits crowded vast Against the West, was sinking fast. A few grey cloud-shreds only were Seen in the vault of heaven to stir, As cobwebs, dim and torn, might wave From the marble dome of a mighty cave. 36 A gust at fitful intervals Fell over the nearer mountain walls, And then with a sound like the motion of snakes Rustled away through the rounding brakes, Or lingered, as it were to pine In the twilight top of the turpentine Its own low dirge—a dying tone In the turpentine’s dense top alone. And once a dusky eagle came In solemn travel o’er the same— Steadily straight, as from afar Came over,—but the airy jar Of his wide wings in swift decrease Soon faded, as with upward sweep He passed beyond the opponent steep— And all around was perfect peace: Yea, so perfect as to be Even frightful, through extremity!

7.

And now the sought-for Grave I found. Against me stretched, its lonely mound Was darkly seen, and by degrees, In the broken shade of the silent trees; Till, on a sudden, a last glance rayedFrom 37 From the setting sun for a moment played O’er it, and then backward ran, As Hope’s last flickering gleam might fade From the face of a doomëd man.

8.

Only one tuft of grass did wave Above that sterile desert Grave Bestrewn with leaves and withered sprays, And only one blue flower did gaze Timidly up, as near I trod, From ‘twixt the dry lips of a broken clod. Oh, ’twas a desolate, dreary thing, That Grave in the else-green lap of Spring— In the else-unbroached primeval sod! As I looked on it then through a starting tear, And the feeling—Alas! for the Sleeper here, In a Grave like that first in the world—, O God! That first bleak scar in the Earth’s new mould That stretched over Abel in Eden of old, Ere Spring’s healing, after many a year, Had flowered it with purple and gold! I would not—I would not one human childOf 38 Of the earth should be laid in the lonely cold Of a Grave so wild!

9.

Thus deep—sea-deep, was the pity I felt For the fate of the lonely dead; Till moved, as it seemed, by a spirit of dread That gathered around in the gloom, Down in that lonesome place I knelt By the side of the lowly tomb. And kneeling thus alone, I drew His Image on that evil day When there first coming on the view Of Felons bent to slay. A visionary Youth, methought, On he came all wonder-wrought With the dreary grandeur mass’d Aloft in shapes so wild and vast, And which his memory oft compared, For contrast, with the rural joy Of scenes in England where he had fared The Oft’nest, when a happy Boy— Scenes glad with loving voices then! And not like those in this drear Glen 39 Which now his wandering vision saw! All silent, or but fitfully Bestartled, when some loud macaw Might send abroad his doleful cry: A sound that waileth through the air Like an utterance of Despair, Or of Sorrow mourning high As she is wont in lonely places, Where but her own wild shade she chases, And no comforter is nigh.

10.

Thus his Image passed before My fancy—till it changed the scene, And through its geographic lore Dreaming, sailed the seas between The Antipodes—those world-wide seas Stretched out between the Antipodes! And onward thence—and onward, o’er The navy-laden waves that roar Along the Anglic coast, to seek For objects there whereon to wreak The burthen of its lonely ruth— A Sire of the same name, 40 With her who bare that doomëd Youth, A sorrow-pallid Dame.

11.

Both I pictured old and grey, And bent with mourning many a day— Mourning hopelessly for one Their age had cherished, and in whom Even through the sacred tie of son, They’d seen again their beings bloom! But saw not, as they blest him then, A lonely Grave in a gloomy Glen, Thought I, recurring to its gloom;— Even this dusk glen—and this lone mound With savage mountains rampired round! Here! where the new-cleft clods between, The nameless flower of a nameless scene So meekly rears its lonesome head! Here! where a kindred creature’s tread Comes but to wake a haggard cry Of wonder in the wild dog’s lair, The Living’s sole abode; and where The Black even, seldom ventures nigh, Nor dares to halt: but striding fast, 41 And, calling them close, points out to his boys What suddenly hushes their thoughtless noise— The Whiteman’s Grave! then hurries past!

12.

And thinking thus of him whose sleep After his morning taste of time, In that close mountain nook did keep Dread memories, even there, of Crime! And eke of those who even afar, who then Might mourn that Sleeper of the Glen, A brother’s sorrow o’er me crept, And seeing I might vainly try To keep mine eyelids longer dry, I groveled by the Grave, and wept: Yea, wept as we are glad to weep When some great load is on the brain, As given by Nature so to keep The saddest thinking sane, And sweet for future use, I trow, When we shall understand at length, What beauty of soul, and grace to know, And wisely sympathetic strength, Was born to us of Woe: Wept, until clouding up the Glen The dark came, following the grey Withdrawing skirts of parted day— 42 And deepened was my spirit when I sped at last away.

13.

Go Sympathy, best mate of Grief, To all who mourn him waft relief: For thou hast much to give, dear elf, To others in extremity, Even when thou hast for thy own self Little—or nothing it may be! Thy sweet sad smiles some lightness Can bring the weariest heart, And thy lustrous tears some brightness To the gloomiest soul impart: As wreaths of flowers are soothing Unto a dying child— As the moon’s beams come smoothing O’er some benighted wild.
