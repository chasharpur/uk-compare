VI.

Onesidedness.

There’s nothing straitens judgment more. Or aids discrimination less, Than that trait in our modern lore Which may be called Onesidedness.

Note.—”What parochial animals we are grown,” said a recent lecturer, and never was anything more scornfully true. If in the huff of our affected liberty we break out of one parish, it is only to imprison ourselves in another. If not wholly of Ballywhack, then we must be wholly of Ballycrack, and in opposing the one all out, we must be sure to side in the same extreme with the other. Thus even Ernest Jones, while decrying in some capital verses, what he calls “signs of glory” in the British army—namely, clasps and ribands and such like trumpery—goes on to say:

“No need to write ‘This man is brave,’ For where is the Briton is not?“

So onesidedly does he start away from one species of clap trap, that he himself falls up to the chin in another. In denouncing Ballywhackism, he must run head long into Ballycrackism. He has clearly nothing else for it. And this makes the absurdity of what he says but the more glaringly absurd: for to disprove what is in- sinuated, we have only to instance the storming of the Redan.
