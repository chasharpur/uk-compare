A Flight of Wild Ducks.

Far up the River, hark! ’tis the sharp boom, Deadened by distance, of some Fowler’s gun; And as into the silence of the scene The noise spreads flattening to like stillness, lo, Right westward, laterally lengthening up Out of the sinuous valley of the waters, And o’er the intervening forest,—up Against the open firmament, a long Dark line comes stretching—a vast Flight of Ducks! Following the windings of the valley, on, Enlarging rapidly, it comes,—until The River, reaching through a group of hills, Leads it, a short while, out of view—and then, Suddenly wheeling with its course, ’tis here! Sweepingly swarming rount the nearest point. And first, now, a swift airy rush is heard Approaching momently—then, all at once, There passes a keen-cutting gusty tumult Of strenuous pinions, with a streaming mass Of instantaneous skiey streaks,—each streak 70 Evolving in particular, and yet Each tangling into all (so rapidly A thousand wings outpointingly dispread In passing tiers, seem from beneath to involve Each other as they beat). Thus seen o’erhead Even while we speak—ere we have spoken, lo, The living cloud is onward many a rood, Tracking, as ‘twere, in the smooth lymph below The multifarious shadow of itself. Far coming—present—and far gone at once! The senses vainly struggle to retain In one impression so manifold an Image: For now again a dark line on the verge Of the horizon, steeping still, it sinks At length into the landscape; where, yet seen, Though dimly, with a long and scattering sweep It fetches eastward, and in column so Dapples along the steep face of the ridge There banking the turned River. Then it drops Below the trees on this side—but to rise Once more, with a quick circling gleam, as touched By the slant sunshine, and then to disappear 71 As instantaneously,—so settling down Upon the reedy bosom of the stream.

Note. The old and in many respects untenable objection, that whatever would not be beautiful in picture is unfit for poetry, was alleged against the foregoing effusion, by a friend of mind, a rather competent critic.

To this I replied, that I considered such an objection generally invalid, for the simple reason, that at least half of the poetical world—and not the least beautiful half of it, lay entirely beyond above th reach of painting: in short, that such the theory which furnished the objection was every whit as narrowly restrictive as mere materialism is with regard to the nature and destiny of spirit.

But confining the objection to descriptive poetry, I asserted, moreover, that though an object might not be strictly poetical in itself, yet that when taken in connexion with its 70 local concomitants, it might constitute the pivot of a subject that was decidedly so; remaining in itself, notwithstanding, altogether unsuitable for picture, either because of its volatile nature, unstationaryness, or what not.

Then waiving the fact as to whether a flight of ducks was in itself an object meet for pictures, I contended that, with its scene concomitants,—the wild and primative aspects of the prospect, the windings of the wood- -fringed river, as trackingly indicated by its rapid approaches; and then in itself, as a multitudinous apparition overhead, with its image, as mirrored in the broad stream below &c;—it became the leading circumstance—the pivot and centre of a subject that was decidedly poetical, and though too many-featured and eventful for pictorial embodiment, as decidedly picturesque upon the whole. And I yet flatter myself that I had the best of the argument.
