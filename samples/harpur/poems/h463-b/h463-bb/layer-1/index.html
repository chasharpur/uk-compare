Milton.

[His Minor Poems.]

As through some stream’s most christal purity, The virgin gold is seen below, we see Through the immortal Mask as palpably, That drossless chastity of heart and thought Which crowned the youthful Poet as he wrought; With all the visionary affluence That tempered the stern grandeur of his sense.

Then in his Carol for the Virgin’s Son, Toned like a trumpet for some victory won Amid the wondering Shepherds placed, we see Descending, Heaven’s high heraldry—and hark! While burning choirs, in star-like clusters, gem 58 The midnight gloom that shroudeth Bethlehem, How swelling up in utterance divine, Their tidings peal through each triumphant line! The theme is changed: wild lamentation loads The ear of Doom; for now the Infernal Gods From their pontific thrones suddenly hurled, Forego, despairing, the re-conquered world! And hellward all, in the tumultuous verse, Like far-off thunders heavily disperse.

With Melancholy then, how sweet to rove, Whilst the wild moon seems wandering lost above, ‘Mid clouds that like drear woods and mountains grey Blind up the track of her accustomed way; And hear in spirit our footstep’s solemn beat Just reach the echo of that still retreat, Whence streaming out, from Thought’s mysterious tower, The lamp’s long blade divides the midnight hour: Or thrown fancy on the shaded grass, To mourn the ocean-whelmëd Lycidas, Under the “opening eyelids” of the day, And till the sun “drops in the western bay.”

Then, for relief, let Fancy woo such dreams As to his youth were known, by haunted streams Sleeping,—till music, like the soul of Love, Came breathing from some Nymph-concealing grove;— Or joyously upstarting, she is free To dance o’er the green earth with glad Euphrosyne! Jest in her train with him who Care derides, And laugh with Laughter, “holding both his sides.” x

He seems to have had a fuller sense and to have enjoyed a more perfect possession of the imaginative presence of the persons and things of his subjects than any other of our poets; and hence it is that a single word in him, by the intense force of its ideal selectness, becomes so often the key to a completeness of effect that strikes like magic; as in the following instances

The floods stood still, like walls of brass, While the Hebr bands did pass, But full soon they did devour The tawny king with all his power.

Or as in this:

Tells how the drudging Goblin sweat To earn his cream-bowl duly set, When in one night, ere glimpse of morn, His shadowy flail had threshed the corn.

Or as in this:

I hear the far off Curfue sound, Over some wide-watered shore, Swinging slow with sullen roar.

Or this:

Let my lamp at midnight hour, Be seen in some high lonely tower

Or again in this:

The air was calm, and on the level brine Sleek Panope with all her sisters played.

Like instances might be given to the extent of a volume from his entire writings. And without this kind of excellence in a prime degree no port can be epical. Its frequency is the spell that can alone reattract us again and again within the sea-like and self-shrinking vastitude of a long heroic Poem. It is that which can alone give sufficient reproductive scope to profound criticism. Still, as in Milton, a chastity of judgment bordering on abstemiousness is essential to its judicious exercise.
