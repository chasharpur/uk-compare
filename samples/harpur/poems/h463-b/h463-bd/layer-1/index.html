His Minor Poems.

As through some virgin Stream’s pellucid flow The pebbled gold is seen to shine below, So see we through his Masque, as by it flows In lengthening music to its happy close, That native worth of feeling and of thought Which stored his youthful spirit as he wrought, With all the visionary affluence That tempered the stern grandeurs of his sense.

Then in his Carol for the Virgin’s Son— When, like a trumpet for some victory won, ‘Tis sounding,—with the wondering hinds, we see Round us descending Heaven’s bright heraldry! And while their Choirs, in star-like clusters, gem The midnight gloom enshrouding Bethlehem, Hark! swelling forth in utterance divine, How their glad tidings peal through each triumphant line! The theme is changed:—wild lamentation loads The ear of Doom! For lo, the Infernal Gods, Down from their shrines and thrones pontific hurled, Forego, disparing, the reconquered world; 287 And hellward all, through the tumultous verse, Like outward wasting thunders heavily disperse!

Now with Il Penseroso let us rove, While the wild moon seems wandering lost above, Like Through clouds that like drear woods and mountains grey Blind up the track of her accustomed way; And hear, in spirit, our footstep’s solemn beat Just reach the echo of that still retreat, Whence streaming forth from Thought’s mysterious tower, The lamp’s long list of light divides the midnight hour.

Or thrown in fancy on the shaded grass, Mourn we the ocean-whelmèd Lycidas, In the new glory of the morn, or while The sun, in setting, crowns some western isle, And, with her visioned train, “sleek Panopè” Figures the shining levels of the sea, And from the “guarded mount”, Thought sees afar The archangel’s glory, like a tower of fire! So shall the countenance of Nature borrow Such life and depth from our melodious sorrow, That ever thenceforth, even the loss we rue Shall make each field fresh, every pasture new.

Note.

Were I asked to give in the fewest words possible, the quality which I think most characterises the poetry of Milton, I should say, a certain picturesque and ideal suggestiveness. He seems to have had a fuller sense, and to have enjoyed a more perfect possession of the imaginative presence of the persons and things of his subjects, than any other of our great poets, with the exception perhaps of Spencer. And hence it is, that a single word in him, by the intense force of its ideal and picturesque selectness, or of its picturesque propriety, becomes so often the key as it were to an effect that strikes upon us like magic—revealing, by disclouding, a whole world of imagination, and alike unlocking 289 the golden gates of heaven or the grim and ghastly portals of hell. Take the following examples of this peculiar happiness.

The floods stood still, like walls of brass While the Hebrew bands did pass But full soon they did devour The tawny king with all his power.

How real and present does that complexional attribute of the Egyptian monarch make the whole picture?

Again:

Tells how the drudging Goblin sweat To earn his cream-bowl duly set, When in one night, ere glimpse of morn, His shadowy flail had threshed the corn.

Here the “shadowy flail” actually substantiates (so to speak) the spiritual operation of the Goblin. In other words, its vagueness is so appropriately in keeping with the nature of what is implied, that it gives to an Imagination somewhat of the body, and all the force of a fact. Again:

I hear the far off curfew sound 290 Over a wide watered shore, Swinging slow with sullen roar.

Here by the magical selectness of a single word, we have the curfew both afar off and near at the same moment of time. We hear it, with the Poet, sounding far away in the watery distance, and yet give the ringer a hand as well at the rope which swings it; tingling even as he does, with its immediate vibrations: and thus the imaginative reality of the thing, and of every associated circumstance, is most sufficingly authenticated.

Let my lamp at midnight hour Be seen in some high lonely tower

Not only do the italicised words in these lines identify the inside of the tower, as the abode of the lonely student but they also give a second sight, as it were, to our imagination, wherewith to define it objectively, as seen from without, and at a distance.

The air was calm, and on the level brine Sleek Panopè with all her sisters played.

291

What a key to the whole calm of the season is there in that one word level; and how the sleekness of Panopè harmonises with and intensifies the wateriness of every thing else.

Again: speaking of the breaking up of the Satanic council in the great hall of Pandemonium:

Their rising all at once, was as the sound Of thunder heard remote.

We are absolutely present, for the moment, at this council: for who has not been one of a large congregation under the sounding roof of a church, when all at once rising at the requirements of the service? or of a great seated meeting in some public hall when suddenly breaking up?

Like instances might be given, to the extent of a volume, from the whole body of his writings. And without this kind of excellence in a prime degree, no poetry can be epical. Its prevalence is that spell which can alone re-attract us, again and again, within the ocean-like and self-shrinking vastitude of a long heroic Poem. It is, moreover, that peculiar quality which affords the most ample scope for profound criticism. Still, as in Milton, a chastity of judgment bordering upon abstemeousness, is requisite to the judicious exercise of the faculty which originates it; for without a correspondency of reserve and tact, it may lead to mere pencilling, or degenerate, as it does sometimes in Shelley, into a laboriously vicious creativeness. The evoked idealism must be in nowise disrobed of either the misty dimness of distance, or the dusky veil of partial concealment, however largely it be made to loom upward into the 293 general subject of the picture. Or the suggestiveness of the effect may be resident (as in most of the foregoing examples) in the marvellous choiceness of a single word—and there it must be left entire. Add aught, however little, and all is instantly overcharged, or brought so far forward as to become familiar: our wonder is glutted, and consequently endamaged.

There is another remarkably spellable quality in Milton’s poetry—if it be another, for it is infused into it no doubt by the same ideal intensity: I mean the singular impressiveness of his poetical geography;—that is, of his imaginative mention of ancient and far off localities. When, for example, he speaks of Satan ranging over the orb of the earth,

“Up beyond the river Ob;”

or when he makes mention of

“Abana and Pharphar, lucid streams;”

not only do we seem to be carried into the presence of those lucid celebrities, but to become one in spirit with all the thousand generations that have lived and died upon their banks and drank of their waters. The localised being of the Past, indefinite of form, but 294 ideally granted, seems at once to overshadow the living verge of the Present, till both are commingled into a kindred consistence;—and thence, for the time, we live as it were in the breath of both—as having dwelt of old, and as still sojourning in the pilgrimage of our thoughts “up beyond the river Ob,” and by “Abana and Pharphar, lucid streams.”

In the midst, however, of all this superinduced dreaminess, we possess of course a pleasing consciousness of the remoteness of these places in absolute fact. Nevertheless, they become thenceforth gifted in our sense of them with a sort of twofold existence—a remotely ideal as well as a material and actual one: being incorporated into the map of physical relations, and blending, at the same time, like the dimly recurring scenes of a wanderer’s childhood, with the reverted prevalence of our souls.

Still, in prosaic fact, nothing more can result from the bare mention of places and things in Milton, than in any other author. But our ideal vision has been already strengthened and spell-touched by communion with the spirit of his poetry—and hence this crowning effect. We have become participants in his ideal intensity, and feel and see by virtue of a doubled poetic con- -sciousness,—his and our own, and the more strongly 295 and vividly the more these may have harmonised.

The minor poems which most exemplify the peculiarly sublimative character of Milton’s genius, are Lycidas and the Hymn on the Nativity. Every stanza in the last mentioned production is the ideal quintessence of some ivied legend, some twilight myth, or time-shadowed tradition—things that have hung so long in the sunset spaces of the Imagination, as to have become saturated, like evening clouds, with the last hues of its nature. Almost every line is a picture, hueing as it were the dim and lifeless regions of the recorded Past with the immortality of thought and passion, as these ray forth everlastingly from the poetic spirit of “one Milton,” as from some mighty star that shineth out of heaven, upon the grey desolation of a Babylon or a Tadmor.— Certainly, in no other poem in the language of equal length, is there anything like the same amount of prime poetry. Then on the mere score of versification how unique and vigorous it is—especially in the profluent sweep and volume of its alexandrines: as in the following examples.

I will add a few words with the style of Paradise Lost. Its prime characteristics are “gravity and ease.” The phraseology is as far removed from anything strained and mouthy as from aught that is bald and frivolous. Neither is there anything merely curious or startch in the general texture of the composition, nor tangled or patched in the evolution of the measures. Take for example the concluding lines of the Poem:

Some natural tears they dropt, but whiped them soon. The world was all before them, where to choose Their place of rest, and Providence their guide: They, hand in hand, with wandering steps and slow, Through Eden took their solitary way.

Thus ends the mightiest Epic in the world, with a quietitude, and easiness of manner, that might have dissatisfied a Poet less epical in his art, but which breathe of the very nature and august presence of the highest epical propriety.

The critical prate at one time so much in vogue about the falling off in the latter books of Paradise Lost, was captious in the extreme, and even silly. But you could not once mention its title, without being met with “Oh, but it falls off as you get far into it.” Now I who have read the poem many times can discern no such declension in any part of it. On the contrary, the treatment of the subject throughout, appears to me to be equally epical. Are not the seventh and eighth Books full of marvellous beauty, world-wide and various as that of Nature herself? while for sublimity of invention, for grandeur of illustration, and the wonderfully diversified modulations of the verse, the tenth Book is certainly quite equal, if not superior, to any that precedes it. Then observe that portion (perfect in every respect) of the eleventh Book, beginning with the 141st. line, and ending with the 250th; and subsequently, in the same Book, the whole of the vision-pictures, commencing with the 429th. line, and thence running all through it. No doubt the tone and sweep of the style varies in the progress of the Poem, agreeably to the differences of the matters dealt with; as the volume of some mighty river contracts and widens, and falls and winds, according to the nature of the country through which it 298 flows: but all this is exactly, not only as it should be, but as it must be. The variously flowing river is the material type, and thence the best critical illustration of the conduct of such a Poem.
