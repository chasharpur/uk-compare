4c To the Editor of the People’s Advocate. Sir,—A few slight persons ssem inclined to impugn what they are pleased to call the war-spirit evinced in certain late publications of mine. But they mistake me considerably; although I still hold to the opinion that the best consecration of liberty is the blood of the patriot. And I have many good reasons for thus adhering to this opinion. All that is supremely valuable and valued in English liberty has been purchased with the blood of Englishmen. The mountain tree of Switzerland’s freedom hath its roots struck deep in the red blood of her bravest children. How much also of all that is glowing in the American’s sense of his unequalled freedom, must arise from the noble memory of its having been died for by Americans? These are some of my reasons for holding fast to the opinion in question. To show, however, that I am disposed to love war for its own sake, or as a matter prolific of mere glory, as little as most men, I submit to you for publication the following essay on the subject, wich I find among my papers, snd remain

Yours, &c.,

CHARLES HARPUR.

MILITARY HEROES AND WAR.

I have little admiration, under any circumstances, for mere military heroes, or for the pride and pomp of what is called glorious war. What have truth and human good to do with the sword? What can it prove, if used offensively, but that men are sometimes worse than wild beasts? And what, even when employed in the defensive, but that murder, violence, wrong and robbery, are sometimes only to be repelled by their own inhuman means? What is there in mere animal bravery worthy of engaging our admiration? The bull-dog is brave. What is there in mere destructiveness worthy of commanding our emulation? The tiger is eminently destructive[.] Should we found our highest enthusiasm on a sense of physical activity and might? or upon displays of the thundrous energy of material forces? Hardly: for all this has its exact analogy in the constitution of the horse, that ‘smelleth the battle afar off,’ and fearfully glories in its dangers. Should we be greatly solicitous to emblazon the shield of our personal pride with ‘None may touch me with impunity, for I sting unto death? ’Hardly: for the very spirit of such a motto is in the mere nature of the serpent. But if indeed we must needs glorify our individualities, and through these our so-called nationalities, let it in the name of sanity be for such things as lift us above, not such as ally us to the 163b brutes:—for those characteristics which indicate our affinity with angels, and not those which sink us into kindred and community with reptiles. What is most marvellous with regard to war, is, that so few or rather none of all the millions who have hitherto traded in it, should have thus expostulated with themselves, whilst plying its devilish enginery to the wholesale destruction of their fellow- creatures: “Verily, if this our work be not Satanic, what is there in the whole universe worthy of damnation? Or rather, is there, can there be, even in the profound of hell itself, aught that may match with us for madness and impiety? a completer set of deadly and desperate devils!” 163d Then for the evils that follow War,—its inevitable train and progeny;—sacked cities and burning villages,—the deflowered virgin and dishonoured matron,—parents bereaved, and orphans in their helpless astonishment of heart—and all these weepng and wailing together around their desolated homes!—where are there words to characterize afflictions so wild & astounding? To be commensurated by art in all their moral enormity, they should be pictured in cloud and fire upon a back ground of perpetual storm.+ There are evils of a magnitude as horrible, that only a king might hold it or even think of with indifference,—only a fiend with complacency; and the uncrowned human nature that does not heartily execrate, at that would not be most sedulous to prevent them, must be surcharged throughout with the fiend of hell! 163c Still I would not be misunderstood. I esteem rational valour a prime public virtue. But to be rational and virtuous it must be acting under the God-stamped sanctions of Liberty and Right. Then it is a true thing espousing a true occasion. Then it is righteous and noble, for it is derived from the righteous suggestions and innate sovereignty of Nature: and they who would not im- plant or uphold their country’s liberties, at the cost, if necessary, of life itself, were unworthy of even that base existence for which these would be thereby unattained or forfeited. The sum of the whole matter is simply this: If in the national systems and governments of the world now obtaining, war is occasionally inevitable, yet woe, and dishonour, and execration be to all them by whom war cometh;—whether it come by aggression, or through an insane spirit of military rivalry, or as a public necessity, from the denial or withdrawal of rights, or the defacement of liberties. This, I say, is the whole sum of the matter.

C. H.

WELLINGTON.

Great captain if you will! great Duke! great Slave! Great minion of the crown!—but a great man He was not! He? the iron instrument Of mere authority! the athiest Of a conventional and most earthy duty! To whom the powers that be were simply not Of God—but in His stead! Shall we belie All righteous instinct and profane all truth, By calling great a man without a soul? One who, apart from the despotic wills Of crowned oppressors, knew no right, no wrong, No faith, no country, and no brotherhood? If such a man were great, may God most High Spare henceforth to our universal race All greatness, seeing it may sometimes be A rigid, kindless battlement of Power Self-throned and sanctioned only by the sword. And if, as Englishmen are proud to boast, He was their greatest countryman—alas! For England’s national sterility! But they who thus belaud him, lie, as all True patriots most feelingly perceive. Besides, he was not England’s son at all: He was an Irishman, to whom the name Of Ireland was a scoff! An Irishman, Who for a hireling’s meed and ministry, Could tear away from his inhuman heart The pleading image of his native land.

CHARLES HARPUR.
