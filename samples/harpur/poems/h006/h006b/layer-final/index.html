THE ADDER.

a solemn “lyrical ballad.”

By Charles Harpur.

An Adder there was in the hole of a tree Where many and many a summer lodged he, And often he’d bask in the sun for a spree, Till he grew just as sleek as an Adder should be.

By that tree with the hole in’t, a man full of care Sat one day, as a weary man sits,—anywhere; When the Adder glode forth just to see what was there— And bit the poor man on the breech, I declare!

O Life it is weary and burthened with dole, And danger revolves with our days as they roll! And wherever a Tree has a rot in its hole, There’s a Snake on the look-out for just such a hole!

Note.—The Moral of this poem is very profound and many- phased. In the first place it is designed to impress upon us the fact of our mundane insecurity at all times and in all places; and in the next, to advise us that we should be very careful as to where we deposit ourselves, whether upon a log in the bush, or in a back- slum taproom, or even in a City or Legislative Council; first examining each place well, its dangers and requirements, together with our own peculiar liabilities and natural or attained appro- priateness. Hence this part of its moral should be commended— nay, it should commend itself to the particular study of all incipient legislators, and a multitude besides of would-be magistrates, and Botany Bay lords, and knights of bunkum, with qualities better fitting them for bumbailiffs and night soil scavengers.

But the “deep thing” of the poem is shut up in the last stanza, which is not only the sum of the other two, but intimates further, that wherever there are haunts everyway adapted for the har- -boring of any evil,—there will that particular evil assuredly be found,—pat, as if by ap- pointment,—and ready to avail itself of their covert; and that the best way to prevent and send it further a-field, is to make short work with all its ascertained receptacles,—namely, unelective Governments, Nominee Upper Houses, disreputable grog- shops, and so forth;—ad infinitum.

Finally, observe the mysterious art of this Poem—the art (almost approaching to finesse) of its kind and class. It leaves us utterly in the dark as to how the man fared from the bite of the Adder. Did he survive it, or did it kill him —who can tell? ‘Most probably he died, and yet he might have got over it,’ says the reader to himself, getting faster and faster on the artistic hook of the poem, with every fresh wriggle of doubt and twist of conjecture. And thus cunningly is he induced to scratch the whole matter into his head, whatever in so doing he may happen to scratch out of it.

C. H.
