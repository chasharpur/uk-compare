Songs, Odes, and other Lyrics.

To the Lyre of Australia.

Lyre of my Country, first falls it to me, From the charm muttering Savage’s rude-beating hand To snatch thee; that so thy wild numbers may be No longer but writ on the winds of the Land? And though he who thy chords now would master, may never Evince in his verse all their rapture of tone, Yet—yet should Australia smile on the endeavour— The glory so won by the Bard were her own.

Wild sounding Lyre of the South, could I vie With a Burns in the strength of his patriot fire, How then would I raise thee in glory on high— The guardian of beauty, of valor the sire! And could I but know, that as music assuages The heart’s fret, when heard amid Life’s toilsome roar, My Country should feel through the moil of her ages The charm of thy wild voice, I’d covet no more.

Her green woods around me, her deep skies above, I lap in thy measures some national dream, And find the free notes ever mighty to move, When Australia’s full morning of life is the theme! And always thy lay, to her sons and her daughters, Should breathe of the Land where the evergreen grows— To men’s souls like the strong wind which swelleth the waters, To the heart like a spring breeze that opens the rose.

1840.
