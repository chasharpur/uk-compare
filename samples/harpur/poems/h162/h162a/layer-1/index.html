Let wise men worship only the Great Fortune. Kings and the so-called leaders of their age So big in their conceit of influence, And indispensable worth, are each and all But like the cloudlets in a summer sky, Which, by a spirit of power they wot not of, Are carried onward, wasting as they go; Into the dim Inane. At best, they are lost, As having lonelin; fuller in the mind From fragmentary loneliness & height For the time being, fuller in the mind Than are the sluggish masses that still keep Upbanking in their wake, as by the same Invisible influence urged surely on, To travel the same track; and be as Is this Great Fortune? It is Providence In union with that ultimate of Manhood, Which is its rarer mind,—a power expressed By Sages, Poets, and the kingliest thinkers From all foregone endeavours, suffering, need And present instincts of capacity For something nobler in the life of Man; Than yet has been attained to, though concei[v]ed; And which, intenerating through all time, The universal substance of the rare, Is levening the whole lump.

Partly suggested by the death of Count Cavour & Lord Palmerston.
