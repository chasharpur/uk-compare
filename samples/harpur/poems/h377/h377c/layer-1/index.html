[A Perfect Poet]

The animal force and feeling of Byron, with the mental sensuousness of Keats, the moral depth of Wordsworth, and the gorgeous ideality of Shelley, in equal proportions and intimately blended in the constitution of one man, would make him, perhaps, a perfect Poet. I say, perhaps—for it still be questionable whether a perfect Poet were a possible character. And it would be also be questionable whether it should be a desirable one; for, as judged from below, or out of its own consumate sphere, it would preclude all typic affinity and appreciable raciness: and partly from this cause, and partly from sheer elevation, the “fit audience” would be “few” indeed, if not altogether wanting. His poems would not tell: they would not hit hard enough upon the coarse materials around them. All the salient points and angles of a general sympathy with an imperfect race would be rounded away from them by the perfection of their author. In a word, they would not cut into common war, and would never for that reason come into popular appliance: for the human can only heartily appreciate the human—not the angelic.
