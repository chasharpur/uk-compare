III.

Providential Design.

Sense and passion, instinct, reason, Time and tide, with every season: The beasts that roam the grassy plain, The birds that ride the viewless air— The flying cloud—the wind and rain— All rest; all motion;—all declare The Great Soul worketh everywhere. And though the wilfulness of Man, May sometimes mar its just design, The ultimately perfect Plan, Is none the less divine.

Note.—There is Providential Design in all things. Observe a numerously stocked sheep-run, and you will find it traversed in many places, by a multitude of narrow tracks, in which every vestige of grass is trodden away—except in such of them as are yet under the pro- cess of formation. These are caused by the habit which sheep have, when kept moving onward, partially grazing, or when on the route of themselves towards some favourite place of pasture, of proceeding, not in a promiscuous mass, but in lines or files, more or less broken, and interrunning longtitudinally, but often extending from front to rear of the flock. The sheep in each particular file—that is, individuals of them, only turn out of it to crop a tempting tuft of grass, and then fall into it again, or into the file next it, and proceed as before. But of course, a grassless track is not all at once, a consequence of this. At first there is but a faintly traced line of foot prints, which, becoming more and more defined upon every subsequent transit of the flock, is finally beaten into a regular track. Now, on observing this, no doubt, your first impression will be that this same habit of sheep is very destructive to the grass. And so, indeed, it is: and yet, if on such occasions, it were the manner of sheep to move forward in a promiscuous mass, the destruction to the grass would be even tenfold greater. As it is, the grass and herbs on each side of these narrow tracts, are not injured, not trodden up in the least; but were their pastures traversed by them in a huddling crowd, instead of these narrow tracks, where they most prevail, there would be broad beaten roads, equally verdureless, as is the case in the gullies and hollows where many of such tracks, of necessity, are run together—soon, however, to diverge again into as many or more separate bye-paths. And in this way, a large part of the run would become eventually as bare as a threshing floor[.] But does the habit here noticed, originate in any provident foresight of the sheep, themselves? Not in the least. It is the consequence of a blind impulse, which is inherent in the animal’s constitution. But however blind in itself, this impulse, or the tendency to it, was, no doubt, im- planted by Providence, to conduce to the very end which is above exemplified. Such at least is the light in which I myself, have always beheld it. And it signifies little whether this view of the matter be assented to or not, in so far as the general fact of their being Providential Design in all things, may be logically affected by it. Deny it, and it matters not; admit it, and the subject is only of a piece with the instinct that prompts a cloud of migratory birds to order their flight in the figure of a wedge—the means by which they elude, to the necessary extent, perhaps, the natural pressure and resistance of the air.
