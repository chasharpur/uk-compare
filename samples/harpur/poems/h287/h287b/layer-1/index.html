V.

Memory’s Genesis.

How few through Memory’s dreamy scope, However resolute of hope, Can view the backward scene where first Their Youth rejoiced, forever crost, And not bewail, like Adam erst, The Eden he had lost! Nor feel, alas, with it compared, The present but a lengthening wild, Whereon young Passion never fared, Young Beauty never smiled!

Yet ’tis a melancholy pleasure, To sit by moon-struck Memory’s side, And list her wild lyre oft remeasure The story of our youthful pride! Hours recalling all too rife With emotions lavished wide Through the Garden of our Life Ere all its spring-time roses died! And like those splendors, when the Sun Remits in his decline from weaving A robe of beauty for the Ev’ning, Fancy’s Elysiums, one by one, Had paled away as the long Night came on.

Yes; ’tis a melancholy sweet! And let Memory oft repeat Life’s first tale, that to the core Re-Tempered by such generous lore, Our sterning spirits, as ’tis meet, May pity the cold world—the world we trust no more.x

x There is nothing which, to my mind, establishes our sprituality more incontestably than that delicate Sense of the Past which is the expression of these Rhymes: that spread of feelings, as it were, over half a life, to be ingathered, upon occasion, to their centre, the soul, like the tracery of a gossamer. Still in connexion with this soul-born faculty there is often much pain, arising from the consciousness of our having become worse;—less generous, less devotional, less loving (in an unselfish sense). Alas! in the great majority of cases, to have become elderly is to have made entire shipwreck of the Spiritual! Strange, that the more we have experience of the precarious and unsatisfact= -ory nature of all merely earthly things, that we should yet grow to them more and more! That is the further we have travelled heavenward, our spirits, once so erect like our bodies, should contract like these, more and more of a tendency to stoop dustward! But such is the case, and whence is it? Mainly from the influence of a corrupt civilization, the wisdom which is only and continually of the earth, earthy; and which (so insidious is it) the generosity of youth can alone counteract for the time—and even for thus long in but a few individuals.
