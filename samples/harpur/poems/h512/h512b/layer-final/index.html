“Filthy Lucre.”

Oh! how the Devil must grin, When Christian pastors old, Can feel it no scandal, and deem it no sin To trust less in God than in Gold.

Note.—The pending simonical barter of the emolu- ments of the fat Bishoprics of London and Durham. by their aged incumbents, for enormous retiring pensions, is assuredly the greatest ecclesiastical scandal of the times. Mammon should henceforth be figured as a very old man wearing a mitre; and certainly, in future, whenever we would stigmatise. by comparison, an unduly manifested love of lucre in any worn out piece of humanity. we shall need only to say: “His clutch upon the things of time and sense appeared but the more eager from the palsied shakings of age and infirmity— being indeed as tenacious to the last, as that of a daily-dying Anglican Bishop.” But too much wealth confined to castes and classes is the very cause of this inordinate attachment to it. This lust after “filthy lucre” is mainly provoked by a constant social contact with its object. An individual may be too rich for his good, and so may the dominant classes of a nation. Of England especially might we say, that she would be more blessed if she were less rich, or if her riches were less in the hands of the Few. Too much wealth is apt to fasten the individual to the earthy, the exclusive, and the present, albeit with chains of gold; and the over wealthy nation is thereby universally Mammonised, and enslaved to grades, and rendered sordidly time-serving. It is wholly subjected to its idol, living for it alone, and worshipping at its shrine, either with a grovelling sub[-] serviency, or with a “pride that licks the dust.” And thus, in process of time, mere Wealth becomes, what it is now in England, the sole measure of respectability, and the prime condition of all social influence—and thence the surest pledge and only palpable token of God’s favor, even with aged Bishops. Still God has endowed all men with a power of resistance to this earthy proclivity, and in that power lies the measure of their iniquity who do not sufficiently resist it.
