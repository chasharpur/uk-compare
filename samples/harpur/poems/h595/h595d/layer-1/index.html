TO A COMET.

BY CHARLES HARPUR.

Thy purpose, heavenly Stranger, who may know But Him who linked thee to the starry whole? We see thou journeyest amid the worlds, And that is all our lore 85b : for of the birth Of Motion, save as the primordial step Of God’s creative power, mankind even yet, With all their boasted science, may but guess And dimly speculate, as did of old The shepherd sages of the mystic East, And this too with a faith less raised than theirs, And cumbered more with crude material helps. Yet may we dream of thee in thy career, As of a wandering symphony amidst The planetary voices of Creation, Singing together in their sun-led choirs, As through unmeasurable voids they wheel, The God-taught anthem of eternal Order.

Thus may we dream of thee—and I, methinks, With special privilege; for I, even I, (Unweetingly indeed) of all who watched Thy coming, in my own land, saw thee first: Then having wandered forth, as was my wont, To steep my heart in the rich sunset’s pomp Of visual splendors, oceaned wide in far Withdrawing depths that more and more refined Into recesses of a loveliness Unutterably divine, and full of God— Thus richly then my heart embathing,—lo, I saw, half-doubtingly, the fading hues Leave thee sole wonder of the twilight sky.

But now, since thou hast travelled high in heaven, Thousands of wondering spirits, all are out Duly each night with upturned looks to drink The mystery of thy beauty.

In thy last Bright visitation, even thus thou saw’st The young, the lovely, and the wise of Earth,— A buried generation,—crowding out In wonder, to behold thee passing forth Beyond the signs of Time—and then to know 87a In all the awful vastitude of heaven 87b Thy place no more: so, when the flaming steps Of thy unspeakable speed, which, of itself, Blows back thy hair, shall carry thee once more Out of our vision, all the eloquent eyes 87c Now opened up in welcome—eyes by love Made tender as the turtle’s, or that speak The fervent soul and the majestic mind, Fast closed in darkness shall have given for aye 87d Their lustre to the grave, ere round the sun Thou driv’st again thy chariot of fire!

87e But eyes as beautiful and loving—yea, More radiant in their wisdom from a more 87f Enlarged communion with the soul of Truth, Shall welcome thee instead, mysterious stranger, When thou return’st anew.

And thus to think Consoles us even while we watch thee passing Out of our times for ever, and to be But seen again by those who shall convene Over the darkling ashes that composed, When last thou swept within the gaze of Earth, The natures they inherit:—Yea, although Some selfish inkling of a kind of truth, At all times mournful, whisper us the while: 86g So shall it be indeed——But ah! how swift, How vague, how shadow-like, the life of Man!
