SONGS, EPIGRAMS, NOTES, AND OPINIONS, &c. BY CHARLES HARPUR.

No. 20.

I.

Protective Duties versus Premiums.

A fiddling difference once was said to be Just that ‘twixt Tweedledum and Tweedledee: So might we say of all Protective Duties (Mocking this ancient jest at rival fiddles), That they are Public do’s—since wrong their root is, While Holt-loved Premiums are just Public diddles.

Note.—Mr. Parker, who seems to have a positive mania for doing all that in him lies, towards diverting the stream of our superfluous cash (if we have any?) in the direction of some far-from-home object, or ultra- flighty enterprise—towards the woeful widows of the heroes of Inkermann—or the sufferers from the late innundations in France—to the neglect of our own sufferers from like causes—or the frogs in the Moon, who must be badly off for water—he, I say, in partially espousing the Premium-on-cotton-growing crotchet of Mr. Holt, attempted to show that there was a total difference of nature between such Premiums and Protective Duties. But he was evidently reasoning in the dark of the matter, philosophical as he himself may have supposed such a line of argument to be. A Protective Dutie is money taken from the public at large, and apportioned to individuals, or classes of individuals, to induce or enable them to continue in some set occupation, for their own benefit: while a Premium of the kind mooted is also money taken from the general public, and granted to individuals, to en- courage them to enter into, or enlarge, some promising though novel pursuit, for their own benefit. There is, then, no essential difference between them; and the assumed reason as to the advantage of both is logically the same —namely, that one in the end does benefit the public at large, and that the other may or will. They are, in fact, equally Premiums. and the difference between them, as to mode and permanence of operation, is but a difference of accident, not of nature.

Mr. Parker’s next main argument in favor of such a Premium was equally fallacious. If the growing of cotton be demonstrably a very profitable undertaking, this fact, so far from showing the necessity, or even the desirability of any such Premium, only goes to prove its injustice on the one hand, and its impolicy on the other. For it shows, and that very conclusively, that the enter- prise should be left to its own inducements, and to that surer knowledge which, in the maturing course of things, will be gradually accreted about the subject, by the unforced experiments of individual producers. By being thus left to the legitimate attractions of its gain- fulness as an occupation, though it grow slowly, it will grow healthily, and on a sound bottom.

No doubt a body of interested individuals might very properly act in such a matter, in the manner which Mr. Parker seems to think would be also proper to the Government. But these individuals would be only doing what they liked with their own, and for their own. No one would question their right to throw forth a sprat in the hope of catching a scnapper, to the ultimate benefit of themselves, and it might be of others. But here we cross the line of a fundamental difference. A Govern- ment should never take upon itself, the liberty of thus speculating with the money of the public, albeit with the intention of ultimately benefitting that public.

All domestic action on the part of a State Government to be wise, must be so far of a merely judicial character, as to lie mainly in a just arbitration between the con- flicting interests of the governed. It must not assume the God, and attempt to originate; it must only decide and regulate according to law, repressing evil and letting good alone. Thus far wisely may the general Govern- ment of a State extend its internal action; but whenever it trenches upon those functions that are alone proper to Municipal Institutions, or whenever it presumes, on its own discretion, to be paternal and fostering, it is perilously liable to become pernicious—to become Louis Napoleonish, and to burst like a bladder.
