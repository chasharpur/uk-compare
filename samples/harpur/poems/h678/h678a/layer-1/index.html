Protective Duties versus Premiums.

A fiddling difference once was said to be Just that ‘twixt Tweedledum & Tweedledee: So might we say of all Protective duties (Mocking this ancient jest at rival fiddles) That they are Public do’s—since wrong their root is, While Holt-loved Premiums are just Public diddles.

Note. Mr. Parkes, who seems to have a positive mania for doing all that in him lies, toward diverting the stream of our superfluous cash (if we have any?) in the direction of some far-from-home object or flighty enterprise—towards the woful widows of the heroes of Inkermann,—or the sufferers from the late innundations in France, to the neglect of our own sufferers from a like catastrophy,—or the frogs in the Moon, who must be badly off for water—he, I say, in partially espousing the Premium-on- cotton-growing crotchet of Mr Holt, attempted to show that [there] was a total difference of nature between such Premiums and Protective duties. But he was certainly reasoning in the dark of the matter, however philosophical he himself such a line of reasoning to be.

A Protective duty is money taken from the public at large, and apportioned to individuals or classes of individuals to induce or enable them to continue in some set occupation—for their own benefit; while a premium of the kind mooted, is also money taken from the public general, and granted to individuals, to encourage them to enter into, or enlarge, some promising though novel occupation—for their own benefit. There is, then, no essential difference between them; and the assumed reason as to the advantage of both is logically the same—namely, that one in the end does benefit the public at large, and that the other may or will. They are, in 677 fact, equally Premiums and the difference between as to mode and permanence of operation, is but a difference of accident, not of nature.

Mr. Parkes next argument in favor of such a Premium as the one urged is equally fallacious. If the growing of cotton be demonstrably a very profitable undertaking, this fact, so far from showing the necessity or even the desirability of any such premium only goes to prove its injustice on the one hand and its impolicy on the other, For it shows, and that very conclusively, that the enterprise should be left to its own inducements, and to that surer knowleged which will be in [unclear] the matured course of things, will be gradually accreted about the subject, by the unforced experiments of individual producers. By being thus left to the legitimate attractions of its gainfulness, as an occupation, though it grow slowly, it will grow healthily, and on a sound bottom.

No doubt a body of interested individuals, might very properly act in such a matter, in the manner which Mr. Parkes seems to think would be also proper to the Government. But these individuals would be only doing what they liked with their own, and for their own. No one would question their right to throw forth a sprat in the hoping of catching a scnapper, to the ultimate benefit of themselves, and it might be of others. But here we cross the line of a fundamental difference. A Government should never take upon itself the liberty of thus speculating with the pu money of the public, albeit with the intention of ultimately benefiting that public.
