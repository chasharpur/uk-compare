IV.

Waller.

A prim, complacently poetic wight, Smooth in his style, and in his matter light. He lived in Milton’s day, more lauded far, And cherished, than that bright peculiar star! With principles as sliding as his rhymes, His pitch-pipe was the tenor of the times: These changed, of paltry Charles without a pang Of shame he sings, as he of Cromwell sang! Cromwell! whose martialness, if well portrayed, Even such a songster had Homeric made.

‘Mid titled jades proud was Sir Bard to sit Though waxing grey, a fashionable wit, And while spiced wine (his better Muse) he’d sip, 299 Dish up heroics on each strumpet’s lip; Or o’er a girdle, in high-doting rhyme, Strain—amorously strain at the sublime.

Even such a Bard was Waller: yet, ’tis true, Some credit was his literary due. With rugged models, studious to refine The pause and cadence of the heroic line, He to Rhyme that periodic pace Which Pope (though running a far bolder race) Adopted, to make perfect in its kind— The matchless vehicle of wit refined.

Note.

The close of this Criticism hardly gives Waller his due. He wrote many bright things. Some of his lyrics are exquisitely finished, and truly beautiful. Still I believe the above estimate of his genius and character is correct in the main. His fashionable tact alone would be conclusive to my mind of his secondary order as a poet. No great one is ever the possessor of this characteristic; for, if he were, it would 300 be fatal to his greatness, because it would inevitably lead him to effect the modal, to the neglect of the primary, both in Nature and Art. It was remarkable in Rogers and Moore, and indicated their order as conclusively as it did Waller’s, and commanded in their regard the same compensations. Like him, they were enabled by it, to assume a prime rank amongst their contemporaries— but, like him, they will hold but a secondary one under the reversive verdicts of the future: while Wordsworth or Coleridge, like Milton in the prior parallel, will have to wait in the grave for the full due of his glory, in the juster appreciations of posterity.

With reference also to Waller’s having improved upon the heroic rhyme of some of the poets then popular, (see Donne &c) it should be observed, that in avoiding their defect, he slipped into an opposite one, and became too smooth and even. Hence, in reading him, we would often exchange his sliding and somewhat feeble monotony of tone for their variety of cadence, even though accompanied with all thier linear ruggedness.
