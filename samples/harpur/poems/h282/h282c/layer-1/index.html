MARY ARDEN.

By Charles Harpur.

When a simple English maiden, Nested warm in Wilmecote, Singing, like a sky-lark, trailing Heavenward up her morning note, Did no English ear that listened, Even then, fore-touched by Fame, Tremble to the prophet-music Fountain-headed in that Name? Mary Arden! Tell me, shade of Mary Arden! Shade of Shakspere’s mother,* tell me!

And to thee, thyself. O tell me, Did no dazzling Vision come, (Like a sun-burst over Stratford, Burning up its boreal gloom) Of the bardic grandeurs waiting On thy matron fate, when he Who in time would call thee mother, Should all time’s subjector be? Mary Arden! Tell me, Shade of Mary Arden! Shade of Shakspere’s mother, tell me!

Then a mother we behold thee, With that babe upon thy breast— That great nascent soul, so bird-like Babbling in its fragrant nest! O what feelings sweetly human— O what instincts mildly wise, Sucked he from those mother-fountains— Drew he from those mother-eyes! Mary Arden! Tell me, shade of Mary Arden! Shade of Shakspere’s mother, tell me!

But shall we, now spirit basking In the noon-blaze of his fame, Fail to read a sign prophetic In this lovely maiden name? No! it is the star that trembled O’er a royal Poet’s birth! And amongst immortal Maries, Second to but one on earth! Mary Arden! Glory be to Mary Arden! Shakspere’s mother, England’s Mary

* “This lady, for such she really was in an eminent sense, by birth as well as by connections, bore the beautiful name of Mary Arden, a name derived from the ancient forest district of the county (Warwick); and doubtless she merits a more elaborate notice than our slender materials will furnish. To have been the mother of Shakspere—how august a title to the reverence of infinite genera- tions, and of centuries beyond the vision of prophecy.”—Thomas de Quincey.

Long after the above poem was written, I came upon this passage in De Quincey, and extracted it at once, because the fine spirit of it so sufficingly justified my own foregone enthusiasm. To have to say this is painful enough: but the provincial leanness of intellectual sympathy thereby implied does not, at all events, reflect upon the writer.

C.H.
