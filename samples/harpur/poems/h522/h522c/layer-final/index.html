SONG.

[By Charles Harpur.]

Oft he thought she could not love him! For with looks so calmly meek, Half in absence, would she speak Of the chance that might remove him. And then he’d turn away inclining All to self, and thus repining: Surely—surely she will fret, Though not yet, not yet.

But when came the day of parting, Plainly then, as in a book, Might he read in every look How her secret heart was smarting: And in the agony betraying Writhe of her pale lip, when saying: Was it but for this we met? Go not yet, not yet.

Then he left her, all relying On the love he’d so mista’en— Only to return in vain When that gentle Girl was dying! And then how bitterly repentance Made his own that by-gone sentence: Was it but for this we met? Go not yet—not yet!*

* The simplicities of song which dallied with the innocence of love and beauty, and centred their melo- dious spells amid the quiet sanctities of Home, are fast giving place in our popular literature to what? —to versified ball-room rivalries and metrical whis- kerandoes. Still the “fit audience though few” ask for the old familiar faces of Poesy, or others equally truthful—and occasionally, equally homely. Of this I am certain, that there is not anything more strikingly indicative of the characters of individuals than the quality of their favourite songs—of the character of ladies especially. Those, for instance, who affect the moonshine of “meet me in the willow glen” popularities, have usually just as much outside display and as little sterling pith as the no-things they execute. Then the would-be “Rover’s Brides” are always rather too remarkable for a sort of melo- dramatic contempt of the mine-versus-thine pro- prieties of ordinary mortals. Those who would ”march, march away” in the quality of “dashing white sergeants,” will march away whenever oppor- tunity offers—be sure of that—and in the indicated direc- tion. Those, on the other hand, who would be “butterflies born in bowers,” and “fairies living in flowers,” are mostly such sexual inanities, morally and physically, as the epistolary Amelia Scraggs: the very best specimens of this species of petticoat being only at home in fabricating indifferent wax- work roses, bad poems, and worse puddings.

But show me amongst our women, those who love with a

quiet yet earnest constancy the simple touching pathos of our old home ditties, and I will show you, reader, the gems richer than all their sex—albeit too often thrown away, Desdemona-like, by the ”base Judeans” of mankind. To speak plainly, I will show you the women whose warm hearts are in the right places; whose modest sympathies are sweet and real as the blooming beauties of Nature herself,—as tender, too, yet strong, as death; and whose love, once gained, is both tender and strong,—inevitably constant,—and far more precious, and rightly estimated, than all the gold in the universe. C. H.
