II.

Women & Stars

(A Rapsody cut short by alimentiveness) and into a diverted to itself gastronomical one.

I dote on Stars and Belles! wherefore Love saith I ought (and so I ought) to have a wife. Better “Earth-treading stars” are these! (the phrase betray’th How love searched Shakesp- with his golden Knife) And when I look at heaven’s, dear Mr. Galbraith Pictured by fancy to the very life Trebles each rare one with her eyes, as rife As they with something too divine for scath! Women and Stars! th[e]y soothe away all strife If largely loved, which largely Love repay’th! Women & Stars! not even the great sun Is more—What Maggy! have you not yet done That mutton chop, or is it frying yet? (When I am hungry I can never spout right) I say, you Maggy! If you dress it ill, 85 We’ll quarrel outright. But here it comes—a chop as long as Scrutton ※ And twice as thick!—Of all meat, give me mutton! Doubtless good beef’s agreeable to swallow, But mutton, juicy mutton, beats it hollow.

X When walking to and fro in the world of Sydney, this individual (now dead) was universally known as “long Scrutton”. He was, I think, nearly seven feet high; but was scarcely quite so thin as the text would have it.
