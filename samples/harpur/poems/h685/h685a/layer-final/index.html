The Wild Cherry Tree.

[A Lay of Love.]

A woe-weary Man who had followed afar The lures of a wanderer’s doom, At length had returned by the mariner’s star To the evergreen Land of his home: And roaming one day ’mid its forest uncouth, By the pale beam of memory, he For the Bard thus upgathered the tale of his youth, At the sight of a Wild Cherry Tree.

Whene’er o’er my path through these solitudes vast That Tree its dark shadow may throw Meseemeth to hear a loved voice from the Past In the sway of its boughs sighing low: For where the Napean, down brimming, careers, With One, the most precious to me, I met through the summers of happier years In the shade of a Wild Cherry Tree.

Its berries hung ruddy and ripe overhead, Its boughs interwove in the breeze, While, like those, in our hearts love was burstingly red And our hopes all were mingled, like these. 163 How fondly I’d talk then of bright coming days And how fondly she’d listen to me— Till blest beyond words, both would dreamingly gaze Out from under that Wild Cherry Tree.

But Fortune, alas! soon compelled me afar From all that had sweetened my youth: And yet, against true love howe’er she may war, It still conquers or dies in its truth! For though now I fared under Indian skies, In spirit there also was she— That fancy-pale Maiden with sorrowful eyes, All alone by the Wild Cherry Tree.

Years passed—and rude changes had broken my strength, In the war-trampled Lands of the Sun; But a prize I had bled for released me at length To return to my beautiful One: Who still (as she wrote) in fond loneliness shed The tear of affection for me; And her sorrow-sweet letters were written, they said, In the shade of our Wild Cherry Tree.

Yet ruth there was none in the fate that again Re-wafted me over the wave! Ah, none! for where then was my true-hearted Jane? Lying low ‘neath the grass of the grave! And now, of love’s bright morning dream of the Past, Alas, there remaineth to me Her Image alone as I looked on her last In the shade of the Wild Cherry Tree.

And yet, though in being thus lonely and tired, At times, like an odorous wind, A faith, by her pitying Spirit inspired, Breathes balmily over my mind: A belief that to love in some happier place, From all mortal solicitude free, 164 Together again shall we stand face to face, As when under that Wild Cherry Tree. x

x As this Lay is placed in contrast with the one immidiately prece- ding, I will transcribe, by way of Note to it also, another passage of the Speech quoted from in illustration of its passionate rival:

“But I have yet in store a far more distressing case of disastrous love—namely, when one, to her lover the most true and beautiful of womankind, and herself the believing priestess of all his soul’s dedicated treasures of undefiled emotion, is not snatched from his happy arms by conflicting worldly interests—by chance or change—but by the cold hand of death! What words may paint the agony—the despair—the desolation of him thus bereived? As Shelley sings,

“Out of the day and night A Joy has taken flight”

to his utter loss forever! The bountiful green earth, late so joyous and all-sufficient in his imagination, as it rounded away in the light of hopes “too bright to last”, is now a dim, dreary prison;— cold, clammy, comfortless as the grave of his vainly loved— his lost one. If of an enduring nature, he may be stung by the deadly weight of his misery, and its mortal need of some world- -wide solace, into the most stupendous efforts in Art, Science, or Literature. There have been instances of the like. Yes, he may be stung thus into greatness, and become renouned in his day and generation. But what are greatness and fame to the dark vacancy of his unforgetting heart? Flying from despair, he may surmount the difficult heights of the Epic or Tragic Muse, and by the searching power of that pathos which is consuming him, cause the stoniest hearts to gush over with emotions beau- -tiful and new—but alas for him! those bright waters of humanity only serve to image back with tenfold force his own forlorn identity—his own terrible loneliness! He may perfect with indefatigable toil some mean by which the march of social progression may be accelerated—but here his interest ends; for society, however improved, can only be to him the emptied habitation of all that was most lovely in the past. Like Newton, he may ascend on the wings of Science almost into the counsels of Omnipotence, and return at length to his wondering generation, to promulge the starry oracles of the Universe—but for him, it is only again to be alone 165 upon the wide face of the earth. Fame may blow her trumpet wherever he appears. Sages may wonder at his discoveries, and at the profundity of his intellect. Love and Valor and Virtue and Beauty may lift up their voices in united praise—but one voice; that which would have been as the consecration of all these—that one is stilled forever in the grave—is lost irreclaimably in the depths of other days.— Think not this an extravagant picture. Time and Space, with Change their offspring, have but little assuasive mastery over the grieving memories of a true and virgin love when thus disastrously broken up by the apparent cruelty of death. The head of the once youthful lover may have waxed white under the winters of many a weary year—he may be in a far land and among strange faces and stranger customs; yet how often in the dream of his memory, shall the beloved of his youth pass still before him, not changed like himself, but even such as he was wont to behold her when dancing like an embodied joy amongst the daughters of his native land. Yes; the old man shall recal her in his memory even thus, and sigh to himself as he again turns to the cold face of the present—”But she died in the blossom of her beauty—she was reft away from my soul in the morning of our love.”—Is a woman the sufferer in a case like this, the pity of it is doubled. The competitions of business, politics travel, warfare, and the arts—these are all open as commons for the solace of the masculine sufferer; but she, by her natural, —perhaps it were more proper to say her unnatural position in society, is hopelessly bound as it were to the stake of her own blasted and branchless individuality. The ocean unspecked by an outward or a homeward bound ship is a wild and dreary prospect. A desert unrefreshed by one bubbling spring—unshaded by a single tree, must be oppressively desolate to look upon. The sultry dome of a summer sky, when beheld from the parched summits of our rock-ribbed mountains, uncrimpled by a solitary cloud, unstreaked even by an over gliding eagle, has a disheartening lone- -liness of aspect: but the hopeless monotony of her days and nights presents to us a face that is yet wilder, and more deso- -late and lonely in its aspect than any—than all of these: for it expresses the wide and weary solitude of a love-broken heart.”

C.H.
