Point in Poetry.

The Muse’s work, however fine each joint, Is made mere Joinery, by too much Point.

Note————This, however, is a[unclear]ing from the point—Point in Poetry. But even our Prose may have too much of this quality, and lose whatever is gained by its shining play and adroitness, in the the greater values of simplicity and weight. It is always also somewhat inimical to directness, for though the term point would seem to imply directness, the thing signified may be so fetched about for, as to militate against it. The final and literary effect of even the abbr[evi]ated letters of Junius is no doubt damaged by too much point; and hence, in reading them more than once, we soon come to take it for less than it is really worth, for a trick of composition, tending to divert attention to itself, attention to itself, than to nail it to the matter.
