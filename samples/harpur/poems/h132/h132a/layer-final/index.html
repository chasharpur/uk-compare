SONGS, EPIGRAMS, NOTES, AND OPINIONS, &c., BY CHARLES HARPUR.

No. 13.

1.

Finish of Style.

A last fine touch must add to, not diminish, The value of all Beauty:—never doubt it! And what deserveth not a perfect finish, Must, on the whole, be very bad without it.

Note.—As a general habit, I would advise all young authors to copy frequently, pruning away, while doing so, every superfluous word, and moulding every thought and image into their predestined completeness: for there is nothing truer on this head, than what Ben Jonson has said aforetime,

“Who casts to write a living line must sweat, —————————— and strike the second heat Upon the Muse’s anvil.”

B

ut we hear much of late to the contrary, from the pens of small scribes and poetasters self-erected into critical authorities: an infinite deal of nonsense, cautioning Poets against “overhandling” their pro- ductions lest their bloom be thereby endangered.—Pope being instanced, par example. But when retouching has this effect, the fault proceeds more from the bad taste of the retoucher, than from any super-finishing process. Who refined his pieces more carefully and continuously than Coleridge, and yet do we find the bloom of these to have been in any wise soiled by his retouching solicitude? The fact is, no true Poet (having that genuine critical instinct which all true Poets have) is in any danger of doing detriment to the bloom of his poetry by frequent handling: he will only thereby give to it a final excellence, complexionally and otherwise. Just as surely will he do this, as that the revisiting influences of Nature herself, will have but the effect of fostering the blooming poetry of a rose into its highest degree of perfection. It would, indeed, be something apart from the general nature of things, if it were not so:—something unaccountably contrary to all pro- gressional tendency and order—all universality of artistic consequence.

T

he sum of the whole matter is this: breadth of view, originality of conceptiòn, and imaginative ex- cellence being premised, then finish of style, however secondary in itself, is the surest pledge (perhaps the only sure one) of the immortality of poetry, or of any kind of literary composition.
