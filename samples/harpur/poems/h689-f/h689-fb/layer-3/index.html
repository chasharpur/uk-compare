My worthy parents tenderly embraced me With many tears, protesting that I was The life light of their eyes—their rose of joy; Then stretched their hands out for the Persian’s gold And hurried off to tell it o’er in secret The dear recovered treasure they so late Had mourned, as lost for ever. An to this end I now arrayed my self in robes that seemed Aereal ultimates of perfect whiteness While scarfs of purple such as only Queen May purchase from the Indus caravans Barred them with rainbow tints; a burnished band Of purest gold, whose midst throughout was raised With a full row of pearls—all perfect ovals— Held in the wavevelets of my sunny hair: A gem that seemed indeed a frozen mass Of white fire, flashed upon my silk soft brow And, in my loveliness, even thus arrayed I swam into his presence (though indeed My hid feet rowed) graceful as a swan!

The Harem Girls applauded: while there seemed Even in is own dul eyes almost a flash As of extorted joy but which became At the next moment a malignant scoul Which had its dark source in a thought like this As I learned after from a Nubian Girl Who allways waited on his baser needs “What did a soft and ignorant mix like me Think to entrap again man like him Who was the sharpest fellow, past all dout In all the orient. “Did a mink like me Think to outwit a piece of such intelligence By floating in before him, like a swan A little better feathered than at first.

Even while the musing had been mustering up The Haram Ladies had been all dismissed The Nubian Girls alone remaining; then A conclave of those singular Gnome-like things Who housed no one knew where, were summoned in Nor was the council long I was that night Was to be poisoned—and with a pomegranate One of a number to be plukct & brought For every lady of his Haram, forth At supper time out of his Summer Garden 751 And one too for himself, though he disliked them

The Nubian had slipping away, and told me All she had heard. Yet her news moved me not So sure I felt that such was not my doom. Or movered me most to this—the packing up Of property, and things that Ladies doat on. But time abides not, we were sumond forth To supper. On the center boar[d] a basket stood Of silver burnt, piled high with crimson goards These soon the king dispensed about the board To every layson—till two at last Alone remained—and one of these being marked This came to me—of course I could but take it. But on the instant fell as in a faint That flurried all the ladies till they thronged About me—flurried even the king himself With infinite dexterity again I came to life Amid this bustled and exchanged the gourds Took his and lift him mine. This I ate. And praised as being the very soul of fruit. He also with a rollac in his eye Ate that which had been poisoned, and avowed He never knew before what fruit it was Nay, that it held with its crimson rind The juce of Paradise.

While this went on. I slipped away wholly unseen to where My goods were packed—and to provide for flight Nor had I long withal—ere to my chamber The Nubian Girl came running; telling me That soon the king had with a mighty start Fall prostrate; while the Ladies all away Had scattered all away like gossamer clouds And that the chamber now was thronged instead To suffocation with a musty mass Of bare-poled hakims and gre[y]haired Physicians; Who all concurred at last in this—the dark Affair in hand was quite unlookt for quite! 703 And had strong symtoms of a “poison-case.” And as she told her news the Nubian laught With infinite delight—so loved are kings. x And I laught also, feeling that I had A natural and a perfect right to do so. But no time might be wasted—and we fled Together, loaded both with costly spoils. Still as we passed beneath the cour Wherein the Monorch suffered, all in vain I strove to dash my womanly desire To have a last look at him. So aloft I mounted, on the slaves bent back—and looked The king was sprawling on the pillowy floor With quite a herd of anxious quaks about him: 704 Jews & Armenians, infidels & slaves, All ranks, all races; and as still he took Their nostrums, with grimaces horrible He still kept threatening them, that if they failed To cure him ere that dreadful night was passed The dawn should see them headless—every man! Then would he curse the whim that made him cho[o]se Pomegranates at his supper. Then in rage He cursed his blunder, and the palpable trick By which a silly woman had o’erreached him! Then, would he sprawl, and bellow like a beast. And so I left him sprawling, while the sound Of His brute ragings followed us beyond The garden walls, as through the dark we fled!
