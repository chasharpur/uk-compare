[HOPE ON]

[...] Friendship turns, itself denying— Yea, itself! the heart to wring: Still, even though all faith be dying, Ever list to Hope replying— “Flowers will come again, with Spring And in the desert way we’re going, There’s one bounteous blessing flowing— And hark! I hear the Angels sing!”

The above is an attempted lyrical embodiment of the philosophy of Hope, with full admission of its vanity. We may know its deceitfulness but too well; but we should know also the utter necessity of its beguiling companionship throughout the weary pilgrimage of life so precariously constituted as it is. Hence, however it may have disappointed us in the past, we should still cherish it with regard to the future in a forgiving spirit: for however it may again befool us, it will keep us alive, at all events, up to that point —and even then take beating as obsequiously as an erring pointer, and still herald our fortunes as before. Let us correct it then as judiciously as we can, but never think of parting company with it. There is a sort of compassionate truth in its very falshood, or rather in the result of it: seeing that human doings are mostly but accumulating failures, and that but for these being touched in the prospect with the enchantment of its promissory light, we should be at once smitten

“With such a misbelief of all the world”— such a mortal cold consciousness of the vanity of all things, as would freeze us into the torpor of that death out of the grave which is every way more deplorable than the darkness [unclear] that house within it—for there the end is both known and natural. Hope on then and live.

C.H.
