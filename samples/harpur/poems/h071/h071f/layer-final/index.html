A Combat.

A Trooper of France, in desperate need Had struggled from under his dying steed, Where Egypt s pyramids appeared In giant bulk, as if giant-reared: While on his black war-horse, the Turk Who had borne him down, to finish his work, 387 Back wheelingly careered.

Taking his resolute stand Foot-deep in his charger’s blood, The soldier of Christendom, sword in hand,— (A trusty blade and good That gleamely out pointing, showed Like the lightning-tongue of settling cloud, By the rush of a whirlblast swept afar Out in the front of the storm of war,)— His foe awaiting stood.

Urged into whirlwind speed, On,—on with a scattering tail Like the hurrying thunderbolt’s smoky trail! On,—on with a streaming mane Like the foremost racks of a hurricane— On came the Tartar steed!

Then passed with a vengeful clash Of blades, and a lunging downward crash, The imminent shock! And when its cloud of dust upthrown Cleared off, there stood that Trooper alone, Firm as a mid-sea rock!

But two steeds and a Moslem in death lay still— And a kite of the Desert that came with a flop In heavy flight from a pyramid’s top, In a hunger-glad mood At the scent of blood, Was circling oer and screaming shrill— Screaming with hunger, and screaming for joy That man the destroyer so well could destroy, As she thought of her famishing brood.
