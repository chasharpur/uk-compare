III.

The Combat.

A Trooper of France, in desperate need, Had struggled from under his dying steed, Where Egypt’s pyramids appeared; While on his black war horse, the Turk Who had borne him down, to finish his work, Back wheelingly careered.

Taking his resolute stand Foot deep in his charger’s blood, The Soldier of Christendom, sword in hand, That gleamily out pointing, showed Like the lightning tongue of a settling cloud, His Foe awaiting stood.

Urged into whirlwind speed, On—on with a scattering tail, Like the hurrying thunder bolt’s smoky trail! On—on with a streaming mane, Like the foremost racks of a hurricane! On came the Arab steed.

Then passed with a vengeful clash Of blades, and a lunging downward crash, The imminent shock! And when its cloud of dust upthrown Cleared off, there stood that Trooper alone, Firm as a rock! But two steeds and a Moslem in death lay still, And a kite of the Desert that came flop flop In heavy flight from a pyramid’s top, In a merry mood At the scent of blood, Was circling o’er and screaming shrill.x

x I found the subject of these Rhymes in some account 15 (I know not whose) of the French acchievements in Egypt, under the mighty murderer, Napoleon. I thought the incident capable of being elaborated into a powerful poetical picture, and siezed upon it accordingly: but having done so, I must record against it the facts, that I consider Bonaparte’s invasion of the East was not simply but monstrously unwarrantable; and that I have but little admiration, under any circum= -stances, for mere military heroism. What have Truth and Human Good to do with the Sword? What can it prove, if used offensively, but that men are sometimes worse than wild beasts? and what, even when employed in the defensive, but that murder, violence, and robbery are sometimes only to be repelled by their own inhuman means? What is there in mere bravery worthy of engaging our admiration? The bull-dog is brave. What is there in mere distructiveness worthy of commanding our emulation? The tiger is eminently distructive. If we must needs glorify our in= -dividualities, let it , in the name of sanity, be for such things as lift us above, not such as ally us to the brutes. Still I would not be misunderstood. I esteem rational valor a prime social virtue; and think that they who would not uphold their Country’s liberties at the cost of life, were unworthy even of that base existence for which, in such case, these would be forfieted. All I would be understood to mean, is, that if in the national systems of the world now obtaining, war is occasion= -ally inevitable, still woe be to them by whom war cometh.
