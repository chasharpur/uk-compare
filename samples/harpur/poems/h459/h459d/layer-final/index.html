THE POET.

By Charles Harpur.

I.

Blissless am I except in this That I delight to sing of bliss; And loveless, save that oft and long I love to sing of Love—a loving Child of Song! Past, thus, like roes that on their mountains skip, All my young years—ay, all! have I let trip Ungathering aught that men call gain! Wherefore the kind world thinks—and I myself Do even sometimes think, poor elf! That I have lived in vain, For letting Fortune give me so the slip! Most when some creature, gilded by its pelf, That, like a gaudy butterfly, Was yesterday a grub, comes flaunting, flaring by.

II.

But soon I turn again and sing: By so much stronger than my will Is this strong love of Song—that still I am happy only caroling; For then joy-flashing eyes, and love-dewed lips And things that company best with these, Gleeful birds on splendid wing, Clear streams, fresh flowers, and shady trees All starred with golden pips, Drive from my thoughts all thought of pelf! Joy-flashing eyes, and love-dewed lips, For ever still of these My Fancy, like a honeysucker, sips; Or suns contentedly in those Her little lazy loving self, Even like some fairy Elf Pillowed the while upon a blushing rose! Still thus, with me, the world by goes, Whilst other—duller men enjoy Its real loaves and fishes— Each rosy substance of my rosiest wishes, Even till they cloy.

III.

But if a thought like this Will mar at times my tuneful bliss, By so much stronger than my will Is still the love of Song—that still I turn me to the Muses’ Spring Therein to steep my heart—and sing Of eloquent eyes, That are of sunny thoughts the sunny skies, And gracious lips, Dashed with such honey-dew as young Passion sips; And what associate best with these, Birds, and streams, and flowers, and trees All starred with golden pips.

IV.

But quitting, sometimes, themes like these:— Luminous fancies of luminous eyes, Whence Love looks glowing, as from sunset skies; Sweet dreams that swarm like honey bees Round honey-dropping lips; Glad birds, fresh streams and flowers, and trees All starred with golden pips:— I clap to the meanings of my Lyre Loud rushing wings of fire! Or more to keep the sensual under, Lap them in soul-shaking thunder! While for the noble and the wise, The valiant and the free, I sing of that millennial sunrise Of hardy human progress yet to be, When all true men shall seek, in foul and fair, To breathe with lion lungs the difficult mountain air Of strenuous, rough, up-climbing Liberty!

V.

And thus employed, my purpose is most clinging: The more, that, as unto the Present I Am nothing, I would be Yet unlike those who, Mammon’s torch enringing Like moths, live only in its blaze to die;— And something too for my sweet singing Unto the better—wiser times My Country yet shall grow to see: Yea, something for the silver-pealing chimes, And for the beauty that fades not by Of soul-enriching Rhymes! A star of poesy in the Future’s sky Throbbing with deathless thought, and to the praise That honeyed lips shall breathe and eloquent eyes shall raise.

VI.

Thus with harmonious aims in lowliest station, And nameless to the passing generation, I labour—labour heartily and long, Even like the bards of old, To fix each rich dream in as rich a song, As one should set some priceless gem In many-figured gold: The gold of a glorious diadem, Which on the heads of a mighty line Of royal Poets yet is doomed to sway The heart of a chosen People, grown divine From birth and nurture in the holier day Shed over the broad Land from Truth’s disclouded shrine.

Note.—If the “musical concordists” of the Month would but carefully study the nodes and modulations of the above, it might, perhaps, correctively benefit them: that is, if they are not abso- lutely predestined to conform for ever to the likings of Mr. Fowler’s phrenological Barrel-organ of Tune; or to make the would-be warble of that

“Scrannel pipe of wretched straw”

on which he grates his imitations, to the dainty horror of the Muses, their only and everlasting scale of harmony.

I recollect reading the following sentence in the preface to a cheap reprint of Shelley, which some smart socialist, as it appeared, had written for it at a venture:—”Though he seems to have been alive, upon occasion, to all the touching graces of versification, his best poems might be read as prose.” Now what is the truth? Why, so far from being in anywise prosaic in their structure, Shelley’s best poems ring all over, like bell metal, with the most wildly musical lines and variously modulated cadences, and are less like prose, perhaps, than those of any other poet of his day, with the single exception of Coleridge. But what adequate treatment could the verse of such a poet receive from the musical insight of a pseudo-Aristarchus like this?—a creature that was either as tuneless as an ox, or every whit as ignorant of what he was dogmatising about, as Mr. Fowler often is—in reality—of the subjects of his criticism. I say this advisedly. And yet this is the man who would assume the most prominent parts; would be Pyramus and Thisbe, and the Lion too. But what one sure promise of supreme literary ex- cellence is there about the character of the man, to justify the assumption? What he has already done amongst us is rather con- demnatory of his pretensions than otherwise. For his poetry, as he himself almost confesses, is next to nought; and his prose, while it is very questionable in quality, is decidedly vicious in style. It is faulty all over. It wants weight and strength in substance, and coherence and so- briety in method and manner. It is too like a chance-medley of glass beads, of all sorts and sizes, slenderly strung together. Or at best, it is but like a deposit of diamond dust—not a display of gems, but a patch of shining granules, petty and valueless for all their glitter. Even when he affects in it to be neat and elegant, as he sometimes does, he only reaches to that smart, small- grained precision which may be proper enough to the theme of a schoolboy, or the paragraph of a Report, but which is certainly not much relished by those who love to listen to the “large utterance of our elder Gods,” or something akin to it.

But then how witty he is! say his admirers. He is sometimes witty; but he is oftener merely farcical. His wit wants ease, as if it were not quite natural to him. It is too eager. Now it is too far sought; anon it is too much set for, as it were with a rat-trap; and, not unfrequently, we must accept it at second-hand—for it is cribbaged. And even when best, it is mostly of that kind which crops so far over the edge of the grotesque, that it may be easily pushed into it. Or it is so conceitedly unforecasted, when it takes the bent of sarcasm, that its flanks might always be turned by an adroit opponent: as, for example, his quotation from Parkes’ Opossum Shooting might be thus Rolanded.

Instantly The ready gun was raised, and, flashing free, Fired in the moon’s pale face. The wounded creature, (The Opossum, not the moon, as that “prim feature,”* Fowler, was lunatic enough to think, When by her Monthly sway o’erflown with—drink!) A moment in the agonies of nature, &c.

His showy mannerism, too, is mostly a mere trick, and there- fore distasteful when once we smell it out. The “pith of the sub- ject” is common property; the “sap of the subject” says Mr. Fowler: sap for pith—it is a mere trick. It has the make- believe of race only—not the thing itself. Even his old Toledo, the only crack weapon in his mental armoury, serves him but to juggle with.

Finally, in the region of Poetry, Mr. Fowler is, beyond all ques- tion, self-damned as a critic. For although a just judge of poetry may not be also a first-rate poet, it will nevertheless follow, that the poetic attempts of such an one, if he make any, will ever be characterised by good taste at least, and sound sense; whereas Mr. Fowler’s attempts of the kind, do positively exhibit in almost every line of them, some gross violation, either in thought or ex- pression, of the chastity of taste, or of the native bent and in- tegrity of every unmystified intellect. And thus stands he, in- evitably self-convicted of incompetency, both as Poet and Critic, before any truly commissioned Rhadamanthus of the Muses.

And yet (I say again, and once for all) this man, thus obviously

defective both in nature and art, has the presumption to talk about founding an Australian Literature! I say nothing about his being ignorantly impudent enough to presume a want of cul- ture in an Australian Poet for being Australian born I suppose; because reckless dogmatism and the rashest assertion, are exactly the weed-crops to be expected from the rank presump- tion aforesaid.
