L.

To Henry Kendall.

Said Chaucer in his old days, when the sun Of his poetic life was wearing low: “To me now from afar come tidings none From spirits that once, with a conginial glow, Did glory in my glory long ago;— Nay, even my neighbors (as in scorn ‘twere done) Ignore me:”—and to a like tune might run My story ever as I older grow. ‘Tis much then in this dearth on every hand Of fellowship, and in my loneness here, To have through votive lines so sweetly bland (Yet bravely worshipful) as thine, such clear Assurance of how well my Native Land Shall yet atone for all—holding my memory dear.

1863.
