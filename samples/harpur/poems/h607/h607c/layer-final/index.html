XII.

To Henry Kendall.

Said Chaucer in his old days, when the sun Of his poetic life was wearing low: “To me now from afar come tidings none From spirits that once, with a congenial glow, Did glory in my glory—long ago;— Nay, even my neighbors (as in slight ‘twere done) Ignore me *:”—and to a like tune might run My story also as I older grow. ‘Tis much then, in this dearth on every hand Of sympathy, and in my loneness here, To have through votive lines so sweetly bland Yet bravely worshipful as thine, such clear Assurance of how yet, throughout the Land, All beautiful souls shall hold my genius dear.
