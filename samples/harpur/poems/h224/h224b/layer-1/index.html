Literary Picking and Stealing.

Who steals our cash, steals fairly: ‘Tis guarded—tempting:— ‘Twas ours—’tis his—and is as good as ever; But he who filches from us our true wit, To mask the robbery, spoils the thing itself.—

Adapted from Shakespear.

It is the worst thing about literary theft, that the necessity of somewhat disguising the stolen goods is sure to dispirit, or dull, or blunt, or deforn them. Thus “Cosmopolitan,” in the Empire, in characterising Mr. Plunkett’s speeches, says: “They open, like the Great Western Road, broadly enough at first, but finish by running up a gum tree.” No doubt, this is witty, and may be characteristic—but then, it is stolen; and what is worse, it is half spoilt in what Ancient Pistol would call the “conveyance.” It is from the sayings of a facetious American, who, in allusion to some specious city-founding speculation of his day, was wont to com- pare it to one of their (the American’s) Great Western Roads, which opened magnificently through a stately avenue, but ended in a squirrel track, and ran up a tree Now in the apparently slight difference between what ”Cosmopolitan” says, and what the American said before him, lies the very finish of the wit. On reading the sentence as plagiarized, one naturally asks himself, why the road should finish by running up a gum tree? A reason, why, is not even suggested. But in the original saying, the why is as plain as the wit is exquisite: the road runs up a tree, because it ends in a squirrel track.
