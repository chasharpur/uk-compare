NO. II.

[SONNET ADDRESSED TO JAMES MACARTHUR, ESQ. (2)]

Thou may’st not be a warrior, and a terror, Defying to fierce fight an alderman; Thou mayst not be a chivalrous fair man, Denouncing women for some fancied error;— Thou may’st not be a squatter-bard, and revel,, In making fabled woes and sufferings plain; Thou may’st not e’en from thine own party gain, The fame of being a bitter, clever devil; But still thou may’st have an opinion, surely, And, as an honest man, express it too; If thou with rogues wilt not have aught to do, Why thou may’st say so, and not look demurely;— But then the rogues, as sure as corks will float, sir! Will strain a little point to cut your throat, sir!
