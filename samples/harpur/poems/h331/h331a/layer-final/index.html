VI.

The “Nevers” of Poesy.

Never say aught in verse that you would be ashamed to say in prose; and never let your head put into heroics what your heart would put into doggerel. Never heed being in favor with the critics, so long as you are in favor with the Muses. Never sing about anything that is not worth thinking about. Never mind whether a verse is canonically smooth or not, so long as it flows sweetly. Never write slip-slop—if you can help it. Never be metaphysical—if you like to be understood. Never transform (after the manner of Poe) the “poor Jacko” of some stray pet magpie, into a blue devilish prediction. Never be persuaded that harshness is the only foil to sweetness; and never endeavour to be graceless, till you have endeavoured in vain to be graceful. Never be Pagan enough to believe that a beauty is the lovelier for some deformity in its neighbourhood, if the said deformity is not there by nature. Never cheat yourself into the notion, that the soul of poetry must, of necessity, be resident in the mere form of verse; and never flatter yourself that reason is easily circumvented with rhyme Never imagine that a great flow of words must always be identical with a spring-tide of thoughts. Never pay for your rhymes with all the meaning of your numbers, or the reader may calculate the price, and think you gave too much for them. Never set one rhyme as a trap for another: it hurts their fellow-feeling. Never force a simile; if it won’t take service without forcing, be true to liberty, and let it pass. Never strain an image: if it won’t suit pretty well without straining, it won’t suit at all with it. Never goad, with your stylus, a dull thought or a sluggish feeling: they both hate to be goaded into song, and, when there, their hatred is death to it. Never displace a homely-sounding word that exactly expresses your meaning, by a courtly or foreign-sounding one that don’t express it at all. Never Procrustianise a regular battalion-verse of ten syllables into a grenadier-verse of twelve. Never describe a cabbage under the name of a rose; and, if you would not be laughed at, never attempt, by dint of rhyme, to transmute a pebble into a diamond. Never mistake a mere vagary of the will for a proper flight of the imagination—a balloon charged with gas for a morning cloud charged with glory; and never let that pass for good poetry which would be bad prose.
