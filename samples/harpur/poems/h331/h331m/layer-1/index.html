The ‘Nevers’ of Poetry.a

“I’m nothing, if not critical.”

Shakspere.

Never say aught in Verse, or grave or gay, That you in Prose would hesitate to say; Or let your head in lofty numbers tell What your heart deems but fit for doggerel.

Never in rhyme pretend to tears, unless True feeling sheds them in unfeigned distress;b Or some dream-grief, with such a mournful strain As night winds have in pine-tops, stirs your brain, To shake them, dew-like, o’er the flowers that bloom In the wild dark round Joy’s imagined tomb; 256 Or save when doubts that over Love may lour Like summer clouds,—break in a sunny shower Out of your gladdened eyes, to freshen all The bowers of Memory with their grateful fall.

259a

Never laugh either in Songs measured tone If all the wit is in the laugh alone, Or all the humour in a “ha-ha-ha” That should clink (very sheepishly) with “baa”.

260a

Never too much affect that polished thing Once so belauded, and called point or sting. The highest and the noblest growths of wit Are never, or but seldom, touched with it: For of the Muse it is but truly born When made the apex of some burst of scorn, Or irony, or hate all torture-torn! Not to improve the passion, but to make The wave full surging, on its object break.

Never, if you’d be readable at all, Aim overmuch at being ethical. The Muse should be a teacher, but refuse To be a mere schoolmistress should the Muse. She should instruct us, but her methods never Be academic ones, however clever. Her morals, like great Nature’s morals, aye Should work themselves out in an unforced way, And not so patly as to hint the while At cryptic ingenuities of style. Whate’er the theme, her ethic lights should shine Full forth, as from a central heat divine, Or heat inherent to the passion wrought Into the chastened harmony of thought, And not be mere extraneous coals of fire Blown for the nonce into factitious ire.

Never write madly, as if nimble wits, Like Arabs, showed best in their wandering fits. 261a Though Song has oft some beauty most divine Which well we feel, yet cannot well define— Some yearning excellence, intense and far, Coming and going like a clouded star— Some awful glory we but half descry, Like a strong sunset in a stormy sky; Yet ne’er be murky of set purpose, since You only thereby shall the more evince (Though through a miserable juggle, which Makes pinching Criticism’s knuckles itch!) That even the Sublime’s but then made sure, When, like a morning alp, it breaks from the obscure.c

262a

Never think either, that your brightest rhymes Must wait for readers in the after times; Or that what puzzles most, must therefore be Over-informed with high mentality; For though at first it overpower our sight, There’s no real darkness in excessive light.

263a

Yet never dread the kingliest critic’s ire So long as genius, like a bride of fire, Lies glowing on thy breast, to mission and inspire; For though thy moods to most be caviäre, They should have voice, or genius were not there: But never sing of aught (even though in love) That is not worth solid thinking of.

264a

Never heed whether a line strictly goes By learnéd rule, if,—brook-like,—it warble as it flows; Or if, in concord with the thought, it fills Fast forward, like a torrent fast flooding from the hills.

265a

Never write slip-slop—never, I would say, If you can help it: only asses bray! And sure ’tis but an assinine sublime That’s best evolved through a jog-trot of rhyme, Or lines like whinnies, harsh, and ending short, Doleful and wheezy, in a kind of snort. And never, if you would be very terse, Be very metaphysical in verse.

276a

Never think harshness the best foil to raise And relish sweetness: few love craggy lays. Yet never be you glib, when Passion’s force Should ridge your style,—as by a tempest hoarse 277a The deep is roughened into waves that roar At heaven,—up heaping, huddling, more and more, To burst at last in booming thunder on the shore.

Never be such a Pagan as to deem That Truth or Beauty must diviner seem For some abnormal set-off, hunched and rude, Prowling for evil in the neighborhood, 278a If such strange opposite breathe not the air Of Nature—being found, not conjured there. And never to be graceless be you fain, Till to be graceful you have tried in vain.o

279a

Never be cheated—never may you be Into the cramp belief, that Poetry Must of necessity in soul be one With the mere form of verse, if it but deftly run, Or power as with a mill-wheel’s vigorous cheer, A rhyming clatter hard upon the ear.p

280a

Never believe that verse a license knows For aught that would be balderdash in prose; Or that all reason may at any time Find a sufficient substitute in rhyme; Or that because with many words you’re fraught, There must be under them some flood of thought.

Never pay with your clinks, however ringing, With all the meaning of the song you’re singing, Lest the too-knowing reader reckon dryly The cost, and vow you bargained for them vilely.

Never set one rhyme, like a rat-trap baited, To catch another; for all rhymes so mated Are held together with no loving clutch, And hurt (of course) in fellow-feeling much. And never, like a tinker shredding tin, Forecut your metal, and then hammer in As much of sense and metre as you may, Calling what comes on’t—a Pindaric Lay.

281a

Never compel a simile that won’t Take service without forcing;—if it don’t, As of itself, into your poem flow, Be true to liberty—and let it go.

Never inflate an image;—if it will Not suit you in its own way, all your skill Shall make this only the more glaring,—just As though it grumbled: “If I must, I must.”

Never with your poetic stylus goad Slow thoughts or feelings: they but overload The jaded Muse, and have a hatred strong ‘Gainst ever being goated into song; And what is sadder—and of this beware— Their hate is certain death to it when there.

Never reject a homely sounding phrase That your whole meaning earnestly displays, For one made current by some courtly wit Which barely indicates a shade of it, Or like the infusion of an alien potion, Flattens the native spirit of the notion; Or which, it may be, (for it so may fall) Does not express what you would mean at all.

282a

Never, Procrustes-like, stretch out a live And regular battalion verse of five Well measured feet, (just to amuse the ear) Into an Alexandrine grenadier.

Never describe a cabbage with great throes Tremendously poetic,—such as those He might indeed p[r]etend to, who’d enhance A love-given Rose’s regal elegance: And never,—if you’d rather not provoke Some grinning Critic’s always frosty joke,— Strive to transmute, by dint of rhyme, mere fossil Coal-stones into black diämonds colossal; Or ween, if only in Parnassian metal, Red beads for orient rubies may be set all: Since froth and make-believe when once we strike Upon their trick, offend all tasks alike.

Never exalt vagàries to a station But due to true flights of Imagination— Gas-charged balloons, put vainly all a-bloat For clouds of God that in the orient float: Theatric thunders, all set brattling for The dread, all-shaking tempest-tramps of Thor: For in the end, all charlatanry must, The more it startle, but the more disgust. And never let aught pass your bardic nose As sweet in verse, that would smell vile in prose.q

283a

Never, too,—if you would be extant long, Imitate England’s newest sons of song;r Those crude, affected, pap-fed mystics, who Rather than not sing in a manner new, 284a Sing horrible whimsies in a style that quite Out-Crashaws Crashaw’s, in its wildest flight! Or of the tiniest matters, with a touch, Of super-daintiness gloved overmuch! 285a Verse ever lax, or barbarously ornate— Verse ever slip-shod in its metric gait— And looking vastly like some splay-foot strain Of psalmody run mad or grown profane! Or verse that fain would be as sweet as honey Through fiddling iterations, quaint and funny, 286a And which, even when most tunefully it runs, Is feeble in its flow, like Tennyson’s.

And lastly, never take for gospel, all Your friends say of your genius, when they call 287a Its merits o’er: but at the same time see That you do never take yourself to be So great an ass as your known foes declare They do most solemnly believe you are.

b The pathetic in Poetry (as in all fiction) is never so truly effective as when it is perfectly unforced, both in subject and manner. To be supremely exquisite, it must be given to flow naturally out of the subject in hand, and not be cast about it with evident forethought, or ostentatiously, like a prepared suit of mourning. Hence, whenever a Poet takes up his pen of set purpose to search our hearts to the very core, it is more than likely, that he will only succeed in searching our patience to a very unpleasant degree, or in disgusting us with some vile and bloated mockery of woe. Any undertaker can bespangle a coffin with tinsel-headed nails: but to be-drop it with tears is another matter, of which nature, and not artifice, must be the 256b principal getter up.

In so much, indeed, does the truest pathos result from an unforced treatment of a doleful matter, that we are always most nearly touched by such pathetic descriptions as appear to come in quite incidentally. There is a fine example of this in Henry Taylor’s Philip Van Artevelde. I know of no passage in any author, more sweetly sorrowful; more holy in the moral depth and beauty of its pathos. It is an account of the appearance in death, of two children who have perished of famine during the siege of Ghent and is spoken by Clara the sister of the hero.

“He plucked aside the curtain of the couch And there two children’s bodies lay composed. They seemed like twins of some ten years of age, And they had died so nearly both together, He could not say which first: and being dead, He put them for some fanciful afection, Each with its arm about the other’s neck, So that a fairer sight I had not seen Than those two children, with their little faces So thin and wan, so calm, and sad, and sweet. I looked upon them long, and for awhile I wished myself their sister and to lie With them in death as they did with each other. I thought that there was nothing in the world I could have loved so much; and then I wept.

Again:—A pathetic affect in Poetry is never more entire 259b than when, by the seeming abruptness of unmixed passion, it is left, as it were, somewhat short of itself—but suggestively so: or than when, in its means, it is brought bluffly out, and as suddenly terminated, like a natural crag,—upon whose rigid verge we are left hanging in the agony of our sympathy, as over some dim unmeasured profound of loss unspeakable. No doubt this fulness of effect is often thus achieved through the suggestion of Art; but then it is true art, itself the suggestion of Nature. There is a short poem by Wordsworth that will strikingly illustrate my meaning.

Lucy.

There dwelt among the untrodden ways Beside the springs of Dove, A Maid whom there were none to praise And very few to love. A violet by a mossy stone Half hidden from the eye— Fair as a star, when only one Is shining in the sky. She lived unknown, and few could know When Lucy ceased to be: But she is in her grave—and oh, The difference to me!

Here this equisite little poem ends—or rather breaks off, as if the Poet could proceed no farther because of the blinding urgency of his tears; and here both Nature and Art declare that it should terminate, however abruptly. The entireness of the pathetic effect would be manifestly impaired by the addition of a single 260b word. And hence there is no other in the Language, perhaps, more suggestive of the conclusion, that true nature and true art are always in harmony, as always identical in their essence.

c See on this head, some of the poems of Robert Browning; a writer who, by way of fishing for a “fit audiance”, has taken Obscurity into his poetical system, not merely, like Coleridge, as a sometimes “welcome guest”, but as a tenth Muse—and this to the exclusion, very frequently, of the whole orthodox Nine. And lo! has he not his reward? For every literary mystic who would be credited with a capacity for seeing farther than other men,—not indeed through a milestone, but through a poetic myth,—has become his critical worshipper! But why his worshipper? Not because of the light that is verily in him, but because of the darkness.

Hence one of these partisan admirers of his genius, when poring after the meaning of some of his poems (of them always the most cherished) may not unaptly be likened to a dapper cockney who, in his suburban rambles, stands staring hard into some turbid pond, which he calls a lake, in the extravagant hope of beholding a shining fish lurking somewhere at the bottom. At last he sees, or thinks he sees, something that may be a fish, though not a very shining one;—far down there amongst the weeds, or between those stones half embedded in the mud. “It must be a fish”, says he to himself; “what else can it be? A fish it most certainly 262b is—and just as certainly must it be, if one could only get it near enough, a fish of the rarest and most ravishing beauty! What eyes are these of minx?”

But admirers of this sort are never true lovers of true poetry. They are intellectually devoted to nothing beyond the mere make-believe (or as Shakspere would have phrased it, the seeming) of a nice men- tal singularity—an exclusive ideal refinement. And from this cause it comes, that any author who happens to be plentifully beshadowed with obscurities, is sure to attach them in shoals; because, moreover, these very obscurities are the vantage grounds—or rather the fastnesses— of their pretensions to a deeper critical insight than their literary neighbors.

For myself, however, I would as lief have my mouth charged with sawdust as be treated to poetry that I cannot make sense of upon some ground either of fact or fable. I care not whence it may bring its freight, so long as this pertains to humanity, old or new, and answers to some felt liberty of the imagination, or probable condition of the soul. I care not how wild it may be, so long as it exhibits, or indicates, some prevailing tendency of thought or passion, though only idiosyncratic, as in the case of an individual, or merely modal, as in the case of a peculiar state of society. Nay, I care not even though it be mad, if it be naturally so,—the dis- -tempered semblance of a real distemper,—and thence intelligible to something within us. But to have sheer absurdity or wilful nonsense palmed upon me for Poetry, puts me beyond my patience, and makes me bristle into a critic.

Not the less am I aware, however, that a certain ideal 263b vagueness is sometimes an element of great beauty in Poetry. But then it must come naturally out of and be warranted by the subject, and not for its own sake be superinduced upon it. It is, in fact, in its integrity, but the result of a true, though subtle, seeing and siezure, in a dark matter, whether terrible or pathetic, of what lies in most,—potentially, and tends most to determine the proper suggestive treatment of what, in the ocular mode of its being, may be either too appalling, or too withdrawn, or too elusive for picture. But this is a very different thing from affected or wilful obscurity, which is always a mere trick, and much upon a par with the smutching of one’s face for the meagre purpose of fright- -ening a child or of bewildering a simpleton.

In the description of the Horse of Death in the Apocalypse, the visionary effect of the epithet “pale” affords a striking example of the kind of vagueness I mean. Now pale, as applied to the color and substance of the horse of such a rider, is the word of all others best calculated to impress us with an idealism at once vague, dreary, and terrible; and the more terrible, and even vast, because of the dreary vagueness.

In like manner, the dreadful sublimity of Milton’s description of Death sitting at the gates of Hell, results mainly, if not entirely, from the terrible vagueness of the images.

268f T

he man who can say memorable things has no need to blush for not doing, or having done, memorable actions,—feats of material skill or of the fleshly arm, or even such as are prompted by moral sentiments; for so surely as thought is the father of all responsible act, so surely will the former beget the latter, with a reproductiveness both overt and esoteric, and thus make the spiritual harvest of such a man’s life, in its prolonged results, a heaping measure which is perpetually doubling itself,—and this it may be at a compound rate. Old Lattimer’s saying at the stake: “Be of good cheer, brother Ridley; we shall this day light such a candle in England, as by God’s mercy shall never be put out”, was a greater feat than his martyrdom. “Homer’s words are as costly to Homer, as Agamemnon’s deeds are to Agamemnon”, said Emerson; and he might have added, When such deeds are enchased with such words, their value is doubled, or more than doubled, because thereby invested with the starry stuff of immortality. Beautifully sung or recounted exploits, though fabulous, are of a worth higher and more exemplary, than the like actions as recorded in the dry pages of a chronicle. The first inspire us, though we know them to be fictions; the last affect us but slightly, if at all, though we believe them to be facts. And thus it comes about, that a great book—a 269b book glowing with spirit and alive with power—is not to be regarded as a great deed in itself merely, but as the matrix in perpetuity—the incunabula of innumerable great deeds, spiritual and corporeal, through the infinite generations of perhaps interminable ages. Who, then, would not rather have written Hamlet or Othello than have won the battle of Waterloo, or have wasted his blood and valor in contributing to the repulse at Inkermann? Would not Mr. Halloran himself? If he would not, being somewhat of a Poet, I pity him exceedingly. To me, indeed (although I have reason to know, that I am by nature much more warlike than he, and could knock him into “almighty smash” in almost any mode of warfare) it seems a species of profanation almost brutal, to disparage the fame of a great Poet by parading against it that of even the greatest warrior; or to defer the claims of a great book to those of a great battle—a sort of matter that is mostly rendered memorable, not by the worth or even the permanency of its moral results, or results of any kind, but by the public wrongs it led to, or by the horrid excess of its bloodshed.

Why, so far from being in anywise [unclear] prosaic in their structure, Shelley’s best pieces ring all over with the like bell-metal, with the most wildly musical lines and variously modulated cadences, and are less like prose than those of any other poet of his day, with the single exception of Coleridge.
