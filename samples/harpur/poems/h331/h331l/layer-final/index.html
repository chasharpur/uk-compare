T

hough thus detatched and published by itself, for particular reasons, the ensuing Poem is the XVI of a series of “Rhymed criticism with Prose Notes.” The metrical style of these pieces generally, (and of this amongst them) may not, to all readers, be wholly pleasing at first. Those especially who still think that Pope and his associates were our model versemen, will be apt, I doubt not, to pronounce it loose: yet I have chosen it advisedly. It is intended to be something betwixt the heroic rhyme of Dryden and that of Cowper, with also a running dash in it, of the manner of the Elder Ma[s]ters. Hence, though I have partially avoided the precise couplet balance of Pope, as being in itself, if continuously adheared to in a poem of any length, a mere mille- fluous monotony; I have yet considered that somewhat of such a balance should be maintained, recurrently, or upon the whole, in our regular heroic rhyme. In other words, this balance, or some- what approximating to it, should only be frequently varied—by unexpected breaks in the metres for the sake of a stronger emphasis, or by running over from the rhyme into a more im- petuous cadence. But in thus giving freedom, and room for invention, to the formal manner of these pieces, I have been careful, on the other hand, not to slide too deeply into that blank- verse-rhymed style (if I may coin a term for it) which was so much used by both Keats and Shelly; because from the meeting of extremes, however strikingly it may have indicated, as in fact it did the prior existence of an opposite abuse.

1) The pathetic in poetry (and in all fiction) is never so truly affectual as when it is perfectly unforced both in subject and manner. To be supremely exquisite, it must be given to flow naturally out of the matter in hand, not be cast about like a mourning cloak. Hence whenever a poet takes up his pen of set purpose to search our hearts to the very core, it is more than likely that he will only search our patience to an unpleasant degree, or disgust us with some bloated mockery of sorrow. Any undertaker can bespangle a coffin with tinsel-headed nails; to bedrop it with tears is another matter, of which nature, and not artifice, must be the principal getter-up.

In so much does the truest pathos result from an unforced treatment of a doleful matter, that we are always most nearly touched by such pathetic descriptions as appear to come in quite incidentally. There is a fine example of this in Henry Taylor’s Philip Van Artevilde. I know of no passage in any author, more sweetly sorrowful; more holy in the moral beauty of its sadness. It is a description in death, or two children, who have perished of famine during the seige of Ghent, and is spoken by Clara the sister of the hero.

I looked upon them long, and for a while I wished myself their sister, and to lie With them in death as they did with each other. I thought that there was nothing in the world I could have loved so much: and then I wept.

2) See on this head some of the poems of Robert Browning; a writer who, by way of fishing for a “fit audience,” has taken Obscurity into his poetical system, not merely as a sometimes “welcome guest,” but as a tenth Muse—and this to the exclusion, very frequently, of the whole orthodox Nine. And lo! he has his reward. Every literary mystic who would be credited with a capacity for looking further than other men—not indeed through a milestone, but through a poetical myth—has become his critical worshipper,—the worshipper, not of the light that is verily in him, but of the darkness.

H

ence one of these wholesale admirers of his genius, when poring after the meaning of some of his poems (of them the most cherished) may be aptly likened to a cockney staring into some deep but turbid pond, in the hope of seeing a shining fish lurking somewhere at the bottom of it. At last he sees, or thinks he sees, something that may be a fish: far down there among the weeds, or between those stones half imbedded in the mud. “It must be a fish,” says he to himself: what else can it be? A fish it certainly is—and if so, it must just certainly be one of the rarest beauty! What eyes I have!

B

ut admirers of this sort are never true lovers of true poetry. They are intellectually devoted to nothing but the seeming of a nice mental singularity—an exclusive ideal refinement. Hence any author who happens to be plentifully beshadowed with obscurity is sure to attack them in shoals; because these very obscurities are the vantage grounds—or rather the fastnesses of their pretensions to a deeper critical insight than their literary neighbours.

F

or myself, however, I would as lief have my mouth charged with sawdust, as to be treated to poetry that I cannot make sense of, upon some ground either of fact or fable. I care not whence it may bring its freight, so long as this pertains to humanity, old or new, and answers to some felt liberty of the imagination or probable condition of the soul. I care not how wild it may be, so long as it exhibits, or indicates, some prevailing tendency of thought or passion. Nay, I care not though it be mad, if it only be naturally so—the destempered semblance of a real distemper, and thence intelligible to something within us. But to have sheer absurdity or wilful nonsence palmed upon me for poetry, puts me beyond my patience, and makes me bristle into a critic.

N

ot the less am I aware, however, that a certain ideal va- gueness is sometimes an element of great beauty in poetry. But then it must come naturally out of, and be warranted by the subject, and not for its own sake be superinduced upon it. It is, in fact, in its integrity, but the result of a true, though subtle seeing and siezure, in a dark terrible matter, of what lies inmost,—potentially, and tends most to determine that outward expression of character, which may still be, itself, too elusive for picture. But this is a very different thing from affected or wilful obscurity, which is always a mere trick, and mostly but on a par with the smutching of one’s face for the purpose of bewildering a simpleton.—In the description of the horse of Death in the Apocalypse, the visionary affect of the epithet “pale” affords a striking example of the kind of vagueness I mean. Pale, as applied to the color and substance of the horse of such a rider, is the word of all others, best calculated to impress us with an idealism at once vast and dreary, vague and terrible; and the more vast and terrible because of its dreary vagueness.

) A ready method of trying whether rhyme be indeed poetry, is to turn it into prose by casting out the rhymes and substituting their synonyms; and if on being passed through the process a passage appears thoroughly dispirited, it could never have been poetry proper; and if it retains no golden cadence—no profluence of expression—no bar of invulnerable melody, it could never have been true verse. Very little of our recent English poetry will bear this test both ways. Should it still retain enough of soul to indicate its ideality (and it will rarely do this); then, as verse, in nine cases out of ten, it will be utterly condemnable. In the matter of versiflcation especially, our newest poets, and even their critics, with but few exceptions, have assuredly adventured upon a voyage to the dogs. A glib, prosaic wash of words is mistaken for metrical ease; ruggedness for strength; uncouthness for nerve; an utter lawlessness for something even better than the fine olden violence which always had method in its madness, and poise in its wildest indulgence; and lastly, the absence of all congruity of rhythm, for a very perfection of it. That, at all events, cannot be a masterly, well-compacted rhythm, the prosodical identity of which hangs and oscillates as it were between good and bad reading and which is often missed even in the best, or lies wholly beyond its redemption. In fine, no rhythm can be good, much less perfect, which does not, of itself, carry out the voice of the reader, and set it swimming, clogless and buoyant, as upon a flowing river of harmony,—now smooth and all but still,—anon sliding—rapid— impetuous, according to the varying of the subject under it; or which has not, when wi[l]fully wild, some concealed artistic restriction, underlying and counterpoising, its very wilfulness: as in the following example from Coleridge:— T

Moreover, observe the utter grotesqueness of such an association of things—lightning and laughters! Why, a very bombshell and a lot of apple-dumplings, dished up together, and all smoking in company, would be every whit as well sorted in nature, and quite as congruous in appearance! Then “baby roses”! Why not rosebuds? which have at least this advantage over baby roses, that they are exempt from all liability to squall even in fancy. In short, a baby rose is just what a very little rose were likely to be called by a very precocious little girl, and this likelehood affords the best test of the poetic value of such a conceit. Th

isposed to decry Tennyson’s genius generally. I admire many of his poems, and even my dislike to others of them is, perhaps, purely natural—that is, constitutional. He is an old world ”towney”—a dresser of parterres, and peeper into Parks. I am a man of the woods and mountains—a wielder of the axe, and mainly conversant with aboriginal nature. Hence Poetry, with him, should be nice and dainty rather than wise and hearty; while, to effect my admiration, it must be free, bold, and open, even at the risk of being rude.
