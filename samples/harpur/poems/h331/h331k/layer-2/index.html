(1.) The pathetic in poetry (as in all fiction) is never so truly effective as when it is perfectly unforced, both in subject and manner. To be supremely exquisite, it must be given to flow naturally out of the subject in hand, and not be cast about it like a mourning cloak. Hence, whenever a poet takes up his pen of set purpose to search our hearts to the very core, it is more than likely, that he will only search our patience to an unpleasant degree, or disgust us with some vile and bloated mockery of sorrow. Any undertaker can bespangle the lid of a coffin with tinsel-headed nails: to bedrop it with tears is another matter, of which nature, and not artifice, must be the principal getter up. In so much does the truest pathos result from an unforced treatment of a doleful matter, that we are always most nearly touched by such pathetic descriptions as appear to come in quite incidentally. There is a fine example of this in Henry Taylor’s Philip Van Artevelde. I know of no passage in any author, more sweetly sorrowful; more holy in the moral depth and beauty of its sadness. It is an account of the appearance in death, of two children, who have perished of famine during the seige of Ghent, and is spoken by Clara the sister of the hero.

He plucked aside the curtain of the couch, And there two children’s bodies lay composed. 172 They seemed like twins of some ten years of age, And they had died so nearly both together, He could not say which first: and being dead, He put them, for some fanciful affection, Each with its arm about the other’s neck; So that a fairer sight I had not seen Than those two children, with their little faces So thin and wan, so calm, and sad, and sweet. I looked upon them long, and for awhile I wished myself their sister, and to lie With them in death as they did with each other. I thought that there was nothing in the world I could have loved so much; and then I wept.

Again:—A pathetic effect in poetry is never more entire than when, by the seeming absuptness of unmixed passion, it is left, as it were, somewhat short of itself—but suggestively so; or than when, in its means, it is brought bluffly out, and as suddenly terminated, like a natural crag,—upon whose rigid verge we are left hanging, in the agony of our sympathy, as over some dim unmeasured profound of loss unspeakable. No doubt this fulness of effect is often thus achieved through the suggestion of Art; but then it is true art, itself the suggestion of Nature. There is a 173 short poem by Wordsworth, which will strikingly illustrate my meaning.

Lucy.

She dwelt among the untrodden ways Beside the springs of Dove, A Maid whom there were none to praise, And very few to love.

A violet by a mossy stone Half hidden from the eye,— Fair as a star, when only one Is shining in the sky.

She lived unknown, and few could know When Lucy ceased to be: But she is in her grave—and oh, The difference to me!

Here this exquisite little poem ends—or rather breaks off, as if the poet could proceed no farther through the urgency of his tears; and here both Nature and Art declare that it should terminate, however abruptly. The entireness of the pathetic effect would be manifestly impaired by the addition of a single word. And hence there is no 174 other poem in the language, perhaps, more suggestive of the conclusion, that true Nature and true Art are identical in their essence. (2.) See on this head some of the poems of Robert Browning; a writer who, by way of fishing for a “fit audiance”, has taken Obscurity into his poetical system, not merely, like Coleridge, as a sometimes “welcome guest”, but as a tenth Muse—and this to the ex- -clusion, very frequently, of the whole orthodox Nine. And lo! he has his reward. Every literary mystic who would be credited with a capacity for looking further than other men—not indeed through a milestone, but through a poetical myth—has become his critical worshipper. But why his worshipper? Not because of the light that is verily in him, but because of the darkness. Hence one of these wholesale admirers of his genius, when poring after the meaning of some of his poems (of them always the most cherished) may be well likened to a dapper cockney who, in his suburban rambles, stands staring hard into some turbid pool, which he calls a lake, in the extravagant hope of beholding a shining fish lurking somewhere at the bottom. At last he sees, or thinks he sees, something that may be a fish though not a very shining one;—far down there amongst the weeds, or between those stones 175 half embedded in the mud. “It must be a fish“, says he to himself; “what else can it be? A fish it most certainly is—and just as certainly must it be, if one could only get it near enough, a fish of the rarest and most ravishing beauty. What eyes I have!” But admirers of this sort are never true lovers of true poetry. They are intellectually devoted to nothing beyond the mere make-believe, or as Shakspere would phrased it the seeming of a nice mental singularity—an exclusive ideal refine- -ment. And from this cause it comes, that, any writer who happens to be plentifully beshadowed with obscurities is sure to attach them in shoals; because, moreover, these very obscurities are the van- -tage grounds—or rather the fastnesses—of their pretensions to a deeper critical insight than their literary neighbors. For myself, however, I would as lief have my mouth charged with sawdust, as be treated to poetry that I cannot make sense of, upon some ground either of fact or fable. I care not whence it may bring its freight, so long as this pertains to humanity, old or new, and answers to some felt liberty of the imagination, or probable condition of the soul. I care not how wild it may be, so long as it exhibits, or indicates, some prevailing tendency of thought or passion though only idiosyncratic, as in the case of an individual, or mere modal, as in the case of a peculiar state of society. Nay, I care not even though it be mad, if it only be naturally so—the distempered semblance of a real distemper, and thence intelligible to something within us. But to have sheer absurdity or wilful nonsense palmed upon me for poetry, puts me beyond my patience, and makes me 176 bristle into a critic. Not the less am I aware, however, that a certain ideal vagueness is sometimes an element of great beauty in poetry. But then it must come naturally out of and be warranted by the subject, and not for its own sake be superinduced upon it. It is, in fact, in its integrity, but the result of a true though subtle seeing and siezure in a dark matter, whether terrible or pathetic, of what lies inmost,—potentially, and tends most to deternmine the proper suggestive treatment of what, in the ocular mode of its being, may be either too appalling, or too withdrawn, or too elusive for picture. But this is a very different thing from affected or wilful obscurity, which is always a mere trick, and, as such, much upon a par with the smutching of one’s face for the purpose of frightening a child, or of bewildering a simpleton. In the description of the horse of Death in the Apocalypse the visionary effect of the epithet “pale“ affords a striking example of the kind of vagueness I mean. Now pale, as applied to the color and substance of the horse of such a rider, is the word of all others, best calculated to impress us with an idealism at once vague dreary, and terrible; and the more terrible, and even vast, because of its dreary vagueness. In like manner, the dreadful sublimity of Milton’s description of Death sitting at the gates of Hell, results mainly, if not entirely, 177 from the terrible vagueness of the images.

A ready method of trying whether rhyme be indeed poetry is to turn it into prose by casting out the rhymes and substituting their synonyms; and if on being passed through this process a passage appears thoroughly dispirited, it could never have been poetry proper; and if it retains no golden cadence—no profluence of expression—no bar of invulnerable melody, it could never have been true verse. Very little of our recent English poetry will bear this test both ways.
