V.

Dryden.

Even as French frippery exposed his times, Art left small room for Nature in his rhymes. o And yet is it careless Art, that sell Of Waller’s school—but a far mightier flame Was his, illumining the skies of Fame.

Though scarce a genius, in his mind intense There wrought the alchymic power of native sense. A satirist—but in satire chiefly great Because the slave of Jealousy and Hate. Well versed in selfish natures, he could hit The lurking purpose with the beam of wit; x And through himself, with skill unerring, draw Some fellow sycophant—some venal wretch Whose very nature, spiced with phrases terse, Lies, like a mummy, coffin’d in his verse.

None more than he accomplished to translate: And did he steal, adopt, or imitate, Much breadth of judgment to the work he brought, Enlarged each image, strengthen every thought.

Proud of the logic of his pointed rhyme, He nor affects, nor reaches, the sublime: 62a If we except the peerless festal song, Melting to pity and love the frantic heart of Wrong; And may not, sometimes, venture to be praise As such, the fury of his jingling Plays.

Not much I love him—yet, perforce, admire The mounting rage of his material fire! And prodigal of verse though coarse of phrase, Built durably are all his better Lays His lines, like brooks, now warble sweet and low And now, like mountain floods, they thunder as they go.+

+ It has sometimes struck me since this Criticism was written, that, from my being at that time too much under certain modern literary influences, I hardly did “glorious John” justice. Still I think he was deficient in that fine breadth of eye and that open relish of heart for the simplicities of Nature, which are the surest indications, as the fruits of them are the most lasting manifestations, of a thorough poetical genius. He was wanting in them, for in modernizing Chaucer, who is streaked all over with evidences of their morning gladness, he conventionalised his pictures into mere stage scene-paintings. And wanting in these, he was wanting in the most enduring tendencies of the Poet; they bring such simply because they cannot find their objects in modes—nor in anything short of the immortalities of Nature. C.H.
