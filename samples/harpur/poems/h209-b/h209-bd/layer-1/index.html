The Kangaroo Hunt: or A Morning in the Mountains.

Part I.

Here in the Forest, afar and free From the City’s enervate luxury— Luxury that ne’er attains To comfort, swallowing all its gains— That seldom knows, and ne’er enjoys Or helth, or peace, for dust and noise; And reached not by those senseless rules That Folly learns in Fashion’s schools;— When first beyond the skiey trees That fringe some dawning height, Aurora spreads her traceries Of grey and gauzy light— Whilst yet the western stars may be Glimmering far and fitfully, As, tarrying, they were fain to pay Apostate homage to the day— Glad as the spirit of the song Poured by the birds from every spray, (1.) And fresh and lithe as are the young Pure airs that from the dayspring throng, And bear in their salubrious play That morning merriment along— From a peace-curtained sleep we rise Such as can never comfort Wrong, To look abroad with grateful eyes, While sallying forth to till the soil Or urge in the Forest some manly toil.

Or while Euroka (2.) first displays His burning rim on the ancient hill, And the yet dusky world o’erlays 91 With fanwise spreading, arrowy rays, And, rising graual, greatens still— Greatens, till in full revealed Like a vast and flaming shield Poised on that glorious eastern height, He floods abroad his golden light In one unbroken mass immense Of life-essential influence: Unveiling the glades of the forest then, And the winding depths of each shadowy glen And with a quick up-bickering gleam Sheathing every tripping stream— While thus Euroka riseth red, Up, even to the kingly head Of some proud eminence we climb, Where, high amid the crags sublime, Australia’s yet unchristened Muse,— A wandering Spirit of beauty rare, Loves oft to gem her streaming hair With heaven’s selectest dews, And scarf her bosom bright and bare With a robe of morning’s richest hues; Giving the while to all objects there, All sounds,—the water-drip, just heard— The hum of insect—voice of bird,— To every echo and every air A poetry unfelt elsewhere.

Lo, bodying forth to Fancy’s eye The glenward hanging clouds among, Her trance-born Train goes gliding by. First, orbed as in a floating sphere Of rainbow-colored splendor, strong Though simple Truth, and his confrere, Even Beauty, both star-crowned, appear,— Twin Eons of all deathless song. Then all in love, though calm of mien, On these still gazing as along She paceth, Contemplation’s seen 92 With dark-eyed Memory hand in hand. Then, followed by a bright-haired band Of Cherub Joys,—lo, Temperance And Chastity, with air serene, In sage fraternity advance. Next pass, with sturdy Industry And Concord for their sure defence, Those philosophic sisters three— Patience, Content and Constancy. Here baby-featured Innocence To Wisdom meditation-pale, And lofty-browed Benevolence, Laughs out her happy tale. There stalks with rapture-lengthened stride Prophetic Feeling, side by side With Liberty,—both eagle-eyed. Then Duty as a shepherd clad, And Piety sedately glad, Precede Integrity long tried, And inward-smiling Peace, and she, A pleading angel,—Charity. And last of all the happy throng Two wingèd Spirits seem to speed, As blameful of the past were they For hindering them so long;— Even Love and Hope, who seem alway About to do thier happiest deed And breathe their sweetest song.

Many an eminent hill sublime Has this Great Isle, like this we climb: Beyond the loud Napean they rise, And by old Hawksbury’s billowy tide, And by the rapid Hunter’s side, And where the Wallombi darkling lies, Or with a sudden and strenuous chide, Or with a broken and lowly moan, Adown its rocky channel lies;— 93 Girt with wood to the craggy cone Up they go into the skies, O’er hills and vales and prairies lone Staring, as with stony eyes.

Now from some Crag brushed by the green Fresh sprays, wind-floated to and fro,— The extremest sprays of stately treen That find their roots in the ravine A dizzy depth below, Send the glad eye their vistas through Out and oer a glorious View! Here, veined with brooks that glisten and glow, Wide, ever-verdant dales, between The mountains, like green bays, are seen— Between the mountains blue; Till brown by distance, and then grey, And into mere gaps narrowing, they Behind those folding mountains go Upon their hidden way. Or else located plains appear As garnished by a genial year With Husbandry’s fair tribes—the soil Confessing well the Settler’s toil. Where into nooks the hollows run, There well-dressed gardens smile While orchards on the higher mould Their globy treasures manifold Hang forth in clusters to the sun Whose course in heaven is now begun: And where lagoons erewhile Slept, and where yet the blind creek (3.) strays, Broad fields of tall and tassalled maize Stretch rank on rank away, Even as embattled legions stand Ere Slaughter’s thunder-bearing hand Hath shattered their array. And where, ’mid all, a line of Homes As from a verdant ocean looms, 94 Hearth-smokes in wreathen pillars grow High in the stirless air— Ascending peacefully heavenward so Like metaphors of prayer: While from the steaming yards about The spotty kine are dotting out, And from the fold on yon bare slope That bounds the cultivated scope, Forth there floods a fleecy flock, E’en as from some mountain lock A sudden inundation flows In volume, spreading as it goes. And as the eye outruns it through Inbroken depths of forest view, Or ‘tween some far creek’s oaken rows, Fresh objects rise,—upcrowding small And smaller still, like spectral shows There Vaguely intervolving all.

But hark! on yon high waving bough That strokes the thin rack as it passeth now, The purple-head (4.) with a flutter gay Gurgles forth his roundelay; And oh! in his melodious moods What thrilling meaning findeth aye Some listening Beauty of the Woods! Again his passion-tingling wings Quiver, and spread;— again he flings Abroad the tale of his innocent thrall; When, answering, from the lower dells A many-warbled chorus swells: The pipe of the brown thrush, to call His wandered mistress lifted high— The wren’s soft trill—the parrot’s cry— With the sounding troll of the valorous (5.) pie, And the bell-bird’s pensive tinkle (6.)—all Mingling in melody, rise and fall; or blend Or Jehu (7.) in a fitful smack Ends his long note; the piercing crack 95 Rages along with a gathering shock As Echo outstarts from her cave in the rock, To wanton around in the hollow breeze And load the ear with her impish glees, Till from below the thronging strain Of the happy birds peals up again.

But oft these near delights foregoing, Soon as the morning star is glowing, The earliest Hunter’s call Is passed along from house to house Some tardy inmate to arouse— Until the younger neighbors all Are forth, each followed by his hounds; Then quickly gathered in a knot Upon the old accustomed spot, They wake the Morn with merry sounds, As off they hie the Forest through To hunt the bounding (8.) Kangaroo.

Far from the City’s dust and noise, Such are the inland dweller’s joys; Joys leaving him little besides to need In yielding him that which is riches indeed! What flows for him from a hundred springs, And rides on every breeze’s wings,— That priceless part of Nature’s wealth, That inward morning sunshine—Health! The sacred source of usefulness— Best smoother of life’s rugge Best lightener of its worst distress! Which absent, even young Love hath Nor will to serve, nor power to bless! Which absent, the imperial crown But weighs its purple wearer down! For to Disease even Fortune’s rays Are warmthless, like the fen-fire’s blaze; And Glory’s animative power 96a Serves but to throne Regret, as showing How flies unseized som star crowned hour— Is only a sunbeam glowing To the heart of a cankered flower.

Notes to Part I.

(1.) It is a very prevalent, but an erroneous notion, that the Australian birds are almost destitute of song. Of indeed, our birds are wanting in sweetness of voice, and their song is yet more often defective, perhaps, in volume and continuity; but this is very far from being the case universally. The magpie, for a familiar instance, has not only a merry, but a most musical chaunt. One set, in particular, of gurgling undertones which this birds warbles as it were to itself, when perched up in the cool,—that is, in some dense-topped oak or apple tree, of a hot summer noontide,—is melodious to a degree almost incredible. There is also a dwarf kind of magpie, sometimes called ‘the musician’, which is notable for the extreme beauty of its voice. When several birds of this kind are assembled together in the same tree, or clump of trees, a concert of sweet sounds is the wonted consequence,—sounds mellow and soothing as the purer tones of the flute, with occasional clarionet-like outgushes that are enchantingly spirited and musical. Of the notes of the true butcher-bird or “old soldier,” I have heard it admitted, even by persons somewhat prejudiced against all things Australian, that in richness and volume they compete with those of the nightingale, though they fail to sustain the comparison, in respect of a certain varied and impetuous continuity. Several of our smaller birds too— birds yet nameless and fast becoming extinct—have a surprising variety of notes; and when many of such birds are heard (as I have heard them) warbling together, the effect is strikingly musical. Even the cries of the many parrot kinds, though somewhat harsh and discordant, are yet exceedingly gladsome. When, indeed,—up aloft in the apple and gum trees, when there happens to be, of a summer evening, a great gathering together of the higher flying kinds— those which never alight on the ground, the blue-mountainer, with the greater and lesser green parrakeet—there is a mad merriment of sound, afloat the air on all sides, that can be only well characterised as being bewilderingly hilarious. In newly settled localities, of a Spring or Autumn morning, I have heart the whole forest vocally alive and electric as it were, with sh, intermixing peans of birds, than which, in many 97 instances, nothing could be more sweet and joyous,—and certainly nothing more primatively exhilarating.

(2.) Euroka is an aboriginal name of the sun. It is at once euphonious and robust, and therefore as soundingly indicative as a mere vocable could be, of the golden progression and godlike part of of that paramount luminary: for which reasons, I have here ventured, for once at least, to adopt it.

(3.) The channels or watercourses of summer-shrunk lagoons are called blind creeks. The term, I believe, is of Colonial growth, and very appropriate; these channels being often so matted over with sedge and other water grasses, as not to be distinguishable from the general bed of the dry part of the lagoon—until the novice in “such branches of learning”, has marched perhaps, like Richmond and his host (though in a different sense) “into the bowells of the land”. I have heard the term also applied to mountain gulches that run only in rainy seasons. Blind, that is without the light of water, as seen generally.

(4.) The purplehead is a little elegantly formed bird, having a remarkably various and pleasing song. Not knowing its name (if it has other than an aboriginal one) I have here called after the most striking part of its plumage.

(5.) The Australian magpie called the ‘black and white’, to distinguish it from another species less spotted, certainly deserves the epithet of ‘valorous’ by which it is characterised in the text: especially in breeding time, when it is fierce even to fearlessness. Not only will the cock magpie readily give battle to any bird of prey that may approach nearer to his young than he is inclined to consider consistent with honesty of intention, but, assisted by his mate, he will pursue to a distance, and sometimes kill the common bird hawk. Nay, he will make good his valorous repute by attacking even the lords of the creation. The author has had his hat dashed off by a bird of this kind, while passingly unwittingly under the tree in which it had built his nest, to his no small alarm at the moment, he being wholly unexpectant of any such pugnacious salute, and quite unadvised also, as to the nature of the assailant. On an occasion like this, the parentally aroused magpie will come whizzing down at you with a really startling impetuosity, and you will hear his horny mandibles snap close against your cheek perhaps, with the severity of an iron rat-trap. Any one familiar with the country must have remarked the exceeding bravery of our native magpie.

(6.) This remarkable little bird—the bell-bird—is chiefly to be found in such 98 mountain ravines as reserve a constant supply of water—its note being an infallible assurance of the vicinity of that element; that it is lying in the rock-tanks somewhere at hand, however hiddenly, or noiselessly sliding through some concealed cleft. Hence, though the tone of its note is ‘pensive’ in seeming, it is exceedingly grateful to the thirsty wayfarer. Each bird emits but a single sound, like the one tinkle of a very small bell; but when there are many of them in the vicinity, as there mostly are, disposed at unequal distances in the surrounding trees, an irregular chime “most musical, most melancholy”, is kept ringing about one with an effect that is very wild and singular, and though always by association lonesome, yet pleasing in the extreme.

(7.) The bird whose note is here described is an inhabitant of densely wooded dells having water in them, and of thick cedar brushes near mountain creeks; for, like its note, like that of the bell-bird, infallibly indicates the vicinity of water. The bird itself is commonly called the ‘coachman’; a name evidently suggested by the resemblance of its note to that peculiar whistle with which carriage horses are sometimes incited forward, and by the further circumstance of its ending in a sharp snap nearly as loud as the crack of a whip, and very much like it. I have written Jehu instead, thinking it equally characteristic, and certainly (though I am anything but squeamish in such matters) a trifle more poetic.

(8.) We say the bounding deer—the bounding horse, and so forth; but how peculiarly emphatic is the epithet when applied, as here, to the motions of the kangaroo!
