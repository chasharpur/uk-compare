POEMS BY CHARLES HARPUR.

I.

The Ineffable.

Words are the special dies of Thought, And well they coin it, as in gold; But Feeling may not so be brought Within their subtlest mould.

With minds on some great theme intent, We seek to them at length; And lo, we utter all we meant, To wonder at their strength!

But when we through them thus would wreak The expression, hardly sane, Of highest joy or grief—how weak We find them, and how vain!

Acting the bliss of Feeling, Thought May catch her very air, And as if to her heaven upcaught, Its glories thus declare:

“I flood with joy, like Horeb’s rill Touched by the Prophet’s rod! I am all light and blissful will, Like a new thought of God!”

But Feeling’s happy self the while, All silently apart, May only wear a glowing smile, And breathe as from the heart.

Or Thought, to imitate he woe, May wildly cry aloud: “My peace is wasting as I go Even like a summer cloud:

My spirit hath so deep a wound, No cure might be devised; And spilt like water on the ground Is all my life hath prized!

While Feeling’s self may only shake Her woful head apart, To know how vainly words would break The silence of her heart.

But most when Music’s vocal fire Comes purpling through the air, Making it such as Gods respire, (And Gods perhaps are there.)

How vainly would the subtlest wit Word-picture as they roll, The clouds of glory it hath lit Like sunset in our soul.

The deep of the Ineffable— A Deep that none may sound, Pours round us with its breathing spell— Immeasurably round.
