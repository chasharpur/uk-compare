III.

Thought, Feeling, and Language.

Words are the special dies of Thought, And well they coin it, as in gold; But Feeling cannot so be brought Within their subtlest mould.

Note.—Language is for thought more than for feeling. All our thoughts can lay hold of it, to some extent; but many of our feelings can only be conveyed through a look, a gesture, a sigh, an air of the head, or a posture of the body. As the symbols of even our subtlest thoughts, words are often most searching and repro- ductive; but as the outward expressions of our finer feelings, they are almost always inadequate. We embody in them, even our strongest mental conceptions, and wonder at their strength; but we endeavour to make them the media of many of our sensations, and are sick at heart because of their weakness. Thought, im- personating the gladness of Feeling, may shout aloud: “I am overflowing as with milk and honey! I am all light, like a thought of God!” whilst Feeling, herself, it may be, can only smile and breathe as from the heart. Or, on the other hand, acting her sorrow, Thought may complain mournfully: “I am cut to the heart! I am poured out like water! I am wasting away like a summer cloud!” whilst Feeling, herself, perhaps— without words, without even tears—can only hang the head and die in silence.

Perhaps there is nothing which attests more strikingly, the adequacy of Language for the embodi- ment of Thought, and its comparative inadequacy for the expression of Feeling, than the subject of Music. How many profound reflections as to its effects and influence, can we readily fix in happy and meaningful words? But while listening to its divine breathings, what meet language is there for the vast, dim, God-like sensations of being, or the vague and shadowy, but infinite, feeling of beauty, which they have developed within our souls? There is none. The great deep of the Ineffable, is poured immeasurably around us.
