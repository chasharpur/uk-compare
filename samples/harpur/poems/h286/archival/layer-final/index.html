6c

II.

Matrimonial Assortment.

The strong are mated with the weak, The stable wedded to the vain; And in their offspring (if we seek) The loss is balanced by the gain.

Note.—In matrimony, it would be well if the parties could be so assorted, that the children should be always an improvement upon their parents. So at least do our social aspirations dispose us to think. But it is unlikely that such can ever be the case, beacuse men of the strongest wills and most valiant natures, are generally more attracted by the gentle and timid of the opposite sex, and even by the weak and will-less, if they be beautiful as well, than they are by what are called masculine women. The former are more in contrast to their own natures, and therefore the more pleasing to them. And the ultimate design of this, for there is providential design in all things, seems to be, that the race, upon the whole, should be kept as much upon a level as possible; or rather, that it should make progress altogether—in a bunch—as much as may be. The weak nature of the woman is strengthened in her child, through the robust nature of the husband. And though in this case something may be lost as well as gained; yet what is lost (granting there is a loss) is clearly referable more to the individual than to the race, and vice versa of what is gained.
