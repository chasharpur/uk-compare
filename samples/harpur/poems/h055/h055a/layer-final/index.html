THE OLD BROOK.

Back from a distant Shore The Wanderer had come: He stood beside the Brook once more That cooled his Childhood’s home; And while it warbled then All adown its native Glen, O, why did I, he murmured,—why did I ever roam?

The life of other days Thrills by me in its flow, But then I miss the laughing gaze And coming footsteps low, Of her, the blue-eyed Maid, Who for fancy, as she said, Used to meet me by its side—such a long time ago.

O how might I forget Those happy summer days, When in this woodland nook we met Beneath the bloomy sprays, Whilst thus, unchanged by time, It keeps ringing its moist chime, Like some glad-hearted Truant who singeth as he strays!

Even yet within my mind How warm the memory lies Of all the love so gaily kind— So giddy, yet so wise, Which then, undreaming sin, She kept storing for me in The wild and piercing sweetness of her most merry eyes!

Alas! but they were flown, Those happy days at last; And I was wandering far and lone The sadder for the past; With disenchanted eye Then to view both Earth and Sky Through those unwholesome mists that load the world’s wintry blast.

As star by star is lost, In Ocean’s clouding sky,— Ere half the gulf of Life be crost, As trouble darkens by, How all the golden things To which Fancy lent her wings, Out one by one are blotted from Hope’s reverting eye!

And from more thoughtful years What gain we in their stead? That self-love which nor soothes nor cheers; And (let the truth be said) That knowledge whose stern part Is to harden still the heart; And that cramp wisdom last which so narroweth the head.

But what of Her who made This woodland Brother so dear? Here with another hath she strayed His rural walk to cheer? Or have her then glad eyes Learned to weep ‘neath other skies?— I know not—I but know that she is not with me here!
