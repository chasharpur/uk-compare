The Creek of the Four Graves.

I verse a Settler’s Tale of the Old Times— One told me by our friend, the kindly sage, Old Egremont; who then went forth with four Of his most trusty and adventurous men Into the wilderness;—went forth to seek New streams and wider pastures, for his fast Encreasing flocks and herds;—went forth on foot; For horses then were cattle of too great price To be much ventured upon mountainous routes, And into brush lands perilously pathless.

So went they forth at dawn: and now the sun, That rose behind them as they journeyed out, Was firing with his nether rim a range Of unknown mountains, that, like rampires, towered Full in their front; and his last glances fell Into the glooming forest’s eastern glades In golden masses, transciently, or flashed Down on the windings of a nameless creek, That fringed with oaks and the wild willow, ran Noiselessly on, between the pioneers And those new eminences.

Wilder grew The Scene each moment,—beautifully wilder! For when the sun was all but sunk beyond Those barrier mountains,—then within a breeze That o’er their rigid and enormous backs Deep fleeced with wood, came whispering down, the wide Slant sea of leaves stirr’d in the slanting rays, Stirr’d interdazzlingly, as though the trees That bare them, were all thrilling,—tingling all Even to the roots for very happ[i]ness; So prompted from within, so sentient, seemed The bright quick motion.

Halting wearied, here Our Travellers kindled for their first night’s camp The brisk and crackling fire, which also looked A wilder creature than ’twas elsewhere wont, Because of the surrounding savageness: And soon in pots of tin the tea was made Fragrant and strong, the fresh-sliced rasher broiled On the live embers, and as soon dispatched By the keen tooth of healthful appetite.

And as they supt, birds of new shape and plume And wild strange voice, nestward repairing by, Oft took their wonder; or between the boles Of the upslanting forest trees, they saw, Perched on the bare abutments of those mountains, The wallaroo look forth: till eastward all The view had faded into formless gloom, Night’s front, and westward, the high massing woods, Steeped in a dusk and deepening beauty, lay 479 Heaped all the more distinctly for their darkness Against the twilight heaven, a cloudless depth Yet luminous from the sunset’s fading sp[l]endor; And thus, for a brief interval, they looked Even like a mighty picture of themselves Hung in some vaster world.

Their supper done, The echoes of the solitary place Came, as in wonder, round about, to meet Strange voices, moulding a strange speech, as then Lifted in glee—but to be hushed ere long, As with the night, in kindred darkness, came O’er the adventurers, each and all, a sense Of lurking danger.

But about the fire Soon settled all, and from their fuming pipes Drawing rude satisfaction, grave discourse Of their peculiar business, brought to each A steadier mood that reached into the night.

The simple subject to their minds, at length, Fully discussed, their couches they prepared Of the green tresses of the willows there: And four, as pre-arranged, reposed their limbs Under the dark boughs of the forest, high O’erdoming, and traced out against the clear Wide gaze of heaven; and trustful of the watch Kept near them by their thoughtful Master, soon Slept heavily; whilst every object round Showed slumbrous too;—the circling forest trees, Their foremost boles carved from a crowded mass Less visible, by the watch-fire’s bladed gleams— And even the shaded and enormous mountains, Their bluff brows glooming through the stirless air, Seemed, in their quiet, solemnly asleep: Yea, thence surveyed, the Universe might have seemed Coiled in vast rest;—only that one dim cloud Crept, as with scrawling legs, across the sky; And that the stars all glowingly dispread In their bright companies and group by groups Revealed, as it moved on, high over all, Looked thoughtfully awake.

And now the moon 480 Up from behind an eastern hill was seen Conglobing, till, full orbed and large, she brought He nether border level with its cone, As thereon resting, edge to edge; when straight Its solid bulk seemed momently to grow Half saturate with her radiance; whilst the trees That fringed its outline,—their huge statures dwarfed By distance into brambles, and yet all Clearly defined,—out of her very face Appear[e]d to swell in bold relief, as they Were sculptured from her substance.

Egremont On all this solemn beauty of the night Looked out, still wakeful; for sweet thoughts of home Ingathered to his heart, as by some nice And subtile interfusion that connects The loved and cherished (then the most, perhaps, When past or absent) with the beautiful And lasting things of Nature. So thought, then, The musing Egremont: when suddenly,—hark[! obscured] A bough cracked loudly in a neighbouring brake, And drew at once, as with a larum, all His spirits thitherward.

He listened long With head bent forward, till his held breath grew A pang, and his ears rung. But Silence there Had recomposed her ruffled wings, and now Brooded, it seemed, even stillier than before, Nested in darkness: so that to his mood Of museful memory he recurred ere long, Calmly and reassured.

But there! again! And hark!—O God! have hell’s worst fiends burst up Into the doomèd world? Or whence, it not From diabolic rage, could surge a yell So horrible as that which now affrights And upward sends the shuddering air? Alas! Even worse are near!—For lo, from the long grass And nearer brakes, at once, a semi-belt Of stript and painted Savages divulge Their bounding forms!—full in the flaring light Thrown up then suddenly by the fire, as though Even it had felt the shock the air received From their so terrible cries!
