THE MINSTREL.

Behold the young Minstrel in life’s first essay, Ere the armies of Hope have sustained a defeat, While Spring’s on his cheek in her virgin array, And while strong are his arms and full light are his feet.

The passionate burst of his genius repeats, Like an echo the strains of the minstrels of yore, And the pride of his spirit dwells over the feats Of the bravest—the brightest of heroes no more.

To Fame oft he weaves a wild anthem of fire, And the stormy achievements of the red battle plain; And a strain of devotion ascends from his lyre To that pale beam of glory that rests o’er the slain.

Or on as he wends softer visions arise, Edens that ever are blooming and fair; For he scorns as the stag doth the wolf he out- flies, The thought that would speak of the future a fear.

Lovely forms float around ’mid the rays of desire, And the voice of the turtle is heard in his way; And Hope like an eagle mounts higher and higher And seems at each moment to brighten his day.

But I mark that his feelings are wound up too high To accord in their tenor with this cold worldly state; And I mark in the glance of his dark beaming eye The wild restless sign of a waywardly fate,

Which proclaims soon the fairies of fancy shall be, As the torch melts in distance shall hope dis- appear; And his billowy bosom so wild and so free Shall be yok’d to the vultures of Grief and Des- pair.

Fur[t]her on, now behold him, Fate’s dark cloud assails, And the tombs of his joy croud in Memory’s eye, Whilst she hovers o’er them like a robb’d bird and wails, And wrings from the Minstrel the heart-rending sigh.

He is doom’d hence to wander alone in despair, With a harp want hath broken,—a heart woe hath torn; As yon frost-smitten rose tree shall never more bear, The smiles of his spirit shall never return.

C. HARPUR.
