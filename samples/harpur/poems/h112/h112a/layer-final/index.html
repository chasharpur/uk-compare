SONGS, EPIGRAMS, NOTES, AND OPINIONS, &c., BY CHARLES HARPUR.

No. 12.

1.

Emerson.

His Genius types the Mind of the world’s youth, Large, but too prone of Thought’s new wine to quaff: So strong his sinewy grasp of half the truth, He’s apt to rend it from the other half. Nothing he loves like “Godlike affirmation!”— I quote him, and he says the thing with fire. He holds, too, that we quit our heavenward station Whene’er we reason, to converse with mire. So up he flies; and when involved in cloud, The Mystic cometh to his succor, pat: “We’re symbols housed in symbols,” wild and loud He shouteth; but what can he mean by that?

Note.—An affectedly mystical mode of expression is the plague-spot of Emerson’s manner[.] ”A Gothic cathedral affirms (he says) that it was done by us, and not done by us.” Now it simply affirms, according to his own showing afterards, that is was done by Man while in a state of imagination—transition, perhaps, from the Druidical grove to the mural temple—and why, in the name of simplicity, could he not state the matter in a Christian-like manner? Next to this mystical jargon, the worst thing about him is his inconsistency. He is the least consistent of writers. His affirmations (for he never reasons) are forever flying in each other’s face. I open his book at a venture, and read: “The world must be just.” I close it to open it again, and read: “When the Gods come among men, they are not known. Jesus was not; Socrates and Shakespear were not.” Again: ”Lucy Ashton is another name for fidelity, which is always beautiful and always liable to calamity in this world”—because the world is unjust to it, as the context implies. The world then, according to this showing, is at once just and unjust; for the postulates are at feud— two against one. The first affirms that there is a necessity of justice upon the world, and the other two tend to shew that there is not. It is as though one should say: “Knives are inevitably sharp,” and then: “I went into the wood to cut a stick, but found my knife blunt and unfit for the purpose,” and finally: “Boiled ham is excellent, and requires delicate carving, but knives at mealtime are never sharp.” And thus does he contradict himself in almost every page, with the fervent recklessness of a Beardy; saying all he thinks, without stint or examination, and not so as to make one thought the test, as it were, of another. Hence, a book of his, even at the best, is exactly what might be expected—an intellectual chaos, or galaxy run mad. Nevertheless, he is a great study; only his statements are to be well tried before we accept them. They are too open over-statements. Like a rifleman at a long shot, he aims above the mark, to hit it; but the bullet, being projected with extraordinary force, holds its own all the way, and flies over. Or like an energetic miner who would make too sure, he sinks his shaft deeper than the deposit, and thereby misses it. If, therefore, we believe in him, it must be with much reservation; for though he utters oracles, he also raves nonsense, and not un- frequently in the same breath. And after all, if it is better, as I think, to be men of flesh and blood than it were to be men of granite, the moody isolation of soul and stoical self-reliance, which he so much affects and preaches with such iron boldness, would only fortify us to our hurt, or only avail us, as individuals, to the hurt of Man.
