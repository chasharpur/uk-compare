The House of Folly.

I.

Full in the centre of a region named The Land of Vain Display, a mansion stands, Built by debauched artificers of old For prodigal Pleasure in her palmiest days, The foundress vain of a vain-glorious race, Profuse though needy, arrogant though light And loose of life, and always in their day As greedy of prominence as void of worth. A structure large, and splendid to the eye, Pillared and porched, but meretricious more Than graceful in its style,—with gilded domes, And glittering pinnacles that flash aloft Like giant lances pointed at the sky, And carven windows in their ribbed rows blazing O’er the long colonades. A Mansion large And solid in its seeming still it stands; But inwardly impaired and worn is now The whole huge structure; in its basement most— Knawed through with damps and crumbling unrenewed.

That is the House of Riot: the set place Of much vain speech and simulated mirth, (For true Mirth seldom quits her sylvan lodge) And fleshly feasting. From its unblest door Simplicity, and Strength, and red-lipped Health, And native Cheerfulness, and staunch Resolve, Wise Care, and Prudence, and domestic Peace, And Piety who loves with these to dwell, For ever, as of old, they spurn away: For as their household deities instead Have they installed Extravagance, in the guise Of Bounty, but in truth the slave of self; And false Refinement, sensually nice But vulgar and indelicate of soul; And Infidelity; and the gamester Craft Dressed in the airs and habit of a sage; And Atheism, muffled in a garb Fashioned as ‘twere the mantle of a seer.

So peopled is that House! And Now, while Thought’s Keen kindled spirit lightens through my , soul, Intent to benefit the young, to thee, O Wisdom, do I cry, and thee invoke To aid the voice that I would pour abroad 125 In a melodious though a warning song! So pure the inspiring motive, that ‘twere well Couldst thou inflame it, as it peals along, With lightnings of intelligence, and all Its monitory tones enforce and swell With [voluble] thunders, such as lost-souls hear For ever roaming o’er their guilty Past!

Give heed then, ye who would be early wise My sober numbers, big with thought, shall be Meet robes for Wisdom in her queenliest plight— Yea, though she sat again enthroned as once With Solomon of old. And know ye not What even the reprobate, though godless, knows; That he who in the morning of his life Good counsel scorns—indubitably good, Shall, a grey prodigal, wander forth with Shame, And vain Remorse, and outcast Poverty, Under the lengthening shadows that portend Its sunset, ending in the dark of death.

Wherefore with reason verily I say, That froward-hearted youth who shall esteem The Preacher’s words but as the profitless stones Roughening the forehead of some sterile hill 126 Shall thrive not!—but, a moving pile of rags, In houseless age shall eat the begger’s dole; Or with stark limbs and aching sinews ply The helot’s axe, or bend beneath his yoke; Or at the mill of never-varying toil Even to the dregs of a brute being grind,— Laboring for others more than for himself. And though he be the exception, not the less Shall he be bondsman to Infirmity: Nay, even though greatness fall within his lot, (Which thing may happen in a graceless age) Yet shall he, as did Bacon, through a need Induced by a vain habit of display, Mush up by his glory with the vilest things, And be at once a scoundrel and a lord!

Oh, we should honor Wisdom, since through her The all-contriving One designed and built This boundless world! Yea, she it was who first Elanced the sun on his imperial course, Day’s monarch, vested with celestial fire; Her hand it was that fashioned the round moon And all the golden stars. Or couldst thou soar Up to the throne of God, there were she found 127 In presence, circled with ineffable splendor; Or to the unfathomable gulfs of Hell Down couldst thou plunge, behold! there—even there, Wisdom would would meet thee, though obscured, as when The day in its meridian strength is seen Darkened with storms, that bellow as they roll.

II.

But lo, the House of Riot!—the set place , Of much vain speech, much [riotous display, And fleshly feasting, and pretensive mirth. There congregate the frivolous of soul, As garrulous parrots gather to the snares Set on the borders of the ripening maize. There Frenzy in the name of earnest Joy (A Spirit native but to honest lives), With wild embarrassed brows, o’erflows the cups With purple ruin, blazing inwardly As it were fiercely conscious of its power, While loose-eyed Passion in the plight of Love Inflames his low and animal fires withal. And there, too, Gluttony takes his constant round As seneschal, high heaping every board 128 With palatable death.

That fatal House! Shun it all ye in thought yet young, in heart Unguarded, and all ye of facile wills; Lest, like its host, ye with like years should cast To sow the world for an abundant yield Of pleasure,—and mistaken, only reap An overwhelming load of debt instead— Of debt, distaste, and vanity, and vexation.

Once entered there,—disastrous is the change Wrought in thy nature: a swift evil change! Thy laughter loud, speech boisterous;—but alas, Thy reason silent as the tongue of death; Thine evil passions as the surging flames That, like red flags, flap over the wild front Of some wide forest fire,—sultry and fierce As legioned demons, jubilant;—but all Thy virtues, like the wintry crust that numbs The living brook till it forgets to flow.

Then ‘neath the growing influence of the fierce And fatal spirit of that evil House, 129 So recklessly familiar shalt thou grow With splendid Ruin, as to kneel and kiss Her jewelled hand, and say unto her, Thou Shalt be mine only spouse; and for thy sake, Here see me—ready to impawn at once The pearls of truth—the sapphires of my peace— The rubies of repute;—yea, all with which I late adorned my soul, for lures and gauds And glittering baubles wherewithal to deck My body, dedicate henceforth to thee.

Into her inmost secret chambers then Straight shalt thou follow her—desperately fond: While lovely Phantoms beckon thee along: Lovely—but fleeting as the shapes that crest A morning cloud: lovely—but weird, and quick With mischief,—bright for evil, like that wild Mysterious light which nightly glides about Some shrouded marsh, misleading him who sees Into the foul ways of a darkling death. And passionate airs, intense and sweet as those The enthusiast, when to reason dying, hears In mountain glens, or wooded vales, or near Some lone lake crimsoned by the setting sun, 130 Shall hail thy presence, vapouring all the air With sweetness—and awakening raptures sweet And spirit-elating;—but delusive all As are the hopes of unrequited love: Sweet—sweet;—but cruel as untimely death.

Then with the histories of royal lust In her right hand displayed, and in her left A chronicle in which the eminent damned Are registered as wise, thy yielded heart Shall she baptise in the lewd mysteries Of fashionable folly; and at last Instruct thee, with a devil’s zeal, how best To smother down in thy disnatured soul, As in a quagmire, the prophetic hope That once, with wings like the unwearying stork’s, Would rise and soar from the brute Earth away, Through the Eternal and the Infinite, To rest at length . within those starry gates That lets into the central peace of God.
