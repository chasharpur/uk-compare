No 6

Castle Carnal.

I.

Full in the centre of a region named The Land of Vain Display, a mansion stands, Built by debauched artificers of old For prodigal Pleasure in her palmiest days, The Foundress vain of a vain-glorious race, Profuse though needy, arrogant yet light An loose of character, and always full As greedy of prominence as void of worth. A Structure large and splendid to the eye, Pillared and porched,—but meretricious more Than graceful in its style, with gilded domes, And glittering pinnacles that flash aloft Like giant lances pointed at the sky, And carven windows in their ribbed rows blazing O’er the long colonades. A Mansion large And solid in its seeming still it stands: But inwardly impaired and worn is now The whole huge structure; in its basement most, Knawed through with damps and crumbling unrenewed.

Even that is Castle Carnal: the set place Of much vain speech and simulated mirth, (For true Mirth seldom quits her sylvan lodge) 10 And fleshly feasting. From its unblest door Simplicity and strength, and red-lipped Health, And native Cheerfulness, and staunch Resolve, And Care, and Prudence, and domestic Peace, And Piety who loves with these to dwell, For ever, as of old, they spurn away: And as their household deities as well Have they installed Extravagance in the guise Of Bounty, but in truth the slave of self; And False Refinement, sensually nice But vulgar and indelicate of soul; And Infidelity; and the gamster Craft, Dressed in the airs and habit of a sage; And Atheism, mantled in a garb Fashioned as ‘twere the mantle of a Seer.

So peopled is that House. And now while Thought’s Keen kindled spirit lightens through my brain, Intent to benefit the young, to thee, O Wisdom, do I cry, and thee invoke To aid the voice that I would pour abroad In a melodious though a warning song! So pure the inspiring motive, that twere well Couldst thou inflame it, as it peals along, With lightnings of intelligence, and all Its monitory tones enforce and swell With spirit thunders, such as lost souls hear For ever roaming o’er the quietly Past.

Give heed then, ye who would be early wise. My sober numbers, big with thought, shall be Meet robes for Wisdom in her queenliest plight— Yea, though she sat again enthroned as once With Solomon of old. And know ye not 11 What even the reprobate, though godless, knows; That he who in the morning of his life Good counsel scorns—indubitably good, Shall, a grey prodigal, wander forth with Shame, An vain Remorse, and outcast Poverty, Under the lengthening shadows that portend Its sunset, ending in the dark of death?

Wherefore, with reason, verily I say That froward-hearted youth who shall esteem The Preacher’s words but as the profitless stones Roughening the forehead of some sterile hill, Shall thrive not,—but, a moving pile of rags, In houseless age shall eat the beggar’s dole, Or with stark limbs and aching sinews ply The helot’s axe, or bend beneath his yoke; Or at the mill of never-varying toil Even to the dregs of a brute being grind,— Laboring for others more than for himself. And though he be the exception, not the less Shall he be bondsman to Infirmity: Nay, even though greatness fall within his lot, (Which thing may happen in a graceless age) Yet shall he, as did Bacon, through a need Induced by a vain habit of display, Mush up his glory with the vilest things, And be at once a scoundrel and a lord!

O we should honor Wisdom, since through her The all-contriving One designed and built This boundless world! Yea, she it was who first Elanced the sun on his imperial course, Day’s monarch, vested with celestial fire; Her hand it was that fashioned the round moon 14 And all the golden stars. Or couldst thou soar Up to the throne of God, there were she found In presence, circled with ineffable splendor; Or couldst thou down to the profoundest depths Of Hades plunge, behold! there—even there Wisdom would meet thee, though obscured, as when The day in its meridian strength is seen Darkened with storms, that bellow as they roll.

II.

Again to Castle Carnal! the set place Of much vain speech, much riotous display, And fleshly feasting, and pretensive mirth. There congregate the frivolous of soul As garrulous parrots gather round the snares Set on the borders of the ripening maize. There Frenzy in the name of Earnest Joy, (A spirit native but to honest lives) With wild embarrassed brow, o’erflows the cups With purple ruin, blazing inwardly As it were fiercely conscious of its power, While loose-eyed Passion in the plight of Love Inflames his low and animal fires withal. And there too Gluttony takes his constant round As seneshal, high heaping every board With palatable death.

That fatal House! Shun it all ye in thought yet young, in heart Unguarded, and all ye of facile wills; Lest, like its host, ye with like years should cast To sow the world for an abundant yield Of pleasure,—and mistaken, only reap 15 An overwhelming load of debt instead— Of debt, distaste, and vanity, and vexation.

Once entered there—disastrous is the change Wrought in thy nature: a swift evil change! Thy language loud, speech boisterous;—but alas, Thy reason silent as the tongue of death! Thine evil passions as the surging flames That, like red flags, flap over the wild front Of some wide forest fire,—sultry and fierce As legioned demons, jubilant; but all Thy virtues, like the wintry crust that numbs The living brook till it forgets to flow.

Then ‘neath the growing influence of the fierce And fatal Spirit of that evil House, So recklessly familiar shalt thou grow With splendid Ruin, as to kneel and kiss Her jewelled hand, and say unto her, Thou Shalt be mine only Spouse; and for thy sake Here am I ready to impawn at once The pearls of truth—the sapphires of my peace— The rubies of repute;—yea, all with which I late adorned my soul, for lures and gauds And glittering baubles wherewithal to deck My body, dedicate henceforth to Thee.

Into her inmost secret chambers then Straight shalt thou follow her—desperately fond! While lovely Phantoms beckon thee along. Lovely—but fleeting as the shapes that crest A morning cloud: lovely—but weired, but quick With mischief,—bright for evil, like that wild Mysterious light which nightly glides about 16 Some shrouded marsh, misleading him who sees Into the foul ways of a darkling death. And passionate airs, intense and sweet as those The enthusiast, when to reason dying, hears In mountain glens, or wooded vales, or near Some lone lake crimsoned by the setting sun, Shall hail thy presence, vapouring all the air With sweetness—and awakening raptures sweet And spirit-elating;—but delusive all As are the hopes of unrequited love: Sweet—sweet;—but cruel as untimely death!

Then with the histories of royal lust In her right hand displayed, and in her left A chronicle in which the eminent damned Are registered as wise, thy yielded heart She shall baptise in the lewd mysteries Of fashionable folly; and, at last, Instruct thee, with a devil’s zeal, how best To smother down in thy disnatured soul, As in a quagmire, the prophetic hope That once, with wings like the unwearying stork’s, Would rise and soar from the brute earth away, Through the Eternal and the Infinite, To rest at length within those starry gates That let into the central peace of God.

III.

But who is this that sceptred with a whip, In yon loud chariot cometh flaming on, With costliest raiment clad, and tricked with gauds Of burnished metal all bedropt with gems? 17 Who so obscure as not to — know The sun of Fashion, and the worshipped star Of prodigal pomp and towering excess?

I know of none so styled. But who is this, With glittering ornaments bedizzened so That he himself is lost in their display?

Even he,—that charisteer, is Fashions sun, And the presideing star of Rivelry! Is there a haunt for deep carousals famed, He is dictator there; and where the vain Hold triumph, in the courts of outward show All know him, all admire him, and cry hail!

Wouldst thou that one with influence so pronounced, Should aid the blest and blessing cause of charity; The shoulder shrugging ribald shall reply He loves not a cold mistress. Wouldst thou he Should help prophetic Genius, or assist Poor Learning, labouring in the mines of Truth To enrich a thankless world? The miscreants means Are all though waste encumbered, or forestalled Long heretofore. What if his country’s need Besought her sons to gather like a storm And sweep Invasion from the sacred coasts, Or thunder signal condemnation down An state-craft, and on all internal wrong? Her heed might move him not: for he is one Whose strength long since, of soul and body both, Have been vain-gloriously drenched away In midnight orgies, by the flowing bowl.

Of him, in thine extremity, go seek 18 Condolence and advice:—no search so vain! The veriest frensy shall be froth thee forth. With the high polish of his boasted wit Go burnish thou thy studious front:—Alas! Tis but a state anthology of the jests That celebrate the stews. His usefulness Hath passed already;—fruitless as a stone Sunk in his sterile yesterdays it lies, The buried talent that shall yet condemn His thrifless being with the Lord of Life; His understanding, with like lumber, rots Already in Oblivion—that dim cave Whose vague-rent wastefull mouth stands wide agape Out in the void of unrecorded time.

IV

Then in the House of Riot seek ye not For pleasure, nor for knowledge from its vain And gaudy Paracites, ye young ones, lest A fever of spirit for the first ye catch, And for the other wed a lasting folly! Rather seek wisdom in the House of Woe, And in the rugged ways of Want and Toil, Where Fortune, though severe, is full of truth; Or meditate her amid ruined tombs Charged with the dust of ages,—dust that once Was the warm human beauty of the world.

But not for sourness, or to gloom the soul, In sorrowfull places should we seek her most, And with a solemn heed; but well to know And weigh the value of our earthly lot; 19 And thence be gaurdfull of so much of weal (In substance solid and in measure just) As may befall us, and (if so it chance) Prepared for loss and eaqual to adversity: Deffering all things in their final ends And ultimate fitness, to the will of Heaven. For central peace, and piety, and a sage And serious power for good, and ever found In final union is his lot, who much Hath thought, much suffered, and yet keep through all A simple readiness if faith in God.

Thus far the Preacher warmeth: and although His lot be lowly and his years but few, Despise ye not his word; for he is one Who much hath laboured in the spiced feilds Where grey Experience sows her golden grain; Reapt there for knowledge with a strenuous care, And theshed and ground for exelence of speach. For thought espoused him in his infancy: And when a child, consorted with his mates In the old woods, where the broad Hawksbury past Its reedy marge goes dimpling, he hath looked Upon the careless and unguarded flowers With eyes that felt, though their presageful heed, The comeing hurricane, his pregnant words Have yeilded fruit even to druid Eld Who chanced to hear them as he passed along.
