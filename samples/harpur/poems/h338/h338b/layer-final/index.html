CONTRIBUTIONS TO AUSTRALIAN LITERATURE.

(Being leaves from Charles Harpur’s Wild Bee of Australia.)

No. 6.

I.

NOON, EVENING, AND EARLY NIGHT.

With what content the forest bowers are blest, And streams of coolness warbling, when the breeze Crawls scorchingly at Noon o’er the opprest And browning herbs in the unsheltered leas; But sweeter yet the gradual degrees Of shadowy Eve. when in the dreamy west Cloud-wrought Elysiums hang in golden rest, And glow forth bliss for every eye that sees Then Early Night’s stilly, mysterious mien How grateful; whilst her solemn birds in flight Glide dimly ‘neath the stars, and o’er the scene The moon comes pacing with a step of light. Alone mid these, in memory’s despite, My soul forgets that wrong hath ever been.

Note.—Evening is the Eve of the Poetical Paradise, as morning is its Adam. And as if led into an artistic sense of this by his sure bardic instinct, how markedly, as well as appropriately, has Milton associated our “first mother” with all the mild beauty and sha- dowy softness of its character:—yet not so obviously as to suggest a familiar comparison, nor so directly as to involve a minute one.

All our greater poets, also have felicitous passages expressive of their evening love. But Collins (a minor only as to the quantity and not the quality of his poetry) in direct description has excelled them all. His Ode to Evening has never been equalled, and cannot be surpassed. The closing picture in the following stanzas (the ninth and tenth) has a dusky magic about it, which seems, for the moment, absolutely to superinduce the circumstances so happily selected and described, even though it shall be in the broad noon- tide that we are studying them (all prime poetry requiring to be studied.) The memory-felt spirit of the Eventide seems to pass duskily over the page, and to darken, while passing, into the very letter-press of the verses.

“But when chill blustering winds, or driving rain Forbid my willing feet, be mine the hut That from the mountain side Views wilds and swelling floods, And hamlets brown, and dim discovered spires, And hears their simple bell, and marks o’er all Thy dewy fingers draw The gradual dusky veil“

Then follows at length,—not in the sonnet above, nor in the verse of Collins, but in the great Poem of the Universe, the deep and solemn noon of Night, with its declining moon for an everlasting type of the mortal mystery of change, and its solid darkness for a similitude of death; while far over all, there are the silently shining habitations of space, and the figures and the intimations of the infinite stages of eternity.
