III.

The Pathetic.

Meer Art may make a poem smooth as satin, But compass feeling can meer Art in no man: How frigid is an elegy in Latin Written by any but an ancient Roman?

Note.—The Pathetic in poetry (and in all fiction) is never so truly effectual as when it is perfectly unforced, both in subject and manner. To be supremely exquisite, it must be given to flow naturally out of the matter in hand, not be cast about it like a mourning cloak. Hence whenever a poet takes up his pen of set purpose to search our hearts to the very core, it is more than likely that he will only search our patience to an un- pleasant degree, or disgust us with some bloated mockery of sorrow. Any undertaker can bespangle a coffin with tinsel-headed nails; to bedrop it with tears is another matter, of which Nature, and not Artifice, must be the principal getter-up.

In so much does the truest pathos result from an un- forced treatment of a doleful matter, that we are always most nearly touched by such pathetic descriptions as appear to come in quite incidentally. There is a fine example of this in Taylor’s Philip Van Artevelde. I know of no passage in any author, more sweetly sorrowful; more holy in the moral beauty of its sadness. It is a description of the appearance in death, of two children, who have perished of the famine occasioned by the siege of Ghent, and is spoken by Clara, the sister of the hero:

“He plucked aside the curtain of the couch, And there two children’s bodies lay composed. They seemed like twins of some ten years of age, And they had died so nearly both together, He could not say which first: and being dead, He put them, for some fanciful affection, Each with its arm about the other’s neck, So that a fairer sight I had not seen, Than those two children, with their little faces So thin and wan, so calm, and sad, and sweet. I looked upon them long, and for a while I wished myself their sister, and to lie With them in death as they did with each other. I thought that there was nothing in the world I could have loved so much; and then I wept.”

Again:—a pathetic effect in poetry is never more entire than when, by the seeming abruptness of un- mixed passion, it is left, as it were, somewhat short of itself—suggestively so: or than when, in its means, it is brought bluffly out, and as suddenly terminated, like a natural crag; upon whose rigid verge we are left hanging, in the spontaneous agony of our sympathy, as over some dim, unmeasured profound of loss unspeakable. No doubt this fullness of effect is often thus achieved through the suggestion of art; but then it is true Art, itself the suggestion of Nature. There is a short piece by Wordsworth, which will amply illustrate my position:

“She dwelt among the untrodden ways, Beside the springs of Dove, A Maid whom there were none to praise, And very few to love.

A violet by a mossy stone Half hidden from the eye; Fair as a star. when only one Is shining in the sky.

She lived unknown, and few could know When Lucy ceased to be: But she is in her grave—and oh, The difference to me!“

Here this marvellous little poem ends—or rather breaks off—and here both Nature and Art declare that it should terminate, however abruptly. The entireness of the pathetic effect would be manifestly impaired by the addition of a single word. Thus are true Nature and true Art identical in their essence: and upon this verity, 5b so far as the pathetic is concerned, the Scriptural account of Ruben’s sorrow, upon his missing Joseph out of the pit, whither he had gone for the purpose of restoring him to his father, will impress an attestation sufficiently final:

“And Reuben returned unto the pit, and behold, Joseph was not in it; and he rent his clothes. And he returned to his brethren, and said: The child is not!— and I—whither shall I go?”
