A Sigh for the Forthcoming.

The only faithless thing is Man, Whose evil plan Excludes Man’s brotherhood: All else are trusty to the rod And Sireship of Almighty God, But Man in his lordly mood.

The honest Moon pays down her light In silver bright; The sun in burning gold; The stars above the hills that rise Like thought-informed and earnest eyes, Keep compact as of old.

The birds their pristine glees prolong— The flowery throng Bloom as they bloomed alway; The beasts amid the woods that won 168 Are loyal to their mould, as on Creation’s earliest day.

‘Tis Man alone—dishonest Man, Whose evil plan Excludes all brotherhood! He only, with disnatured mind, Becomes the butcher of his kind: He! in his lordly mood.

‘Tis Man—’tis recreant Man alone, Who builds a Throne, And makes it his belief! Who preaches—only to betray! Who teaches—to enslave or slay! Who joys—at others’ [unclear]

But shall there not be yet a time, When this stern rhyme Shall be no longer true? When Man shall recreate the mood Which guileless kept that brotherhood He erst in Eden knew?

There shall! The very dream that we Might faithful be, Assures us that there shall! 169 When Freedom, to her mission true, Shall commonise the all-grasping Few, So to enoble all

Note. I sometimes think myself fortunate in the worldly experience of the last five years of my life; but at other times, I can scarcely forbear accusing Providence of something akin to malignity in inflicting it upon me: for it has all but ruined my faith in human nature; giving to its whole moral aspect the furtive and cowardly experience of a lie. Pretence—mean, miserable pretence, would indeed seem its universal mainstay and only anchor of rely, whether at sea or in port, at home or abroad! And is there no redemption for it? The cold preachments of mere Church-systems, – the stale ethics and frouthy legalities of mere class schools and colleges,—the meagre moralities of mere conventions, social and political;—these cannot redeem it: they have been tried, and approve themselves, each and all, but a part of its pretensiveness.

Well; and what then remains to be done? Must we even heave it overboard, as a radical deformity, a thing incor Let us first try another remedy.

The brave unpattering habitude of perfect, educated social equality, of absolute, instructed, soul-ennobling individual liberty,—that is to say, entire freedom from all false, fashionable, and juggling necessities, would assuredly work out for it, in all truthful regards, a more effectual and abiding redemption: and let us first try that. To shackle it down as hitherto, is but to cramp and straighten it into falshood. To liberate it thoroughly, were to give it, at all events, its best remaining and most honest chance of escape into Truth.

All lies and shuffling pretences are the impositions of a slavery of some kind; and the perfect truthfulness of man to man, can be born only of perfect liberty—of a perfect and conscious freedom of man from man: in fine, such a measure of liberty, in all directions, to the individual, as shall be bounded only by the co-extensive liberties of all other individuals.

But would an individual Freedom, thus extensive, tend in anywise to relax our sense of social obligations, – such as grow naturally out of our humanity, and are sanctions both by reason and affection? In nowise would it do so. But it would infinitely exalt our moral willinghood. The right dischar of all our true duties would more than ever the conscious glory of our manhood: because, by being separated from all false and mistifying requirements, their essential sacredness would be but the more clearly discernible. They would then act upon us with the directness and energy which are the inevitable attributes of Truth, when it is divested of all “baser matter.” But no obediential neccessities, enjoined by the selfishness of mere authority, could any longer be juggled into the place and dignity of duties. The Text was written on my reading certain articles in Blackwood’s Magazines, in which the writer labors to deduce whatever incapacity Man has hitherto evinced for a state of democracy, from the doctrine of Original Sin. To this, it will be seen, I so far assent as to admit that to some great depravation of his moral nature is owing the fact of his having fallen away from such a state; but I hold at the same time, that the democratic principle is that alone which can eventually restore him to such a measure of social perfection as may be proper to the present stage of his spiritual progress. It is the Alpha and Omega – the first and the last stage of human community: the lost birthright of Equality, upon the regainment of which depends the moral, social, and political salvation of the world. For man must be denied all adventitions rank and power on the one hand, and educated out of all servile respect such obedience to them on the other, before his heart and mind can breathe and act freely, and with perfect social and political integrity.
