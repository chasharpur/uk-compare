Nobility.

There is no gauge of worth Or in fortune or in birth; Nay, kingly state is only in itself a gilded rod: But he who would not slight For his life, the cause of Right, Or choose, to gain a star, the path by splendid Evil trod, Though he lack the power of gold, And his robe be worn and old, He—he is the true Noble, the gentleman of God.

A man may live in state With the wealthy and the great, He may mingle with the gifted, and muster with the brave; Yet if he acts a part That’s Condemned by his own heart, And takes, though from a monarch’s hand, what justice never gave, Or numbers with the throng Who defend a thronëd Wrong, He’s a coward and a traitor, a liar and a slave.

We know that Man is prone To bow down Power alone, Or right or wrong, for earth glooms will cloud on earthy wit; But in his heart, though dark, There yet glows one truthful spark, The kin-spark of the Angel, whose first brightness grows from it, And if he never swerve, From the cause its light would serve, On his high throne of Manhood a sun-born God might sit.

Then let the truth be sown Wheresoever men are grown, That kingly state is only in itself a gilded rod: That he who acts a part That’s Condemned by his own heart, Is treading with a villain’s foot where Honour never trod: While in the lowliest wight Who swerves never from the right, There stands a very Noble—a gentleman of God.
